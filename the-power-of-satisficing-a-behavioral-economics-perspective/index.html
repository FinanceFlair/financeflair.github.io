The Power of Satisficing: A Behavioral Economics Perspective


In the world of economics, most people are either goal-oriented or satisficing. You’re reading a blog post about satisficing — an intriguing concept that’s halfway between “satisfy” and “optimizing.” This behavioral economics perspective leans into the idea that good-enough is often good enough, offering practical wisdom for everyday decision-making.

Introduction to Satisficing

The term “satisficing” doesn’t appear in any economics textbook, but it’s a handy concept that emerged in the 1950s. Dr. Duncan Luce and Dr. John Raiffa, two prominent behavioral economists, developed the idea to address inconsistencies they found in some economic theories. They believed behavior wasn’t always about achieving the best outcome — oftentimes, good-enough would suffice, as people set tolerable limits on what they aimed to achieve.

Satisficing can be seen as an alternative to the two prevalent strategies:

Optimization: This is the process of searching for the best possible solution from available alternatives, a common perspective in traditional economics and game theory. In real life, however, such a fully optimizing approach is rare. Hours and hours of research are needed to optimize every decision, which is impractical for most.

Satisfaction: On the other hand, this strategy is about setting a threshold where you’d be happy with an outcome meeting the minimum requirements. The problem with satisfaction is it lacks direction and might lead to lowering our expectations.

By proposing satisficing, these economists aimed to merge these theories, proposing people would become satisfied once their threshold or “satisficing point” was met.

Applying Satisficing

To understand how satisficing works in various aspects, let’s explore a few common situations:

Jobseeking: A jobseeker doesn’t aim to optimize their job search by viewing every possible posting. Instead, they set a level (salary, job title, location) at which they’ll be content and keep applying or searching until they find a job that fits within that range.

Shopping: When shopping, few people try to find the absolute best price on a product. Instead, they set a boundary (their budget) and aim to satisfy it by selecting the best product that fits within their constraints.

Health & Fitness: Think of a fitness goal like losing weight. Many of us don’t aim to simply become ‘healthy’, instead, we set a number (weight loss), then satisfice by engaging in activities with the hope of reaching that weight by consuming fewer calories, engaging in some physical activities, and monitoring progress.

The Art of Setting Satisficing Points: Behavioral Economics

The key to satisficing lies in setting realistic, yet ambitious, satisficing points. Behavioral economists recommend considering a few factors:

  1. Goals and aspirations: Reframe your goals to reflect a balance between aspiration and feasibility. If your aspirations are too impossible, you may feel stressed, but if they’re too easy, you might not challenge yourself enough. Finding this balance is crucial.

  2. Sunk costs: Factor in the costs so far — time, money, and effort put into something. You should account for these ‘sunk costs’ to avoid throwing good money after bad and settling for a suboptimal path.

  3. Environmental factors: Why stop at one satisficing point when the environment uniquely offers more opportunities to satisfice? Consider expanding your options if they’re there for the taking.

  4. Pseudo-testing: People often make better decisions when visualizing the decision-making process, then reviewing it afterward. Reverse-engineer your reasoning by asking, ‘What factors would influence my decision?’

Conclusion

Satisficing offers an alternative to traditional views on decision-making. By setting a satisficing point and acting upon it, we achieve a healthy balance between good-enough and a perfect outcome. Adopting this behavior can lead to more effective decision-making, less stress and generally, improved quality of living.

In today’s fast-paced world filled with information overload, satisficing serves as a useful tool, even for non-economists. The next time you’re confronted with a decision, consider this behavioral economics perspective and opt for satisficing. You might just find good-enough is, in fact, good enough.
