How to Use the Income Approach in Corporate Valuation


The income approach is one of the most widely recognized methods of estimating the value of a company. Also known as the discounted income approach or the capitalized income method, this technique focuses on a company’s future earnings to value it in the present. Here’s a step-by-step guide on how to put this method to use.

Step 1: Identify the company’s future earnings

The first essential step is to forecast the company’s future earnings. This could be sales revenue, earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization (EBITDA), or any other earnings metric that seems suitable. An accurate estimation of future earnings is crucial for the application of the income approach.

To produce an accurate forecast, analysts often:

  1. Examine the company’s historical financial results.
  2. Assess the trends in the industry.
  3. Gauge market conditions and current economic situation.
  4. Study competitor performances.

Step 2: Determine the terminal value

Terminal value represents the estimated worth of a company at the end of the forecast period. As the income approach primarily focuses on present value, determining the terminal value aids in deriving a more accurate valuation. There are three primary methods to calculate the terminal value: the perpetuity growth method, the exit multiple method, and the book value method.

  1. Perpetuity Growth Method: This method assumes that the company’s earnings will continue indefinitely at a steady rate. The formula for terminal value using this method is: TV = (EBIT x (1 + g)) / (r - g), where EBIT stands for Earnings before Interest and Taxes, g is the growth rate, and r represents the discount rate.

  2. Exit Multiple Method: This method assumes that the company will be sold at the end of the forecast period. In this case, the terminal value is calculated as TV = (EBITDA / E) * M, where EBITDA stands for Earnings before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization, E represents the income tax rate, and M is the exit multiple, often industry-specific.

  3. Book Value Method: This method states that the terminal value should equate to the book value of the company at the end of the forecast period. Hence, TV = BV.

Step 3: Calculate the discounted cash flow

Discounted cash flow refers to the present value of a company’s future earnings. Using the free cash flow (FCF) formula as an example:

FCF = (EBIT + depreciation) - (Capex + tax)

The formula helps analysts calculate the FCF, which is then discounted at an appropriate rate (usually the weighted average cost of capital, WACC). Summing up the discounted cash flows for the entire forecast period offers an estimate of the company’s current worth.

Step 4: Compare and validate with other methods

A competent analysis should use multiple techniques, including the income, market, and asset-based approaches, to arrive at a more accurate assessment of the company’s worth. Comparing the outcomes will highlight any discrepancies, helping you arrive at a reliable valuation.

The Income Approach: A Final Word

The income approach offers a compelling framework for corporate valuation that focuses on future earnings. However, it’s vital to remain attentive to assumptions, methods, and predictive data to ensure a reliable and precise analysis. It’s equally crucial to validate findings with data gathered from other valuation techniques to reduce risk and improve accuracy.
