The 3 Most Essential Cash Flow Metrics Every Business Owner Should Track


As a business owner, it’s crucial to understand how your company’s cash flow works. By keeping track of essential financial metrics, you can make informed decisions, maintain financial stability, and ultimately propel your business towards success.

In this guide, we’ll explore the three most vital cash flow metrics every business owner should track:

  1. Free cash flow
  2. Operating cash flow
  3. Cash conversion cycle

Each of these metrics provides valuable insights into various aspects of your business’s financial health. Let’s delve into each one and understand its significance.

1. Free Cash Flow

Free cash flow is the amount of cash a company generates during a period after accounting for cash operating expenses, investments, and major capital expenditures. Calculating free cash flow requires a clear understanding of your company’s operations and investments.

Free cash flow can be computed using the following formula:

Free Cash Flow = Net Income + Depreciation + Other Non-Cash Expenses - Capital Expenditures - Investments

Tracking your free cash flow allows you to assess your company’s financial performance and capital requirements. By monitoring this metric, you can make informed decisions about investments, acquisitions, and divestitures, ultimately leading to a more profitable and stable business.

2. Operating Cash Flow

Operating cash flow is the cash a company generates from its core business operations, such as selling goods or services. It consists of cash inflows and outflows directly related to daily operations. Understanding your operating cash flow provides insight into how well your business is managing its core activities.

To calculate your operating cash flow, subtract your operating expenses from your operating income. This can be represented in the following formula:

Operating Cash Flow = Operating Income - Operating Expenses

Regularly tracking your operating cash flow enables you to:

  • Assess your operational efficiency
  • Make better decisions about inventory management and pricing strategies
  • Predict upcoming cash needs and plan accordingly

3. Cash Conversion Cycle

The cash conversion cycle (CCC) is a measure of how long it takes for a business to convert its investments in inventory and other operating assets into cash flowing from its customers. A shorter CCC indicates more efficient inventory management and quicker collection of accounts receivable.

To calculate your cash conversion cycle, follow these steps:

  1. Calculate your days inventory outstanding (DIO) by dividing your average inventory by your cost of goods sold (COGS) and multiplying by the number of days in the period.
  2. Calculate your days accounts receivable outstanding (DARO) by dividing your average accounts receivable by your net revenue and multiplying by the number of days in the period.
  3. Add your DIO and DARO to get your CCC.

A shorter CCC signifies better inventory and accounts receivable management, which can result in increased cash flow and better overall financial health.

By tracking these crucial cash flow metrics, you’ll gain a deeper understanding of your business’s financial operations. With this knowledge, you can identify areas for improvement and make informed decisions to optimize your company’s cash flow and ensure its ongoing success. As industry-leading software repositories will always need effective cash flow management, settling for below-average cash flow metrics can lead to missed opportunities or, worse, financial instability.

In conclusion, mastering the three most essential cash flow metrics is essential for any entrepreneur looking to improve their financial wellbeing. Free cash flow, operating cash flow, and cash conversion cycle provide invaluable insights into your business’s financial health, ultimately paving the path towards your company’s Read more about financial success. So, track, analyze, and optimize these metrics to elevate the financial foundation of your business, powering it towards success.
