How to Use ATR for Better Trend Following Entries


When it comes to trading, particularly trend-following strategies, precision in entry timing can make a significant difference in overall performance. One lesser-used but highly effective tool for enhancing your entry points is the Average True Range (ATR). ATR helps traders gauge volatility, enabling them to refine their approach to entering trades during trending markets. In this blog post, we’ll explore how to use ATR to improve your trend-following entries, making your trading both smarter and potentially more profitable.

Understanding ATR: The Foundation

Before diving into practical applications, it’s important to comprehend what ATR represents.

Developed by J. Welles Wilder Jr., the Average True Range measures market volatility by averaging the true range values over a specified period—usually 14 days. The “true range” considers the greatest of three price ranges:

  • Today’s high minus today’s low
  • Absolute value of today’s high minus previous close
  • Absolute value of today’s low minus previous close

By capturing these measurements, ATR gives you an idea of how much price typically moves within a period, regardless of direction.

What makes ATR unique among technical indicators is its focus on volatility, not direction. It’s not a momentum indicator, nor does it suggest trend reversals. Instead, it quantifies market noise and price movement magnitude, which helps traders adjust their position sizes, stops, and entries to reflect current conditions.

Why ATR Matters for Trend Following

Trend following is fundamentally about riding price momentum in a chosen direction while protecting oneself from reversals or excessive drawdowns. Getting into a trade too early can result in whipsaws—price bouncing back and forth around a level before the trend takes off. Conversely, being too late can reduce profit potential.

ATR can refine the entry process in the following ways:

  • Volatility-adjusted entries: Instead of using static entry levels, ATR helps identify dynamic entry points that adapt to current market volatility.
  • Avoiding market noise: Using ATR to filter out entries when volatility is too low or erratic helps prevent getting caught in false moves.
  • Better stop placement: ATR aids in positioning stops far enough away to avoid being stopped out prematurely while still managing risk.

By understanding and applying these principles, trend followers can improve their trade timing and position management.

Step-by-Step Guide: Using ATR for Trend Following Entries

1. Set Up Your ATR Indicator

Most charting platforms have ATR built-in. The default setting is usually a 14-period ATR based on daily prices, which works well for many traders. However, depending on your trading timeframe (intraday, daily, weekly), you may want to adjust the ATR period to smooth or sensitively capture volatility.

2. Identify the Trend Direction

ATR doesn’t signal trends by itself, so begin by establishing the trend direction via other tools—moving averages, price action, or other trend-following indicators like the ADX.

For example, you might use the 50-day and 200-day moving averages as your trend filters: if the 50-day is above the 200-day, you’re in an uptrend and vice versa.

3. Define Entry Levels with ATR Multiples

Instead of entering at a fixed price level, calculate your entries based on ATR multiples. For instance:

  • During an uptrend, consider entering when the price breaks above a recent high plus a fraction or multiple of the ATR (e.g., High + 0.5 × ATR).
  • During a downtrend, enter on a breakdown below a recent low minus a fraction or multiple of the ATR.

This method allows your entries to dynamically adjust to changing volatility. When volatility spikes, your entry thresholds widen, reducing the chance of false breakouts. When volatility contracts, entries become tighter, catching earlier moves.

4. Use ATR to Set Stops

One of the classic applications of ATR in trend following is setting stop-loss levels. A common approach is to place stops at a multiple of ATR away from the entry price (e.g., 1.5 × ATR).

For example, if you enter a long position at 50, and the ATR is 2, a stop might be placed at 50 - (1.5 × 2) = 47. This method respects the current volatility; wider ATRs mean stops sit farther away to prevent premature exit, while tighter ATRs result in closer stops.

5. Position Sizing and ATR

In addition to entries and stops, ATR can guide your position size. The logic is simple:

  • When volatility is high (high ATR), reduce position size to keep risk constant.
  • When volatility is low (low ATR), you can increase your position size safely.

This risk-adjusted sizing prevents overexposure during choppy, volatile conditions and maximizes opportunity during calmer markets.

6. Combine ATR with Price Action Confirmation

While ATR sets dynamic thresholds, it’s still wise to confirm entries with price action patterns. For example, wait for bullish engulfing patterns or clean breakouts above resistance on an ATR-adjusted level before initiating a trade.

7. Adapt for Different Markets and Timeframes

ATR’s utility spans across instruments—from equities to forex, commodities, and cryptocurrencies—but you should calibrate ATR settings for your specific market’s volatility characteristics.

For example, intraday forex timelines might benefit from a shorter period ATR (e.g., 7 periods on a 15-minute chart), while weekly charts on stocks might use longer periods for smoother ATR measures.

Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

  • Ignoring the broader trend: ATR is not a standalone trend indicator. Always use it in conjunction with trend direction tools.
  • Using static multiples blindly: Customize ATR multiples based on historical volatility and personal risk tolerance.
  • Overreacting to ATR spikes: Sudden ATR increases often coincide with news events that can distort typical price behavior. Consider these events when planning entries.
  • Setting stops too close or too wide: Test your ATR-based stop levels with historical data to find multiples that balance between avoiding stop-hunting and protecting capital.

Advanced Techniques with ATR

ATR Trailing Stops

Instead of fixed stops, use ATR to create trailing stops that move with price. For example, trail your stop at a fixed ATR multiple below the highest price achieved after entering a long position. This method locks in profits while allowing the trade to run.

ATR Bands for Entry Zones

Similar to Bollinger Bands, create bands around a moving average using ATR multiples. Entries in the direction of the trend can be triggered when price breaks above (or below) these bands.

Volatility Breakout Strategies

Use ATR to detect volatility squeezes (periods of low ATR) as precursors to big price moves. Entering when volatility expands—confirmed by a price breakout—can be an effective trend-following approach.

Real-World Example: Applying ATR in an Uptrend

Imagine trading a trending stock on a daily chart:

  • The 50-day MA is above the 200-day MA, confirming an uptrend.
  • ATR over the past 14 days is 1.8.
  • Recent swing high is at $100.

Instead of entering immediately on a break above $100, set an entry at $100 + (0.5 × 1.8) = $100.90. Wait for the price to reach $100.90, confirming a more robust breakout adjusted to volatility.

Place your stop loss at entry minus (1.5 × ATR) = $100.90 - 2.7 = $98.20. This stop respects daily price swings, helping avoid exits on normal pullbacks.

Position size is adjusted so that risk (stop distance × size) matches your predefined risk tolerance.

Summary: Why ATR Is a Trend Follower’s Secret Weapon

Incorporating ATR into your trend entry strategy:

  • Helps make entries volatility-sensitive, reducing false signals
  • Improves risk management via dynamic stop placement
  • Enables smarter position sizing to manage risk
  • Assists in defining more precise breakout and pullback levels

Trend following success hinges on timing and risk control, and ATR offers a data-driven means to sharpen both. By adapting your entries and exits to actual market movement magnitudes, you stay aligned with how the market behaves—not how you wish it would.

Experiment with ATR alongside your existing trend filters and refine your methods through backtesting. Over time, this approach can help elevate your trend trading from guesswork to a disciplined, volatility-aware strategy.
