Trend Following and Position Sizing: A Winning Combo


In the realm of trading and investing, surviving and thriving often boils down to a combination of strategy and risk management. Among the myriad approaches, trend following stands out as a time-tested strategy focused on capturing substantial market moves, while position sizing acts as the protective ballast that controls exposure and preserves capital. Together, these two elements form a powerful synergy—a winning combo that can elevate your trading performance and tame the inherent volatility of financial markets.

In this comprehensive post, we’ll unpack the nuances of trend following, delve into the critical importance of position sizing, and explore how coupling these two can create a balanced, effective trading system suited for both new and experienced market participants.

Understanding Trend Following

Trend following is built on a simple yet powerful premise: prices tend to move in recurring directional patterns or “trends.” By identifying and riding these trends instead of trying to predict market turns or reversals, traders can align themselves with the market’s momentum, potentially capturing sizable profits.

The Core Principles

  1. Markets Trend: Financial markets are not purely random; they swing with momentum. Although periods of sideways movement exist, trends emerge and persist over time, often driven by collective investor psychology, macroeconomic factors, or structural shifts.

  2. Ride the Wave: Instead of attempting to pinpoint the exact start or end of a trend—which is notoriously difficult—trend followers aim to enter once a trend is identified and hold their positions until there are clear signs the trend is reversing.

  3. Polarity Over Prediction: The approach favors reacting to market moves rather than trying to predict news or fundamentals. This reliance on price action makes trend following quite adaptable across different asset classes and market conditions.

Popular Trend Following Techniques

  • Moving Averages: Simple Moving Averages (SMA) or Exponential Moving Averages (EMA) are often employed to signal trend direction. For example, a trader might consider an asset “trending” up when the 50-day EMA crosses above the 200-day EMA—commonly referred to as a “golden cross.”

  • Breakouts: Breakouts from established price ranges or consolidation zones often mark the beginning of new trends. Trend followers enter positions when price breaks past key resistance or support levels.

  • Channel Breaks: Using technical tools such as Donchian channels, traders look for price breaking out of a recent high or low to confirm trend momentum.

  • Momentum Indicators: Tools like the Average Directional Index (ADX) help measure trend strength, enabling traders to filter out weak or uncertain trends.

The Psychological Edge

Trend following requires discipline and patience. Losses happen, often repeatedly, before trends emerge. Managing emotions and sticking to predefined rules is imperative for longevity. The strategy naturally embodies a “cut losses fast and let profits run” philosophy—a mindset that separates successful traders from the rest.

The Importance of Position Sizing

While trend following can identify attractive directional opportunities, managing how much of your capital to allocate on each trade is equally critical. Position sizing controls risk, protects your portfolio from significant drawdowns, and ensures you can stay in the game long enough for trends to develop.

What is Position Sizing?

Position sizing is the process by which a trader determines the number of units (shares, contracts, lots, etc.) to purchase or sell, based on the acceptable risk on the trade and the stop-loss distance.

Why Position Sizing Matters

  • Limits Losses: By sizing positions according to risk tolerance and stop-loss levels, you prevent any single trade from crippling your account.

  • Enables Consistency: Consistent position sizing allows for steady growth and reduces the emotional impact of sudden losses.

  • Adapts to Market Volatility: Different assets and trends exhibit various degrees of volatility. Position sizing allows traders to adjust exposure to account for these fluctuations dynamically.

Common Position Sizing Methods

  1. Fixed Dollar Amount: Risk a constant dollar amount per trade regardless of trade specifics. Easy to implement but doesn’t adjust for volatility.

  2. Fixed Percentage of Account: Risk a fixed percentage (e.g., 1-2%) of total account value on each trade. Helps maintain proportional risk.

  3. Volatility-Based Sizing: Adjust position size based on the volatility of the asset, often measured by Average True Range (ATR). Larger ATR means smaller position size.

  4. Kelly Criterion: Uses statistical probabilities of winning and losing trades to optimize position sizing theoretically. While mathematically sound, practical implementation can be risky.

Calculating Position Size

At a basic level, position sizing can be expressed as:

Position Size = (Account Risk Amount) ÷ (Trade Risk per Unit)

Where:

  • Account Risk Amount = Total capital × Risk percentage per trade (e.g., $100,000 × 1% = $1,000)

  • Trade Risk per Unit = Entry price − Stop-loss price

For example, if you’re willing to risk $1,000, with a $10 stop-loss per share, your position size would be 100 shares.

The Synergy of Trend Following and Position Sizing

Individually, trend following offers a directional edge, and position sizing offers risk control. Combine the two, and you create a system that systematically captures upside potential while managing downside risk effectively.

Why This Combo Works

  • Maximizes Profit Potential: Trend following identifies opportunities where momentum is on your side. Correct position sizing lets you capitalize fully without overexposing yourself.

  • Minimizes Emotional Impact: Knowing that each trade risks only a small fraction of your capital enables you to adhere to your trend following signals without fear or hesitation.

  • Enhances Longevity: Markets go through stages—trends, consolidations, and reversals. With sensible position sizing, you can survive losing streaks—a reality in every trend-following strategy—while continuing to seek out the next big move.

  • Adapts to Market Conditions: In highly volatile markets, position sizing automatically scales back exposure, reducing drawdowns. During stable, strong trends, position sizes can be relatively larger to maximize gains.

Real-Life Application and Example

Consider a trend-following strategy on a stock using a 50-day moving average crossover to enter trades, combined with a stop-loss set just below the 50-day EMA to limit risk.

  1. Identify the trending stock with an upward crossover.

  2. Determine your acceptable risk (e.g., 1% of a $50,000 portfolio = $500).

  3. Calculate the distance from entry price to stop-loss (say, $2).

  4. Position size = $500 ÷ $2 = 250 shares.

You enter 250 shares, letting the trend work while knowing you cannot lose more than $500 on this trade.

If a big move materializes, your gains can far outweigh minor losses from earlier trades, exemplifying the famous trend-following mantra: “cut losses short, let winners run.”

Practical Tips for Implementing Trend Following and Position Sizing

1. Stick to a Trading Plan

Discipline is vital. Define your entry and exit rules, risk tolerance, and position sizing formula upfront. Follow this plan rigorously to avoid impulsive decisions.

2. Use Stop Losses Religiously

A critical component of position sizing is the stop-loss. Place it carefully — tight enough to prevent large losses but wide enough to avoid being stopped out by normal market noise.

3. Keep Emotional Bias in Check

Trend following is no silver bullet. It involves waiting for the market to confirm trends, which sometimes means missing early moves or taking small losses frequently. Trust the system.

4. Adjust Dynamically

Don’t be rigid. Volatility or changes in portfolio value warrant adjustments in position size. A stop-loss distance might widen in a more volatile environment, reducing the number of units to maintain risk.

5. Backtest and Forward Test

Thoroughly backtest your trend following rules and position sizing on historical data. Use forward testing or a demo account before committing real capital.

Common Challenges and How to Overcome Them

Challenge 1: Drawdowns During Sideways Markets

Trend following can struggle in choppy, non-trending markets, triggering small losses repeatedly.

Solution: Use filters such as the ADX indicator to avoid entering trades when trends are weak or non-existent and reduce position sizes during such periods.

Challenge 2: Determining Stop-Loss Levels

Too tight leads to premature stops; too wide inflates risk.

Solution: Base stops on technical levels or volatility measures rather than arbitrary distances. For example, below recent swing lows or ATR multiples.

Challenge 3: Position Sizing Complexity

Multiple assets with varying volatilities can complicate sizing.

Solution: Employ volatility-adjusted sizing to keep the risk consistent across different trades.

Conclusion

The marriage of trend following and position sizing is an elegant pairing in trading disciplines. Trend following drives you toward trades with momentum, while position sizing ensures you never overreach and are always protected against unpredictable market swings. When applied correctly, this combo not only improves your win ratio and profitability but also fortifies your psychological resilience.

Building a system around these principles demands dedication, constant learning, and adaptability. Still, the reward is a disciplined approach that can navigate the market’s ebbs and flows with confidence. Whether you’re navigating stocks, futures, forex, or cryptocurrencies, trend following paired with robust position sizing provides a solid foundation on which to build your trading success.

Embrace the dynamic duo. Let the market’s trends guide you, and let prudent position sizing guard your capital. Together, they’re a true winning formula.
