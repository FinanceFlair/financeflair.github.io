How to Build a Diversified Trend Following Portfolio


Trend following is one of the most powerful investment strategies employed by both professional traders and long-term investors alike. It involves capitalizing on established market movements, betting that the current trend (whether bullish or bearish) will continue for a certain period. However, simply following trends isn’t enough to build a robust portfolio. One of the most critical components of successful trend following is diversification. A diversified trend-following portfolio helps reduce risk, enhance returns, and ensure stability during unpredictable market conditions. In this post, we will explore how to build a diversified trend-following portfolio that aligns with your investment goals and risk tolerance.

Understanding Trend Following

Before diving into portfolio construction, it’s essential to understand the fundamentals of trend following. At its core, this strategy involves identifying assets that exhibit a clear upward or downward movement and then making trades based on that trend. The idea is to enter the market early in a trend and ride it until signs of reversal emerge.

There are various ways to identify trends, but the most common tools include moving averages, momentum indicators, and trendlines. The goal is to buy when prices are rising (a bullish trend) and sell when prices are falling (a bearish trend). Successful trend followers use these indicators to make data-driven decisions rather than relying on subjective forecasts.

The Importance of Diversification

Diversification is the practice of spreading your investments across different asset classes, sectors, or markets. It’s a key risk management technique that prevents your entire portfolio from being exposed to a single point of failure. For trend followers, diversification can be especially valuable because trends can be volatile and short-lived. A diversified portfolio helps balance out these fluctuations and ensures that you have multiple sources of potential returns.

In the context of trend following, diversification means not only trading across different asset types (stocks, commodities, bonds, currencies) but also diversifying across multiple timeframes, trend-following strategies, and market conditions. By doing so, you can capture trends in various markets and mitigate the risks associated with unexpected reversals or flat periods in individual markets.

Steps to Building a Diversified Trend Following Portfolio

1. Choose the Right Assets for Your Portfolio

The first step in creating a diversified trend-following portfolio is selecting the assets that will make up your investment mix. This is where your understanding of different markets comes into play. Trend following can be applied to a wide range of assets, including:

  • Stocks: Equities are often the first choice for trend followers, especially large-cap stocks with high liquidity. However, smaller or mid-cap stocks can also offer high potential returns, though they tend to be riskier.

  • Commodities: Commodities like gold, oil, and agricultural products often exhibit strong trends, particularly during periods of economic instability. Commodities can be an excellent way to diversify because they often behave differently from equities.

  • Bonds: Although bonds are typically more stable than stocks, certain bond types, like long-term government bonds, can experience significant price movements during periods of economic change.

  • Currencies: The forex market provides abundant opportunities for trend followers, with currency pairs often experiencing prolonged uptrends or downtrends. Currency pairs are affected by macroeconomic factors, such as interest rates and geopolitical events.

  • Real Estate: Real estate investment trusts (REITs) and other real estate assets can provide trends that are largely driven by interest rates and demographic changes. Real estate can be a solid component in a trend-following portfolio, especially for longer-term trends.

Once you’ve decided on your asset classes, the next step is to analyze their historical performance and volatility. Certain assets might show more consistent trends than others, and this is important for determining how much weight each asset will have in your portfolio.

2. Incorporate Multiple Timeframes

A key principle of trend following is to trade in a manner that matches the timeframe of the trend. Some trends are short-term (lasting weeks or even days), while others may persist for years. By using multiple timeframes, you can increase the probability of capturing profitable trends while balancing risk.

For instance:

  • Short-Term Trends: These trends typically last anywhere from a few days to several weeks. You can capture these by using shorter moving averages, like the 20-day or 50-day moving averages. These trends often appear during periods of high volatility, so they can be more difficult to ride.

  • Medium-Term Trends: Trends in this category may last several months. Medium-term trend-following strategies can utilize tools like the 100-day or 200-day moving averages. They provide a balance between risk and reward and allow you to stay in the market for longer.

  • Long-Term Trends: These trends can last for years. The key to identifying long-term trends is a strong fundamental backdrop, such as economic growth or significant shifts in industry sectors. Long-term trends can often be tracked with simple trendlines or long-term moving averages.

By integrating strategies for each of these timeframes, you’ll create a more resilient portfolio capable of capturing profits from both short-term volatility and long-term growth.

3. Use a Combination of Indicators

Another essential aspect of building a diversified trend-following portfolio is the use of a variety of technical indicators to identify trends. Relying on just one indicator can be risky, as each has its limitations. A diversified approach to indicators can improve the reliability of your signals and help you avoid false trends.

Common indicators for trend following include:

  • Moving Averages: Moving averages smooth out price data and help identify the direction of a trend. Common strategies include using simple moving averages (SMA) or exponential moving averages (EMA) to spot when trends are gaining or losing momentum.

  • Relative Strength Index (RSI): This momentum oscillator measures the speed and change of price movements. It can help you identify overbought or oversold conditions, signaling potential reversals or trend exhaustion.

  • Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD): The MACD is another popular trend-following indicator that tracks changes in the strength, direction, momentum, and duration of a trend. It’s especially useful for confirming the existence of a trend and its likely continuation.

  • Bollinger Bands: These bands help visualize volatility and can signal when an asset is overbought or oversold. Trend followers use Bollinger Bands to identify breakout opportunities and assess whether a trend is likely to continue.

Combining multiple indicators, especially those that work in tandem, increases the robustness of your trend-following system.

4. Adjust Your Portfolio Based on Market Conditions

The market is dynamic, and trends evolve. It’s essential to stay flexible and adjust your portfolio based on changing market conditions. For instance, during periods of high volatility, you might choose to reduce your exposure to riskier assets or increase your cash allocation. On the other hand, during strong bull markets, you might want to increase your exposure to stocks or commodities that are showing strong upward trends.

Additionally, trends are often cyclical. Some asset classes may perform better during certain periods. For example, commodities often perform well during inflationary environments, while stocks may perform better in periods of economic growth. By staying attuned to macroeconomic trends and adjusting your portfolio, you can ensure that your trend-following strategy remains aligned with broader market movements.

5. Manage Risk and Position Sizing

Risk management is crucial in any investment strategy, and trend following is no exception. Since trends can reverse quickly, it’s essential to set stop-loss orders, position size properly, and have an exit strategy in place for each trade.

A common approach to position sizing in trend following is the fixed fractional method, where you risk a fixed percentage of your total capital per trade (for example, 2%). This approach ensures that no single trade will significantly impact your overall portfolio, regardless of how large or small your capital is.

Additionally, you should consider implementing risk management strategies such as:

  • Stop-Loss Orders: These orders automatically close your position if the asset moves against your position beyond a certain threshold, preventing large losses.

  • Trailing Stops: A trailing stop moves with the market, locking in profits as the trend moves in your favor while protecting you if the trend reverses.

  • Diversification: As previously mentioned, diversification is essential for mitigating risk in trend following. By spreading your investments across different assets and strategies, you reduce the likelihood that a single market downturn will wipe out your portfolio.

Conclusion

Building a diversified trend-following portfolio requires careful planning, selection of the right assets, use of multiple indicators, and strong risk management techniques. By diversifying across different asset classes, timeframes, and strategies, you can position yourself to capitalize on a variety of market trends while minimizing the impact of volatility. Remember, the key to success in trend following is staying disciplined, flexible, and patient, allowing the trends to develop and ride them to their full potential.
