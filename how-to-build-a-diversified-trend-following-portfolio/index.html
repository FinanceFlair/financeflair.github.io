How to Build a Diversified Trend Following Portfolio


Trend following is a time-tested approach to investing that seeks to capitalize on sustained movements in price, whether those are upward or downward trends. Unlike strategies focused on predicting market reversals or company-specific fundamentals, trend followers ride the waves of momentum, aiming to capture profits as trends develop and persist. But success with trend following often depends on constructing a well-diversified portfolio that spreads risk across a variety of markets and instruments.

Creating a diversified trend following portfolio involves more than just picking a handful of trending assets. It requires a strategic blend of market exposures, systematic risk management, and consistent discipline. In this comprehensive guide, we will explore the principles behind trend following, the methodology to select and combine assets, and the steps to build a robust, diversified portfolio that can weather different market environments.

Understanding the Basics of Trend Following

Before diving into portfolio construction, it’s vital to understand what trend following entails. This strategy relies on identifying and following market trends—periods during which prices persistently move in one direction. These can last for weeks, months, or even years. The underlying idea is that assets that are trending are more likely to continue that trend than to reverse suddenly.

Trend followers use various technical indicators such as moving averages, breakout patterns, and momentum oscillators to guide their decision making. The strategies can be applied across asset classes, including equities, commodities, currencies, and bonds. One key principle is to let profits run by staying in the trade as long as the trend holds and to cut losses quickly when it reverses.

The Importance of Diversification in Trend Following

Diversification is a foundational concept in investing, designed to spread risk and reduce the impact of any single losing position. Although trend following strategies inherently involve systematic trading rules, they still benefit greatly from diversification. Here’s why:

  • Market Volatility Differs by Asset Class: While one market may be trending strongly, another might be range-bound or volatile. Diversification allows exposure to multiple markets where trends might be emerging at different times.

  • Reduced Portfolio Drawdowns: If one asset experiences a significant drawdown, other trending assets can help offset losses, smoothing overall portfolio performance.

  • Capturing Trends Everywhere: Trends are a universal phenomenon. By including a range of assets, you increase the chances of riding profitable trends wherever they occur.

  • Risk Reduction Through Low Correlations: Some markets have low or negative correlations with each other, which can stabilize returns when combined in a portfolio.

Selecting Markets for a Diversified Portfolio

The first step in building a diversified trend following portfolio is market selection. A broad and well-thought mix of markets is crucial to capture a wide array of trends over time. Here are common asset categories and considerations when including them:

1. Equities

Stock markets globally provide countless trading opportunities across sectors, countries, and company sizes. Key indexes or ETFs tracking broad markets like the S&P 500, NASDAQ, FTSE, or emerging markets indices can serve as trend following instruments.

  • Advantages: Usually have high liquidity and good trend persistence.
  • Considerations: Equity markets can be influenced by macroeconomic factors and often correlate during stress periods.

2. Commodities

Commodities such as oil, gold, silver, agricultural products, and industrial metals provide exposure to physical goods. These often trend based on supply-demand imbalances, geopolitical events, and seasonal factors.

  • Advantages: Commodities often have different drivers than equities, providing diversification benefits.
  • Considerations: Volatility can be high; storage and cost structures matter if trading futures.

3. Currencies (Forex)

The foreign exchange market is the largest and most liquid market globally. Currency pairs trend due to interest rate differentials, economic data releases, and geopolitical events.

  • Advantages: High liquidity and 24-hour trading.
  • Considerations: Currency markets can be influenced by central bank policies and macro shifts.

4. Fixed Income

Bond markets, including government bonds, corporate bonds, and interest rate futures, can offer diversification as their behavior often contrasts equities.

  • Advantages: Lower volatility and sometimes negative correlation with stocks.
  • Considerations: Bonds may not trend as strongly or as frequently as other assets.

5. Alternative Assets

Other instruments like cryptocurrencies or real estate investment trusts (REITs) may also be added, though they require specialized consideration due to liquidity or regulatory factors.

Building the Portfolio: Asset Allocation and Weighting

Once you select which markets to include, the next critical decision is how to allocate capital across them. The goal is to maximize exposure to profitable trends while controlling overall portfolio risk.

Equal Weighting

Assigning equal weights to each asset or market simplifies portfolio management and ensures no single market dominates. This approach can work well especially when markets are carefully chosen to be lowly correlated.

Risk Parity

This method weights assets based on their volatility so that each contributes equally to overall portfolio risk. For example, more capital might be allocated to less volatile assets like government bonds and less to riskier commodities.

Dollar Volatility Targeting

Some trend following funds adjust exposure dynamically based on volatility estimates, increasing size in stable markets and decreasing in turbulent ones to maintain a consistent risk profile.

Dynamic Allocation Based on Trends

A more active approach involves shifting weightings toward assets currently exhibiting strong trends while reducing exposure to non-trending or weakly trending assets. This can improve returns but requires careful risk management to avoid overconcentration.

Developing Entry and Exit Rules

Trend following is most effective when combined with systematic rules that determine when to enter or exit trades. Common methods include:

  • Moving Average Crossovers: For example, buying when a short-term moving average crosses above a longer-term moving average, and selling when it crosses below.

  • Breakouts: Entering long positions when price breaks above a defined resistance level, or short when it breaks below support.

  • Volatility-Adjusted Signals: Incorporating volatility to filter out false trends during choppy markets.

Regardless of the method, consistency and discipline in following the system are essential to capturing trends over time.

Risk Management in a Trend Following Portfolio

Managing risk is as vital as detecting trends to avoid devastating losses. Key principles include:

  • Position Sizing: Limit exposure on each trade to a fraction of total capital, often based on recent volatility.

  • Stop Loss Orders: Automatically exit positions when price moves against the trend beyond a defined threshold.

  • Maximum Drawdown Controls: Set limits on total portfolio drawdowns to trigger rebalancing or pause new entries.

  • Regular Rebalancing: Adjust weights and exposures periodically to maintain diversification and risk targets.

Handling Drawdowns and Psychological Discipline

Trend following can produce periods of underperformance, especially during sideways markets. Accepting drawdowns as part of the process and maintaining confidence in the system is critical.

  • Backtesting: Reviewing historical performance to understand typical drawdowns helps manage expectations.

  • Avoiding Overtrading: Sticking to the rules prevents chasing false trends or reacting emotionally.

  • Continuous Review: Monitoring the portfolio and market conditions allows adaptation without abandoning discipline.

Monitoring and Adjusting the Portfolio

Trend following requires ongoing monitoring for changing market dynamics. This doesn’t mean frequent trading but rather:

  • Evaluating whether the portfolio remains diversified as market correlations shift.

  • Adding or removing markets if certain instruments no longer contribute to diversification or trend capture.

  • Periodically reviewing risk parameters and adjustments as volatility regimes change.

Tools and Platforms Used for Trend Following

Implementing a diversified trend following portfolio can be enhanced with technology:

  • Trading Platforms with Automated Strategies: Many brokers offer tools to implement moving average or breakout strategies automatically.

  • Portfolio Analytics Software: To track performance, risk, and correlations effectively.

  • Market Data Feeds: Accurate, real-time data is critical for timely entry and exit decisions.

  • Backtesting Frameworks: To refine and validate trend following rules over historical data.

Advantages of a Diversified Trend Following Portfolio

  • Smoother Returns: Reduces volatility by spreading risk.

  • Exposure to Multiple Opportunities: Captures trends across various market cycles and conditions.

  • Risk Mitigation: Limits impact of any single market crash or event.

  • Systematic Approach: Removes emotional decision-making common in discretionary trading.

Potential Challenges to Be Aware Of

  • Complexity: Managing many markets and signals can become operationally demanding.

  • Slippage and Costs: Frequent trading across asset classes may result in high transaction fees.

  • Market Regime Changes: Some environments are less conducive to trend persistence.

  • Data Quality: Poor market data can lead to misleading signals.

Summary and Final Thoughts

Building a diversified trend following portfolio combines the art and science of investing. It requires selecting a broad range of markets that complement each other, implementing systematic entry and exit rules, and applying disciplined risk management. The beauty of this strategy is its universality—opportunities arise in many asset classes and geographies, allowing for a resilient portfolio that can navigate various market cycles.

While trend following isn’t a get-rich-quick scheme and involves periods of drawdown and uncertainty, its mechanical, evidence-backed approach offers a compelling alternative or complement to traditional buy-and-hold strategies. Investors willing to embrace its disciplined mindset and diversify thoughtfully stand to benefit from the persistence and momentum that drive financial markets over time.
