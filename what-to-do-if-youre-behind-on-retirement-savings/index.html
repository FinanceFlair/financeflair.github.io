What to Do if You're Behind on Retirement Savings


Retirement is an essential goal for most individuals, and the earlier you start saving, the better. However, if you find yourself behind on retirement savings, you’re not alone. Many people struggle to set aside enough for retirement, whether due to life circumstances, unexpected expenses, or simply not knowing where to start. The good news is, it’s never too late to take action and put yourself back on track to a financially secure retirement. In this post, we will discuss practical steps you can take to get back on course, how to manage your savings efficiently, and how to make the most of your time, no matter how far behind you feel.

Step 1: Assess Your Current Financial Situation

The first step in catching up on retirement savings is to take a close look at where you currently stand. You need to know how much you have saved, how much you owe, and what your projected retirement expenses will be. This process involves gathering details about your:

  • Retirement accounts: Review your 401(k), IRA, or other retirement accounts. How much have you contributed so far? Are you taking full advantage of employer contributions, if available?
  • Other savings and investments: Do you have additional savings in taxable accounts or brokerage accounts that can be used for retirement?
  • Debt levels: Consider any outstanding debts, especially high-interest debt such as credit card balances. Paying off these debts should be a priority because it frees up more money for your retirement contributions.
  • Income and expenses: Take a detailed look at your income versus your monthly expenses. Are there any areas where you can reduce spending in order to save more for retirement?

By performing this financial audit, you’ll gain clarity on your current situation and be able to identify areas where you can make improvements.

Step 2: Calculate How Much You Need for Retirement

Once you know where you stand financially, the next step is to estimate how much you will need for retirement. This is a critical aspect of retirement planning because it helps you set concrete goals.

Here are some key factors to consider:

  • Retirement age: Decide when you want to retire. The earlier you retire, the more you’ll need to save.
  • Life expectancy: Plan for a retirement that could last several decades. It’s generally recommended to assume you’ll live into your 90s, though this varies depending on your family history and lifestyle.
  • Desired lifestyle: Do you want to travel, live in a luxurious home, or maintain a more modest lifestyle? Your desired lifestyle will impact how much money you’ll need to live comfortably in retirement.
  • Healthcare costs: Don’t overlook the cost of healthcare in retirement. As you age, healthcare costs are likely to increase, and you’ll want to factor in long-term care and insurance premiums.
  • Inflation: Inflation erodes the purchasing power of money over time, so you’ll need to account for it when estimating future retirement needs.

How to Estimate Your Retirement Needs

A common rule of thumb is that you’ll need around 70-80% of your pre-retirement income to maintain your standard of living. However, this figure may not be accurate for everyone. For instance, if you have a mortgage paid off or fewer expenses, you might need less.

To calculate the exact amount, use a retirement calculator, which can help you estimate how much you should save monthly or annually based on your desired retirement age, income needs, and lifestyle. Alternatively, consult with a financial planner who can help you develop a personalized plan.

Step 3: Increase Your Savings Rate

One of the most effective ways to catch up on retirement savings is to increase the amount of money you are saving each month. This might feel challenging at first, but even small changes can have a significant impact over time. Here’s how to boost your savings rate:

Maximize Contributions to Retirement Accounts

  • 401(k): If your employer offers a 401(k) plan with matching contributions, make sure you’re contributing enough to take full advantage of this match. This is essentially “free money” that can accelerate your savings. Try to increase your contribution by 1-2% each year.
  • IRA: If you’re eligible, consider opening a Traditional or Roth IRA. These accounts offer tax benefits and can help your money grow faster. For 2025, the contribution limits for IRAs are $6,500 per year (or $7,500 if you’re 50 or older).
  • Catch-up Contributions: If you’re over 50, you can make catch-up contributions to your retirement accounts, such as an additional $1,000 to an IRA or $6,500 to a 401(k). These catch-up contributions can make a big difference in reaching your retirement goals.

Automate Your Savings

Set up automatic transfers from your checking account to your retirement accounts. By automating your savings, you ensure that you are consistently contributing, even when life gets busy. Treat your retirement savings like any other bill that needs to be paid, and prioritize it.

Adjust Your Budget to Save More

Review your budget to find areas where you can cut back. This might mean reducing discretionary spending on things like entertainment, dining out, or vacations. Use the extra savings to increase your retirement contributions.

If you’re struggling to find areas to cut back, consider speaking with a financial advisor who can help you prioritize your spending and set up a budget that aligns with your retirement goals.

Step 4: Invest Wisely

It’s not just about how much you save but also how you invest your money. Proper investment choices can help grow your savings more efficiently over time. Here are some tips for smarter investing:

Diversify Your Portfolio

Diversification is a key principle of investing. Instead of putting all your savings into one type of asset (such as stocks or bonds), spread your investments across different asset classes. This will help reduce risk and improve the chances of higher returns over the long term.

You can diversify through a mix of stocks, bonds, index funds, real estate, and other investment vehicles. Consider using low-cost index funds or exchange-traded funds (ETFs), which are designed to track the performance of a broad market index and offer broad exposure with minimal fees.

Understand Your Risk Tolerance

As you get closer to retirement, you may want to adjust your asset allocation to reflect a more conservative approach. However, if you’re still many years away from retirement, a more aggressive portfolio can offer higher growth potential. Knowing your risk tolerance is crucial when deciding on the appropriate mix of investments.

Take Advantage of Tax-Advantaged Accounts

Tax-deferred growth in accounts like 401(k)s and IRAs can give you a significant advantage. By contributing to these accounts, your money grows without being taxed until you withdraw it in retirement, allowing your savings to compound faster.

Step 5: Consider Delaying Retirement

If you’re significantly behind on retirement savings, delaying retirement might be a smart option. By working for a few more years, you can continue to earn income, save more, and let your investments grow. Here are some benefits of delaying retirement:

  • More time to save: The more years you have to save, the more money you can accumulate.
  • Social Security benefits: If you wait until after your full retirement age to start receiving Social Security benefits, you’ll receive a larger monthly payment. The longer you wait (up until age 70), the more you can collect.
  • Employer benefits: If you’re still working, you may be able to continue receiving employer-sponsored health insurance or other benefits that can help reduce costs in retirement.

Delaying retirement doesn’t mean you have to work full-time forever. Many people choose to gradually reduce their working hours or take on part-time or freelance work in retirement, which can help supplement their savings.

Step 6: Seek Professional Help

Retirement planning can be complicated, especially if you’re trying to catch up. A financial advisor can help you develop a plan that’s tailored to your situation. They can provide guidance on everything from investment strategies to tax planning to help you make the most of your time and resources.

When choosing an advisor, look for someone with experience in retirement planning and who works on a fee-only basis. This ensures they have your best interests at heart, rather than earning commissions on product sales.

Conclusion

While being behind on retirement savings can be stressful, it’s important to remember that it’s never too late to start making changes. By taking the right steps—assessing your finances, increasing your savings rate, investing wisely, considering delaying retirement, and seeking professional advice—you can put yourself on a path to financial security in retirement. Every little step you take now can add up to significant progress over time, so don’t hesitate to start today!
