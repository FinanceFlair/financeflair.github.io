Tax Planning for New Parents: Understanding Child Tax Benefits


Having a child is one of the most exciting and transformative experiences in life. However, alongside the joy comes a variety of new financial responsibilities. For new parents, understanding how to manage these financial changes, especially when it comes to taxes, is crucial. One of the most significant areas where new parents can save money is through child tax benefits. These benefits can significantly reduce your tax liability and help alleviate the financial burden of raising a child. In this blog post, we’ll explore the ins and outs of tax planning for new parents, focusing on the child tax benefits available to you.

The Importance of Tax Planning for New Parents

When you welcome a new child into your family, your financial landscape will change. There are more expenses to consider, from diapers to healthcare costs to education savings. However, the tax system provides a variety of credits and deductions that can help you reduce your taxable income and lower the amount of taxes you owe. Understanding these options and planning ahead can save you money and reduce financial stress during a time that’s already full of changes.

Tax planning, in this case, involves organizing your finances in a way that maximizes your child-related benefits, minimizes your tax liabilities, and ensures that you’re fully compliant with tax laws. It’s not only about reducing your tax burden but also about taking full advantage of the tax credits and deductions designed specifically for families with children.

Understanding Child Tax Credits

One of the main tax benefits available to parents is the Child Tax Credit. This credit directly reduces the amount of taxes you owe, making it one of the most valuable tax breaks for new parents. However, not everyone is eligible for the full amount, and there are income limits to consider.

What is the Child Tax Credit?

The Child Tax Credit (CTC) allows parents to claim a certain amount per qualifying child under the age of 17. As of recent tax reforms, the amount you can claim per child is up to $2,000, but not all of this is refundable, meaning that if your tax liability is less than the credit, you may not receive the full refund. However, up to $1,400 of the credit can be refunded to you, depending on your income.

Eligibility for the Child Tax Credit

To qualify for the Child Tax Credit, you must meet certain criteria. The child must be:

  • Under the age of 17 at the end of the tax year
  • A U.S. citizen, U.S. national, or U.S. resident alien
  • Your dependent, meaning you provide more than half of their financial support

Additionally, there are income limits for the Child Tax Credit. The credit begins to phase out when your modified adjusted gross income (MAGI) exceeds certain thresholds. For married couples filing jointly, the phaseout begins at $400,000 of MAGI, and for single filers, it starts at $200,000.

How to Claim the Child Tax Credit

When filing your tax return, you will report your qualifying children on Form 1040. The Child Tax Credit is then calculated automatically based on your reported income and number of eligible children. If you qualify for the refundable portion of the credit, it will be applied to your tax refund.

Additional Child Tax Benefits

Aside from the Child Tax Credit, there are other tax benefits that parents may be eligible for. These can further reduce your tax liability or help you save for the future. Let’s dive into some of these additional child-related tax benefits.

The Additional Child Tax Credit (ACTC)

The Additional Child Tax Credit is a refundable portion of the Child Tax Credit. If your tax liability is less than the credit you are entitled to, you may be able to receive a refund of up to $1,400 per child, depending on your income and other factors. This is an important benefit for parents who have little or no federal income tax liability but still need assistance.

Child and Dependent Care Credit

For working parents who incur expenses for the care of their children, the Child and Dependent Care Credit can help offset some of these costs. This credit allows you to claim a percentage of the money you spend on care for children under the age of 13 while you work or look for work. The percentage varies based on your income, but it can be up to 35% of qualifying expenses, with a maximum of $3,000 for one child or $6,000 for two or more children.

To claim the credit, you need to report the care provider’s information on your tax return, and the care must meet certain criteria. This credit is especially beneficial for parents who rely on daycare services or other forms of child care to balance their work and family obligations.

Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)

The Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) is another tax benefit that is available to low- and moderate-income working parents. The amount of the credit depends on your income, number of children, and filing status. Unlike the Child Tax Credit, the EITC is designed to benefit lower-income families, and it is refundable, meaning that if the credit exceeds your tax liability, you will receive the difference as a refund.

The EITC can provide significant financial relief for parents, especially those with multiple children. However, it’s important to note that the EITC has specific income requirements, and the credit amount decreases as your income rises.

Adoption Credit

If you’ve recently expanded your family through adoption, you may qualify for the Adoption Credit. This credit is designed to help offset the high costs of adopting a child. The credit can cover adoption expenses such as legal fees, court costs, and adoption agency fees. As of recent tax reforms, the Adoption Credit can be as much as $14,440 per child, though the amount is subject to income limits and may phase out for higher earners.

If the adoption credit exceeds your tax liability, it is refundable in some cases. Be sure to keep thorough records of your adoption expenses to substantiate your claim.

Deductions for Parents with Children

In addition to credits, there are several tax deductions that can help lower your taxable income. Deductions reduce the amount of income that is subject to tax, which can ultimately lower the amount of taxes you owe.

Dependent Exemption

While the personal exemption for dependents was eliminated starting in 2018, parents can still claim the Child Tax Credit and other tax benefits for their dependents, which can achieve a similar effect by lowering the amount of taxable income.

Standard Deduction

The standard deduction is the amount of income that is automatically deducted from your taxable income. While it’s not specific to parents, families with children can take advantage of the higher standard deduction for married couples filing jointly. For example, the standard deduction for 2025 is $27,700 for married couples filing jointly. This deduction can significantly reduce your taxable income, which ultimately reduces your overall tax liability.

Medical Expenses Deduction

If you incur significant medical expenses for your child, you may be able to deduct those expenses if they exceed a certain percentage of your adjusted gross income (AGI). For 2025, the threshold for deducting medical expenses is 7.5% of your AGI, meaning you can only deduct expenses that surpass this amount. However, medical costs related to pregnancy, childbirth, and child healthcare can quickly add up, and this deduction can provide much-needed relief.

Saving for College and Tax Benefits

As you plan for your child’s future, it’s important to consider saving for their education. There are tax-advantaged accounts available to help you save for college, which can provide additional benefits down the road.

529 College Savings Plans

A 529 Plan is a tax-advantaged account that allows you to save for your child’s education expenses. While contributions to a 529 plan are not deductible on your federal tax return, the earnings grow tax-free, and withdrawals used for qualified education expenses are also tax-free. This makes 529 plans a powerful tool for parents looking to save for college without paying taxes on the growth of their savings.

Coverdell Education Savings Accounts

Another option is the Coverdell Education Savings Account (ESA), which allows you to contribute money to an account that can be used for educational expenses. Unlike 529 plans, Coverdell ESAs allow for tax-free distributions for a broader range of education-related expenses, including K-12 expenses. However, there are contribution limits, and the amount you can contribute is phased out for higher-income earners.

Conclusion: Tax Planning for New Parents

As a new parent, managing your finances can feel overwhelming, but with proper tax planning, you can reduce your tax liability and ensure that you’re taking full advantage of the child tax benefits available to you. From the Child Tax Credit to the Earned Income Tax Credit, child and dependent care credits, and education savings plans, there are numerous opportunities to ease the financial burden of raising a child.

By understanding these tax benefits, staying organized, and planning ahead, you can maximize your tax savings and make your family’s financial future more secure. Be sure to consult with a tax professional to ensure you’re making the most of these tax benefits and that your tax filing is accurate and efficient. The right tax strategy can help you enjoy the financial peace of mind you deserve as you embark on this exciting new chapter of parenthood.
