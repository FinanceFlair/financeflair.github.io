How Geopolitical Events Influence Exchange Rate Fluctuations


Have you ever wondered why the exchange rate between currencies fluctuates? While several factors contribute to these fluctuations, geopolitical events play a vital role. Central banks, governments, investors, and economists monitor these forces, adjusting strategies accordingly. In this blog post, we’ll delve into the realm of international politics and forex, examining how these events affect the exchange rate.

Political Instability

Political instability in a country is often linked to fluctuations in that nation’s currency. Investors and traders seek stability and predictability. When a nation faces political unrest or turmoil, investors pull their funds from that market, causing a decrease in demand for that currency. This decreased demand drives down its value. For instance, during the Arab Spring revolution in Egypt, many investors chose to withdraw their investments. This move caused fluctuations in the Egyptian pound and undermined its value.

Political events also influence perspectives on currencies. For instance, some investors prefer stable economies with lower political risks, leading them to the Swiss franc or German Deutsche Mark. Conversely, others may be wary of currencies from countries prone to political instability.

Trade Relations and Sanctions

Trade between nations is an essential determinant of currency value. Countries with stronger trade relations tend to have more prominent currencies. When two nations enjoy robust business ties and a sound political relationship, their currencies’ values often align. Trade partnerships also influence decisions regarding sanctions. Sanctions are restrictions imposed on trade by one nation against another due to various reasons, e.g., human rights violations or national security concerns.

Sanctions can significantly impact exchange rates. For example, if the United States imposed sanctions on Russia, U.S. dollar inflows would decrease, driving down the value of the Russian ruble. Likewise, the Russian ruble would strengthen against the dollar. Similarly, Chinese investments in African countries have had a considerable impact on the value of various African currencies.

Inflation and Currency Devaluation

Inflation is a crucial factor impacting currency values. Inflation refers to the increase in the price of goods and services over time. Inflation devalues a currency, as it erodes purchasing power. Governments that experience high inflation may devalue their currency to offset the price increase, making their exports more competitive.

Geopolitical events, such as wars or natural disasters, can significantly affect inflation. For instance, the 2014-2015 steep fall in oil prices heavily impacted the economies of oil-exporting nations, leading to currency declines.

Currency Wars

A currency war occurs when a government systematically devalues its currency to gain a competitive advantage in international trade. During the 2010s, there were “currency wars” among several emerging market countries including Brazil, India, South Korea, and Taiwan. In these cases, the central banks applied quantitative easing – a combination of loose monetary policies that aim to increase liquidity and stimulate the economy – to weaken their currencies.

These currency wars create unpredictable forex environments, resulting in exchange rate fluctuations. They can be especially unpredictable when multiple nations engage in such practices simultaneously.

Conclusion

Geopolitical events’ impact on exchange rates is a vast and complex topic. Factors such as political stability, trade relations, inflation, and currency wars influence these fluctuations. Understanding these dynamics can help investors make informed decisions and central banks govern appropriately.

Avoiding such shifts requires knowledge of international politics and foresight. While complexity may breed confusion, recognizing these elements can provide insightful perspective on currency valuation changes driven by geopolitical incidents.
