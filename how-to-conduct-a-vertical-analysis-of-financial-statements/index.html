How to Conduct a Vertical Analysis of Financial Statements


Understanding financial statements is key to making informed business decisions. One commonly used method of measuring financial performance is vertical analysis, which provides a detailed picture of an organization’s financial standing by analyzing each item as a percentage of a base figure within a specific financial statement. This approach is straightforward, yet its insights can be incredibly powerful in helping identify trends, inefficiencies, and opportunities.

In this guide, we’ll walk you through the practical steps to conduct a vertical analysis of financial statements, explore its advantages, and discuss how this process can help you interpret financial data for better decision-making.

Introduction to Vertical Analysis

Vertical analysis, also known as common-size analysis, breaks down a financial statement by expressing every line item as a percentage of a base figure. In an income statement, the base figure is typically total revenue, while in a balance sheet, the base figure is often total assets. This method enables easy comparison across time periods or with peers, regardless of absolute differences in size.

The benefit of vertical analysis lies in its simplicity and ability to standardize financial data, making it optimal for benchmarking and internal assessments. By converting financial data into percentages, organizations can identify how resources are allocated, assess cost structures, and determine the financial efficiency of operations.

Steps to Conduct a Vertical Analysis

1. Choose the Financial Statement

Vertical analysis can be performed on the income statement, balance sheet, or cash flow statement. Start by selecting the statement you want to analyze based on your objectives.

  • For profitability assessment, focus on the income statement.
  • To evaluate solvency or liquidity, analyze the balance sheet.
  • To track cash inflows and outflows, review the cash flow statement.

2. Identify the Base Figure

Each financial statement has a unique base figure that serves as the denominator for every calculation.

  • For the income statement: Use total revenue (also called net sales) as the base figure.
  • For the balance sheet: Use total assets or total liabilities and equity, as they both represent the accounting equation.
  • For certain sections of the cash flow statement, the base might differ depending on the organization’s focus.

3. Convert Line Items to Percentages

For each item on the financial statement, divide its value by the base figure and multiply by 100 to express it as a percentage. Here’s the formula:

Percentage = (Line Item Amount / Base Figure) × 100

For example, if an expense in an income statement is $50,000 and total revenue is $500,000, its percentage is:

(50,000 ÷ 500,000) × 100 = 10%

This calculation is repeated for each line item on the financial statement.

4. Analyze the Results

The percentages obtained offer insights into the composition of revenues, expenses, assets, liabilities, or equity. Analyze these percentages with the following considerations in mind:

  • Consistency: Are the percentages consistent over time, or do they show trends?
  • Industry Benchmarks: How do the percentages compare with industry standards or competitors?
  • Budget Alignment: Are the percentages aligned with organizational goals and budgets?

Example of Vertical Analysis

To better understand this process, let’s perform a vertical analysis on a simplified income statement. Below is an example:

Income Statement

  • Revenue (Base Figure): $1,000,000
  • Cost of Goods Sold (COGS): $600,000
  • Gross Profit: $400,000
  • Operating Expenses: $200,000
  • Net Income: $200,000

Vertical Analysis (% of Revenue)

  • COGS: ($600,000 ÷ $1,000,000) × 100 = 60%
  • Gross Profit: ($400,000 ÷ $1,000,000) × 100 = 40%
  • Operating Expenses: ($200,000 ÷ $1,000,000) × 100 = 20%
  • Net Income: ($200,000 ÷ $1,000,000) × 100 = 20%

From this analysis, we see that 60% of revenue is spent on COGS, leaving a 40% gross profit margin, of which half is used to cover operating expenses. The net income of 20% reflects the company’s profitability post-expenses.

Benefits of Vertical Analysis

1. Trend Identification

By standardizing financial data, vertical analysis makes it easy to compare percentages across multiple time periods. Trends often become more visible, such as a gradual increase in operating expenses that might erode profit margins if left unchecked.

2. Cross-Company Comparisons

Percentages eliminate the noise of scale, enabling businesses of varying sizes to be directly compared. For example, two companies can assess their cost structures relative to revenue, even if one generates $1 million in revenue and the other $1 billion.

3. Pinpoints Opportunities and Risks

Vertical analysis can highlight areas that deserve attention, such as a high percentage of revenues spent on certain expenses. For instance, if two competitors allocate vastly different percentages to marketing, it warrants investigation into the effectiveness of those investments.

4. Facilitates Benchmarking

Organizations can use vertical analysis to benchmark against industry standards or best practices. Identifying alignment or deviations from the norm can serve as a foundation for process improvements or strategic adjustments.

Limitations of Vertical Analysis

Though invaluable, vertical analysis does have its limitations. It does not provide absolute figures, which can sometimes be necessary for decision-making. Additionally, the tool is less effective when comparing entities with vastly different business models or balance sheet structures. To gain a comprehensive understanding, vertical analysis should often be paired with other analyses, such as horizontal analysis or ratio analysis.

Tips for Best Practices in Vertical Analysis

  1. Use Multiple Periods for Trend Analysis
    Analyzing a single year only provides a snapshot. To fully understand financial performance, compare percentages over several periods to recognize patterns or anomalies.

  2. Incorporate Peer and Industry Comparisons
    Make your findings more actionable by benchmarking your organization’s percentages against competitors or industry averages.

  3. Combine with Horizontal Analysis
    For deeper insights, complement vertical analysis with horizontal analysis, which looks at changes over time using absolute dollar amounts.

  4. Ensure Accuracy of Base Figures
    The base figure you use must be accurate and aligned with the purpose of your analysis. An error here can render the entire analysis misleading.

  5. Maintain Context
    Percentages can’t tell the whole story. Always consider the broader context, including market conditions, business strategy, and macroeconomic factors, when interpreting the results.

Conclusion

Vertical analysis is a powerful yet simple tool for uncovering meaningful insights about an organization’s financial performance. By standardizing data into percentages, it allows for easy comparisons across time periods, companies, and industries, breaking down complex financial information into digestible proportions.

Whether you’re managing a small business or evaluating an investment opportunity, vertical analysis offers a logical approach to understanding financial statements in depth. Take the time to practice and refine this technique, and you’ll develop the skills needed to identify financial strengths, address weaknesses, and confidently guide decisions toward success.
