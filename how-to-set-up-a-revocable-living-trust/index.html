How to Set Up a Revocable Living Trust


Setting up a revocable living trust can be a powerful way to manage your assets, avoid probate, and ensure that your wishes are honored after you pass. A revocable living trust allows you to retain control over your assets during your lifetime while providing a mechanism for distributing them when you’re gone. Here, we’ll walk you through the steps of creating a revocable living trust, from understanding the basics to implementing it in your estate plan.

What is a Revocable Living Trust?

A revocable living trust is a legal document that outlines how your assets will be handled during your lifetime and after your death. As the creator of the trust (the grantor), you transfer ownership of your property into the trust. While you’re alive, you can modify or revoke the trust as needed, making it “revocable.” After your death, the trust becomes irrevocable, and your chosen trustee manages and distributes the assets to the beneficiaries according to your instructions.

Key Benefits of a Revocable Living Trust:

  1. Avoiding Probate: One of the main reasons people create a revocable living trust is to avoid the lengthy and expensive probate process. Since the assets in the trust do not go through probate, your heirs can receive their inheritance faster and without unnecessary legal hurdles.

  2. Control Over Distribution: You can specify exactly how and when your assets will be distributed, even after your death. This can help ensure your beneficiaries receive what you intend, in the manner you prefer.

  3. Privacy: Unlike a will, which becomes a public document when it goes through probate, a revocable living trust remains private. This helps keep your estate details confidential.

  4. Disability Protection: If you become incapacitated, the successor trustee can step in and manage your affairs without the need for a court-appointed guardian or conservator.

  5. Flexibility: Since the trust is revocable, you have the flexibility to modify or dissolve it at any time, allowing it to adapt to changes in your circumstances.

Step 1: Decide What You Want to Include in the Trust

The first step in setting up a revocable living trust is to decide what assets you want to include. Essentially, you can transfer any type of asset into the trust, but some of the most common ones include:

  • Real estate
  • Bank accounts
  • Investment accounts
  • Retirement accounts (with special provisions)
  • Life insurance policies
  • Business interests
  • Personal property such as jewelry, antiques, or collectibles

Once you’ve decided which assets to include, you’ll need to change the title or ownership of these assets to reflect the trust’s name. This process is often referred to as “funding the trust.” Without proper funding, your revocable living trust won’t serve its purpose effectively.

Step 2: Choose a Trustee

The trustee is the person or institution responsible for managing the trust and carrying out its instructions. As the grantor of the trust, you’ll typically serve as the initial trustee, giving you full control over the assets while you’re alive and competent. However, you also need to select a successor trustee, who will take over the management of the trust after your death or if you become incapacitated.

When choosing a trustee, consider the following:

  • Trustworthiness: Your trustee should be someone you trust to manage your assets responsibly.
  • Financial Savvy: The trustee should be capable of handling financial matters, especially if your estate is complex.
  • Willingness to Serve: The trustee should be willing to take on the responsibilities associated with managing the trust.
  • Alternatives: Choose one or more successor trustees in case your primary trustee is unable or unwilling to serve.

Many people opt for family members, friends, or professional fiduciaries (e.g., an attorney or financial institution) to act as trustees. However, a professional trustee can bring expertise but may charge fees for their services.

Step 3: Draft the Trust Agreement

The trust agreement is the legal document that outlines the terms of your trust, including how it will be administered, how assets will be distributed, and the responsibilities of the trustee. While you can find trust templates online, it’s recommended that you work with an estate planning attorney to ensure the document is legally sound and properly reflects your wishes.

The trust agreement should cover:

  • Trustor/Grantor Information: Your name and information.
  • Trustee and Successor Trustee: Names and roles of your initial and successor trustees.
  • Beneficiaries: Who will receive the assets in the trust, and what they will receive.
  • Distributions: How and when the assets will be distributed to beneficiaries. You may specify that distributions be made immediately, at a certain age, or when specific conditions are met (e.g., for educational expenses).
  • Asset Management Instructions: How you want the assets managed, including instructions for investments, property maintenance, and more.
  • Succession Plan: Instructions for what happens if a trustee or beneficiary is unable to serve or inherit.

Your attorney will ensure that your trust complies with state laws and meets your needs.

Step 4: Fund the Trust

Once the trust agreement is drafted, you’ll need to fund the trust by transferring your assets into it. For most assets, this involves changing the title or ownership to the name of the trust. Some common examples include:

  • Real Estate: You’ll need to record a deed transferring ownership of real property into the trust.
  • Bank Accounts: Change the name on your checking, savings, or other financial accounts to reflect the trust’s name.
  • Investment Accounts: Notify your brokerage or financial institution to retitle your accounts into the name of the trust.
  • Life Insurance and Retirement Accounts: You’ll need to change the beneficiary designation on your life insurance policy or retirement accounts to the trust, although this can be more complicated and requires careful planning.

It’s essential to properly fund the trust for it to be effective. If you forget to transfer certain assets into the trust, those assets may end up in probate.

Step 5: Sign and Notarize the Trust Agreement

Once the trust agreement is complete, you’ll need to sign it in front of a notary public. The notary will verify your identity and witness your signature, making the trust legally binding. Some states require additional witnesses to sign the trust, so be sure to follow your state’s specific requirements.

In some cases, you may need to have the trust agreement recorded in certain jurisdictions (e.g., for real estate). Your attorney will guide you through these steps.

Step 6: Store the Trust Document Safely

After the trust is created and signed, you must store the original trust document in a safe place. This could be a fireproof safe in your home, a safe deposit box at a bank, or with your estate planning attorney. Make sure your trustee and key family members know where to find the trust document when the time comes.

If you make any changes to the trust in the future (such as adding or removing assets or modifying the terms), be sure to update the original document and store it accordingly.

Step 7: Review and Update the Trust Periodically

A revocable living trust is a flexible tool, but it’s important to review and update it regularly to reflect changes in your life. Some common life events that might prompt you to update your trust include:

  • Marriage or Divorce: A change in marital status can significantly impact your estate plan.
  • Birth or Adoption of Children: You may want to include provisions for your children or update beneficiary designations.
  • Significant Changes in Assets: If you acquire new assets or experience significant changes in wealth, you may need to adjust your trust.
  • Changes in State or Federal Law: Tax laws or estate planning laws can change, and it’s important to keep your trust up to date with these changes.

Step 8: Inform Your Family and Beneficiaries

It’s essential to keep your family members and beneficiaries informed about the trust. While you may not need to reveal all of the details, making them aware of the trust’s existence and where to find it is important. This can prevent confusion or disputes later on.

If you’re making significant changes to the trust, such as changing beneficiaries or trustees, discuss these changes with the relevant parties to avoid surprises.

Conclusion

Creating a revocable living trust is an effective way to manage your estate and ensure that your assets are distributed according to your wishes. By following the steps outlined above, you can establish a trust that provides flexibility, control, and peace of mind. Remember to consult with an estate planning attorney to ensure your trust is properly drafted and funded, and to regularly review and update it as necessary to reflect changes in your life and circumstances. A well-executed trust can simplify the estate administration process and protect your loved ones from unnecessary legal complexities.
