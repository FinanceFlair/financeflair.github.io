Asset-Based Valuation: When and How to Use This Method


Asset-based valuation is a vital method for determining the worth of a company, especially for small businesses and start-ups. This approach values a business based on the value of its tangible and intangible assets. It’s crucial to understand when and how to use this method to get the most accurate valuation.

When to Use Asset-Based Valuation

Asset-based valuation is most beneficial when the subject company has significant liquidation value or if the management plans to sell the company’s assets. This method is more suitable for businesses with a minimal emphasis on intangible assets like brand recognition or patents.

There are several reasons to opt for an asset-based valuation instead of an income approach or market comparison methods:

  1. Liquidation: When a company faces financial troubles, asset-based valuation can be used as an alternative to income or market-based methods. It helps determine the value of the company’s assets and provides a basis for a liquidation.
  2. Specialized Assets: Companies with specialized assets, such as machinery, land, or patents, benefit from an asset-based approach. This method allows for a precise determination of the value of these assets, minimizing discrepancies.
  3. Start-ups: For start-ups, determining the value of intangible assets can be challenging. In such cases, an asset-based approach can provide a reliable valuation by focusing only on tangible assets, such as inventory and equipment.

How to Use Asset-Based Valuation

To use the asset-based valuation approach, you must list all the assets and liabilities of the business. Then, calculate the total value of assets and subtract outstanding liabilities to arrive at the company’s valuation.

Step 1: List Assets and Liabilities: Identify the company’s assets, including current assets like inventory and accounts receivable, fixed assets such as land and buildings, and intangible assets such as patents or copyrights. Conversely, list all outstanding liabilities, including accounts payable and loans.

Step 2: Valuation of Assets: Determine the value of each asset in the list. This valuation takes into consideration factors like current market value, depreciated value of the fixed assets, or license revenue for intangible assets. It’s essential to be thorough and realistic in the evaluation process.

Step 3: Deduction of Liabilities: Subtract all outstanding liabilities from the total asset value. The final calculation provides the company’s value based on its assets.

Case Study

A food processing company had been struggling financially. The owner wanted to determine the value of the business, perhaps to secure a loan or explore a sale. Since the company had a significant amount of machinery and equipment, an asset-based valuation seemed appropriate.

Listing all assets and liabilities, we determined the fair market value for each. As the machinery and equipment were still in good condition, depreciation was minimal. After a thorough calculation, we arrived at a value that exceeded expectations.

The owner was able to secure a loan and, later on, sold the business successfully.

This case study highlights the utility of asset-based valuation in specific scenarios. It is essential to recognize its strengths and drawbacks to decide when and how to use this method effectively.

Conclusion

In conclusion, asset-based valuation is a practical and reliable method for businesses with significant tangible assets. This approach provides valuable insights for start-ups and struggling companies and help determine the value of a business accurately. It’s crucial, however, to recognize its limits and understand that it may not always account for a company’s entire worth. To get the most precise valuation, use this method judiciously, combined with other approaches when needed.
