What Central Banks Don't Tell You About Their Policies


Central banks, such as the Federal Reserve and the European Central Bank, are pivotal institutions in modern economies. Their policies dictate interest rates, money supply, and inflation targets, influencing everything from personal saving decisions to the cost of daily goods. However, there’s an untold aspect to these policies that seldom grabs mainstream attention. In this blog post, we’ll peel back the layers—delving into topics seldom discussed—but crucial to understanding the complex world of monetary policy.

The Invisible Hand Behind Money Supply

When we talk about money supply, the first thing that comes to mind is printing new currency or digital transfers in online banking applications. However, central banks control this by buying/selling government bonds, a practice known as “open market operations.” What many don’t realize is this activity isn’t static—the quantity depends on what is beneficial to achieving low unemployment or steering the economy from recession.

Moreover, the “money multiplier” concept is a myth perpetuated by economics textbooks. It proposes a ratio between an increase in bank reserves and deposit expansion. But in the real world, banks and depositors’ behavior lead to unpredictable and uncontrollable money supply growth—effectively making the multiplier a variable inconsistent from one economy to another.

Interest Rate Myth Busting

Interest rates are profoundly significant—they dictate borrowing costs for corporations, firms, and individuals. When rates rise, spending tends to fall; when they decline, spending typically increases. However, just as with the money multiplier, these rates aren’t solely decided by real-world economic factors. Rather, they are outcomes of central bankers’ forecasts, based on models that might not fully capture real-world economic performance.

Another overlooked facet is how these rates affect different segments of society differently. The lowest interest rates in the history of the 20th century, for example, have been partly instrumental to the amassing of wealth inequality. Those with the resources to invest in assets like stocks benefit disproportionately.

Quantum Economics: Digital Currencies in Banking Policy

Blockchain technology and cryptocurrencies have shifted paradigms—changing how central banks approach financial policy. Bitcoin and Etherium, for example, perceive money differently. They question traditional reserve requirements and banking systems. In some cases, central banks themselves are issuing digital currencies, attempting to balance innovation with financial stability in this new paradigm.

Expectations about Economic Policy

Central bank communication is more complex than it seems. Most people only hear about interest rate changes or announcements, but behind closed doors, central banks are constantly trying to shape the expectations of the public. For instance, if a central bank president signals a shift towards more inflation-friendly policies, this shift in expectation can, by itself, push the economy towards its desired outcome.

Furthermore, central banks attempt to forecast their actions based on economic modelling and statistical analysis, assuming some stability in the economic variables. But the real world isn’t static—global events, political changes, and technological shifts alter expectations and the economy’s path in unexpected ways.

Conclusion

Central banks have a prominent role in managing an economy. However, the full extent of their influence isn’t widely understood. Their policies are complex, often shaped by forecasts and expectations that result in outcomes sometimes unintended and even counterproductive. Understanding these aspects can help us better comprehend financial policy decisions, and their effects on our everyday lives. The next time you hear an announcement about interest rates or money supply, remember the layers of decision-making that led to these choices. Central banks are not just gatekeepers of stability, but part of an intricate web that shapes our economic realities.
