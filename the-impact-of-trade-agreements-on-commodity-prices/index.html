The Impact of Trade Agreements on Commodity Prices


Trade agreements have long been a cornerstone of global economic interaction. These agreements, whether bilateral or multilateral, shape the rules and conditions under which countries trade goods and services with one another. Among the most significant outcomes of these trade pacts is their ability to influence commodity prices. Commodities—such as oil, gold, agricultural products, and metals—serve as the backbone of the global economy. As nations enter into trade agreements, these agreements can either raise or lower commodity prices based on the terms of trade, tariffs, quotas, and other factors. Understanding the mechanisms through which trade agreements influence commodity prices is critical for businesses, investors, and policymakers alike.

What Are Trade Agreements?

Trade agreements are legal accords between countries that establish the rules governing their international trade relations. These agreements can cover a wide range of issues, including tariffs, quotas, market access, intellectual property rights, and regulatory standards. Broadly, trade agreements are categorized into:

  1. Bilateral Agreements: These involve two countries and focus on reducing trade barriers between them.
  2. Multilateral Agreements: These involve more than two countries and aim to establish more extensive trade relationships, often under the guidance of international organizations like the World Trade Organization (WTO).
  3. Regional Agreements: These involve countries in a specific geographic region, such as the European Union (EU) or the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA), which was later replaced by the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA).

While these agreements can involve a variety of sectors and industries, their influence on commodity markets is profound due to the central role that commodities play in international trade.

The Link Between Trade Agreements and Commodity Prices

Trade agreements can directly and indirectly affect commodity prices. The primary ways in which these agreements impact commodity prices include changes to supply and demand dynamics, trade barriers, and the movement of capital across borders. Let’s delve deeper into how each of these factors contributes to shifts in commodity prices.

1. Supply and Demand Shifts

Trade agreements can significantly alter supply and demand relationships for key commodities. For example, a trade agreement that facilitates easier access to raw materials between two countries could increase the supply of that commodity in the global market, potentially lowering prices. Conversely, if a trade deal restricts supply—perhaps due to tariffs or export restrictions—prices could rise as the commodity becomes scarcer.

A good example of this is the agreement between China and Australia, which has had a significant effect on iron ore prices. When both countries sign trade deals that promote iron ore trade, Australia benefits from higher demand for its resources, and global supply increases, keeping prices relatively stable or even lowering them. On the other hand, when trade tensions arise, as seen in the trade dispute between China and Australia in recent years, iron ore prices can surge due to the reduction in exports, leading to price volatility.

2. Tariffs and Trade Barriers

One of the most immediate effects of trade agreements is the alteration of tariffs and trade barriers. Tariffs are taxes imposed on imported goods, which can increase the cost of those goods. When countries enter into trade agreements that reduce or eliminate tariffs, it often results in a decrease in the cost of imports. This can have a cascading effect on commodity prices.

For instance, the United States’ trade agreement with Mexico and Canada (USMCA) has helped reduce trade barriers, particularly for agricultural commodities. Lower tariffs on products like corn, soybeans, and wheat can make these commodities more affordable to consumers and businesses, which ultimately leads to price adjustments in the global market.

Conversely, the introduction of tariffs can raise the cost of imported commodities, which in turn pushes domestic prices higher. A good example of this can be seen in the U.S.-China trade war, where tariffs on Chinese goods led to a rise in the price of Chinese agricultural products and other commodities in the U.S. market. Such trade barriers can create price fluctuations that ripple through the global economy.

3. Increased Competition and Market Access

Trade agreements can also foster increased competition in commodity markets by providing new market access. When countries lower trade barriers, they open their economies to more competitors, which can lead to greater efficiency and lower prices.

For example, the Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership (CPTPP), which includes countries like Japan, Canada, and Australia, has created new opportunities for agricultural exports to new markets. By increasing competition, these countries may experience lower prices for certain agricultural commodities, as producers strive to offer better value and efficiency in a more competitive environment.

In the energy sector, the U.S.-Mexico-Canada Agreement has allowed for increased access to energy resources, including oil and gas. As a result, energy companies in the U.S. and Canada can secure more stable and affordable supplies, which helps stabilize prices in the North American energy market.

4. Currency and Exchange Rate Fluctuations

Another aspect of trade agreements is their potential impact on currency exchange rates. When trade agreements are signed, they can influence capital flows, which in turn can affect the value of national currencies. A stronger currency can reduce the cost of importing commodities, while a weaker currency can have the opposite effect.

For example, the Brexit negotiations led to a significant depreciation of the British pound. As a result, the price of commodities imported into the U.K., such as oil, gold, and agricultural products, increased due to the weaker pound. Similarly, when countries enter trade agreements that promote economic stability and growth, it can lead to stronger currencies, which might lower the cost of importing essential commodities.

5. Investor Sentiment and Market Perceptions

The signing of a major trade agreement can also influence investor sentiment and market expectations, which indirectly affect commodity prices. Investors closely watch trade negotiations and agreements, as they can provide insight into future market trends. For example, when the U.S. and China reached a phase-one trade deal in early 2020, commodity markets reacted positively, with increased demand for metals like copper and agricultural products like soybeans. The optimism surrounding such agreements often drives commodity prices higher as market participants adjust to the expectations of increased trade activity.

On the flip side, trade uncertainty, such as during the ongoing negotiations surrounding Brexit or trade wars, can lead to price volatility. Investors may react to uncertainty by pulling back from commodity markets, causing prices to dip in response to reduced demand.

Case Studies: How Trade Agreements Have Affected Commodity Prices

The North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA)

NAFTA, which was signed between the U.S., Canada, and Mexico in 1994, provides a prime example of how trade agreements can affect commodity prices. The agreement led to a significant reduction in tariffs and trade barriers between the three countries, particularly in the agricultural sector. This has resulted in lower prices for commodities like corn, wheat, and dairy products, benefiting both consumers and businesses in North America.

Additionally, NAFTA facilitated the integration of supply chains across North America, leading to more efficient production and distribution of commodities. The U.S. gained easier access to cheap labor and raw materials in Mexico, which reduced production costs for various industries and ultimately lowered commodity prices.

However, the renegotiation of NAFTA into the USMCA has had a different impact on certain commodities. For instance, the U.S. imposed new tariffs on Canadian lumber imports, leading to an increase in prices for U.S. consumers. Despite these challenges, the overall impact of NAFTA on commodity prices was one of price stabilization and reduced volatility across various sectors.

The European Union (EU) Trade Agreements

The EU has long been a key player in international trade, and its numerous trade agreements have had far-reaching effects on commodity prices. The EU’s trade agreements with countries in Africa, Asia, and Latin America have opened up new markets for agricultural products and natural resources, helping to reduce prices in the global market. EU policies, such as Common Agricultural Policy (CAP), have also played a role in stabilizing prices for agricultural commodities within Europe.

Additionally, the EU’s trade agreements with major energy-producing countries, such as Russia and Norway, have ensured a steady supply of oil and natural gas. These trade agreements have kept energy prices relatively stable in Europe, despite fluctuations in global markets.

The China-Australia Trade Agreement

The China-Australia Free Trade Agreement (ChAFTA), signed in 2015, is another example of how trade agreements influence commodity prices. The agreement has significantly increased Australian exports to China, particularly in the mining and agricultural sectors. China is one of the world’s largest consumers of iron ore, coal, and other minerals, and ChAFTA has boosted demand for these commodities, leading to higher prices on the global market.

However, recent political tensions between the two countries have led to trade disruptions, particularly in the agricultural sector. As a result, Australian farmers have faced reduced demand for their products, leading to price fluctuations in the commodity market.

Conclusion

The impact of trade agreements on commodity prices is complex and multifaceted. By reducing tariffs, facilitating market access, and influencing supply and demand dynamics, these agreements can both stabilize and drive volatility in commodity markets. For businesses, investors, and policymakers, understanding how trade agreements shape commodity prices is essential for making informed decisions in an increasingly interconnected global economy.

Trade agreements are more than just political tools—they are powerful drivers of economic activity that can have lasting effects on the prices of the goods and resources that fuel the global economy. By navigating these agreements wisely, countries can harness the benefits of free trade to bolster economic growth and ensure stability in the critical markets for commodities that shape our world.
