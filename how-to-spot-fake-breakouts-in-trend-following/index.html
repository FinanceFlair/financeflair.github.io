How to Spot Fake Breakouts in Trend Following


As a trend follower, spotting fake breakouts is a crucial skill to master. Fake breakouts can lead to costly mistakes, but with the right knowledge and tools, you can avoid them and stay on track. In this post, we'll explore the characteristics of fake breakouts and provide you with practical tips on how to spot them. Understanding Fake Breakouts

A fake breakout occurs when a stock or market appears to break out of a trading range, but in reality, it's just a temporary move that doesn't lead to a sustainable trend. Fake breakouts can be caused by various factors, including:

Order flow imbalance: A sudden influx of buy or sell orders can create a fake breakout. Liquidity providers: Market makers or other liquidity providers can artificially create a breakout by placing large trades. Market manipulation: Insider trading or other forms of market manipulation can cause a fake breakout.

Characteristics of Fake Breakouts

Fake breakouts often exhibit certain characteristics that can help you identify them. Here are some common traits to look out for:

Lack of volume: A fake breakout often occurs with low volume, which can indicate that the move is not supported by strong buying or selling pressure. No confirmation from other markets: A fake breakout may not be confirmed by other markets or assets, which can indicate that it's not a genuine trend. Rapid reversal: Fake breakouts often lead to rapid reversals, which can indicate that the move was not sustainable. No fundamental justification: A fake breakout may not be supported by fundamental analysis, such as earnings reports or economic data.

How to Spot Fake Breakouts

So, how can you spot fake breakouts? Here are some practical tips to help you:

Monitor volume: Pay attention to volume levels and look for signs of low volume or unusual trading activity. Use multiple time frames: Analyze charts from different time frames to identify potential fake breakouts. Watch for divergences: Identify divergences between price and other indicators, such as the Relative Strength Index (RSI) or the Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD). Focus on the big picture: Consider the overall market trend and look for signs of fake breakouts in the context of the broader market.

Case Study: The 2008 Financial Crisis

The 2008 financial crisis is a classic example of a fake breakout. In the months leading up to the crisis, the S&P 500 index appeared to break out of a trading range, reaching new highs in the summer of 2007. However, this breakout was ultimately a fake, and the index plummeted in the fall of 2008. In hindsight, the signs of a fake breakout were there. The breakout occurred with low volume, and the market did not confirm the move. Furthermore, the fundamentals of the market were weak, with a housing bubble bursting and a global credit crisis unfolding. Conclusion

Spotting fake breakouts is a critical skill for trend followers. By understanding the characteristics of fake breakouts and using practical tips to identify them, you can avoid costly mistakes and stay on track. Remember to monitor volume, use multiple time frames, watch for divergences, and focus on the big picture. In the world of trend following, fake breakouts are a constant threat. But with the right knowledge and tools, you can outsmart them and achieve success in the markets.
