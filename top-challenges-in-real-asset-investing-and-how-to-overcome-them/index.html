Top Challenges in Real Asset Investing and How to Overcome Them


Investing in real assets, such as real estate, infrastructure, commodities, and natural resources, offers tangible value and diversification benefits to portfolios. However, entering this arena is riddled with unique challenges that require strategic foresight and practical approaches to navigate successfully. Understanding these hurdles and implementing effective solutions can not only protect your capital but also enhance returns and build long-term wealth.

In this comprehensive guide, we delve into the most significant challenges investors face in real asset investing and explore actionable strategies to overcome them.

Understanding Real Asset Investing

Before diving into challenges, it’s important to clarify what constitutes real assets. Unlike financial assets such as stocks or bonds, real assets are physical assets with intrinsic value. Examples include:

  • Real Estate: Residential, commercial, industrial properties
  • Infrastructure: Roads, bridges, airports, utilities
  • Natural Resources: Oil, gas, minerals, timberland
  • Commodities: Precious metals, agricultural products

These investments often serve as a hedge against inflation and provide steady income streams, but their complexity requires expertise and due diligence.

1. Illiquidity and Long Holding Periods

The Challenge

Real assets commonly come with limited liquidity. Unlike publicly traded stocks that can be sold instantly, divesting from physical property or infrastructure holdings often takes months or even years. This illiquidity can lock up substantial capital, making it less flexible in rebalancing portfolios or responding to market changes.

Moreover, real asset investments often require long holding periods to realize expected returns, which can mismatch with an investorâ€™s need for liquidity or shorter-term financial goals.

How to Overcome

  • Portfolio Diversification: By diversifying across asset classes and investment horizons, investors can reduce the impact of illiquidity in any single holding.
  • Access Real Asset Funds: Utilizing real estate investment trusts (REITs) or infrastructure funds can provide exposure to real assets without the liquidity constraints of direct ownership.
  • Structure Exit Strategies Early: Mindfully planning exits through staggered sales, lease buyouts, or joint ventures helps manage timing risk.
  • Maintain Sufficient Cash Reserves: Having liquid assets on hand to meet unexpected needs avoids forced sales in unfavorable markets.

2. Valuation Complexity and Transparency Issues

The Challenge

Unlike equities, which are priced daily on exchanges, the valuation of physical assets is often opaque and subjective. Market comparables may be limited, and asset characteristics can be highly heterogeneous. Infrastructure projects or natural resource fields, for instance, have unique operational variables influencing value.

This opacity can hinder accurate assessment of asset worth, performance tracking, and negotiation in buying or selling.

How to Overcome

  • Engage Specialized Appraisers: Use experts with deep knowledge of local markets and asset types to perform rigorous, defensible valuations.
  • Leverage Technology and Data Analytics: Tools such as satellite imaging, IoT sensors, and big data analysis enhance transparency by providing real-time operational and market insights.
  • Regular Revaluations: Conduct periodic assessments rather than relying solely on initial appraisal figures.
  • Demand Financial Disclosure: When investing through intermediaries or funds, prioritize those committed to comprehensive and timely financial reporting.

3. Regulatory and Legal Challenges

The Challenge

Real assets are governed by complex legal frameworks including zoning laws, environmental regulations, property rights, and tax codes, often varying widely across regions. Non-compliance can result in costly penalties, delays, or even loss of property rights.

Additionally, infrastructure and natural resource investments may be subject to political risks, such as changes in policies, expropriation, or social opposition.

How to Overcome

  • Conduct Thorough Due Diligence: Research all applicable laws and regulations early in the investment process.
  • Seek Local Expertise: Collaborate with legal and regulatory advisors familiar with the jurisdiction and sector.
  • Advocate Effective Risk Mitigation Clauses: In contracts, include provisions addressing political risk insurance, force majeure, and dispute resolution mechanisms.
  • Monitor Regulatory Changes Continuously: Proactively stay informed on evolving policies that could affect asset performance.

4. Operational and Management Risks

The Challenge

The management of real assets involves operational complexities ranging from tenant lease negotiations in real estate to maintenance scheduling in infrastructure and extraction efficiency in natural resource holdings. Poor management can erode returns through increased costs, downtime, or asset degradation.

How to Overcome

  • Hire Experienced Professional Managers: Whether third-party firms or in-house teams, management expertise is vital.
  • Implement Robust Asset Management Systems: Adopt software solutions for tracking operations, maintenance, and financials.
  • Regular Performance Audits: Review operational efficiency and cost control regularly to identify improvement areas.
  • Focus on Preventative Maintenance: Proactively addressing issues lowers long-term repair costs and downtime.

5. Market Volatility and Economic Cycles

The Challenge

Real assets, while often considered stable, are impacted by macroeconomic cycles, supply-demand changes, and global events. For example:

  • Real estate markets can swell and crash based on interest rate fluctuations;
  • Commodity prices experience volatility due to geopolitical tensions or weather;
  • Infrastructure usage can decline during economic downturns.

Market shocks can impair valuation, rental yields, or cash flows unexpectedly.

How to Overcome

  • Adopt a Long-Term Perspective: Real assets tend to recover and appreciate over longer horizons despite short-term swings.
  • Use Hedging Strategies: Financial derivatives or insurance products can protect against commodity price falls or other specific risks.
  • Diversify Geographically and by Sector: Avoid overexposure to any single market or type of asset.
  • Conduct Sensitivity Analysis: Predict how various economic scenarios might affect returns and prepare contingencies.

6. High Capital Requirements and Financing Challenges

The Challenge

Purchasing and developing real assets usually demands significant upfront capital, posing barriers to many investors. Securing financing presents its own complexities related to creditworthiness, interest rates, loan terms, and covenants.

High leverage may amplify downside risk, while insufficient capital restricts investment scale and diversification.

How to Overcome

  • Partner with Institutional Investors: Syndicates and joint ventures pool resources, share risk, and improve access to financing.
  • Explore Creative Financing Structures: Options like green bonds for sustainable infrastructure or revenue-sharing agreements in natural resources can offer flexibility.
  • Maintain Strong Financial Metrics: Demonstrating solid cash flows and collateral improves borrowing terms.
  • Build Relationships with Lenders: Long-term partnerships can result in more favorable and responsive funding arrangements.

7. Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) Challenges

The Challenge

Increasing attention on sustainable investing means real asset investors face pressures around environmental impact, social responsibility, and governance standards. Infrastructure projects may disrupt local communities; resource extraction can degrade ecosystems; and energy consumption in real estate has carbon implications.

Failing to address ESG factors risks reputational damage, regulatory penalties, and exclusion from certain funds or markets.

How to Overcome

  • Incorporate ESG Criteria into Investment Decisions: Evaluate potential impacts and benefits before acquisition.
  • Adopt Transparent Reporting Practices: Share ESG performance data openly with stakeholders.
  • Implement Sustainability Initiatives: Pursue energy efficiency upgrades, community engagement programs, and responsible sourcing.
  • Engage with Stakeholders Proactively: Collaborate with local governments, communities, and NGOs to align objectives.

8. Technological Disruption and Adaptation

The Challenge

Technology is rapidly changing the landscape for real asset investing. Innovations like automation, smart infrastructure, blockchain, and advanced analytics offer new opportunities but also create uncertainty on asset valuation and operational models.

Traditional assets risk becoming obsolete or underperforming if they fail to adapt to technological progress.

How to Overcome

  • Invest in Tech-Enabled Assets: Prioritize properties and projects with integrated smart systems and digital capabilities.
  • Continuous Learning and Innovation: Stay updated on emerging trends and pilot relevant technologies.
  • Collaborate with Tech Partners: Work with startups and tech firms to enhance asset management and value creation.
  • Assess Technological Risks During Due Diligence: Evaluate how susceptible assets are to disruption and plan accordingly.

Conclusion

Real asset investing offers compelling diversification, inflation protection, and income-generation benefits, but navigating its unique challenges requires a blend of strategic planning, specialized knowledge, and flexibility. Illiquidity, valuation difficulties, regulatory complexity, operational risks, economic cycles, capital intensity, ESG considerations, and technological disruption all pose significant hurdles.

By proactively addressing these areas through careful due diligence, professional management, diversification, advanced analytics, and stakeholder engagement, investors can harness the full potential of real assets while mitigating downside risks. This thoughtful approach lays the foundation for resilient, rewarding long-term real asset portfolios that weather volatility and adapt to evolving market landscapes.
