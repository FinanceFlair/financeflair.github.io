The Top Trend Following Strategies for Beginners


Riding the market’s momentum can seem intimidating to newcomers, but trend following is one of the most approachable paths to profitable trading. Instead of fighting the current, you learn how to identify trends—movements in price that have some staying power—and ride them for as long as possible. Certain strategies have been refined over decades and stand out as particularly effective for those taking their first steps into the world of trading.

Understanding trend following takes us into a rich tradition, with legendary traders and simple, systematic rules proving their worth across stocks, forex, futures, and cryptocurrencies. This guide will give you a tour of the most valuable trend following strategies a beginner can learn, how to apply them, and common pitfalls to avoid.

What is Trend Following?

Before diving into the specifics, it helps to clarify what trend following is. The strategy centers on identifying the direction of a market—upward (bullish), downward (bearish), or sideways (range-bound)—and taking trades that align with that direction. The trend follower’s mantra: “The trend is your friend.”

Trend following doesn’t try to predict tops and bottoms. It seeks to capture the middle chunk of a move. By doing so, losses are generally small (cutting losing trades early) and winners have the potential to be much larger (letting profitable trades run until the trend shows signs of ending).

The mechanics of trend following rely on simple tools: moving averages, breakouts, and price action. Combining these methods can give you an edge, especially when other traders are caught up in emotional decision-making.

Strategy 1: The Moving Average Crossover

How It Works

Few indicators are as widely used as the moving average. A moving average smooths out price data over a specified number of periods, making it easier to spot the underlying trend.

A moving average crossover strategy is straightforward, making it ideal for beginners:

  • Choose two moving averages: One short-term (e.g., 10-period) and one long-term (e.g., 50-period).
  • Buy Signal: When the short-term average crosses above the long-term average, it suggests a change to an upward trend.
  • Sell Signal: When the short-term average crosses below the long-term average, it indicates a possible downward trend.

Why It Works for Beginners

It’s simple to implement on any platform and offers clarity during periods of market noise. It helps you avoid trading based on emotion and keeps you following price action.

Key Tips

  • Test various combinations to match your preferred time frame (day trading, swing trading, investing).
  • Use stop-loss orders to manage risk, as false signals do occur during sideways markets.
  • The double crossover is a classic starting point, but you can progress to triple or adaptive moving averages for more nuanced signals.

Strategy 2: Breakout Trading

How It Works

Breakout strategies involve entering trades when the price breaks through a significant level of support or resistance. This often signals the start of a new trend, offering an early entry point.

Implementation Steps:

  1. Identify Key Levels: Find areas where price has reversed multiple times—these are major support or resistance levels.
  2. Wait for a Breakout: Enter the trade when the price breaks through these levels with increased volume.
  3. Use Confirmation: A breakout is more reliable if the price closes above resistance or below support rather than just wicking through it.

Why It Works for Beginners

Visual and objective markers make breakouts easy to spot. This approach teaches discipline, as you only act when a clear signal emerges.

Key Tips

  • Avoid trading every breakout. False breakouts (fakeouts) are common. Use volume and confirmation candles to filter out weaker signals.
  • Set stop-loss orders just below/above breakout points to protect against failed moves.
  • Combine with other indicators (e.g., RSI) to stack the odds in your favor.

Strategy 3: The Donchian Channel System

How It Works

Richard Donchian, often called the “father of trend following,” introduced this concept in the 1940s. The Donchian Channel plots the highest high and lowest low over a set look-back period (often 20 days).

  • Buy Signal: When the price closes above the channel high, enter a long position.
  • Sell Signal: When the price closes below the channel low, take a short position or close long positions.

Why It Works for Beginners

This system enforces patience, as trades are only taken when a significant price level is breached. The rules are mechanical, eliminating overthinking.

Key Tips

  • The longer the look-back period, the fewer but stronger the signals.
  • Use a trailing stop (such as the opposite channel line) to let winners run and limit losses.

Strategy 4: Price Action and Higher Highs/Higher Lows

How It Works

Price action trading centers on reading raw price movements rather than relying solely on indicators. For trend following, this usually means buying when the chart forms a pattern of higher highs and higher lows (uptrend), and selling or shorting in lower lows and lower highs (downtrend).

Why It Works for Beginners

It sharpens your chart-reading skills and helps develop an intuitive sense of market structure. This technique can be applied to any time frame and any market.

Key Tips

  • Look for consecutive higher swing highs and swing lows for an uptrend.
  • Pay attention to reversal candlestick patterns (e.g., pin bars, engulfing bars) that can signal the end of a trend.
  • Combine with other tools (moving averages or channels) for extra conviction.

Strategy 5: Trendlines and Channels

How It Works

Trendlines are drawn directly on a price chart, connecting at least two major highs for resistance or two major lows for support. Channels are simply parallel trendlines that contain price action within a range.

When price consistently bounces off a trendline, it suggests the trend is intact. Breaks of these lines can signal a change in direction.

Why It Works for Beginners

Drawing trendlines makes it easier to visualize the market’s direction and trains your eye to spot trend changes early.

Key Tips

  • The more times a trendline is tested without breaking, the stronger it becomes.
  • Only draw lines that connect clear highs or lows—forced trendlines are unreliable.
  • Channels can help you identify profit targets and stop-loss levels.

Strategy 6: The Simple “Buy the Pullback” Approach

How It Works

In strong trends, prices rarely move in a straight line. Instead, they advance, pull back, then continue. A pullback strategy waits for a retracement in a trend before entering a trade in the direction of the overall move.

Why It Works for Beginners

Buying on pullbacks means you’re getting a better entry price, often with less risk and bigger reward potential.

Key Tips

  • Use moving averages or Fibonacci retracement levels to time entries.
  • Look for confirmation from price action (bullish reversal candles in an uptrend, bearish reversals in a downtrend).
  • Keep your stop loss just beyond the most recent swing low or high.

Strategy 7: Combining Indicators for Confirmation

How It Works

Many trend following systems perform better when two or more indicators are combined. For example, pair a moving average crossover with a momentum indicator like the Relative Strength Index (RSI):

  • Only take crossover signals when RSI aligns with your trade direction (e.g., above 50 in an uptrend).
  • This filters out weak signals and increases accuracy.

Why It Works for Beginners

It teaches you not to rely on a single tool and builds a more robust trading mindset. Dual-confirmation approaches help keep you on the right side of major moves.

Key Tips

  • Don’t overload your chart with indicators—two to three max is plenty.
  • Test different indicator combinations on historical data before trading live.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

While trend following strategies are approachable, beginners often stumble into the same traps. Watch out for these pitfalls:

  • Overtrading: Don’t force trades in choppy or sideways markets; trend following only works when markets are actually trending.
  • Ignoring Risk Management: Always use stop-loss orders and keep position sizing modest. Even the best trends can suddenly end.
  • Chasing the Market: Entering late often results in buying at a top or selling at a bottom. Wait for clear signals and confirmation.
  • Setting Unrealistic Expectations: Not every trade will be a winner. Good trend followers aim for a high reward-to-risk ratio, not a high win rate.
  • Changing Systems Too Quickly: Give your chosen approach time to prove itself. Switches made out of frustration can sabotage long-term progress.

How to Get Started

Jumping into trend following doesn’t require a complicated setup or advanced software. You can start with a free charting tool, a demo trading account, and a willingness to test, learn, and adapt. Here’s a simple path to follow:

  1. Pick One Strategy: Choose the approach that makes the most sense to you and study it in detail.
  2. Backtest: Apply your rules to past price data to understand how the system performs. Many platforms have built-in backtesting tools.
  3. Start Trading on a Demo Account: Practice without risking real money until you consistently follow your rules and generate results.
  4. Go Live With Small Positions: Once comfortable, trade small amounts to keep risk in check.
  5. Keep a Journal: Log every trade, especially your thoughts and emotions. This will help identify patterns and areas to improve.

Building Your Trader’s Mindset

Success with trend following isn’t just about rules and entries—it’s about cultivating patience, discipline, and self-awareness. The best trend followers can sit through long periods with no trades, remain emotionless when markets get choppy, and let profits grow without grabbing them too soon.

  • Trust Your Process: The true power of trend following is in consistency over time, not in chasing every move.
  • Review Regularly: Assess your trades weekly or monthly to find strengths and weaknesses.
  • Stay Curious: Read about the experiences of trader greats like Richard Dennis, Ed Seykota, or the legendary “Turtle Traders” for inspiration and insight.

Final Thoughts

Trend following strategies have stood the test of time, offering a reliable way for beginners to approach the markets without drowning in complexity. Whether you gravitate toward moving averages, breakout systems, price action, or trendlines, the key is to find a method you can follow with discipline.

Trading trends isn’t about being right all the time; it’s about losing little when you’re wrong and winning big when you’re right. The edge comes from cutting losses quickly and holding winners as long as the market moves in your favor. Start simple, be relentless in your self-education, and let the markets teach you over time.

The adventure begins with that first chart, that first trade, and the willingness to stick with a proven process when the path gets rocky. With patience, trend following can be your ticket to consistent progress, no matter where the market winds blow.
