Would a Modern Monetary Theory Approach Help Control Inflation?


Modern Monetary Theory (MMT) has been gaining attention in recent years, as a potential solution to address economic issues like inflation, unemployment, and wealth inequality. As a heterodox economic theory, MMT argues that governments can issue currency and eliminate debt by financing government spending with the creation of new money. In this article, we will examine whether the implementation of the MMT approach could help control inflation.

Understanding Inflation

Inflation is the rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services is increasing and subsequently purchasing power decreases. Central banks commonly aim to keep inflation in check by adjusting interest rates and implementing monetary policy. Inflation can be categorized into demand-pull and cost-push, whereby an imbalance between supply and demand causes prices to rise.

Key Principles of Modern Monetary Theory

The primary principles of MMT include:

  1. Governments operating within a fiat money system have the ability to create and issue currency.
  2. Government debt is not a constraint, as it can be repaid with newly created money.
  3. A nation with its own currency should focus on issues like full employment and price stability rather than a balanced budget.
  4. Taxes serve as a tool to manage economic activity, rather than as means to finance government spending.

Limiting Inflation with MMT Approach

With these guiding principles in mind, let’s consider how MMT could help control inflation:

  1. Fiscal Policy Coordination: One major advantage of MMT is the flexibility it provides governments to utilize fiscal policy as tool to manage the economy. Governments can actively stimulate or restrain economic activity through their budgets, progressively reducing the need for central banks to adjust monetary policy. Thus, by integrating fiscal policy, MMT advocates believe that governments can counteract inflation effectively.
  2. Adjusting Currency Supply: MMT proposes that governments control inflation by adjusting the quantity of money in circulation, aligning it with the nation’s productive capacity. This concept is based on the theory of effective demand, that is, price levels adjust to balance demand and supply in an economy. Consequently, the government possesses the means to control inflation.
  3. Price Stability: By focusing on the priority of keeping prices stable, MMT allows governments to manage inflation. Instead of striving for a balanced budget, governments can center their efforts on full employment and price stability.
  4. Breaking Structural Inflation: MMT approach can contribute to addressing structural inflation rooted in supply-side factors. By tackling bottlenecks in production, education, and infrastructure, MMT can help break the cycle of rising prices.

Nevertheless, critics argue that the MMT approach risks creating instability by relying on government spending to stimulate an economy. Furthermore, MMT does not account for international trade or capital movements, limiting its applicability to global economic problems.

Conclusion

While the Modern Monetary Theory Approach might offer potential in controlling inflation, its practical implementation in today’s complex global economy remains debated. Ultimately, due to its heterodox nature, MMT challenges conventional economic theory and steps away from the traditional view held by central banks. Whether or not a true MMT approach could be attained in modern times remains an open question, but the ideas merit exploration within the realm of macroeconomic policy.
