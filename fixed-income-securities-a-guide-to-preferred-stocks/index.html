Fixed Income Securities: A Guide to Preferred Stocks


Fixed income securities are a staple in many investors’ portfolios, providing stability and a reliable source of income. Within the world of fixed income investments, preferred stocks have garnered attention for their unique characteristics and benefits. Preferred stocks sit at the intersection of stocks and bonds, combining the potential for equity-like appreciation with the predictable income streams that are characteristic of debt instruments.

In this guide, we will explore everything you need to know about preferred stocks, from their structure and benefits to risks and key considerations for investors.

What Are Preferred Stocks?

Preferred stocks, also known as “preference shares,” are a type of equity security that have characteristics of both common stocks and bonds. Like common stockholders, preferred stockholders have an ownership interest in a company, but they hold a higher claim on the company’s assets and earnings. This gives them priority over common shareholders in the event of liquidation or bankruptcy.

However, preferred stocks are somewhat more like bonds in that they typically pay a fixed dividend, similar to the fixed interest payments received by bondholders. These dividends are often paid on a quarterly basis and are usually fixed as a percentage of the stock’s par value or a specified amount.

Key Characteristics of Preferred Stocks:

  1. Priority Dividend Payments: Preferred shareholders receive dividends before common stockholders. In most cases, these dividends are fixed and are paid regularly, typically on a quarterly basis.

  2. Non-Voting Rights: Unlike common shareholders, preferred stockholders typically do not have voting rights in corporate matters such as electing the board of directors.

  3. Convertible Features: Some preferred stocks are convertible, meaning they can be exchanged for a set number of common shares under certain conditions. This feature allows investors to participate in the upside potential of the company if its stock price appreciates.

  4. Callability: Many preferred stocks are callable, meaning the issuing company can redeem the shares before the maturity date, often at a premium. This provides companies with flexibility, but it can limit the upside potential for investors.

  5. Liquidation Preference: In the event of a liquidation, preferred stockholders are paid before common stockholders, but after debt holders. This means they are in a relatively safer position compared to common shareholders if the company faces financial difficulties.

Types of Preferred Stocks

There are several different types of preferred stocks, each offering unique features suited to different investor needs. The primary distinctions lie in the dividend payments, the ability to convert to common stock, and the specific terms regarding redemption or call features.

1. Cumulative Preferred Stocks

Cumulative preferred stocks are the most common type of preferred stock. They provide a key benefit to investors: if the company is unable to pay a dividend in a given period, the dividend accumulates and must be paid in the future before any dividends are paid to common shareholders. This feature offers a layer of protection for investors who rely on regular income.

2. Non-Cumulative Preferred Stocks

Unlike cumulative preferred stocks, non-cumulative preferred stocks do not accumulate unpaid dividends. If the company skips a dividend payment, it is not obligated to make it up in the future. While this can be riskier for investors seeking a consistent income stream, non-cumulative preferred stocks may offer higher dividend yields in return for the increased risk.

3. Participating Preferred Stocks

Participating preferred stocks allow shareholders to receive dividends in addition to their fixed dividend. After the company has paid its preferred stock dividend, participating preferred stockholders can also share in any excess earnings that are distributed to common shareholders. This type of preferred stock provides the potential for higher returns if the company performs well.

4. Convertible Preferred Stocks

Convertible preferred stocks allow the holder to convert their preferred shares into common stock at a predetermined ratio. This feature provides the potential for capital appreciation if the company’s common stock performs well. Convertible preferred stocks offer a blend of fixed-income security and potential equity upside.

5. Perpetual Preferred Stocks

Perpetual preferred stocks have no maturity date, meaning they do not expire and the issuing company is not required to redeem them. The dividends are paid indefinitely, as long as the company remains solvent. While this offers a stable source of income, it also means that the investor is tied to the investment indefinitely unless the stock is called or sold.

6. Redeemable Preferred Stocks

Redeemable preferred stocks are those that the issuing company has the option to redeem or buy back after a certain date, typically at a premium over the initial purchase price. This feature gives the company flexibility, but it also means that the investor’s investment might be called away if interest rates fall or if the company decides to restructure its capital.

Benefits of Investing in Preferred Stocks

Preferred stocks offer a range of benefits that appeal to different types of investors. They provide many of the benefits associated with bonds, such as a fixed income stream, but also offer equity-like potential for growth. Below are some of the key advantages of investing in preferred stocks.

1. Reliable Income Stream

One of the most attractive features of preferred stocks is the reliable income they provide. The fixed dividends ensure that investors receive a steady flow of income, making them an appealing option for income-focused investors, such as retirees. Compared to common stocks, which may not pay dividends at all, preferred stocks are far more dependable in terms of regular payouts.

2. Priority Over Common Stockholders

Preferred stockholders have a higher claim on a company’s assets and earnings than common stockholders. In the event of liquidation, preferred stockholders will receive payment before common stockholders. This makes preferred stocks relatively safer in situations where the company faces financial distress.

3. Potential for Capital Appreciation

While preferred stocks are generally seen as fixed-income investments, they do offer some potential for price appreciation. Convertible preferred stocks, in particular, allow investors to benefit from increases in the value of the company’s common stock. This provides a unique opportunity for investors to benefit from both fixed-income payments and the potential for capital gains.

4. Tax Advantages

In many jurisdictions, dividends paid on preferred stocks are taxed at a lower rate than ordinary income. This can make preferred stocks a more tax-efficient income-producing investment compared to bonds, which are often taxed at a higher rate.

5. Less Volatility Compared to Common Stocks

Preferred stocks are generally less volatile than common stocks because they are more focused on providing stable income rather than capital appreciation. This makes them an appealing option for risk-averse investors who seek a stable income stream but don’t want to deal with the market volatility often associated with common equities.

Risks of Investing in Preferred Stocks

While preferred stocks offer several benefits, they also come with their own set of risks. It is crucial for investors to understand these risks before adding preferred stocks to their portfolios.

1. Interest Rate Risk

Preferred stocks are sensitive to changes in interest rates. When interest rates rise, the price of preferred stocks tends to fall, as newer issues offer higher yields. This inverse relationship between interest rates and preferred stock prices can expose investors to potential losses if rates increase. Conversely, when interest rates fall, the value of existing preferred stocks may rise.

2. Credit Risk

If the issuing company faces financial difficulty, there is a risk that it may not be able to pay the preferred stock dividends. While preferred stockholders have a higher claim on company assets than common stockholders, they are still subject to the company’s ability to meet its financial obligations. In the worst case, if the company goes bankrupt, preferred stockholders may lose their investment.

3. Call Risk

Some preferred stocks are callable, meaning the company has the option to redeem them before the maturity date, often at a premium. While this may seem like a benefit, it can limit the upside potential for investors, especially if interest rates fall. If the stock is called, investors may have to reinvest their funds at lower rates, reducing their overall return.

4. Lack of Voting Rights

Preferred stockholders generally do not have voting rights in company matters, such as electing the board of directors. While this is typical of fixed-income securities, it may be a drawback for investors who wish to have a say in the direction of the company.

5. Liquidity Risk

Preferred stocks are generally less liquid than common stocks, meaning they may not be as easy to buy or sell in the market. This can be particularly problematic for investors who need to liquidate their holdings quickly.

How to Invest in Preferred Stocks

Investing in preferred stocks is relatively straightforward, but there are a few different methods to consider. Investors can purchase preferred stocks directly through a brokerage account, much like they would with common stocks. Alternatively, they can invest in exchange-traded funds (ETFs) or mutual funds that focus on preferred stocks.

1. Direct Investment

For investors comfortable with selecting individual securities, buying preferred stocks directly can be an effective way to gain exposure to this asset class. Direct investment allows for greater control over the selection of specific issues, though it also requires more research and monitoring.

2. Preferred Stock Funds

For those looking for more diversification, preferred stock mutual funds and ETFs can be a great choice. These funds pool together a variety of preferred stocks from different sectors and companies, reducing individual security risk. Additionally, funds provide easier liquidity and are generally more tax-efficient than individual investments.

3. Dividend Reinvestment Plans (DRIPs)

Some companies offer Dividend Reinvestment Plans (DRIPs) for their preferred stockholders, allowing them to reinvest dividends back into additional shares of the same security. DRIPs can help investors compound their returns over time and build a larger position in the preferred stock.

Conclusion

Preferred stocks offer a unique combination of fixed income reliability and equity-like potential for growth. They can be an excellent addition to a diversified investment portfolio, providing investors with regular income and a relatively lower risk compared to common stocks. However, they come with their own set of risks, including interest rate risk and credit risk, which must be carefully considered before investing.

Understanding the various types of preferred stocks and their features can help investors make more informed decisions and tailor their investments to their specific needs. Whether you are an income-seeking investor or someone looking for a safer way to participate in the stock market, preferred stocks are worth considering as part of your broader investment strategy.
