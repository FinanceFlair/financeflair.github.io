What Are the Best Ways to Finance Your Business in Emerging Markets?


Navigating the financial landscape in emerging markets is both an exhilarating challenge and a daunting adventure for entrepreneurs. With rapid economic growth, evolving consumer bases, and government reforms, new market entrants and local players alike are seeking financial solutions uniquely suited to environments where traditional banking and credit facilities may lag behind developed norms. The question on every founder’s mind: How do you best finance your business in an emerging market context, where risks and rewards run equally high?

Below, you’ll find a comprehensive exploration of financing strategies that actually work on the ground, demystifying options from grassroots microfinance to sophisticated capital markets. Whether you’re a driven solo founder in Lagos, a family enterprise in Jakarta, or a scaling startup in Bogotá, this guide will offer not just theoretical possibilities, but actionable strategies rooted in the real dynamics of emerging markets.

1. Lean Startup Self-Funding and Bootstrapping

Starting with your own resources—savings, personal assets, and even “friends and family” capital—is the most common and often the only feasible early funding source in many emerging markets. Why?

  • Limited Formal Credit: Traditional banks may require extensive collateral, high-interest rates, or credit history that first-time and informal entrepreneurs simply don’t possess.
  • Control and Flexibility: Bootstrapping grants you full control over operations and decision-making in the critical early days.

Tips for Bootstrapping:

  • Start as lean as possible: prioritize essential expenses and reinvest profits aggressively.
  • Tap into family lending circles (“chit funds,” “tandas,” or “rotating savings and credit associations”) where relevant.

Yet, as you scale, personal funds often run thin. That’s when outside capital becomes a necessity.

2. Microfinance and Community-Based Lending

Microfinance institutions (MFIs) have revolutionized access to credit for tiny startups and small businesses across Africa, South Asia, and Latin America. These groups specialize in extending microloans—sometimes a few hundred dollars—to individuals and collectives that lack access to formal banking.

  • Advantages:

    • Low or no collateral requirements.
    • In-depth local knowledge and support, including business training and peer accountability.

  • Considerations:

    • Interest rates can be relatively high, reflecting the costs and risks of lending at scale.
    • Loan amounts are usually best suited for micro and small enterprise needs, rather than ambitious expansion.

For many, MFIs are the first external step in building creditworthiness and a formal financial record—a stepping stone to bigger funding rounds.

3. Angel Investors and Early-Stage Networks

As the digital economy booms and innovation hubs appear in places like Nairobi, Bangalore, and São Paulo, angel investors are seeking not just profits but purpose. These are usually individuals with business acumen—often ex-entrepreneurs themselves—willing to take risks on bold, early-stage ideas.

  • Why Angels Thrive in Emerging Markets:

    • Lower valuations mean investors can make impactful bets with less capital.
    • Entrepreneurs often receive valuable mentorship and networking opportunities, not just cash.

  • How to Attract Angel Investment:

    • Join local accelerator programs, pitch competitions, or innovation hubs.
    • Build a solid pitch deck and demonstrate strong market insight.

Emerging market angels might not write checks as big as their counterparts in Silicon Valley, but their presence adds growing dynamism and guidance to local startup ecosystems.

4. Venture Capital (VC) and Private Equity

The rise of unicorns and major exits in emerging economies has caught the eye of regional and international venture capital and private equity funds. With increased smartphone penetration, mobile payments, and savvy founders, investors are scouting for the next big opportunity beyond developed markets.

  • What Attracts VC to New Markets:

    • Fast-growing, underserved consumer segments.
    • Solutions tackling “leapfrog” opportunities — for example, mobile banking in the absence of widespread traditional banking.

  • VC Realities:

    • Competition for funding is fierce, especially as VCs often focus on bets that can scale regionally or globally.
    • Expectations on reporting, compliance, and governance can be stringent, and valuation negotiations may feel unfamiliar.

  • Strategies to Secure VC Interest:

    • Leverage traction—users, customers, and real-world impact matter more than pitch decks in most emerging contexts.
    • Target regionally-focused funds that understand local nuances, rather than only chasing international capital.

5. Government Grants and Incentive Programs

Many governments, well aware of the multiplier effect of robust entrepreneurship, offer targeted grants, tax breaks, or subsidized loans to catalyze certain sectors—technology, agriculture, manufacturing, and green initiatives, among others.

  • Pros:

    • Non-dilutive capital—no need to surrender equity.
    • Support may include technical assistance and access to networks.

  • Challenges:

    • Application processes can be slow and bureaucratic.
    • Funding windows are sometimes irregular or one-off opportunities.

Connecting with local chambers of commerce, industry associations, and government incubators is a smart move. These organizations are often the first to know about upcoming initiatives.

6. Crowdfunding Platforms

Crowdfunding has met the spirit of grassroots entrepreneurship like few other funding methods. From rewards-based models (think KickStarter) to equity crowdfunding, platforms are emerging that cater specifically to developing markets.

  • Advantages:

    • Builds a loyal community or customer base before launch.
    • Offers early brand visibility and market validation.

  • Barriers:

    • Regulatory frameworks in emerging markets are evolving, so compliance can be tricky.
    • Campaigns require strong storytelling and digital marketing skills—often overlooked but mission-critical.

Platforms like M-Changa in Kenya and Catarse in Brazil illustrate how tailored solutions are blossoming.

7. Strategic Partnerships and Corporate Venture

Large corporations, both local and global, are searching for innovation by integrating with nimble startups. Corporate venture capital arms, supplier financing programs, and distribution partnerships are all ways that smart businesses in emerging markets can secure not just capital, but market access and credibility.

  • What’s On Offer:

    • Joint ventures, pilot projects, or outright strategic investments.
    • Sharing resources—technology, marketing muscle, or distribution channels.

  • Landing a Partnership:

    • Focus your pitch on mutual advantage—how will both parties benefit?
    • Start small and seek proof of concept milestones before scaling.

These partnerships require careful negotiation and clarity on intellectual property, but they’re a potent way to leapfrog growth barriers.

8. Bank Loans and Trade Credit

Traditional banking is making slow but steady inroads in emerging markets, particularly with the rise of fintech platforms streamlining everything from loan applications to credit scoring.

  • Advantages:

    • Larger loan amounts for established businesses.
    • Sometimes more predictable and regulated terms than informal lending.

  • Barriers:

    • Collateral and audited financial statements are usually required.
    • High interest rates and bureaucracy remain persistent obstacles.

Trade credit—in which suppliers allow delayed payment for goods—can also serve as an invisible, crucial form of working capital. Negotiating favorable terms here can make a huge difference, especially for businesses with predictable cash flows.

9. Development Finance Institutions (DFIs) and Impact Investors

Development banks and impact-oriented investment funds have a mandate to foster economic growth, sustainability, and social well-being in emerging markets. These actors include heavyweights like the International Finance Corporation (IFC), African Development Bank (AfDB), and an ever-growing list of private impact investors.

  • Benefits:

    • Patient capital—longer investment horizons.
    • Non-financial support, such as industry expertise and connections.

  • Common Requirements:

    • Rigorous standards for reporting environmental or social outcomes alongside financial performance.
    • Thorough due diligence—sometimes a long lead time before funds are deployed.

Impact investment is best suited for businesses with a strong mission and measurable positive outcomes, such as renewable energy, affordable healthcare, or financial inclusion.

10. Export Credit Agencies and International Trade Finance

For export-oriented companies, leveraging instruments like export credits, guarantees, and insurance from local and international export credit agencies (ECAs) can provide working capital, safeguard against non-payment, and lower financing costs.

  • Why This Works:
    • Stimulates global expansion by reducing risk for both exporters and importers.
    • Often comes bundled with advisory support to navigate cross-border regulations.

Emerging market exporters should investigate what’s available from local ECAs or multilateral agencies like the Export-Import Bank.

11. Digital and Fintech Innovations

Digital lending, mobile payments, and peer-to-peer platforms are transforming the financing landscape across Asia, Africa, and Latin America. By leveraging alternative data (such as mobile money histories or social media engagement), these fintech startups are bridging the historic gap left by brick-and-mortar banking.

  • Examples:
    • Kiva (online micro-lending)
    • Branch, Tala, and other mobile-first lenders

These innovations are democratizing access to capital—though borrowers must remain vigilant about privacy, terms, and digital security.

Weaving It All Together: Choosing the Best Path

With so many options, how do you choose? The answer will depend on your business’s size, stage, industry, and unique local context. Here’s a streamlined approach:

  1. Start Close to Home: Leverage personal savings, family, or community-based microfinance to prove your concept.
  2. Build a Track Record: Use early traction to approach angel investors, small VCs, or participate in accelerator programs.
  3. Leverage Non-Dilutive Sources: Apply for grants, government incentives, or secure trade credit where possible.
  4. Diversify: Don’t rely on just one source—blend microloans, crowdfunding, and supplier credit as you scale.
  5. Think Strategically: If your business aligns with corporate needs, seek partnerships; if you deliver social impact, woo impact investors.
  6. Embrace Fintech: Explore whether digital lenders, peer-to-peer networks, or innovative payment platforms can unlock new opportunities.

Final Thoughts

Financing a business in an emerging market requires inventiveness, persistence, and a keen sense of local realities. The best strategies often blend tradition and innovation, combining tried-and-tested relationship networks with cutting-edge technology. Entrepreneurs who thrive are those who remain agile—not just in business planning, but in creative capital-raising.

The landscape will keep changing. As policymakers, investors, and communities join forces to build the next generation of global businesses from every corner of the world, the opportunities to finance your venture will only keep multiplying. The secret? Know your local ecosystem, stay connected, and never be afraid to mix and match the tools that suit your business and dreams.
