Understanding the Difference Between Testamentary and Living Trusts


As individuals navigate the complex world of estate planning, they often come across the terms "testamentary trust" and "living trust." While both are used to manage and distribute an individual's assets after their passing, they serve distinct purposes and have different implementations. Testamentary Trusts Testamentary trusts, also known as will trusts or testamentary trusts with a will, are trusts that are created under the terms of a deceased person's will. The trust typically becomes effective after the individual's passing, when the will is admitted to probate. The assets that are transferred to the trust are those left behind in the deceased person's will. Testamentary trusts are commonly used to manage assets such as real estate, bank accounts, and investments. Living Trusts Living trusts, on the other hand, are trusts that are established by an individual during their lifetime. These trusts are known as revocable trusts because they can be altered, amended or revoked by the grantor at any time. The grantor retains control over the assets in the trust, as he or she can remove assets from the trust and even terminate the trust. Upon the grantor's death, the assets are distributed according to the terms of the trust. Living trusts are commonly used to manage assets such as real estate, investments, and other assets such as retirement accounts and businesses. Key Differences One major difference between testamentary trusts and living trusts is the timing of when they become effective. Testamentary trusts become effective after the individual's passing, while living trusts become effective immediately upon its creation. Testamentary trusts require a probate proceeding in order to become effective, whereas living trusts typically do not. Another significant difference is the control and management of the assets. Testamentary trusts are formed during probate, which is a public proceeding that can be expensive and lengthy, whereas living trusts remain private and do not require probate. As a result, living trusts provide more flexibility and control over the management of the assets. Advantages of Testamentary Trusts There are situations where testamentary trusts might be a preferable option. Testamentary trusts are usually less expensive than living trusts to create, and may also be more convenient for individuals who do not have large estates or complex requirements. Disadvantages of Testamentary Trusts There are also scenarios where testamentary trusts might not be the best option. Testamentary trusts may require probate, which can be a lengthy and expensive process. Additionally, testamentary trusts may also have tax implications, such as the creation of a taxable estate. Advantages of Living Trusts Living trusts offer several advantages. One significant advantage is that they provide more control and management over the assets included in the trust. It also avoids the lengthy and expensive probate process. Disadvantages of Living Trusts One limitation of living trusts is that they may be more expensive to create than a testamentary trust. Additionally, they require ongoing maintenance and upkeep, such as updating beneficiaries and asset values. When to Use Each Both types of trusts serve useful purposes in estates. Testamentary trusts are suitable for individuals with straightforward wishes and relatively small estates. Testamentary trusts are also a more affordable option upfront. Living trusts, on the other hand, are more suitable for individuals with larger or more complex estates. They also offer the advantage of privacy and are suitable for individuals wishing to avoid the probate process. Estate Planning Considerations When deciding whether to utilize a testamentary trust or a living trust, estate planners and individuals must consider various factors. Estate planning involves multiple considerations, which may ultimately determine whether an individual selects a testamentary trust or a living trust. Factors to consider may include, but not be limited to, property value, ownership, state of residence, the deceased person's wishes, and cost. In conclusion, both testamentary and living trusts can be essential tools in estate planning. Testamentary trusts are more suitable for individuals with straightforward estate wishes and smaller estates. Living trusts, while potentially more expensive in the long run, offer increased control and management of the included assets, reduced probate expenses, and several tax advantages. It is advisable that individuals seeking estate planning explore all relevant options before making an informed decision.
