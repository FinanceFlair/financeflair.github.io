How to Use Trend Following to Trade with the Trend


In the dynamic world of financial markets, the phrase “the trend is your friend” is more than just an adage; it’s a proven philosophy embraced by traders who seek consistent growth in their portfolios. Understanding how to trade with the trend through the art and science of trend following can make the difference between sporadic gains and long-term success. This in-depth guide explores practical strategies for identifying trends, executing trades, managing risks, and adapting to changing market conditions. Whether you’re a beginner itching to step into the markets or an experienced trader refining your system, there’s plenty of actionable wisdom here.

What is Trend Following?

Trend following is a trading methodology based on the belief that securities which have performed strongly (or weakly) will continue to do so. Rather than trying to anticipate reversals or predict market tops and bottoms, trend followers let the price dictate their moves. The goal is to enter early enough in trending moves and ride the wave as long as possible, extracting profits from significant, sustained price swings.

Unlike short-term speculation or high-frequency strategies, trend following is a patient approach. Entry and exit decisions are guided by signals tied to price momentum, moving averages, and other tools that reflect the underlying directional bias of the market.

Why Trade with the Trend?

Markets are dominated by periods where prices move decisively in one direction, whether up or down. Attempting to call turning points is notoriously difficult—even for professionals. Trading with the prevailing direction offers several advantages:

Psychological Edge: Riding established trends is less stressful than constantly battling market tides.

Simplicity: The core logic is straightforward—trade in the direction of the broader move.

Asymmetrical Returns: Catching even a portion of a strong trend can make up for multiple small losses from whipsaws or false signals.

How to Identify Trends

Before implementing a trend-following system, you must learn to spot real trends. Here’s how to recognize them with clarity:

1. Price Action Analysis

Price movement is the ultimate indicator. An uptrend forms higher highs and higher lows; a downtrend, lower highs and lower lows. Zooming out to high timeframes helps filter short-term noise and focus on significant shifts.

  • Visual inspection: Scroll through charts looking for clear, sustained movement in one direction.
  • Breakouts: Trends often begin after a breakout from range-bound action or consolidation.

2. Moving Averages

Moving averages smooth out price data, making trends more discernible. Two ways to use them:

  • Single moving average: Price staying above a long-term average (like the 200-day) signals an uptrend; below, a downtrend.
  • Dual moving average crossovers: When a short-term average (e.g., 50-day) crosses above a long-term (200-day), it’s bullish; crossing below is bearish.

3. Price Channels and Trendlines

Drawing trendlines that connect successive highs or lows visually encapsulates the trend. Channels expand on this, outlining the path of the move and offering potential zones for pullbacks and reversals.

4. Technical Indicators

Tools like the Average Directional Index (ADX), MACD, and Parabolic SAR can help confirm trend strength and direction. While no indicator is perfect, when several align, the case grows stronger.

Constructing a Trend Following System

A well-structured system combines entry signals, trade management, and exit criteria in a repeatable framework. Here’s how to construct your own:

Defining the Trading Universe

  • Asset selection: Focus on markets with strong trending tendencies—major stock indices, leading individual stocks, currencies, or commodities.
  • Timeframes: Daily and weekly charts often offer the clearest trends and are less noisy than lower timeframes.

Entry Signals

  • Breakouts: Enter when price closes above a recent high (for long trades) or below a recent low (for shorts).
  • Pullbacks: Wait for price to retrace to a support level or moving average before entering in the trend direction.

Position Sizing

Determine how much capital to allocate per trade. Classic trend followers often risk a fixed percentage (like 1–2%) of their account on each position, calibrating size based on market volatility.

Stop Losses

Wide enough to avoid getting picked off by normal price fluctuations, but tight enough to protect the account from major setbacks. Common methods:

  • ATR-based stops: Use the Average True Range to measure recent volatility and set stops accordingly.
  • Swing point stops: Place stops just beyond the most recent swing high (for shorts) or swing low (for longs).

Exits and Profit-Taking

Many trend followers let the market dictate exits, only closing positions when the trend genuinely ends. Approaches include:

  • Trailing stops: Ratchet stop loss orders higher as price advances, so profits are locked in as the move continues.
  • Moving average cross: Exit when price closes below a key moving average.

Managing Risk in Trend Following

No system is immune to drawdowns. Risk management isn’t just a defensive measure—it’s central to long-term survival and growth.

  • Diversify: Hold positions in multiple, non-correlated markets. Trends rarely occur everywhere at once.
  • Keep losses small: Accept small losses without hesitation. Large winners from persistent trends will compensate in the long run.
  • Never add to losing positions: Unlike mean-reversion or averaging-down approaches, trend following thrives by doubling down on winners, not losers.

Backtesting and Optimization

Backtesting assesses how your system would have performed historically, helping you build confidence and spot weaknesses.

  • Use accurate, quality data: Historical price data must reflect the reality of the markets you wish to trade.
  • Include all costs: Slippage, commissions, and fees must be factored in.
  • Avoid overfitting: Simpler rules often perform better in real markets than hyper-optimized, curve-fitted models.

Common Challenges and Solutions

Even robust trend-following systems hit rough patches. Here’s how to handle them:

1. Whipsaws and False Breakouts

Choppy, sideways markets produce the dreaded whipsaw—quick losses as price reverses right after a trade entry.

Solution: Focus on longer timeframes, add filters such as minimum volatility thresholds, and avoid overtrading.

2. Waiting for the Start of a Trend

Timing can be frustrating; entering too early leads to losses, too late means leaving profits on the table.

Solution: Accept that catching every move is impossible. Focus on capturing the bulk of trends, not the absolute start or end.

3. Emotional Discipline

Trend following means sitting tight during periods of volatility or minor corrections—a test of patience and composure.

Solution: Pre-define all rules and trust the system. Journaling trades and reviewing performance helps maintain discipline.

Case Study: Classic Trend Following in Action

Many legendary traders built fortunes using trend following. One stand-out example is the story of the Turtle Traders. In the 1980s, legendary commodities trader Richard Dennis taught a group of novices a simple, rules-based system. The rules focused on buying breakouts in trending futures markets and cutting losses quickly. Over several years, this group achieved spectacular profits, proving that following trends with discipline works across markets and eras.

Trend Following in Modern Markets

While the mechanics change—algorithms trade faster, and data flows never stop—the principles still apply. Trend following can be adapted for:

  • Stock markets: Capture persistent uptrends in growth stocks or major indices.
  • Forex: Trade long-term currency cycles driven by economic forces.
  • Commodities: Ride booms and busts triggered by supply-and-demand imbalances.
  • Cryptocurrencies: Volatility favors those who can ride huge up- and down-swings.

Building Your Personalized Trend Following Approach

Every trader has unique risk tolerance, lifestyle, and financial goals. The key is to customize a trend-following strategy that suits you:

  • Automated or discretionary? Some traders prefer fully automated systems; others like the hands-on approach.
  • Frequency of review: Decide if you want to check trades daily, weekly, or less often.
  • Personal filters: Include only assets or signals you fully trust and understand.

Measuring Performance and Iterating

Trade journaling is an underrated edge. Record every trade, rationale, win, loss, and lesson. Regularly review results to find:

  • Winning trade characteristics: What do your biggest winners have in common?
  • Losing trade patterns: Did you follow the rules? Was the signal clear?
  • Align with market conditions: Sometimes, markets trend less; adapting position sizes and expectations can help stay in the game.

Mistakes to Avoid in Trend Following

  • Overcomplicating: Cramming too many indicators or signals creates confusion and inaction.
  • Impatience: Sitting out during non-trending markets is part of the process.
  • Ignoring risk: A single large loss can undo months of steady gains.
  • Deviation from plan: Doubting the system in tough times leads to unstructured decision-making.

Final Thoughts: Mastering the Art of Riding Trends

Mastering trend following is a journey, not a destination. The most successful traders aren’t fortune tellers; they’re diligent, disciplined risk managers who let the market show its hand first. Embrace the philosophy of trading with the trend, invest time in developing a clear, repeatable process, and let your winners run longer than your losers. The ultimate power of trend following lies in its simplicity, robustness, and alignment with the core logic of market movement.

By consistently leaning into the rhythm of the market, staying humble, and letting price guide your actions, you’ll not only survive the markets’ inevitable storms—you’ll find ways to thrive.
