The Impact of Survivorship Bias in Investing: Learning from Failures as well as Successes


Survivorship bias is a phenomenon that often goes unnoticed in the world of investing. This bias occurs when we focus only on the successful outcomes and ignore the failures. In other words, we tend to study and learn from the winners, while neglecting the losers. But what if we told you that by understanding and analyzing the failures, we can actually gain valuable insights that can help us make better investment decisions? In this blog post, we will explore the impact of survivorship bias in investing and discuss how learning from failures as well as successes can lead to more informed and successful investment strategies. Survivorship bias is a cognitive bias that occurs when we concentrate on the outcomes that have made it through a particular selection or filtering process, while disregarding those that didn’t. In the context of investing, survivorship bias occurs when we only focus on the successful companies or investments that have thrived and ignore the ones that have failed or underperformed.

Let’s take an example to illustrate this bias. Imagine you are studying the performance of a group of mutual funds over a ten-year period. If you only consider the funds that have survived and are still operational at the end of the period, you are inadvertently excluding the funds that have failed or been dissolved. This exclusion can skew your analysis, as you are only looking at a select group of successful funds, rather than considering the entire universe of funds that were available at the beginning of the period.

The Dangers of Survivorship Bias

Survivorship bias can lead to misleading conclusions and flawed investment strategies. By focusing solely on successful investments, we may develop a distorted view of the market and believe that success is more achievable than it actually is. This can result in overconfidence and excessive risk-taking, as we underestimate the potential for failure.

Moreover, survivorship bias can lead to the adoption of strategies or approaches that may not be effective in the long term. By disregarding failed investments, we miss out on valuable lessons and insights that could help us identify potential pitfalls and avoid making similar mistakes in the future.

Learning from Failures

To overcome survivorship bias, it is crucial to learn from failures as well as successes. By analyzing failed investments, we can identify common patterns, understand the warning signs, and gain a deeper understanding of the risks involved. This knowledge can help us make more informed decisions and develop robust investment strategies.

One effective way to learn from failures is to conduct post-mortem analyses. By examining failed investments, we can identify the reasons behind their underperformance or failure. Was it due to poor management, industry shifts, or unforeseen circumstances? By answering these questions, we can gain valuable insights that can inform our future investment decisions.

Additionally, learning from failures also requires a mindset shift. Instead of viewing failures as a setback or a loss, we should embrace them as opportunities for growth and learning. Each failure presents a chance to reassess our strategies, refine our approach, and improve our decision-making processes.

Embracing a Balanced Approach

To navigate the world of investing successfully, it is essential to embrace a balanced approach that considers both successes and failures. By acknowledging and studying the failures, we can gain a more realistic perspective of the market and make more prudent investment decisions.

Here are a few practical steps to adopt a balanced approach:

  1. Diversify your portfolio: By diversifying your investments across different asset classes, industries, and geographies, you can mitigate the impact of individual failures and increase your chances of overall success.

  2. Conduct thorough research: Before making any investment, conduct thorough research and due diligence. Analyze the historical performance, assess the risks, and consider both the successes and failures of similar investments.

  3. Seek expert advice: Consulting with financial advisors or experts can provide you with valuable insights and help you navigate the complexities of the investment landscape. They can offer unbiased opinions and challenge your assumptions, reducing the risk of succumbing to survivorship bias.

  4. Learn from others: Engage with investment communities, attend conferences, and participate in discussions to learn from the experiences and insights of others. By embracing a collaborative mindset, you can gain diverse perspectives and avoid the pitfalls of survivorship bias.

In conclusion, survivorship bias is a prevalent cognitive bias in investing that can lead to flawed decision-making and ineffective investment strategies. By learning from failures as well as successes, we can gain a more accurate understanding of the market and make more informed investment decisions. Embracing a balanced approach that acknowledges both sides of the coin is essential for long-term success in the world of investing.
