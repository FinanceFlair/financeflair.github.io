A Basic Guide to Preference Shares: An Income-Investing Perspective


Preference shares represent a unique form of equity investment that combines elements of both debt and equity securities. As a potential income-investing option, they offer a steady and predictable stream of dividends. In this guide, we will explore the essential aspects of preference shares and how they can fit into an investment portfolio.

What are Preference Shares?

Preference shares are a type of hybrid security that shares some characteristics with both bonds and common shares. Unlike common shares, which carry an ownership interest in a company, preference shares represent a separate class of stock with a lower claim on the company’s assets and earnings. This distinction gives preference shareholders preferential treatment when it comes to receiving dividends and other corporate distributions.

Advantages of Investing in Preference Shares

  1. Steady Income: One of the primary benefits of preference shares is their potential for providing a steady stream of income. Given their prioritized position for dividends, preference shareholders often enjoy more reliable and predictable payouts compared to common shareholders.
  2. Capital Appreciation: Depending on market conditions and a company’s financial performance, preference shares can potentially appreciate in value over time, offering the prospect of capital gains in addition to dividend income.
  3. Relative Safety: As a hybrid instrument, preference shares are less risky than bonds and more secure than common shares. This makes them an attractive option for investors seeking a relatively low-risk investment with income potential.
  4. Liquidity: Many preference shares are traded on public stock exchanges, which can make it easier to buy and sell them compared to some other types of investments, increasing their overall liquidity.

How do Preference Shares Work?

Preference shares work as follows:

  1. Dividend Rights: Preference shareholders usually receive dividends before common shareholders but after any debt holders. The amount of dividends is often fixed or can be linked to a specific benchmark, offering a more predictable income stream.
  2. Fallen Angel Status: If a company’s financial situation worsens, and it can no longer afford to pay the regular preference share dividends, the shares might be labeled as “fallen angels.” This change can impact their market value and attractiveness to investors.
  3. Conversion and Redemption: Certain types of preference shares can be converted into common shares, providing an additional avenue for potential gains. Additionally, preference shares can have a maturity date at which they are redeemable at the issuer’s discretion or according to a predetermined schedule.
  4. Dividend Reinvestment Plans: Some companies offer dividend reinvestment plans, allowing preference shareholders to automatically reinvest their dividends in additional shares. This strategy can compound returns over time.

What to Consider Before Investing in Preference Shares

  1. Credit Risk: As with any investment, it’s essential to evaluate the issuer’s creditworthiness, including its financial strength and its ability to make payments on both debt and preference shares.
  2. Category Complexity: Remember that not all preference shares are created equal, so it’s vital to understand the differences between various categories, such as cumulative, non-cumulative, and redeemable preference shares, and to weigh these factors accordingly.
  3. Market Volatility: Preference shares can be subject to market volatility, as equity investments tend to be. Be prepared for fluctuations in value and consider diversifying your portfolio to spread out risk.
  4. Tax Considerations: Depending on where you live, the tax treatment of preference share dividends may differ. Consult a tax professional to understand any potential implications on your personal tax situation.

In conclusion, preference shares can be a compelling investment option for those seeking a steady and predictable stream of income. By understanding their characteristics, advantages, and potential risks, you can make informed decisions and potentially build a well-diversified income-generating portfolio. Remember always to do your own due diligence and consult with a professional before making any investment decisions.
