What Is a Health Savings Account (HSA), and How Does It Work?


For individuals looking to take control of their healthcare costs while enjoying potential tax benefits, a Health Savings Account (HSA) provides a valuable solution. HSAs are increasingly gaining popularity, especially as healthcare costs rise and people strive to budget their medical expenses effectively. This guide will walk you through what an HSA is, who qualifies for one, how it works, and the benefits it can offer. By the end, you’ll have a thorough understanding of whether an HSA might be right for your needs.

What Is a Health Savings Account (HSA)?

A Health Savings Account, or HSA, is a special type of savings account designed to help individuals pay for qualified medical expenses. It is available to those enrolled in a high-deductible health plan (HDHP) and comes with unique tax advantages that set it apart from standard savings or checking accounts.

The primary goal of an HSA is to allow you to save and spend money on eligible healthcare expenses tax-free. Money contributed to the account can be used for expenses like doctor visits, prescription medications, dental care, vision services, and much more, as long as they qualify under the guidelines set by the IRS.

While HSAs are primarily healthcare-focused, what makes them stand out is their flexibility as both a savings tool and an investment option. Funds contributed to an HSA roll over year after year, meaning that unused money remains in the account, growing tax-free, and can even be invested to build long-term savings.

Who Qualifies for an HSA?

Not everyone can open or contribute to an HSA. To be eligible, you must meet the following requirements:

  1. Enrollment in a High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP): To open an HSA, you must be covered by an HDHP, which is a health insurance plan with a higher annual deductible and out-of-pocket limit than standard plans. HDHPs are designed to encourage individuals to take a more proactive role in managing their healthcare costs.

    For 2024, the IRS defines an HDHP as a plan with:

    • A minimum deductible of $1,600 for individuals or $3,200 for families.
    • Maximum out-of-pocket expenses (including deductibles, copayments, and coinsurance) of $8,050 for individuals or $16,100 for families.

  2. No Coverage by a Non-HDHP: You cannot be covered by any other health insurance plan that is not an HDHP. This includes traditional employer-sponsored plans with lower deductibles.

  3. Not Enrolled in Medicare: If you’re already covered by Medicare (Part A or Part B), you are not eligible to contribute to an HSA.

  4. Not Claimed as a Dependent: You can’t be claimed as a dependent on someone else’s tax return and still contribute to your own HSA.

If you meet these qualifications, you can open an HSA and enjoy the benefits of tax-advantaged healthcare savings.

How Does an HSA Work?

HSAs are designed to be straightforward and user-friendly. Here’s how they work, step by step:

1. Opening an Account

Once you’re enrolled in an HDHP, you can open an HSA through a bank, credit union, insurance company, or other financial institution. Some employers may also offer HSAs as part of their benefits package. Choose an HSA provider that meets your needs in terms of fees, investment options, and account management tools.

2. Contributing to the Account

Each year, the IRS sets contribution limits for HSAs. For 2024, the maximum you can contribute is:

  • $4,150 for individuals with self-only HDHP coverage.
  • $8,300 for families with HDHP coverage.

Additionally, those age 55 or older can make a “catch-up” contribution of up to $1,000 per year.

Contributions can be made in several ways:

  • From your paycheck (pre-tax) if offered by your employer.
  • Directly from your bank account (post-tax), which you can deduct on your tax return.

3. Using the Funds

Once money is deposited into your HSA, you can use it to cover a wide range of qualified medical expenses. These expenses include:

  • Doctor visits and hospital care.
  • Prescription medications.
  • Medical equipment like wheelchairs or hearing aids.
  • Dental and vision services, including braces and eyeglasses.

Funds can be accessed using an HSA debit card, checks, or reimbursement processes depending on your provider.

4. Tax Advantages

The tax benefits of an HSA are threefold:

  • Pre-Tax Contributions: Money you contribute to the account is not subject to federal income tax, reducing your taxable income.
  • Tax-Free Growth: The money in your HSA grows tax-free through interest or investments.
  • Tax-Free Withdrawals: Distributions used for qualified medical expenses are not taxed.

5. Rollover and Flexibility

One of the biggest perks of an HSA is that there’s no “use-it-or-lose-it” rule; the funds roll over each year. Whether you use them immediately or save for future medical expenses, the balance carries forward indefinitely.

What Happens to an HSA After Age 65?

By age 65, you may no longer be enrolled in an HDHP, as most people transition to Medicare. However, your HSA funds remain available. Here’s what changes:

  • If used for qualified medical expenses, withdrawals remain tax-free.
  • If used for non-medical expenses, withdrawals are taxed as regular income, similar to a traditional IRA.

This flexibility allows you to treat your HSA as an emergency fund for healthcare or a supplemental retirement account.

What Are the Advantages of an HSA?

HSAs offer several key benefits that make them an attractive choice for managing healthcare costs:

  1. Triple Tax Advantage: With tax breaks on contributions, growth, and withdrawals, HSAs are one of the most tax-advantaged savings vehicles available.

  2. Savings for Retirement: Over time, HSA funds can grow through investments, functioning as a secondary retirement account that focuses on healthcare costs but offers flexibility for other needs.

  3. Portability: The account belongs to you, not your employer. If you change jobs or leave your HDHP, you can keep your HSA and use it as needed.

  4. Encourages Smart Spending: Because HSAs are tied to HDHPs, they encourage individuals to make informed decisions about their healthcare, which can lead to cost savings.

  5. No Time Limit: Unlike Flexible Spending Accounts (FSAs), HSAs do not require you to use the funds by the end of the year.

Potential Drawbacks to Consider

While HSAs offer numerous advantages, it’s important to consider potential downsides:

  1. High-Deductible Requirement: HDHPs typically involve higher out-of-pocket costs, which may not suit everyone, particularly those who require frequent or costly medical care.

  2. Limited Investment Choices: Depending on your provider, investment options and returns may be limited, requiring careful evaluation.

  3. Penalty for Non-Medical Use: Withdrawals for non-qualified expenses are subject to a 20% penalty and income tax. This penalty disappears after age 65, but taxes still apply.

Is an HSA Right for You?

Determining whether an HSA is the right fit requires evaluating your healthcare needs, financial goals, and risk tolerance. HSAs are ideal for individuals who:

  • Are in good health and have low ongoing medical costs.
  • Want to save for future healthcare expenses or retirement.
  • Have a stable income, allowing for regular contributions to the account.

On the other hand, individuals who expect significant medical costs in the near future may prioritize other healthcare plans with lower deductibles.

Final Thoughts

A Health Savings Account is much more than just a tool for managing medical expenses—it’s an investment in your financial future. The combination of immediate tax savings, long-term growth potential, and flexibility makes HSAs a powerful option for those enrolled in HDHPs. However, as with any financial decision, it’s crucial to assess your personal circumstances and consult with a healthcare or tax professional if needed.

By understanding how an HSA works and leveraging its benefits wisely, you can take confident steps toward managing healthcare costs while building a more secure financial foundation for the future.
