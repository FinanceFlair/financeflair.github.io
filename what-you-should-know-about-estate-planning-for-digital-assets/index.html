What You Should Know About Estate Planning for Digital Assets


Today, our digital lives stretch across dozens of platforms, dozens of passwords, and dozens of data clouds. Social media profiles, online banking, cryptocurrency wallets, cloud storage, email—these are just a fraction of the digital assets people accumulate. But when it comes to estate planning, the digital world is often overlooked, leaving loved ones burdened with confusion and possible financial loss.

Let’s explore everything you should know about estate planning for digital assets—what counts as a digital asset, why you need to consider them in your estate plan, the risks of neglecting them, and exactly how you can proactively secure your digital life for peace of mind.

Defining Digital Assets

Digital assets are any electronic records you own, manage, or control. Their scope is much broader than most people realize. Some examples include:

  • Email and cloud accounts: Gmail, iCloud, Outlook, and all the treasures or details these hold.
  • Social media: Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, X (Twitter), Snapchat, LinkedIn, and others.
  • Financial accounts: Bank accounts managed primarily online, crypto wallets, payment services like PayPal or Venmo.
  • Subscription services: Streaming media (Netflix, Spotify, Apple Music), ebooks, domain names, and gaming accounts.
  • Personal content: Photos and videos stored online, blogs, websites, and unpublished manuscripts.
  • Intellectual property: Any digital copyrights or trademarks, including digital art, music, or code.

Digital assets are not just addresses and passwords—they can represent real financial value or irreplaceable sentimental worth.

Why Digital Assets Matter in Your Estate Plan

Neglecting digital assets creates significant headaches for heirs. Here’s why it matters:

  • Financial losses: Unaccounted cryptocurrency can simply vanish. Recurring bills tied to online accounts can drain balances with no oversight.
  • Identity theft: Unmonitored social media or email accounts become ripe targets for hackers.
  • Personal legacy: Family photos, home videos, and important correspondence can be lost forever if no one knows how to access them.
  • Legal battles: Family disputes drag on longer when ownership of online assets isn’t clear or accessible.

The reality is that digital assets aren’t subject to traditional probate processes in the way physical property is. Most tech companies have strict privacy rules that prevent family from accessing accounts, even if they’re named in your will. You need to take additional, purposeful steps.

What Happens If You Don’t Plan?

Without a clear plan for your digital estate, family or executors could face:

  • Difficulties finding and identifying all your accounts or subscriptions
  • Legal roadblocks if companies refuse to hand over access
  • Lost assets, abandoned funds, or erased profiles
  • Ongoing costs from forgotten auto-renewals

And perhaps the worst: cherished memories wiped clean, simply because no one knew they existed.

How to Start Your Digital Estate Planning

Getting a handle on your digital assets can be daunting, but the actual process boils down to a series of manageable steps.

1. Compile a Comprehensive Inventory

Start by listing all your digital assets. Include:

  • Account names and login URLs
  • Associated email addresses
  • A general description of what’s stored, why it matters, or its value

Skip putting passwords directly in your will; it’s not secure. But do clearly identify each account so someone trustworthy knows what to look for.

2. Store Credentials Securely

Passwords and PINs shouldn’t be lying around in a notebook. Consider using a password manager that allows you to designate a digital heir. Some modern password vaults feature emergency access for trusted contacts. Alternatively, maintain an encrypted file, secure USB drive, or even a physically locked document that only your executor can access.

Whatever method you pick, keep it updated. Make it clear where and how to find your key credentials.

3. Understand Platform-Specific Policies

Most major platforms dictate, in their terms of service, what happens to your data when you die.

  • Google: Use Google’s Inactive Account Manager to determine what happens to your data if your account is inactive.
  • Facebook: Set a legacy contact to manage your page, memorialize your account, or delete it.
  • Apple: Digital Legacy allows a person to access your iCloud data after you pass.

Investigate major services you use. Follow their recommended procedures to ensure your wishes are honored.

4. Designate a Digital Executor

A digital executor is someone empowered specifically to manage your digital assets after you’re gone. This might be the same person as your traditional executor, or someone more tech-savvy.

Give this person clear instructions about what to do with your accounts—close, memorialize, transfer, or delete them. Write this out and store it with your estate planning documents.

5. Update Your Legal Documents

Work with your estate attorney to explicitly include digital assets in your will or trust. Be detailed about your intent, the types of assets, and who should manage them. State laws vary, but more states are recognizing digital executor roles and the legality of bequeathing digital property. This helps prevent confusion or legal disputes later.

6. Make Your Wishes Clear

For some assets, like online photos or social media, your primary concern may not be financial. Tell your heirs which accounts matter for sentimental reasons, which accounts to delete, and whether any digital property (like code or writing) can be freely shared or published.

Tips for Handling Digital Assets in Estate Planning

A few extra tips to keep you organized and ahead of the curve:

  • Review regularly: Update your inventory yearly, or whenever you add new accounts or change significant passwords.
  • Check every platform: Many online services now offer specific legacy or inheritance options. Take five minutes per account to activate these.
  • Double-check state law: Some jurisdictions have adopted versions of the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA), which clarifies digital estate law. Know your local rules.
  • Think beyond passwords: Consider digital property rights—ebooks, purchased movies, gaming items, domain names, or unpublished works.
  • Consider privacy: Not every email or file should pass to family. Be deliberate about what should be seen, kept, or erased.

Special Considerations

Cryptocurrency and NFTs

Cryptocurrencies (Bitcoin, Ethereum, etc.) and NFTs bring extra risk. With no central authority to reset a forgotten password, your crypto wallet is truly lost without a recovery phrase or seed key. Protecting and passing on crypto assets means securing those credentials and making sure trusted heirs know where to find them.

  • Use a hardware wallet with clear, written instructions.
  • Document the existence and location of wallets.
  • Designate a crypto-savvy digital executor if possible.

Business Assets

Digital assets tied to a business—domain names, website logins, client databases—can be critically valuable. Identify, organize, and secure these separately, as losing them could mean major operational or financial risk for your heirs or business partners.

Subscription Fatigue and Hidden Costs

Recurring subscriptions, from streaming sites to services like Amazon Prime, can quietly continue charging after you’re gone. Be sure your inventory includes all paid digital services, so these can be shut down and budgets can be protected.

How to Talk to Loved Ones About Digital Asset Planning

Conversations about estate plans rarely feel fun, and the digital side only makes things more abstract. Yet, open communication makes things dramatically easier.

  • Share your inventory’s location with your chosen executor or a trusted family member.
  • Explain your motives: Emphasize why you’ve made certain decisions, especially if some assets are intended for specific people.
  • Provide tech support: If you have less tech-savvy heirs, walk them through accessing your password manager or the basics of handling your accounts.

The Future of Digital Estate Planning

The pace of digital advancement keeps accelerating, and the value tied up in digital platforms is growing. Estate planning is already adapting, but keeping up is a personal responsibility—technology moves much faster than the law.

Over the next decade, we can expect new tools that make digital inheritance easier. Still, a thoughtful, hands-on approach will always be better than relying on one-size-fits-all software.

Wrapping up

Digital assets are now core to modern life—personally, professionally, and financially. Including these in your estate plan isn’t just smart; it’s becoming absolutely necessary. By cataloging your accounts, securing your passwords, appointing a digital executor, and making your intentions known, you can simplify things dramatically for loved ones, preserve your legacy, and keep your digital footprint safe.

Take some time today to start your digital inventory, explore the legacy options on your favorite platforms, and make sure your digital story doesn’t end with a password lost to the ages.
