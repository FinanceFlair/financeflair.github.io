A Guide to Common Size Financial Statements


In the world of financial analysis, understanding the financial health and performance of a business is key for making informed decisions. One of the most effective tools for achieving this is the common size financial statement. By expressing each line item as a percentage of a key figure, common size financial statements allow for easy comparison across companies, industries, or even time periods. This guide explores the concept of common size financial statements, their importance, how to prepare them, and how to use them for better business insights.

What are Common Size Financial Statements?

Common size financial statements are a type of financial statement where each line item is presented as a percentage of a selected base. These base figures are typically total revenue (for income statements) or total assets (for balance sheets). Common size analysis simplifies the comparison between companies of different sizes and helps track changes in financial performance over time.

The main types of financial statements that can be converted into common size statements are:

  1. Common Size Income Statement: In this version, each item on the income statement is expressed as a percentage of total revenue (or sales).
  2. Common Size Balance Sheet: In this version, each item on the balance sheet is expressed as a percentage of total assets.

This transformation allows stakeholders, analysts, investors, and even internal management to quickly assess the financial health and efficiency of a company. Common size analysis is a powerful tool for benchmarking, trend analysis, and cross-sectional comparisons.

The Importance of Common Size Financial Statements

1. Enhanced Comparability

A primary benefit of common size financial statements is that they make it easier to compare companies of different sizes. For instance, comparing a small start-up with a multinational corporation can be difficult due to the disparity in their revenue figures. Common size analysis eliminates this challenge by converting all figures into percentages. This allows for more meaningful comparisons, as the focus shifts to financial ratios and trends rather than absolute numbers.

2. Trend Analysis

Common size statements also make it easier to analyze a company’s financial trends over time. By looking at the percentage of each line item relative to total revenue or total assets over several periods, analysts can quickly detect patterns. For example, if the percentage of revenue spent on research and development is increasing, it might signal a shift in the company’s strategy or focus.

3. Identify Potential Issues

A common size income statement or balance sheet can highlight areas where a company is overspending or underperforming. If a company’s gross margin (as a percentage of revenue) is declining, or if its debt level (as a percentage of total assets) is rising, these signals can serve as early warnings for potential financial distress.

4. Industry Benchmarking

Different industries have different financial characteristics, and what might be considered a healthy profit margin for one industry could be a red flag for another. Common size financial statements make it easy to benchmark a company’s performance against industry standards. For instance, you can compare a company’s operating expenses as a percentage of revenue with the average for its industry.

How to Create Common Size Financial Statements

1. Common Size Income Statement

To create a common size income statement, each line item on the income statement is expressed as a percentage of total revenue (or net sales). The formula to calculate each line item as a percentage is:

[ \text{Percentage} = \left( \frac{\text{Line Item Value}}{\text{Total Revenue}} \right) \times 100 ]

For example, consider an income statement with the following values:

  • Total revenue: $500,000
  • Cost of goods sold (COGS): $300,000
  • Operating expenses: $100,000
  • Net income: $50,000

To calculate the common size percentages:

  • COGS percentage: ( \frac{300,000}{500,000} \times 100 = 60% )
  • Operating expenses percentage: ( \frac{100,000}{500,000} \times 100 = 20% )
  • Net income percentage: ( \frac{50,000}{500,000} \times 100 = 10% )

Thus, each line item on the income statement would now be expressed as a percentage of total revenue, making it easier to compare and analyze.

2. Common Size Balance Sheet

In a common size balance sheet, each item is expressed as a percentage of total assets. The formula to calculate each line item as a percentage is:

[ \text{Percentage} = \left( \frac{\text{Line Item Value}}{\text{Total Assets}} \right) \times 100 ]

For example, consider a balance sheet with the following values:

  • Total assets: $1,000,000
  • Cash: $100,000
  • Accounts receivable: $200,000
  • Liabilities: $500,000
  • Equity: $500,000

To calculate the common size percentages:

  • Cash percentage: ( \frac{100,000}{1,000,000} \times 100 = 10% )
  • Accounts receivable percentage: ( \frac{200,000}{1,000,000} \times 100 = 20% )
  • Liabilities percentage: ( \frac{500,000}{1,000,000} \times 100 = 50% )
  • Equity percentage: ( \frac{500,000}{1,000,000} \times 100 = 50% )

Now, the balance sheet shows each item as a percentage of total assets, allowing you to assess the structure of the company’s financial position.

How to Use Common Size Financial Statements

1. Compare Performance Across Time

Analyzing common size statements over multiple periods can help identify trends. For instance, if a company’s cost of sales as a percentage of total revenue is increasing over time, it may indicate rising production costs. Similarly, an increase in operating expenses as a percentage of revenue might suggest inefficiencies or rising administrative costs. These trends can help managers make strategic decisions about cost control and resource allocation.

2. Evaluate Operational Efficiency

Common size income statements allow you to evaluate operational efficiency by analyzing various cost components. For example, you can examine the percentage of revenue spent on sales and marketing, R&D, or administrative expenses. A sharp increase in any of these areas might raise concerns about how efficiently resources are being utilized.

3. Assess Financial Structure

On the balance sheet side, common size financial statements help assess a company’s financial structure. By comparing the percentage of assets funded by debt versus equity, you can get an idea of the company’s leverage and risk level. For example, a company with a high percentage of debt compared to equity might be more vulnerable to economic downturns due to its higher financial risk.

4. Benchmark Against Industry Peers

Common size financial statements are invaluable for industry benchmarking. By comparing your company’s financial ratios with industry averages, you can assess whether your business is performing better or worse than competitors. For example, if your company’s gross margin is higher than the industry average, it may suggest that your company is more efficient in producing its products or services.

5. Understand Profitability and Cost Structure

A common size income statement helps break down profitability by showing how much of each dollar of revenue is consumed by various expenses. By identifying the percentage of revenue that is going toward cost of goods sold, operating expenses, and other costs, management can understand where improvements are needed to boost profitability. If the operating expenses percentage is too high, it may be time to explore cost-cutting measures.

6. Identify Potential Financial Risks

By examining a common size balance sheet, investors and analysts can identify potential financial risks, such as over-leveraging or reliance on a small group of customers for revenue. A high percentage of total assets tied up in inventory, for example, might signal a potential liquidity problem. Similarly, if liabilities make up a disproportionate percentage of total assets, it could indicate excessive financial risk.

Common Size Financial Statements in Financial Analysis

Incorporating common size financial statements into your financial analysis toolkit can provide invaluable insights into a company’s performance, efficiency, and financial structure. These statements allow for easier cross-company comparisons, trend analysis, and financial benchmarking.

Key Takeaways:

  • Enhanced comparability: Common size financial statements simplify the comparison of companies of different sizes.
  • Trend analysis: They help identify trends in a company’s financial performance over time.
  • Risk identification: By analyzing common size data, analysts can identify potential risks and inefficiencies.
  • Benchmarking: They allow for industry comparisons, which can help businesses understand their position relative to competitors.

Whether you’re a business owner, investor, or analyst, understanding and using common size financial statements is an essential step in gaining a clearer, more meaningful view of a company’s financial health. By leveraging this tool, you can make more informed decisions and improve your overall financial analysis process.
