Crowdfunding for Nonprofits: The Top Platforms to Know


The digital fundraising landscape keeps evolving at breakneck speed, opening up new ways for nonprofits to mobilize support and donations. Whether you’re running a grassroots community project or managing a large charitable foundation, crowdfunding offers a potent way to reach new supporters, raise awareness, and collect vital funds. With so many crowdfunding platforms on the market, figuring out which best aligns with your goals can quickly become overwhelming. This comprehensive guide lays out the landscape, compares leading crowdfunding options designed for nonprofits, and explores best practices to maximize success.

Understanding Crowdfunding for Nonprofits

At its core, crowdfunding is about pooling small to medium donations from a large group of people, typically online, to reach a specific fundraising target. For nonprofits, this approach can democratize fundraising, allowing organizations of all sizes to tap new networks, run targeted campaigns, and build loyal donor bases.

Crowdfunding platforms for nonprofits typically offer dedicated tools for storytelling, donor management, and compliance with charitable giving regulations. The best platforms help nonprofits engage supporters through campaign pages, integrated social sharing, and easy donation processing. Some focus on funding a single project, while others enable ongoing monthly giving or peer-to-peer fundraising where supporters run their own mini-campaigns.

Why Crowdfunding Works for Nonprofits

  1. Broadened Reach
    Digital platforms break down geographic barriers. Crowdfunding allows organizations to reach supporters far beyond their local area, building relationships with potential donors across the world.

  2. Built-in Virality The interactive, shareable nature of crowdfunding campaigns means supporters often promote causes through social media and email, multiplying the campaign’s exposure.

  3. Emotional Storytelling Crowdfunding thrives on compelling stories and visual narratives. Nonprofits can showcase their mission, impact, and specific funding needs to engage supporters on a personal level.

  4. Immediate Feedback Fundraising progress is tracked in real-time, creating momentum and encouraging donors to help push towards the goal.

  5. Lower Barrier to Entry Compared to traditional grant writing or major donor cultivation, crowdfunding requires less upfront investment and can yield quick results—ideal for nonprofits seeking fast, flexible funding.

Choosing the Right Crowdfunding Platform

Selecting the right platform means weighing factors like transaction fees, ease of use, support for nonprofit compliance, integration with existing tools, and the availability of peer-to-peer features. Here’s a rundown of the top players in the field, each with their unique advantages.

GoFundMe Charity (Part of GoFundMe)

What Sets It Apart:
GoFundMe Charity is the nonprofit-specific arm of the world’s largest crowdfunding site. It comes with no platform fee for U.S.-based 501(c)(3) organizations, only standard payment processing charges. The interface is streamlined for storytelling, with tools for goal-setting, event ticketing, and campaign updates. Peer-to-peer fundraising is built-in, encouraging supporters to set up their own pages on an organization’s behalf.

Strengths:

  • Massive user base for organic discoverability.
  • Robust social media integration for sharing.
  • Support for recurring (monthly) donations.
  • No platform fee for eligible nonprofits.

Drawbacks:

  • Limited customization on campaign pages.
  • Primarily focused on the U.S. market.

Classy

What Sets It Apart:
Classy is a premium platform built specifically for nonprofit fundraising, supporting everything from crowdfunding to recurring giving, event registration, and peer-to-peer campaigns. Its strength lies in its flexibility and powerful donor management tools, helping organizations cultivate long-term support.

Strengths:

  • Highly customizable, professional campaign pages.
  • Peer-to-peer, charity events, and tribute fundraising options.
  • Works at scale for organizations running multiple simultaneous campaigns.
  • Integrates with Salesforce, Double the Donation, and other nonprofit CRMs.

Drawbacks:

  • Monthly fees may be out of reach for smaller nonprofits.
  • Some learning curve with its advanced features.

FundRazr

What Sets It Apart:
FundRazr combines flexibility with a social-good focus. Nonprofits can fundraise for a wide range of causes (e.g., medical, education, disaster relief), either for one-time projects or recurringly. It supports “microprojects,” enabling organizations to break big goals into small, actionable parts.

Strengths:

  • Simple campaign setup and transparent cost structure.
  • Supports international projects.
  • Options for both all-or-nothing and keep-what-you-raise campaigns.
  • Good analytics and donor engagement tools.

Drawbacks:

  • Limited third-party integrations.
  • Limited customization beyond basic branding.

MightyCause

What Sets It Apart:
MightyCause (formerly Razoo) tailors itself to small and medium-sized nonprofits with user-friendly design, affordable pricing, and advanced options like team fundraising and event ticketing. It is a staple of Giving Days—24-hour campaigns that drive urgency and participation.

Strengths:

  • No monthly fees for basic fundraising.
  • Team and event fundraising features.
  • Modern campaign page layouts.
  • Well-suited to regional and community campaigns.

Drawbacks:

  • Advanced donor CRM features require a paid plan.
  • Smaller user base than some competitors.

Donorbox

What Sets It Apart:
Donorbox is widely known for its simple, embeddable donation forms, but it has evolved into a full-featured fundraising platform supporting crowdfunding, recurring donations, and peer-to-peer campaigns. It’s ideal for organizations that want to collect donations directly from their website while benefiting from crowdfunding campaign features.

Strengths:

  • Easy-to-embed forms integrate with existing websites.
  • Supports Apple Pay, Google Pay, ACH, and credit cards.
  • Advanced features like matching gifts and donor management.
  • Transparent, low platform fees.

Drawbacks:

  • Not a discoverability-focused crowdfunding platform—traffic must be self-driven.
  • Limited social feed or campaign update functionality.

JustGiving

What Sets It Apart:
JustGiving, popular in the UK and globally, offers widespread campaign customization, peer-to-peer capability, and easy social sharing. It supports a variety of payment processing options and allows existing charity supporters to set up independent fundraising pages.

Strengths:

  • Extensive network in the UK, Europe, and beyond.
  • Good for both direct campaigns and supporter-run fundraisers.
  • Event fundraising toolkit.
  • Streamlined donation process.

Drawbacks:

  • Processing fees are higher for international donations.
  • Interface and features geared to UK fundraising norms.

Facebook Fundraising

What Sets It Apart:
Facebook Fundraising makes it possible for registered nonprofits to create campaigns directly from their Facebook pages, and for supporters to create birthday or tribute fundraisers on the nonprofit’s behalf. Donations are funneled through Facebook Pay, and the platform charges no processing or platform fees to qualifying nonprofits.

Strengths:

  • Huge audience potential—tap into supporters’ existing social networks.
  • Built-in virality and easy shareability.
  • No payment processing or platform fees for eligible charities.
  • Integrated with Facebook’s tools for event promotion, live streaming.

Drawbacks:

  • Limited control over donor data—email addresses and contact details often aren’t shared with the organization.
  • Campaign format is less customizable than standalone platforms.
  • Funding is only available to registered nonprofits in certain countries.

Indiegogo (Charity & Social Impact Section)

What Sets It Apart:
Indiegogo’s “Charity & Social Impact” category, while smaller than those of other platforms, provides international reach for innovative cause-driven projects. Ideal for organizations running a one-time campaign with a strong narrative or pioneering approach.

Strengths:

  • Large, active, global user base.
  • Suited to creative, story-driven campaigns.
  • Offers both “all-or-nothing” (fixed goal) and “flexible funding” options.
  • Strong focus on project timeline updates.

Drawbacks:

  • Less tailored to nonprofits; minimal donor management.
  • No recurring giving features.

Kickstarter (Nonprofit Projects with Creative Focus)

What Sets It Apart:
Kickstarter does not accept fundraising for typical charitable purposes but is a powerhouse for nonprofits with creative projects—arts, publishing, performance, or educational initiatives. The platform calls for a detailed project timeline and high production value.

Strengths:

  • Engaged, creative community of backers.
  • Good for highly specific, measurable projects.
  • Excellent storytelling tools.
  • All-or-nothing funding—creates urgency.

Drawbacks:

  • No tax-deductible receipts for donors.
  • Highly competitive.
  • Not suited to general fundraising or ongoing operational costs.

How to Optimize Your Crowdfunding Campaign

The right platform is just one piece of the puzzle. To unlock crowdfunding success, nonprofits should focus on the following strategies:

1. Tell a Compelling Story

Narrative is your main asset. Share exactly how donations will be spent, showcase the people impacted by your mission, and use specific examples and outcomes. Include high-quality testimonials or success stories, and use short, emotive videos and powerful images where possible.

2. Set a Realistic Goal

Break projects into achievable segments and set targets that are ambitious but attainable. Early momentum is crucial for social proof—most platforms see a spike in donations once a campaign reaches 30-40% of its target.

3. Rally Your Network

Reach out to your best supporters and stakeholders before launching. Ask your board, volunteers, and inner circle to make early gifts and amplify your campaign. Remember, early donations encourage public participation.

4. Promote Widely

Run coordinated communication through social media, email, your website, and physical events. Don’t be shy with updates—let supporters know about milestones, setbacks, and successes. Share real-time updates to keep the energy high.

5. Leverage Peer-to-Peer Fundraising

Empower your community to fundraise on your behalf. Many platforms make it easy for supporters to set up their own pages, share their “why,” and recruit donations from their personal networks.

6. Reward and Recognize

While charitable giving isn’t about perks, small tokens—a thank-you note, shout-out on social, virtual event access, or donor wall recognition—can make donors feel valued. Prompt thanks and public acknowledgment go a long way.

7. Follow Up and Report Back

Stay engaged after the campaign ends. Follow up with donors about impact, use of funds, and next steps. This not only builds trust but also primes your supporters for future campaigns.

Considerations When Evaluating Crowdfunding Options

  • Fees and Pricing:
    Review transaction fees, platform fees, and any costs for advanced features. While some platforms claim “no fees,” payment processing charges almost always apply.

  • Integration with Existing Tools:
    If your organization uses a donor management system or email marketing platform, check whether the crowdfunding site integrates smoothly.

  • Donor Data Ownership:
    Ensure you can access donor email addresses and contact information—critical for stewardship and future appeals.

  • International Fundraising:
    If you target global supporters, look for platforms that accept a wide range of currencies and support international donors.

  • Customization and Branding:
    Match the campaign experience to your nonprofit’s personality and brand.

  • Compliance and Tax Receipts:
    Confirm the platform issues tax-deductible receipts (if eligible) and complies with relevant fundraising laws in your country.

Practical Steps to Start Your Crowdfunding Journey

  1. Assess Your Needs:
    Clarify the goal: Is it a one-time project, an emergency appeal, or recurring operational support? Evaluate your in-house resources for campaign planning, communications, and follow-up.

  2. Choose Your Platform:
    Use the comparisons above to shortlist platforms that match your size, donor demographics, geographic scope, and technical requirements.

  3. Build Your Campaign:
    Invest time in scripting your story, setting a funding goal, and preparing visuals. Draft updates and FAQs in advance.

  4. Engage Early Adopters:
    Share the campaign privately before launch—your “core team” should help seed early momentum.

  5. Go Public and Promote:
    Use every channel at your disposal. Monitor progress daily, adjusting strategies and boosting appeals as needed.

  6. Acknowledge and Steward Donors:
    Automate thank-you notes, send personalized follow-ups, and share photo/video updates on progress. Cultivate your new supporters for long-term engagement.

What Lies Ahead for Nonprofit Crowdfunding?

Nonprofit crowdfunding keeps evolving as technology advances and supporter expectations shift. Integration with social platforms, mobile giving, blockchain transparency tools, and impact dashboards will reshape how nonprofits connect with supporters and demonstrate accountability. More platforms are focusing on peer-to-peer fundraising, virtual events, and hybrid in-person campaigns, allowing nonprofits to combine the reach of digital with the intimacy of real-world engagement.

Wrapping Up: Finding the Right Fit

Your cause is unique, and so is your community of supporters. Finding the optimal crowdfunding platform is a combination of self-assessment, experimentation, and learning from each campaign. Whichever option you choose, authenticity and clear communication will drive campaign momentum, foster relationships, and fuel your nonprofit’s impact far beyond your initial fundraising goal.

Getting started may feel daunting, but with the right mix of digital tools, creativity, and dedication, nonprofit crowdfunding can become a sustainable pillar of your fundraising strategy. Dive in, tell your story, and watch your community rally for good.
