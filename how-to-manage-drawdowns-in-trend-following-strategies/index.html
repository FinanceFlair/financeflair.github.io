How to Manage Drawdowns in Trend Following Strategies


Trend following strategies have long been a popular approach for traders and investors seeking to profit from the market’s natural fluctuations. The idea is simple: identify and follow the prevailing market trends, whether upward or downward, and ride them for as long as possible. However, despite their historical success and widespread use, trend following strategies are not without their pitfalls—chief among them, drawdowns.

Drawdowns refer to the decline from a peak to a trough in the value of an investment portfolio, typically measured as a percentage. While drawdowns are a natural part of any trading strategy, their magnitude and frequency can greatly impact a trader’s long-term success. In this post, we will explore how to manage drawdowns effectively in trend following strategies, providing actionable insights that can help mitigate their adverse effects.

Understanding Drawdowns in Trend Following Strategies

Before delving into strategies for managing drawdowns, it is crucial to understand what they are and why they occur in trend following systems. In essence, a drawdown occurs when a trader or investor experiences a loss from the peak value of their portfolio during a particular period. In trend following, this typically happens when the market reverses or enters a consolidation phase after a sustained trend.

Trend following strategies are designed to capture large, extended moves in the market. However, markets are not always trending. At times, they may enter sideways or range-bound periods, which can trigger false signals and result in drawdowns. These periods of unfavorable price action, or “whipsaws,” can erode the profits from previous successful trades, leading to a drawdown.

A typical characteristic of trend following strategies is that they usually experience larger drawdowns compared to more conventional trading approaches. This is because trend followers are often positioned for significant market movements and may hold onto positions during corrective or sideways phases.

The Impact of Drawdowns on Traders and Investors

Drawdowns are not just a technical consideration—they can also have a psychological impact on traders. Extended periods of drawdown can test a trader’s resolve, leading to emotional decision-making, overtrading, or abandoning the strategy altogether. This phenomenon is often referred to as “loss aversion,” where traders become more sensitive to losses than to gains. It is one of the leading reasons why many traders abandon their strategies prematurely, missing out on the long-term benefits of trend following.

For investors, a deep drawdown can significantly reduce the overall value of their portfolio, especially if it occurs during a critical period. For example, if an investor experiences a 50% drawdown, they need a 100% return just to break even. Managing drawdowns becomes essential for ensuring that a strategy remains profitable over time and avoids significant losses that can be difficult to recover from.

Risk Management: The Foundation of Drawdown Control

The first and most important step in managing drawdowns is effective risk management. This involves setting limits on the amount of capital you are willing to risk on each trade, as well as diversifying your investments to reduce the overall volatility of your portfolio. By carefully controlling risk, you can minimize the chances of experiencing severe drawdowns.

Position Sizing

One of the most effective ways to manage risk in trend following strategies is through proper position sizing. Position sizing refers to the amount of capital allocated to each trade. By adjusting your position size based on the volatility of the market or the size of your portfolio, you can ensure that no single trade has an outsized impact on your overall performance.

For example, if a market is experiencing higher volatility, you may want to reduce your position size to limit your exposure to potential losses. Conversely, if the market is calmer and the trend is strong, you can increase your position size to capitalize on the movement.

A common technique for determining position size in trend following systems is the “Kelly Criterion.” This formula calculates the optimal position size based on the expected returns and the risk of loss. By following the Kelly Criterion, you can ensure that you are taking on an appropriate amount of risk relative to your portfolio size and the potential for return.

Diversification

Diversification is another key component of effective risk management. By diversifying your trades across different asset classes, markets, or timeframes, you reduce the risk of experiencing large drawdowns in any one position. For example, instead of focusing solely on equities, you might also include commodities, currencies, and bonds in your portfolio. This reduces the impact of a negative trend in one market and can help to smooth out the overall performance of your strategy.

Additionally, you can diversify within your trend following strategy itself by trading multiple timeframes. A strategy that works well on a daily chart may not perform as well on a shorter timeframe. By employing a multi-timeframe approach, you can reduce the risk of drawdowns associated with single timeframe systems.

Use of Stop Losses and Trailing Stops

While trend following strategies rely on riding the market for as long as possible, it’s essential to incorporate mechanisms that allow for risk mitigation when trends reverse. One of the most common tools for this purpose is the stop loss. A stop loss is a predetermined level at which you exit a trade if the price moves against you by a certain amount. It helps to protect your capital and prevents a small loss from turning into a much larger one.

There are several ways to implement stop losses in trend following strategies:

  • Fixed Stop Loss: This is a stop loss set at a fixed percentage or dollar amount from the entry price. While it offers simplicity, it may not always be the most effective, especially during periods of high volatility.

  • Volatility-Based Stop Loss: This stop loss adjusts based on the volatility of the asset being traded. For instance, a trader might set a stop loss at two times the average true range (ATR) of the asset, which accounts for normal market fluctuations.

  • Trailing Stop: A trailing stop is a dynamic stop loss that moves with the price as the market trends in your favor. For example, if the market moves in your favor by 50 points, your trailing stop might move up to lock in profits while still giving the trade room to move further. Trailing stops help to protect profits and minimize losses in a trend-following strategy.

While stop losses are valuable tools for managing drawdowns, they should be used judiciously to avoid getting stopped out too early, particularly in volatile markets. This is where the ability to assess market conditions and adjust your risk parameters accordingly comes into play.

Psychological Strategies: Staying the Course During Drawdowns

One of the biggest challenges in trend following is managing the emotional side of trading, especially during drawdowns. A key aspect of surviving and thriving in this type of strategy is developing the psychological resilience to endure periods of loss without abandoning the system.

Maintaining a Long-Term Perspective

In trend following, the key to success is a long-term perspective. While drawdowns can be uncomfortable in the short term, it is important to remember that trend following strategies are designed to capture big moves over time. These strategies may experience extended periods of underperformance, but staying the course and adhering to your system’s rules is essential for reaping the long-term rewards.

It’s easy to get discouraged during drawdowns, but the most successful traders are those who remain calm and focused on their long-term goals. Patience is essential, as the next big trend could be just around the corner, providing a significant opportunity to recover from past losses.

Keeping a Trading Journal

Another useful tool for managing the psychological impact of drawdowns is keeping a trading journal. A trading journal allows you to track your trades, emotions, and thought processes during each phase of the market. By reviewing your journal regularly, you can identify patterns in your behavior, such as reacting impulsively during drawdowns, and make adjustments accordingly.

In addition, keeping a journal provides a valuable reference point to remind yourself of the successful trades you’ve made in the past and the strategies that have worked for you over time. This can help to instill confidence during tough periods and reduce the emotional pressure that often accompanies drawdowns.

Drawdown Recovery: Key to Long-Term Success

Even the best trend-following strategies will experience drawdowns. The goal is not to avoid them altogether, but rather to manage them effectively to ensure long-term profitability. Effective risk management, sound position sizing, diversification, and psychological resilience are all critical components of drawdown management.

By incorporating these strategies, traders and investors can better weather the ups and downs of trend following and emerge with stronger, more consistent performance over time. With the right mindset and tools, the inevitable drawdowns become just another phase in the ongoing journey of successful trend following.
