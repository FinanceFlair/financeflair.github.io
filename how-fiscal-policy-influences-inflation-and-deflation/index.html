How Fiscal Policy Influences Inflation and Deflation


Fiscal policy is an essential tool used by governments worldwide to influence the economy’s health. It refers to government spending and taxes or, in other words, the government’s altering the tax structure and public expenditures to monitor and steer the country’s economic path. It’s a powerful lever that can impact inflation and deflation, something vital for economic stability.

Key Components of Fiscal Policy

Fiscal policy consists of two main components: government spending (expansionary policy) and taxation (contractationary policy).

  1. Government Spending: This is also known as expansionary policy. It is about the government correctly incentivizing and investing in public projects, infrastructure, welfare, Medicare, Medicaid, and other social services. This increased spending fosters economic growth as it increases employment, boosting demand and, ultimately, leading to increased production.

  2. Taxation: This is the contractionary policy. It involves the government adjusting the tax rate to cool down an overheating economy. As taxes increase, consumer spending tends to decrease, leading to a lesser demand and, critically, a decreased rate of inflation.

How Fiscal Policy Influences Inflation

When inflation is maintained within the expected range, it generally contributes positively to the economy. However, uncontrolled inflation can damage the country’s credibility and create other economic problems. This is where fiscal policy plays a crucial role.

Fiscal Expansion and Inflation:

Increased government spending usually leads to greater AD (aggregate demand) in the economy. This adds upward pressure on prices, contributing to inflation. If businesses are unable to increase production to meet this enhanced demand, they will have to raise their prices.

In the course of corrections, the government might have to step in and readjust their fiscal policy with contractionary measures to control the inflation rate.

Fiscal Contraction and Inflation:

Decreased government spending or increased taxes lead to reduced AD. This brings about deflation or a decrease in general price levels as the demand for goods and services lowers.

How Fiscal Policy Influences Deflation

While deflation may seem desirable due to the prospect of cheaper goods, it’s not always beneficial. Deflation can lead to an economic downturn, as consumers delay purchases in anticipation of lower prices. This can disrupt markets and lead to an economic slowdown.

Fiscal Expansion and Deflation:

In a recession period, government spending can stimulate economic recovery, helping to counteract a deflationary mindset.

Fiscal Contraction and Deflation:

Similarly, increased taxes or decreased spending can be used to tackle deflation during hyper-deflationary periods. However, this can be tricky. Remember that while fiscal contraction can fight deflation, it can also exacerbate a recession, so it needs to be handled with caution.

Case Study: Japan

In the late 1990s, Japan saw a period of persistent deflation, termed “The Lost Decade.” The Japanese government attempted to jump-start the economy with fiscal expansion (increasing government spending), but with little success. Abandoning fiscal contraction plans, they waited until the 2000s for an economic recovery. While fiscal policy can be an effective tool, inconsistent or poorly implemented fiscal policy can have disastrous long-term effects.

In conclusion, a government’s fiscal policy has a direct impact on the level of price stability in an economy. It’s an effective tool to handle inflation and deflation with caution and a bit of economics expertise. If not wielded correctly, however, it can lead to economic instability, as in the case of Japan. Policy-makers must balance public needs and fiscal conditions to ensure optimum economic health.
