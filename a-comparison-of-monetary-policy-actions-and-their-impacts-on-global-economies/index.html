A Comparison of Monetary Policy Actions and Their Impacts on Global Economies


In this complex and interconnected world, central banks and governments worldwide employ monetary policies to manage an economy’s financial system – the currency, money supply, interest rates, and exchange rates. It aims to achieve desired macroeconomic objectives, like economic growth, low inflation, and overall stability. The effectiveness of monetary policy actions varies from country to country due to differences in economic structures, institutional setups, and policy frameworks.

The following sections compare and analyze how four key developed economies – the United States, the Euro Area, Japan, and the United Kingdom – have implemented distinct monetary policy tools, like interest rate adjustments, quantitative easing, balance sheet management, and forward guidance, and the impacts on their respective economies.

  1. United States: The Federal Reserve’s open market operations, interest rate adjustments, and expansionary monetary policy

A primary monetary policy tool in the U.S. is the Federal Reserve’s (Fed) control over short-term interest rates. By raising or lowering the federal funds rate, it influences borrowing costs and facilitates economic stability.

Following the 2008 Global Financial Crisis, the Fed began implementing quantitative easing (QE) to stabilize financial markets and boost economic growth. By purchasing long-term securities, such as bonds, the Fed increased the money supply, drove down borrowing costs, and pushed investors towards riskier assets. Between 2008 and 2015, the efficiency of the QE was evident in the surge of the stock markets and house prices.

In contrast, after the Global Financial Crisis, as the U.S. economy gradually recovered, the Fed began raising federal funds rates from near-zero levels in December 2015; the Fed went through three rate hikes in 2017, three in 2018, and one in 2019, totaling seven rate hikes from December 2015 to June 2019 to prevent overheating the economy and to avoid inflationary pressures.

  1. The Euro Area: European Central Bank’s QE, Negative Interest Rates, and banking union

The European Central Bank (ECB) spearheaded a QE program in 2015 to address low inflation, sluggish growth, and high unemployment rates. By purchasing public securities, the ECB increased money supply, spurring borrowing and investment.

An unconventional approach adopted by the ECB was implementing negative interest rates in June 2014. Despite criticisms and concerns surrounding the sustainability of negative interest rates, the strategy aimed to prompt banks to boost lending and fiat money circulation.

In 2012, the Euro Area introduced the banking union, enhancing economic stability by ensuring the stability of the banking system through centralized supervision and resolution tools. Hence, the Euro Area’s monetary policy constituents – ECB’s QE, negative interest rates, and banking union – have proven vital to revive the economy post the sovereign debt crisis.

  1. Japan: The Bank of Japan’s QE and Haruhiko Kuroda’s leadership

Emphasizing the importance of achieving its 2% inflation target, the Bank of Japan (BOJ) embarked on a massive QE program in 2013, adopting unconventional policies such as yield curve control and aggressive easing, later referred to as “Abenomics.”

After Haruhiko Kuroda occupied the BOJ’s leadership in 2013, he strengthened the QE program, increasing the money supply and forcing investors into riskier assets like stocks. This strategy alleviated deflationary pressures, slowly uplifted economic growth, and stabilized the Japanese yen, enhancing Japan’s export competitiveness.

  1. United Kingdom: The Bank of England’s response to global and eurozone shocks

The Bank of England (BoE) has taken a more cautious approach to monetary policy, often being forced to adapt due to international and neighborhood (the Euro Area) determinants.

In response to the 2008 Financial Crisis, the BoE reduced its base rate, initiated QE, and expanded its balance sheet. In 2016, the unexpected outcome of the Brexit referendum induced immediate volatility in currency, stock markets, and the economy. In response, the BoE opted for currency intervention, capacity to increase QE, and recurrent rate cuts.

Conclusion

Across the United States, the Euro Area, Japan, and the United Kingdom, central banks have heavily relied on monetary policies to steer their economies through various crises and cyclical challenges. It is evident that the comparison of these economies’ monetary policy actions and their impacts underlines the importance of understanding the unique structures and requirements of individual economies. Although central banks might pursue similar strategies, their institutional setup and the specific features of their national economies determine their effectiveness.

As economic landscapes and global market conditions are ever-evolving, the critical role of sound domestic and international economic policies, tailored and adaptive monetary tools, and forward-looking communication from central banks continues to serve as the bedrock for financial market stability and economic vitality in the years to come.
