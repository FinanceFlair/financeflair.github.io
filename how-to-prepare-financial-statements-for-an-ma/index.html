How to Prepare Financial Statements for an M&A


Mergers and acquisitions (M&A) are complex corporate maneuvers that involve many moving parts, with financial statements playing a central role. Whether you are a buyer, seller, or advisor, preparing accurate and comprehensive financial statements is critical to the success of any M&A transaction. These documents not only provide a clear snapshot of a company’s financial health but also underpin valuation, due diligence, and negotiation processes.

This guide will walk you through the essential steps to prepare financial statements specifically tailored for M&A activities. Covering everything from understanding transaction context to presentation best practices, the insights here will empower you to approach M&A financial reporting confidently.

Why Are Financial Statements Crucial in M&A?

Financial statements serve as the foundation for evaluating a business during an acquisition or merger. They provide a detailed view of past performance, current standing, and projected potential, impacting decisions in several vital ways:

  • Valuation: Comprehensive financials enable accurate business valuation, affecting deal terms.
  • Due Diligence: Buyers scrutinize financials to uncover risks or liabilities.
  • Negotiation: Trustworthy statements serve as negotiation leverage.
  • Regulatory Compliance: Well-prepared records ensure adherence to legal and accounting standards.
  • Integration Planning: Post-M&A integration hinges on understanding financial realities.

Because of their importance, financial statements must be prepared with heightened scrutiny, transparency, and adherence to standardized accounting principles.

Step 1: Understand the Specifics of Your M&A Situation

Not all M&A deals are identical; how you prepare financial statements depends on the nature of the transaction and the companies involved. Consider:

  • Type of M&A: Is it an asset purchase, stock purchase, merger, or consolidation?
  • Buyer’s Requirements: What financial data is the acquiring party requesting?
  • Industry Standards: Certain sectors have unique financial reporting needs.
  • Regulatory Environment: Are standards such as IFRS, GAAP, or others applicable?

Be proactive in communicating with stakeholders to clarify expectations. This will ensure the financial statements focus on relevant information and comply with necessary frameworks.

Step 2: Collect and Verify Relevant Financial Data

Accuracy is non-negotiable when preparing M&A financial statements. Begin by gathering all necessary financial records:

  • Historical Financial Statements: At least three years of audited or reviewed income statements, balance sheets, and cash flow statements.
  • Interim Financials: Recent interim statements can reveal ongoing trends.
  • Adjusting Entries: Identify non-recurring, extraordinary, or one-time expenses or revenues that may skew the picture.
  • Off-Balance Sheet Items: Loans, leases, and guarantees must be factored in.
  • Contingent Liabilities: Potential liabilities that could affect valuation.

Verification involves reconciling all figures, corroborating with underlying documentation such as bank statements, invoices, and contracts, and rectifying discrepancies.

Step 3: Adjust for M&A-Specific Considerations

Preparing statements tailored for M&A means making adjustments so the numbers reflect the operating reality and are comparable between buyer and seller. Some key modifications include:

  • Normalized Earnings: Remove one-off or non-recurring items such as lawsuit settlements or extraordinary gains.
  • Pro Forma Adjustments: Incorporate anticipated changes post-transaction, like synergies, cost savings, or restructuring expenses.
  • Reclassification of Accounts: Align categorization with buyer’s accounting standards for easier analysis.
  • Exclusion of Owner’s Compensation Above Market Rates: Especially common in privately held companies to present a clearer picture of economic profits.
  • Inventory and Asset Valuation: Assess write-downs or adjustments to fair value according to deal terms.

These tailored adjustments provide a more accurate depiction useful for valuation and due diligence.

Step 4: Prepare the Core Financial Statements

Now, it’s time to compile the key financial statements with the necessary adjustments:

1. Income Statement (Profit & Loss Statement)

The income statement shows profitability over a specified period. For M&A:

  • Present revenues, costs, and expenses with clear separation between recurring and non-recurring items.
  • Highlight EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization) as it serves as a crucial metric for valuation and comparability.
  • Show trends over multiple periods to identify growth patterns.

2. Balance Sheet

The balance sheet reveals the company’s assets, liabilities, and equity at a snapshot in time:

  • Confirm fair value accounting for assets and liabilities if required by the deal.
  • Make note of any intangible assets, intellectual property, or goodwill.
  • Identify liabilities—both current and long-term—that could affect the transaction.

3. Cash Flow Statement

Cash flow gives insight into liquidity and the company’s ability to generate cash:

  • Separate operating, investing, and financing cash flows clearly.
  • Emphasize free cash flow as it indicates funds available for debt repayment or reinvestment.

Step 5: Include Supplementary Disclosures and Schedules

Financial statements alone may not tell the full story. Supplementary disclosures provide transparency and context:

  • Detailed Notes: Explain accounting policies, assumptions, and unusual transactions.
  • Debt Schedule: Full breakdown of loans, interest rates, maturity dates, and covenants.
  • Fixed Assets Schedule: Listing of property, plant, and equipment with depreciation.
  • Customer and Supplier Concentrations: Risks associated with dependency on few entities.
  • Pending Litigations or Regulatory Actions: Impact on future financial performance.
  • Employee Benefit Obligations: Pension plans, bonuses, or stock options.

Careful and clear disclosures build trust and smooth negotiations.

Step 6: Conduct Internal and External Reviews

Before sharing financial statements for M&A purposes, rigorous review processes are essential:

  • Internal Review: Accountants and management should thoroughly vet statements for accuracy and completeness.
  • External Audit or Review: An independent CPA firm’s involvement enhances credibility and may be required by many buyers.
  • Legal Counsel Review: To ensure risk disclosures and compliance with regulatory frameworks.

These reviews minimize surprises and reinforce confidence among deal participants.

Step 7: Present Financial Statements Effectively

Presentation can dramatically affect perception and ease the decision-making process:

  • Clear Formatting: Use straightforward tables, headings, and summaries.
  • Executive Summary: Provide a concise synopsis highlighting key financial metrics and trends.
  • Comparability: Where possible, present side-by-side historic periods or benchmarking data.
  • Digital Formats: Use PDFs or financial data rooms designed for secure dissemination.

Being transparent, organized, and accessible can accelerate deal processes and foster goodwill.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

In preparing financial statements for M&A, watch out for:

  • Over-Optimistic Projections: Inflating figures without basis can backfire.
  • Inconsistent Accounting Policies: Causes confusion and undermines trust.
  • Neglecting Off-Balance Sheet Liabilities: Leads to surprises during due diligence.
  • Lack of Documentation: Hinders verification and audit processes.
  • Ignoring Tax Implications: Can affect valuation and post-deal cash flows.

Avoiding these mistakes preserves integrity and smoothens the transaction journey.

Final Thoughts

Preparing financial statements for mergers and acquisitions requires more than routine accounting—it demands strategic insight, attention to detail, and a firm grasp of transaction dynamics. Accurate, transparent, and well-presented financial statements not only facilitate smoother negotiations but also reduce risk for everyone involved.

Whether you are preparing the documents internally or engaging external experts, following the detailed steps outlined above will help ensure your financial reporting aligns perfectly with the unique demands of an M&A transaction. The result? A solid financial foundation upon which value can be fairly assessed and a successful deal built.

By mastering the preparation of M&A financial statements, companies position themselves to navigate acquisition complexities confidently and capitalize on growth opportunities with greater assurance.
