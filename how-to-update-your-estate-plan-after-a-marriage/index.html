How to Update Your Estate Plan After a Marriage


Marriage is a significant milestone that brings with it many exciting changes—new experiences, responsibilities, and financial decisions. One of the most important tasks newlyweds should not overlook is updating their estate plan. An estate plan ensures that your assets are distributed according to your wishes, and that your loved ones are cared for in case of incapacity or death. If you’re recently married, or even planning to get married soon, here’s a comprehensive guide to updating your estate plan.

Why You Need to Update Your Estate Plan After Marriage

An estate plan serves as a legal roadmap for how your affairs will be handled after your death or if you’re unable to manage them due to incapacity. It involves a variety of documents, including a will, trust, powers of attorney, and healthcare directives. When you marry, your legal, financial, and familial circumstances change, so it’s essential to revisit your estate plan. Here’s why:

  1. New Legal Status: Marriage changes your legal status, and this affects how your assets will be divided. For example, in most states, property acquired during the marriage is considered marital property, which is subject to division in the event of divorce or death.

  2. Beneficiary Updates: Many estate planning documents, such as life insurance policies and retirement accounts, require you to designate beneficiaries. After marriage, it’s crucial to update these designations to include your spouse, ensuring they inherit your assets if something happens to you.

  3. Healthcare and Financial Decisions: Marriage also changes your personal and financial responsibilities. You may want to designate your spouse as your primary decision-maker for healthcare and financial matters in the event you become incapacitated. Without proper updates, the wrong person could be given that power.

  4. Tax Considerations: Marriage can have tax implications. Understanding how your new marital status affects your estate planning, including your tax liabilities, is crucial to ensure that you maximize any available tax benefits.

Steps to Take When Updating Your Estate Plan After Marriage

1. Review Your Will

Your will is one of the most crucial components of your estate plan. It dictates how your assets will be distributed after your death. After marriage, it’s important to ensure that your spouse is included in your will as a primary beneficiary.

  • Designate Your Spouse as a Beneficiary: If your previous will named someone else as the primary beneficiary (such as a parent or a friend), you’ll need to update it to reflect your spouse as the primary heir.
  • Consider Joint Property: If you and your spouse acquire property together, such as a home or joint bank accounts, your will should reflect how these assets will be distributed.
  • Guardianship of Children: If you have children, it’s also important to update your will to reflect who will act as the guardians for your children in case something happens to both you and your spouse.

2. Update Beneficiary Designations

Many financial accounts and insurance policies have beneficiary designations that override the terms of a will. These include:

  • Life Insurance: If you have life insurance policies, ensure that your spouse is named as the primary beneficiary.
  • Retirement Accounts: Similarly, update the beneficiary designations on your retirement accounts, such as a 401(k) or IRA, to include your spouse.
  • Bank Accounts and Investments: Joint accounts typically pass to the surviving spouse automatically, but make sure the titles are correct. If you have accounts that are not joint, update them to include your spouse.

3. Create or Update a Trust

If you already have a trust or are considering creating one, marriage can necessitate changes. A trust allows you to place assets into an entity that can be managed and distributed according to your wishes, without going through probate.

  • Revocable Trust: You can designate your spouse as a beneficiary of your revocable trust. In the event of your passing, your spouse would have direct access to the assets held in the trust.
  • Irrevocable Trust: If you have an irrevocable trust, you may need to update its terms to reflect your marriage, though making changes to an irrevocable trust can be more complex.

Incorporating your spouse into your trust can provide peace of mind, knowing that your assets will be managed and distributed according to your wishes without court involvement.

4. Designate Powers of Attorney

A durable power of attorney (POA) allows you to designate someone to make financial decisions on your behalf if you become incapacitated. This is an essential document for ensuring that someone you trust manages your finances, bills, and legal matters if you can’t.

  • Financial Power of Attorney: Make sure your spouse is designated as your financial POA, so they can make decisions regarding your finances if you are incapacitated.
  • Healthcare Power of Attorney: Similarly, appoint your spouse as your healthcare POA, giving them the authority to make medical decisions for you if you’re unable to communicate your wishes.

Without a POA, the courts may have to appoint a guardian or conservator to make these decisions, which can be time-consuming and costly.

5. Establish or Update a Living Will or Advance Healthcare Directive

A living will or advance healthcare directive allows you to specify your preferences for medical treatment in case you become seriously ill or incapacitated. This document outlines whether you want to receive life-sustaining treatment or if you prefer palliative care.

  • Designate Your Spouse as a Healthcare Proxy: You may want to designate your spouse as your primary healthcare proxy, giving them the authority to make decisions about your medical care if you’re unable to do so.

This ensures that your spouse can act in your best interest during a time when you are incapacitated or unable to express your wishes. In addition to the living will, make sure that any hospital or healthcare providers are aware of your updated directives.

6. Review Your Tax Strategy

Marriage can have significant tax implications, and your estate plan should be aligned with your new tax status. In many cases, married couples benefit from tax exemptions and deductions that reduce the estate tax burden.

  • Spousal Exemption: Under federal tax law, spouses can transfer assets to each other without triggering estate taxes. This is known as the unlimited marital deduction. However, if you’re married and have a large estate, it’s important to plan for how your assets will be handled upon the death of both spouses.
  • Portability: The IRS allows a surviving spouse to claim the deceased spouse’s unused estate tax exemption, which can help reduce the estate tax burden. This provision is known as portability, and it can be a key planning tool in reducing taxes.

Consulting with a financial advisor or estate planning attorney can help you navigate the tax implications of marriage and ensure that you’re maximizing the available benefits.

7. Consider Life Insurance

Life insurance is a valuable tool for ensuring that your spouse and family are financially secure in the event of your death. Review your current life insurance policy to determine whether it’s sufficient to cover your spouse’s financial needs.

  • Policy Updates: If your life insurance policy is outdated, you may need to increase your coverage to account for new financial responsibilities, such as a mortgage or children’s education.
  • Beneficiary Designation: Ensure that your spouse is listed as the primary beneficiary of your life insurance policy.

8. Review and Update Titles and Ownership of Assets

After marriage, it’s important to review the titles and ownership of your assets. For example, if you purchase a home together, make sure both names are listed on the title. The same goes for bank accounts, cars, and any other valuable assets.

  • Joint Ownership: Assets held jointly with right of survivorship automatically pass to the surviving spouse. Review your ownership structure to ensure that your spouse is included wherever appropriate.
  • Update Titles: If you’ve changed your name after marriage, you may need to update the titles on vehicles, property deeds, and financial accounts.

9. Consider Changing Your Name

If you’ve changed your name after marriage, be sure to update your name on all estate planning documents, financial accounts, and legal documents. Failing to do so can create confusion and potential complications down the line.

10. Work With an Estate Planning Attorney

While updating your estate plan may seem straightforward, it’s often beneficial to work with an experienced estate planning attorney. An attorney can guide you through the process, help you avoid common mistakes, and ensure that your estate plan reflects your current circumstances and wishes.

An attorney can also help you with the legal aspects of transferring assets, creating or updating trusts, and ensuring that your estate plan complies with local laws.

Conclusion

Marriage is a life-altering event that affects many aspects of your life, including your estate plan. By taking the time to update your will, trust, beneficiary designations, and other legal documents, you ensure that your spouse and loved ones are protected and that your assets are distributed according to your wishes. Updating your estate plan after marriage is a vital step toward securing your future and the future of your loved ones.

Remember, estate planning is an ongoing process. As your life evolves, you should continue to revisit your estate plan to reflect changes in your family, financial situation, and goals. By staying proactive and keeping your estate plan up-to-date, you provide peace of mind for both yourself and your spouse.
