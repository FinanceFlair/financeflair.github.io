How to Update Your Estate Plan After a Divorce


Divorce is a significant life change that affects almost every aspect of your personal and financial life. Among the many important tasks you will need to address during or after a divorce, updating your estate plan is often overlooked. However, it is crucial to review and revise your estate plan to ensure that your wishes are honored and that your assets are distributed according to your new circumstances.

In this post, we’ll walk you through the steps of updating your estate plan after a divorce, covering key documents and decisions you need to make to protect your future and the future of your loved ones.

Why You Need to Update Your Estate Plan After Divorce

An estate plan includes a collection of legal documents that outline how your assets will be handled when you’re no longer around or if you’re incapacitated. Divorce fundamentally changes your relationship with your ex-spouse, and if your estate plan isn’t updated to reflect that, your ex could still have a say in how your assets are distributed.

Additionally, divorce may impact the beneficiaries you’ve designated in your will, your power of attorney, your health care directive, and your trust. It’s important to review these documents and make necessary adjustments to prevent any unintended consequences. Updating your estate plan ensures that:

  • Your ex-spouse is no longer a beneficiary if you do not want them to inherit your assets or make decisions on your behalf.
  • Your new beneficiaries are clearly named, which might include children, other family members, or even a new partner.
  • Your healthcare and financial decisions reflect your new wishes, particularly if your ex-spouse was previously your designated decision-maker.

1. Review and Update Your Will

Your will is one of the most important documents in your estate plan. It outlines how your assets will be distributed after your death and names the individuals responsible for carrying out your wishes. If your will still lists your ex-spouse as the beneficiary, this can cause complications upon your death. It may lead to legal disputes or, worse, your ex-spouse inheriting assets you no longer intend for them to receive.

Here’s what you need to do:

  • Remove your ex-spouse as a beneficiary or executor of your will. If they are currently listed as the executor, you should appoint a new executor to carry out your wishes. Typically, this will be a trusted friend or family member, or a professional fiduciary.
  • Update your beneficiaries to reflect the people you now wish to inherit your estate, whether it’s children, other family members, or a new partner. Make sure that your beneficiaries’ names, addresses, and contact information are current.
  • Consider a new will altogether. If your divorce was particularly complex or if your estate has changed significantly, it may be easier to create a new will rather than simply revising the old one.

2. Modify Trusts

If you have a trust in place, it’s essential to review and modify it after your divorce. A trust is a legal arrangement where a trustee manages assets on behalf of beneficiaries, and like your will, it can specify how your assets are distributed.

Some steps to take include:

  • Revoking or modifying your ex-spouse’s role as a trustee or co-trustee if they were previously appointed.
  • Changing the beneficiaries of the trust. Ensure that your ex-spouse is no longer listed as a beneficiary, and update the beneficiaries to reflect your current wishes.
  • Consider establishing a new trust if your divorce resulted in a significant shift in your assets, and you want more control over how they are distributed.

Since trusts can be complex and often involve significant sums of money, consulting an estate planning attorney to ensure the proper adjustments are made is a good idea.

3. Update Powers of Attorney

A power of attorney (POA) grants another person the authority to make financial, legal, and medical decisions on your behalf if you are unable to do so. During your marriage, you likely appointed your spouse as your power of attorney. After a divorce, you must update this document to ensure that someone you trust can make decisions for you.

There are two primary types of powers of attorney:

  • Financial Power of Attorney (POA): This document allows someone to manage your finances, pay bills, and handle investments on your behalf. If your ex-spouse was previously your financial POA, you’ll need to update this designation to another trusted person, such as a family member, close friend, or professional adviser.

  • Healthcare Power of Attorney (POA): This document gives someone the authority to make medical decisions for you if you become incapacitated. If your ex-spouse was your healthcare agent, you should update this designation as well, especially if you don’t want your ex to make health-related decisions on your behalf.

Be sure to execute a new POA document and provide copies to all relevant parties, including your healthcare provider and financial institutions.

4. Update Your Beneficiaries on Life Insurance and Retirement Accounts

Many people forget to update the beneficiaries on their life insurance policies and retirement accounts after a divorce. These designations supersede what is written in your will, meaning that if you do not update them, your ex-spouse could still inherit these assets upon your death, even if your will states otherwise.

To make sure your assets are distributed as you intend, follow these steps:

  • Contact your insurance provider and retirement plan administrator to update the beneficiary information on life insurance policies, 401(k) plans, IRAs, and any other retirement accounts.
  • Review all beneficiary designations, and ensure that your ex-spouse is removed, unless you intend for them to receive a portion of the benefits. Make sure that the new beneficiaries are clearly identified.

It’s also a good idea to periodically review these designations to ensure they still reflect your wishes as life circumstances change.

5. Update Your Healthcare Directive

A healthcare directive, also known as a living will, specifies your medical care preferences in the event that you become incapacitated. It also designates someone to make medical decisions on your behalf (usually referred to as your healthcare proxy). If your ex-spouse was your healthcare proxy, you should appoint a new individual to act as your healthcare decision-maker in case of an emergency.

  • Revise your healthcare proxy and any other components of your healthcare directive to designate a trusted person who will follow your medical wishes.
  • Review and update your living will if necessary, to ensure that it reflects your current medical treatment preferences.

6. Consider Setting Up a New Trust for Your Children

If you have children from your marriage and want to ensure they are financially supported, consider setting up a new trust for their benefit. A trust allows you to specify how your children will receive their inheritance and at what age or stage in life they should inherit the funds. This can prevent disputes and ensure that your children’s needs are met according to your wishes.

Here’s how to go about it:

  • Consult with an estate planning attorney to create a trust that meets your family’s needs.
  • Designate a trustee who will be responsible for managing the trust until your children are of the appropriate age or maturity to manage the assets themselves.
  • Determine how the trust will be funded and specify how the assets will be distributed to your children.

A trust can provide peace of mind that your children will be taken care of after your death, especially if they are still minors or young adults.

7. Don’t Forget About Digital Assets

In today’s digital age, many people have a variety of digital assets that need to be managed after death. This includes social media accounts, online banking, digital currencies, and even digital files and photos.

Take the following steps:

  • Make a list of your digital assets and passwords, and ensure someone you trust has access to this information.
  • Decide how your digital assets will be managed after your death, and include this in your estate plan.
  • Consider creating a digital will to ensure that your online presence is handled according to your wishes.

8. Consult with an Estate Planning Attorney

Updating your estate plan after a divorce can be complex, especially if your estate involves trusts, significant assets, or complicated beneficiary designations. An experienced estate planning attorney can guide you through the process, ensure that your estate plan reflects your wishes, and help you avoid any legal challenges in the future.

Conclusion

After a divorce, updating your estate plan is crucial to ensure that your assets are distributed according to your wishes and that the individuals you trust are in charge of important decisions. By reviewing and revising your will, trust, powers of attorney, beneficiary designations, healthcare directives, and digital assets, you can protect yourself and your loved ones.

Remember that estate planning is not a one-time task—it’s something you should revisit regularly as life circumstances change. A well-structured and up-to-date estate plan can provide peace of mind knowing that your wishes will be respected no matter what the future holds.
