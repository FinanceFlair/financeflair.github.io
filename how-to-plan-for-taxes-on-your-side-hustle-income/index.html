How to Plan for Taxes on Your Side Hustle Income


Starting a side hustle is an exciting way to earn extra income, but along with the benefits comes the responsibility of managing taxes. Many side hustlers, especially those new to the world of freelance work, can be caught off guard when tax season arrives. Not planning properly for taxes can lead to unexpected financial burdens and even penalties. In this blog post, we’ll guide you through how to plan for taxes on your side hustle income, ensuring that you stay on top of your financial responsibilities.

Understanding Your Tax Obligations

Before diving into the nitty-gritty of tax planning, it’s essential to understand what tax obligations you may face when running a side hustle.

Self-Employment Tax

Unlike a traditional job where your employer withholds taxes from your paycheck, when you earn money from a side hustle, you’re considered self-employed. This means you’re responsible for paying both the employee and employer portions of Social Security and Medicare taxes, known as self-employment tax.

The self-employment tax rate is 15.3%, which consists of:

  • 12.4% for Social Security
  • 2.9% for Medicare

If your net earnings exceed a certain threshold, you could be subject to additional Medicare taxes. The threshold for this additional 0.9% tax is $200,000 for single filers or $250,000 for married couples filing jointly.

Income Tax

In addition to self-employment tax, you’ll also need to pay income tax on your side hustle earnings. The amount depends on your total income (from your side hustle and any other sources), as well as your filing status. The more you earn, the higher your tax rate will be, as the U.S. uses a progressive tax system, meaning tax rates increase with income.

Your side hustle income will be added to your other sources of income, and your total taxable income will determine which tax bracket you fall into.

Estimated Quarterly Payments

One of the key differences between traditional employment and self-employment is that taxes aren’t automatically withheld from your earnings. Instead, you’re expected to make estimated quarterly tax payments to the IRS. These payments are due in April, June, September, and January, and they help you stay on top of your tax obligations throughout the year.

Deductions and Expenses

The good news is that as a self-employed individual, you can deduct certain business expenses from your taxable income. This reduces the amount of income subject to tax and ultimately lowers your tax bill.

Common deductions for side hustlers include:

  • Home office expenses
  • Business-related travel and meals
  • Marketing and advertising costs
  • Equipment and supplies necessary for your side hustle
  • Educational expenses related to your business

Step-by-Step Guide to Planning for Taxes

Now that you have a clearer picture of your tax obligations, let’s break down the steps you can take to effectively plan for taxes on your side hustle income.

1. Keep Track of All Income

The first step in managing taxes is to ensure that you accurately track all your income. Whether you’re paid in cash, check, or through digital platforms like PayPal, Venmo, or Stripe, it’s crucial to maintain a detailed record.

Using accounting software like QuickBooks, Xero, or FreshBooks can make this process easier, as they allow you to categorize income and expenses. Alternatively, you can use a simple spreadsheet to log payments, making sure you note the source and date of each transaction.

If you earn over $600 from a single client in a year, they are required to send you a 1099-NEC form. While this form will help you keep track of income from clients, remember that it’s still your responsibility to report all income, even if you don’t receive a 1099.

2. Track Your Business Expenses

The next step in tax planning is tracking your business expenses. As mentioned, the IRS allows you to deduct certain expenses from your taxable income, which reduces the amount of tax you owe.

Here are some common business expenses that are typically deductible:

  • Supplies: Office supplies, software subscriptions, and any other materials you use for your side hustle.
  • Home Office Deduction: If you use a portion of your home exclusively for business purposes, you may be able to deduct some of your housing costs, including rent, utilities, and insurance.
  • Travel Expenses: Business-related travel, including transportation, lodging, and meals, can be deducted. However, make sure you keep receipts and only claim expenses that are directly tied to your business.
  • Business Use of Your Car: If you use your personal vehicle for business purposes, you can deduct the mileage and associated costs. Keep a log of your miles driven for business, and be sure to track things like gas, repairs, and insurance.

3. Set Aside Money for Taxes

One of the biggest mistakes side hustlers make is failing to set aside money for taxes throughout the year. Since taxes aren’t automatically withheld from your income, it’s easy to forget that you’ll need to pay the IRS at the end of the year.

To avoid a tax shock, it’s essential to set aside a portion of your earnings for taxes. A common recommendation is to save 25-30% of your income for tax purposes, but this percentage can vary based on your tax bracket, deductions, and other factors.

Opening a separate bank account for tax savings is a great way to keep this money separate from your regular income. This helps ensure that you have enough funds available when it’s time to make your estimated quarterly payments.

4. Make Estimated Quarterly Payments

As a self-employed individual, you’re required to make estimated quarterly payments to the IRS. These payments cover your income tax and self-employment tax and are due on the following dates:

  • April 15
  • June 15
  • September 15
  • January 15 (of the following year)

To avoid underpayment penalties, make sure you submit your payments on time. If your side hustle income fluctuates throughout the year, you may want to adjust the amount you pay each quarter to reflect your earnings.

The IRS provides an online tool called the Estimated Tax Payment Worksheet to help you calculate your quarterly payments. Alternatively, many tax software programs can assist with this calculation.

5. Utilize Tax Deductions

Take full advantage of the tax deductions available to self-employed individuals. These deductions can significantly reduce your taxable income, which ultimately reduces your tax bill.

As a side hustler, you may be eligible for deductions like:

  • The Qualified Business Income Deduction (QBI): If you’re a sole proprietor, independent contractor, or freelancer, you might qualify for a 20% deduction on your qualified business income, subject to certain limits.
  • Health Insurance Premiums: If you pay for your own health insurance, you may be able to deduct the cost from your taxable income.
  • Retirement Contributions: Contributions to retirement accounts, such as a SEP IRA or Solo 401(k), are tax-deductible and can help you save for the future while reducing your taxable income today.

Keep in mind that not all expenses qualify as deductions, and it’s important to keep detailed records to substantiate your claims. If you’re unsure about a particular deduction, consider consulting with a tax professional.

6. Prepare for Tax Season

When tax season arrives, it’s important to be organized. Start by gathering all relevant documents, including:

  • 1099 forms from clients or platforms that paid you $600 or more
  • Receipts for business-related expenses
  • Bank statements showing business income and expenses
  • Records of estimated tax payments made during the year

You can file your taxes either by yourself or with the help of a tax professional. If your side hustle is simple, you may be able to file on your own using tax software like TurboTax or H&R Block. However, if your finances are more complex or you want peace of mind, working with an accountant or tax professional can be a wise investment.

The Importance of Staying Organized

The key to successfully managing taxes on your side hustle income is staying organized throughout the year. By keeping detailed records of your income and expenses, setting aside money for taxes, and making estimated quarterly payments, you can avoid tax surprises and minimize your tax liability.

If your side hustle grows, or if you start earning a significant amount of income, it may be worth consulting with a tax professional to ensure that you’re following all tax laws and taking full advantage of available deductions.

Final Thoughts

Planning for taxes is a crucial step in running a successful side hustle. By understanding your tax obligations, keeping track of your income and expenses, and setting aside money for quarterly payments, you can avoid costly mistakes and ensure that you’re in compliance with the IRS.

Remember, taxes can be complex, but with the right planning and preparation, you’ll be able to manage your side hustle income efficiently and keep more of your hard-earned money. Whether you’re just starting out or have been side hustling for years, taking the time to plan for taxes is a smart move that will benefit you in the long run.
