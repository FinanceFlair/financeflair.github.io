The Best Practices for Closing a Credit Card Account


Closing a credit card account might seem like a straightforward process, but there are several important considerations to ensure you’re making the best decision for your financial health. Whether you’re looking to simplify your finances, eliminate unnecessary debt, or avoid high fees, it’s essential to understand the best practices when closing a credit card. In this guide, we will walk you through the process, potential consequences, and the steps you should take to close your credit card account properly.

Understand Why You Want to Close the Account

Before diving into the logistics of closing a credit card account, it’s important to be clear about why you’re considering it. Here are some common reasons:

  • High Fees: Some credit cards charge annual fees or have high interest rates, which can be frustrating if you’re not using the card frequently enough to justify the cost.
  • Avoiding Temptation: If you find yourself overspending or relying on credit to make purchases, closing the account may help reduce the temptation to accumulate debt.
  • Simplification: Managing too many credit cards can become overwhelming. Closing a few accounts might simplify your financial life and make it easier to stay organized.
  • Improving Credit Score: In some cases, people close cards to avoid the temptation of using them, believing it will help boost their credit score. However, this is a double-edged sword and requires careful consideration.

While these are common reasons, it’s important to evaluate your unique financial situation before proceeding.

Assess the Impact on Your Credit Score

One of the most significant concerns when closing a credit card is the potential impact on your credit score. The two most important factors to consider are your credit utilization ratio and the length of your credit history.

Credit Utilization Ratio

Your credit utilization ratio is the percentage of your available credit that you’re currently using. This ratio is a key component of your credit score. When you close a credit card, your total available credit decreases, which can increase your credit utilization ratio if you carry balances on other cards. A higher ratio can negatively affect your credit score.

For example, if you have a $5,000 credit limit on your credit card and carry a $2,000 balance, your credit utilization ratio is 40%. If you close that credit card and you still have $2,000 in debt, your credit utilization ratio will rise if your total available credit decreases significantly. If you carry balances on multiple cards, this could cause a noticeable drop in your score.

Length of Credit History

The length of your credit history makes up about 15% of your credit score. Older accounts contribute positively to your credit score because they demonstrate a long history of responsible credit use. If you close an old credit card, you may shorten your credit history, which could also affect your score.

Hard Inquiry

When you close a credit card, there’s typically no hard inquiry involved. However, if you apply for a new credit card afterward, the application could result in a hard inquiry, which can temporarily lower your credit score.

Considering these factors, it’s generally recommended that you only close a credit card account if you’ve carefully weighed the impact on your credit score and overall financial goals.

Pay Off the Balance First

Before closing any credit card, ensure that the balance is fully paid off. Many people make the mistake of thinking they can close a card with an outstanding balance, but doing so can lead to complications.

  • Interest Charges: If you leave a balance on the card, you’ll likely still accrue interest, even if the card is closed. If you want to avoid this, be sure to pay off the entire balance before initiating the closure.
  • Credit Report: A credit card with an outstanding balance can negatively impact your credit report and score. This is especially true if you close the card and fail to address the debt. It’s essential to resolve any balances before proceeding.

You’ll want to be certain that no automatic payments or pending charges are tied to the card either. Double-check for any recurring subscriptions or pending transactions that could complicate the closing process.

Contact the Card Issuer

Once you’ve paid off the balance and are clear on the impact to your credit score, it’s time to officially close the account. Contact your card issuer to inform them of your decision. Be sure to:

  • Request Confirmation: Always ask for written confirmation that your credit card has been closed and the balance is zero. This can be useful if any issues arise in the future.
  • Close Accounts in Writing: While most companies allow you to close an account over the phone, it’s a good idea to follow up with a written request. This ensures there is a formal record of your request.
  • Ask About Rewards or Points: If your card offers rewards or points, inquire about how to redeem them before closing the account. Some rewards programs might expire, so it’s important to make sure you get the most out of your points.

Consider Alternatives to Closing

If you’re hesitant to close the account due to concerns over your credit score, there are alternatives that can achieve similar results without having as much impact:

  • Request a Credit Limit Reduction: Instead of closing the card, you could contact the issuer and request a credit limit reduction. This would limit your spending power while keeping the account open, which can help you avoid overspending while maintaining a low credit utilization ratio.
  • Freeze the Card: Some card issuers allow you to temporarily freeze your card, which can be an effective way to prevent any further charges while keeping the account open for the sake of your credit score.
  • Keep the Account Open with No Activity: Another option is to simply not use the card anymore. Keeping the card open but inactive allows you to preserve the account’s age and credit limit, both of which contribute positively to your credit score.

Timing Is Key

Timing is another important consideration when closing a credit card account. If you are planning to apply for a major loan or mortgage soon, such as a home loan or car loan, it may be wise to wait before closing any credit card accounts. Lenders typically look at your credit score when making approval decisions, and the closure of a credit card could lower your score in the short term.

Additionally, consider closing accounts when you’re not reliant on credit for emergencies. If you find yourself needing access to credit in the near future, it might be best to delay the closure until you’re in a more stable financial position.

Keep an Eye on Your Credit Report

After closing your credit card account, it’s essential to monitor your credit report to ensure that everything is handled properly. In some cases, mistakes can happen, and the account might show as open or report inaccurately. You can request a free copy of your credit report once a year from each of the major credit bureaus to verify that everything is in order.

If you notice any discrepancies, contact the card issuer to have them corrected. It’s also wise to regularly monitor your credit score to see how it’s affected by the closure.

What to Do if You’re Closing Multiple Accounts

If you’re closing several credit cards, it’s important to be strategic about the process. Closing too many accounts at once can have a more significant impact on your credit score due to changes in your credit utilization ratio and credit history length.

Consider spacing out the closures over time rather than closing multiple accounts all at once. This allows you to manage your credit score more effectively and reduce the risk of a significant dip in your score.

Conclusion

Closing a credit card account requires careful thought and planning to ensure that it doesn’t negatively impact your credit score or overall financial health. By paying off your balance, contacting the issuer, and monitoring your credit report, you can successfully close an account without harming your credit standing. Remember to consider alternatives to closing the account if you’re concerned about the impact on your credit score, and be mindful of timing if you plan to apply for credit in the near future. With these best practices, you can make informed decisions about managing your credit and closing your credit card accounts responsibly.
