How to Plan for Healthcare Decisions in Your Estate Plan


Estate planning is a critical process that ensures your wishes are honored after you pass away, but it’s not just about passing on your assets. Equally important is planning for healthcare decisions in the event you become incapacitated and are unable to make decisions for yourself. This part of estate planning is crucial because it provides clear guidance to your loved ones and healthcare providers about your desires when it comes to medical treatment, end-of-life care, and other healthcare-related matters.

In this article, we will discuss the importance of planning for healthcare decisions in your estate plan, key documents to include, and steps you can take to ensure your healthcare wishes are respected.

Understanding Healthcare Decisions in Estate Planning

When most people think of estate planning, they typically consider wills, trusts, and the distribution of assets. However, healthcare decisions are just as important, as they determine how medical and health-related decisions will be made on your behalf if you are no longer capable of making them yourself. This could happen if you are severely injured, suffer from an illness that impairs your mental faculties, or enter a state of unconsciousness or comatose condition.

Without a plan in place, your family and medical providers may be left to make difficult decisions without understanding your wishes, potentially leading to conflict or confusion. In some cases, the lack of a clear healthcare directive may lead to court involvement to determine who gets to make decisions for you. That’s why healthcare planning should be a fundamental part of any estate plan.

Key Documents for Healthcare Decisions

The following documents are essential components of planning for healthcare decisions in your estate plan:

1. Advance Healthcare Directive (Living Will)

An advance healthcare directive, also known as a living will, is a legal document that outlines your preferences for medical treatment in the event that you are unable to communicate your wishes. This document can address issues such as:

  • Whether you want life-sustaining treatments, like mechanical ventilation or CPR, if you are in a terminal or persistent vegetative state.
  • Your preferences regarding organ donation.
  • Any specific wishes you have about end-of-life care, such as palliative care or hospice care.

By putting your healthcare preferences in writing through an advance healthcare directive, you relieve your family from the burden of making these decisions for you, and ensure your wishes are honored.

2. Healthcare Power of Attorney

A healthcare power of attorney (HCPA) is another vital document. This document designates someone you trust to make medical decisions on your behalf if you are incapacitated and unable to communicate. The individual you appoint as your healthcare agent can make decisions regarding medical treatment, surgeries, and the overall management of your care.

It’s important to choose someone who understands your healthcare values and wishes, and who will advocate on your behalf when necessary. This person should also be willing and able to act as your representative in healthcare matters if the need arises.

3. Do-Not-Resuscitate (DNR) Order

A Do-Not-Resuscitate (DNR) order is a medical order that instructs healthcare providers not to perform cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) in the event of cardiac arrest or respiratory failure. While the DNR order is typically issued in a hospital or healthcare setting, it can be part of your estate planning documents if you wish to ensure your wishes are respected in such situations.

A DNR is typically signed by a doctor, but you can include your wishes regarding resuscitation in your advance healthcare directive, and your healthcare agent can carry out those decisions.

4. Organ and Tissue Donation Form

If you wish to donate your organs or tissues after death, you should include your intentions in your estate plan. While this may not be a legally binding part of your will or trust, indicating your desire for organ donation in your healthcare directive or a separate organ donation form ensures that your wishes are known. In some states, you can register as an organ donor with the Department of Motor Vehicles (DMV), and that designation will appear on your driver’s license.

5. Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment (POLST)

A POLST form is a medical order that provides specific instructions about the types of life-sustaining treatment you want in case of a medical emergency. Unlike an advance healthcare directive, which typically comes into play when you’re no longer able to communicate your wishes, a POLST is intended for individuals who are already seriously ill or at the end of life. POLST forms are usually completed with the guidance of a healthcare provider.

POLST forms are legally binding and should be taken seriously. They are often used for patients with chronic illnesses or advanced age who may require decisions about life-sustaining treatments.

Steps to Take for Healthcare Decision Planning

Step 1: Reflect on Your Values and Wishes

Before you start drafting legal documents, take some time to reflect on your personal values and healthcare preferences. Think about your views on:

  • Life-sustaining treatments.
  • End-of-life care and when to stop treatment.
  • Organ and tissue donation.
  • The role of family members in making decisions on your behalf.

Understanding what you want can help guide your decisions as you create your healthcare plan.

Step 2: Choose a Trusted Healthcare Agent

Your healthcare agent will have significant responsibility in making medical decisions on your behalf if you are unable to communicate. Choose someone who is trustworthy, compassionate, and capable of making tough decisions in high-stress situations. It should be someone who respects your wishes and is likely to follow your instructions, even if it’s difficult.

Discuss your wishes with the person you choose to ensure they understand your values and are prepared to make decisions on your behalf.

Step 3: Create and Sign Legal Documents

Once you’ve decided on your healthcare preferences and agent, it’s time to formalize the process. Work with an estate planning attorney to create the necessary legal documents, including your advance healthcare directive, healthcare power of attorney, DNR order, and any other relevant documents. Your attorney will ensure that these documents are legally valid and recognized in your state.

Step 4: Share Your Plan with Your Family

Estate planning can be a sensitive topic, but it’s important to have open and honest conversations with your family members about your healthcare wishes. Let them know what you want and where to find your legal documents. This can help avoid confusion and disagreements when the time comes.

Step 5: Review Your Plan Regularly

Your healthcare preferences may change over time, so it’s important to review and update your healthcare plan regularly. Life events such as marriage, divorce, or changes in health can affect your decisions, so make sure your documents reflect your current wishes.

The Importance of Planning for Healthcare Decisions

Planning for healthcare decisions is just as important as planning for the distribution of your assets. Without clear directives, your loved ones could be forced to make difficult and emotional decisions on your behalf, potentially leading to disagreements or confusion. Having a clear and legally binding plan in place ensures that your wishes are followed and that your family is spared from the burden of making life-or-death decisions.

By addressing healthcare decisions in your estate plan, you also have the opportunity to avoid lengthy legal battles or court involvement, which can further delay the process of honoring your wishes. Creating these documents in advance can bring peace of mind to both you and your family, knowing that your medical treatment will align with your personal values.

Conclusion

Healthcare decisions are a critical part of estate planning that should not be overlooked. By taking the time to create a healthcare plan that includes important documents such as an advance healthcare directive, healthcare power of attorney, and DNR order, you can ensure that your healthcare wishes are followed when you are unable to communicate them.

Planning ahead for healthcare decisions helps prevent confusion, stress, and potential conflicts among family members, and it ensures that your wishes are honored at a time when you are no longer able to advocate for yourself.

Don’t wait to make these important decisions—take control of your healthcare future today by integrating it into your estate plan.
