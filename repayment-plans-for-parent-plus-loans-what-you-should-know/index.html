Repayment Plans for Parent PLUS Loans: What You Should Know


Parent PLUS Loans are federal student loans that parents can borrow to help cover the cost of their child’s education. While these loans provide critical financial support, understanding the variety of repayment options is essential for managing debt responsibly and minimizing financial stress. If you are a parent holding a PLUS loan, or considering borrowing one, it’s important to explore your repayment plan choices and know how these options impact your monthly payments, loan duration, and total interest paid.

What Are Parent PLUS Loans?

Parent PLUS Loans are offered by the federal government to parents of dependent undergraduate students to help pay for educational expenses not covered by other financial aid. These loans generally cover tuition, room and board, textbooks, and other school-related costs. Unlike student loans taken directly by the student, Parent PLUS Loans are taken out by the parent, which means the parent assumes full responsibility for repayment.

Key Features of Parent PLUS Loans

  • Fixed interest rate: Parent PLUS Loans have a fixed interest rate, which means the percentage charged stays the same throughout the life of the loan.
  • Credit check required: Approval depends on a credit check to ensure the borrower has an acceptable credit history.
  • Loan limits: Borrowers can take out loans up to the total cost of attendance minus any other financial aid the student receives.
  • No grace period after disbursement: Repayment typically begins immediately after the loan is fully disbursed, but parents can request deferment while the student is still in school and during grace periods.

Knowing these characteristics helps set the stage for choosing a repayment plan that fits your financial situation.

Standard Repayment Plan for Parent PLUS Loans

The standard repayment plan is the default option if you don’t apply for other repayment programs. Here’s what you should know:

  • Term: Up to 10 years.
  • Monthly payments: Fixed and generally higher than alternative plans because they’re spread over a shorter timeframe.
  • Total cost: Less interest paid overall since the loan is repaid quickly.
  • Best for: Borrowers who can afford consistent monthly payments and want to minimize interest costs.

While this plan is straightforward, higher payments could strain your monthly budget.

Extended Repayment Plan

If you’re unable to handle the standard repayment’s amount, the extended repayment plan might help:

  • Term: Up to 25 years.
  • Monthly payments: Lower than the standard plan due to the longer repayment period.
  • Eligibility: Available to borrowers with more than $30,000 in outstanding federal student loans, including PLUS loans.
  • Total interest: Higher than the standard plan because of the extended repayment period.
  • Best for: Borrowers seeking manageable monthly payments but willing to accommodate a longer payoff timeline.

Note that the extended repayment plan can reduce immediate financial pressure but increases the total interest you’ll pay.

Graduated Repayment Plan

This plan starts with lower payments that increase every two years, intended for borrowers expecting their income to grow over time.

  • Term: Up to 10 years.
  • Monthly payments: Begin low and rise gradually.
  • Best for: Parents currently unable to make higher payments but expecting to earn more in the future.
  • Interest costs: Typically higher than the standard plan due to initially lower payments.
  • Eligibility: Available if you have at least $30,000 in outstanding federal student loans.

Graduated payments can offer breathing space early on, but you should plan carefully to handle future payment increases.

Income-Contingent Repayment (ICR) Plan for Parent PLUS Loans

Parent PLUS Loans aren’t eligible for many of the popular income-driven repayment plans like Income-Based Repayment (IBR) or Pay As You Earn (PAYE). However, they can qualify for the Income-Contingent Repayment (ICR) plan if you consolidate them into a Direct Consolidation Loan.

  • How it works: After consolidation, you can repay based on your income, family size, and total loan amount.
  • Monthly payments: Calculated annually, typically 20% of your discretionary income or what you would pay under a fixed 12-year plan, whichever is less.
  • Term: 25 years, after which any remaining balance may be forgiven.
  • Best for: Borrowers with high income variability or those struggling with fixed payments.
  • Pros and cons: Offers lower monthly payments for those with limited income but a longer repayment duration and possible tax consequences for forgiven amounts.

The ICR plan provides flexible payments but requires consolidation, which may impact your interest rates and loan terms.

Deferment and Forbearance Options

Sometimes, temporary relief from loan payments is necessary:

  • In-School Deferment: Parents can postpone payments while the student is enrolled at least half-time.
  • Economic Hardship Deferment: Available if you meet certain income criteria.
  • Forbearance: Allows you to pause or reduce payments for up to 12 months if you’re facing financial difficulty, illness, or other issues.

Interest continues to accrue during deferment or forbearance unless it’s subsidized, which Parent PLUS Loans are not, so your balance will grow.

Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF) for Parent PLUS Loans

Parent PLUS Loans aren’t directly eligible for PSLF, but there is a workaround:

  • By consolidating your Parent PLUS Loans into a Direct Consolidation Loan, you become eligible for PSLF if you make 120 qualifying payments under an Income-Contingent Repayment plan while working full-time for a qualifying public service employer.

PSLF can significantly reduce your debt if you meet the requirements, but it requires diligent record-keeping and meeting strict criteria for payments and employment.

Tips for Managing Parent PLUS Loan Repayment

  • Evaluate your budget carefully: Understand how monthly payments impact your finances.
  • Explore federal repayment assistance: Use government’s loan simulators to test payment scenarios.
  • Consider loan consolidation cautiously: While it can open income-driven repayment options, it might increase your interest rate.
  • Stay current on payments: Avoid default, which can have severe financial consequences.
  • Keep documentation: For loan forgiveness programs, maintain detailed records of payments and employment.
  • Contact your loan servicer: They can provide guidance tailored to your circumstances.

Effective management and clear understanding of repayment options can relieve much of the stress associated with Parent PLUS Loans.

Final Thoughts

Navigating repayment for Parent PLUS Loans can seem daunting due to its unique characteristics and fewer repayment options compared to other federal student loans. However, with thoughtful planning, you can select a repayment plan that aligns with your financial realities and goals. Whether choosing the standard plan for its simplicity and quick payoff or opting for income-driven options through consolidation, staying informed helps reduce uncertainty and keeps your financial future on track.

Parent PLUS Loans may be a significant financial commitment, but by understanding the repayment landscape, you’re empowered to tackle this debt strategically and confidently.

If you need personalized advice, consider consulting a financial advisor specialized in student loans to help map out the best path for repayment tailored to your unique financial situation.
