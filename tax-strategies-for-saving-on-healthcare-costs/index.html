Tax Strategies for Saving on Healthcare Costs


Healthcare costs continue to rise each year, presenting a growing financial challenge for individuals and families alike. Medical expenses, whether for routine care or unexpected treatments, can quickly eat into your budget, leaving you searching for ways to save money. While healthcare itself may seem like a never-ending expense, tax strategies provide a significant opportunity to alleviate some of these costs. By making informed decisions regarding tax-advantaged accounts, deductions, and credits, you can reduce your tax burden and ultimately save on healthcare expenses.

In this guide, we’ll walk through several effective tax strategies that can help you lower your healthcare costs while maintaining or improving your access to quality care. From Health Savings Accounts (HSAs) to tax credits and deductions, we’ll cover practical tips that can make a big difference in your financial outlook.

1. Health Savings Accounts (HSAs)

One of the most powerful tax-saving tools available for healthcare expenses is the Health Savings Account (HSA). This tax-advantaged account allows you to save money for medical expenses while enjoying three main tax benefits:

Contributions are tax-deductible

Contributions you make to your HSA are tax-deductible, which reduces your taxable income for the year. For example, if you contribute $3,000 to your HSA, that amount will be subtracted from your taxable income, effectively lowering the amount you owe in taxes.

Earnings grow tax-free

Any money you contribute to an HSA grows tax-free. This includes interest, dividends, or investment gains within the account. Over time, this can result in substantial growth, especially if you invest the funds in stocks or mutual funds.

Withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free

When you use the funds in your HSA for qualified medical expenses, such as doctor visits, prescription medications, and hospital bills, your withdrawals are tax-free. This means you won’t pay any taxes on the money when you use it for healthcare needs, which can provide significant savings.

To qualify for an HSA, you must be enrolled in a high-deductible health plan (HDHP). The 2025 contribution limits for HSAs are $3,850 for individuals and $7,750 for families. If you’re 55 or older, you can also make an additional $1,000 “catch-up” contribution.

By maximizing your HSA contributions, you can reduce your taxable income each year and grow your savings tax-free, all while having money available for healthcare expenses.

2. Flexible Spending Accounts (FSAs)

Another option for reducing your taxable income while saving for healthcare expenses is the Flexible Spending Account (FSA). Like HSAs, FSAs allow you to set aside pre-tax money to pay for qualified medical expenses. However, there are some key differences between the two:

Contributions are tax-free

Contributions to an FSA are made through payroll deductions, and since the money is deducted before taxes, your taxable income is reduced by the amount you contribute. The money you contribute to an FSA is then available for healthcare expenses.

Use it or lose it

Unlike an HSA, the money in an FSA must be used by the end of the year, unless your employer offers a grace period or the option to carry over a small amount (typically up to $500). Because of this, FSAs work best if you can estimate your healthcare costs for the year and use the entire amount before the deadline.

Limited contribution amounts

For 2025, the contribution limit for an FSA is $3,050. While this amount is lower than the HSA limit, an FSA can still be an effective way to save on taxes and healthcare costs if you anticipate significant medical expenses during the year.

FSAs can be used for a wide range of medical expenses, including doctor visits, prescription medications, dental and vision care, and even over-the-counter items. By contributing to an FSA, you reduce your taxable income and ensure you have funds set aside for healthcare costs.

3. Health Reimbursement Arrangements (HRAs)

A Health Reimbursement Arrangement (HRA) is another tool employers may offer to help employees save on healthcare costs. HRAs are employer-funded accounts that allow employees to reimburse themselves for medical expenses. While they differ from HSAs and FSAs in terms of ownership and management, HRAs can still be an excellent tax-saving option.

Employer contributions are tax-free

The key benefit of an HRA is that the money you receive from your employer to cover healthcare expenses is tax-free. This means you don’t pay taxes on the money your employer contributes, which can be a significant advantage.

Can cover a broad range of expenses

HRAs can be used for a wide variety of healthcare costs, from premiums to deductibles and copayments. In some cases, HRAs can even be used to reimburse employees for certain types of wellness or fitness-related expenses, depending on the employer’s plan.

Employer-designed plans

The structure and features of HRAs vary from one employer to another. Some employers offer HRAs alongside traditional health insurance plans, while others may use HRAs as a standalone solution to help employees manage their healthcare costs. Be sure to consult your employer to understand the specifics of any HRA offerings.

4. Medical Expense Deductions

If your healthcare expenses exceed a certain threshold, you may be eligible to deduct them from your taxes. The IRS allows taxpayers to deduct medical and dental expenses that exceed 7.5% of their adjusted gross income (AGI). This deduction applies to both individuals and families.

Who qualifies for medical expense deductions?

To qualify for this deduction, your total unreimbursed medical expenses must surpass 7.5% of your AGI. For example, if your AGI is $50,000, you would need to have more than $3,750 in eligible medical expenses to qualify for a deduction.

What expenses qualify for the deduction?

The IRS allows a wide range of medical expenses to be deducted, including:

  • Doctor visits and hospital care
  • Prescription medications
  • Vision care, including glasses and contact lenses
  • Dental care, including procedures like fillings and cleanings
  • Long-term care and assisted living expenses
  • Mental health services and therapy

Be sure to keep detailed records of all your medical expenses throughout the year, including receipts and invoices, to ensure you can claim the maximum deduction possible.

5. Tax Credits for Health Insurance

In addition to tax deductions, you may also be eligible for tax credits that can help reduce your healthcare costs. Tax credits directly reduce the amount of tax you owe, making them a valuable option for those who qualify.

Premium Tax Credit (PTC)

The Premium Tax Credit (PTC) is available to individuals and families who purchase health insurance through the Health Insurance Marketplace. This credit is designed to make health insurance more affordable by reducing the monthly premiums you must pay.

The amount of the Premium Tax Credit is based on your income, household size, and the cost of coverage in your area. Those with income between 100% and 400% of the federal poverty level (FPL) may qualify for this credit. To determine eligibility, you’ll need to file a federal tax return and provide information about your income and family size.

Child and Dependent Care Credit

If you have children or dependents and pay for healthcare expenses related to their care, you may qualify for the Child and Dependent Care Credit. This credit helps offset the cost of child care, including medical care and related expenses, making it easier to balance work and caregiving responsibilities.

This credit can be especially helpful for working parents or caregivers who need assistance with medical expenses for their dependents. Be sure to check the eligibility requirements and keep detailed records of any care expenses.

6. Itemized Deductions and Medical Expenses

If you decide to itemize deductions rather than take the standard deduction, you can include certain medical expenses as part of your total deductions. However, as mentioned earlier, you’ll need to meet the 7.5% threshold based on your AGI.

Examples of deductible medical expenses

Beyond doctor visits and prescriptions, other eligible expenses can include:

  • Travel expenses related to medical care, such as mileage or parking fees
  • Certain home improvements made for medical reasons, such as ramps or lifts
  • Expenses for smoking cessation programs or weight loss treatments prescribed by a doctor
  • Care for a dependent relative, including medical expenses and other caregiving costs

If your medical expenses are substantial, it may make sense to itemize deductions to take advantage of these savings. However, be sure to compare the total of your itemized deductions against the standard deduction to determine which option is best for your situation.

7. Long-Term Care Insurance and Tax Savings

Long-term care insurance (LTCI) can help you manage the cost of care in your later years. The good news is that premiums for LTCI may be deductible, depending on your age and the amount of premiums paid.

Deduction limits based on age

The IRS allows you to deduct long-term care insurance premiums based on your age. The older you are, the higher the allowable premium deduction. The deduction limit for 2025 is as follows:

  • Age 40 or younger: $460
  • Age 41 to 50: $880
  • Age 51 to 60: $1,770
  • Age 61 to 70: $4,470
  • Age 71 and older: $5,640

By purchasing long-term care insurance, you not only protect yourself against future care costs but also benefit from a tax deduction, which can provide substantial savings in your overall tax bill.

Conclusion

Managing healthcare costs can be a daunting task, but with the right tax strategies, you can make the process more manageable. From contributing to HSAs and FSAs to claiming medical expense deductions and utilizing tax credits, there are numerous ways to save on healthcare costs while reducing your overall tax burden. By staying informed about the available options and taking full advantage of these tax-saving tools, you can effectively manage both your healthcare and tax expenses.

If you’re unsure of which tax strategies apply to your specific situation, consider consulting with a tax professional. They can help you navigate the complex rules and ensure you’re maximizing your savings, ultimately improving your financial health.
