Anchoring and Adjustment: How It Shapes Investment Choices


When it comes to making investment decisions, our minds often rely on shortcuts and biases that can lead us astray. One such cognitive bias that greatly affects our investment choices is known as anchoring and adjustment. Anchoring and adjustment is a cognitive bias that occurs when we rely too heavily on an initial reference point, or anchor, when making decisions. This anchor can be any piece of information that we use as a starting point, such as the price of a stock or the value of a property. Once the anchor is set, we tend to make adjustments based on that initial reference point, rather than considering all the available information.

For example, let’s say you’re considering investing in a stock. You read an article that states the stock is currently priced at $50 per share. This information becomes your anchor. Instead of thoroughly evaluating the stock based on its fundamentals, such as the company’s financial health and growth prospects, you may subconsciously adjust your perception of the stock’s value based on this initial anchor.

If the stock price subsequently rises to $60 per share, you may perceive it as a good investment because it has increased from the anchor price. On the other hand, if the price drops to $40 per share, you may view it as a bad investment because it has fallen below the anchor price. In both cases, your decision is influenced by the initial anchor, rather than a comprehensive analysis of the stock’s true value.

Anchoring and adjustment can also manifest in other ways. For instance, investors may anchor their expectations of future returns based on past performance, without considering changes in market conditions or the underlying factors that drove those returns. This bias can lead to overconfidence or unrealistic expectations, which can have detrimental effects on investment outcomes.

To overcome the anchoring and adjustment bias in investment decision-making, it’s important to be aware of its influence and take steps to mitigate its effects. Here are a few strategies to consider:

  1. Diversify your portfolio: By spreading your investments across different asset classes and sectors, you can reduce the impact of anchoring on individual investments. Diversification helps to minimize the influence of any single anchor on your overall portfolio performance.

  2. Conduct thorough research: Instead of relying solely on a single anchor, take the time to thoroughly research and analyze investment opportunities. Consider multiple sources of information, evaluate the underlying fundamentals, and seek expert opinions to form a well-rounded view.

  3. Regularly reassess your investments: Avoid becoming too attached to your initial anchor. Regularly reassess your investments based on current information and market conditions. This will help you make more informed decisions and prevent you from being overly influenced by past anchors.

  4. Seek advice from professionals: Financial advisors and investment professionals can provide valuable guidance and help you navigate the biases associated with anchoring and adjustment. Their expertise can help you avoid common pitfalls and make more rational investment choices.

By understanding the anchoring and adjustment bias and taking steps to mitigate its effects, you can make more rational and informed investment decisions. Remember, investing is a complex and dynamic process, and it’s important to consider all available information, rather than relying solely on initial anchors.
