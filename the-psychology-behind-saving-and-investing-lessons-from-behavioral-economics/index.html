The Psychology Behind Saving and Investing: Lessons from Behavioral Economics


How many times do people attempt to take shortcuts in life? In the race towards achieving success, many are tempted to make decisions that can prove advantageous at the moment but detrimental in the long term. When it comes to saving and investing, understanding our psychological tendencies towards impulsive decisions can prevent long-term financial pitfalls.

Behavioral economics is an application of psychology to economic decision-making. It studies the decision-making process of individuals and posits that individuals do not always act rationally. In this post, we’ll examine how behavioral economics affects our saving and investing habits and provide lessons on developing better financial planning strategies.

Loss Aversion

In behavioral finance, loss aversion refers to the phenomenon that people tend to be more sensitive to the potential loss rather than the gain. This has implications for finance decisions, as people may be unwilling to sell assets even when it would be better to cut their losses.

Lesson from loss aversion: To overcome this tendency, create a plan to minimize potential risks and establish an objective perspective on the swing of returns. Analyze investment decisions frequently to ensure that the value it brings outweighs the potential costs.

The Endowment Effect

Another phenomenon in behavioral economics, the endowment effect, considers the idea that a person’s ownership of an object increases the perceived value of it. This often results in an irrational overvaluation of their own possessions.

Lesson from the endowment effect: Develop a clear understanding of the value in your investment to avoid overvaluing. This might mean exploring different perspectives to minimize the psychological attachment to your investment decisions.

Hyperbolic Discounting

Hyperbolic discounting is a cognitive bias that curves the perceived value of immediate rewards compared to delayed rewards. This results in an impulsive choice between immediate gratification versus the long-term gain.

Lesson from hyperbolic discounting: Create an intuitive planning strategy for financial goals. Develop an early habit of saving and investing which will help to resist the pull of impulsive decisions that focus on immediate gains.

The Gambler’s Fallacy

The gambler’s fallacy arises when individuals believe that past events increase the probability of future outcomes. This results in rash decision-making when betting on future occurrences.

Lesson from the gambler’s fallacy: Understand that past trends may not wholly predict the future. Instead, develop a careful approach to investing that considers long-term trends, and employ strategies that do not rely on a single event or data point.

The House Money Effect

In the context of betting and investing, the house money effect refers to a tendency to be more risky and prone to loss with gains compared to using their original money.

Lesson from the house money effect: Consciously balance your risk management strategy. While increase in gains is encouraging, it does not mean dismissing original capital in favor of risky investments.

In conclusion, saving and investing are behavioral actions that parallel psychological phenomena. Understanding these aspects allows us to create better financial habits, minimizing irrational decisions that often control or impede our financial goals. Develop a long-term, detailed and disciplined plan that fits your risk tolerance and invest in your financial intelligence. By doing so, the journey to financial management and planning is achievable.
