Using MACD Histograms in Trend Following Systems


The world of trading and technical analysis is rife with tools and indicators that help traders decipher market movements. Among these, the MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence) stands out as a powerful momentum and trend-following indicator. But beyond the classic MACD line and signal line crossover, the MACD histogram offers nuanced insights that can significantly enhance trend-following systems. This blog post delves deeply into the use of MACD histograms, explaining their construction, interpretation, and how they can be effectively integrated into trend-following strategies.

Understanding the Foundation: What is the MACD Histogram?

At its core, the MACD is calculated by subtracting the longer period exponential moving average (EMA) from the shorter period EMA — typically the 26-period EMA minus the 12-period EMA. The resulting line is the MACD line. A 9-period EMA of the MACD line is then plotted on top of it as the signal line.

The MACD histogram represents the difference between the MACD line and its signal line. By plotting this difference as bars that oscillate above and below a zero line, the histogram provides a visual representation of the momentum within the trend. When the MACD line is above the signal line, the histogram is positive; when below, it is negative. The height and direction of these bars reflect the strength and acceleration or deceleration of price momentum.

Why the MACD Histogram Matters in Trend Following

Trend-following strategies typically aim to enter trades in the direction of the prevailing trend and ride that trend until momentum wanes. The MACD histogram is particularly valuable because it captures momentum shifts before they become evident in price action or the lines themselves. Here’s why:

  • Early Signal of Momentum Changes: The histogram peaks or troughs often precede crossovers of the MACD and signal lines, offering earlier entry and exit signals.
  • Visualizing Momentum Strength: The expansion or contraction of the histogram bars conveys the acceleration or slowing of momentum, crucial for trend confirmation.
  • Identifying Divergences: Divergences between price action and the MACD histogram can signal potential trend reversals or corrections.

Building a Robust Trend Following System Using the MACD Histogram

Creating a trend-following system that incorporates the MACD histogram involves melding its signals with well-defined entry, exit, and risk management rules. Below is a step-by-step approach to leveraging the histogram effectively.

Step 1: Define Timeframes and Settings

While the default MACD settings (12, 26, 9) are widely used, tailoring these parameters to your trading style and asset class can improve signal accuracy.

  • Shorter timeframes (e.g., 5,13,1) can generate more signals but may include noise.
  • Longer timeframes (e.g., 24,52,18) smooth signals and suit position trading.

Experimentation with settings on historical data helps find an optimal balance between sensitivity and false signals.

Step 2: Entry Signals – Using Histogram Turning Points

The histogram’s directional changes are fertile ground for entry points:

  • Histogram Crossing Above Zero: When the histogram moves from negative to positive, it implies the MACD line has crossed above the signal line, indicating a potential trend shift to bullish momentum. This is a solid buy signal in a trend-following context.
  • Histogram Bars Growing in Height: Increasing histogram bars suggest strengthening momentum, confirming the current trend’s vitality.
  • Histogram “Bottoming Out” and Turning Up: Often, the histogram reaches a trough as momentum wanes and then reverses upward before the MACD crossover, signaling an early entry opportunity.

Step 3: Trade Confirmation Techniques

Confirming signals is essential to avoid false positives. Incorporate the following:

  • Trend Confirmation via Moving Averages: Confirm the MACD histogram entry signal with a longer-term EMA (e.g., 50 or 200 EMA) to ensure alignment with the overall trend direction.
  • Volume Analysis: An uptick in volume with a rising histogram increases the probability of a trend continuation.
  • Price Action Confirmation: Support and resistance levels or candlestick patterns reinforcing momentum shifts can be reliable confirmations.

Step 4: Exit and Stop-Loss Rules

Due to the lag in most trend indicators, timely exits are just as important as entries:

  • Histogram Crossing Below Zero: A reversal in the histogram from positive to negative often signals the momentum is slowing or the trend is reversing, suggesting an exit.
  • Divergence Signals: Bearish divergence occurs when price makes a higher high, but the histogram forms a lower high—indicative of potential trend exhaustion and exit cue.
  • Trailing Stops Based on MACD Histogram: Some traders trail stops below recent swing lows or under dynamic moving averages, adjusting stops as the histogram confirms momentum.

Advanced Uses of MACD Histogram in Trend Following

Now that the fundamentals are clear, let’s explore some sophisticated ways to exploit the histogram:

1. Measuring Momentum Strength with Histogram Slope

Instead of merely noting positive or negative values, calculating the slope of the MACD histogram bars (i.e., the rate of change) provides a more precise read on increasing or waning momentum. This helps differentiate between a weak pullback in a strong uptrend and an outright reversal.

2. Using Histogram Oscillations to Time Entries within Trends

Within a long-term uptrend, histogram swings (from positive to less positive or slightly negative) can signal pullbacks, offering opportunities to enter “on dips” rather than chasing breakouts. This tactic enhances entry timing, reduces risk, and improves reward-to-risk ratios.

3. Combining with Other Indicators for Hybrid Systems

The MACD histogram pairs well with other tools, for example:

  • Relative Strength Index (RSI) for momentum confirmation.
  • Average True Range (ATR) for volatility-adjusted stop placement.
  • Volume indicators to verify strength behind moves.

By blending indicators, traders can reduce whipsaws common to trend-following.

Common Mistakes When Using the MACD Histogram

Even though the MACD histogram is a powerful tool, traders often falter due to misconceptions:

  • Overtrading on Every Histogram Oscillation: Not every change in the histogram indicates a meaningful momentum shift. Patience and combining signals with trend filters help avoid noise.
  • Ignoring Trend Context: Using the histogram signals in sideways or choppy markets can generate false signals, leading to losses.
  • Neglecting Risk Management: Even with great signals, neglecting stop losses or position sizing can spell trouble.

Real-World Example: MACD Histogram in Action

Consider a daily chart of a trending stock. Early signs of strengthening momentum appear when the MACD histogram bars begin rising from a previous trough, but crucially before the MACD line crosses above the signal line. A trader recognizing this can enter the trade earlier, benefiting from the full trend.

As the trend develops, the histogram bars grow taller, reinforcing the bullish momentum. Periodic pullbacks show as shrinking bars or small dip below zero, signaling short pauses but not exits. The eventual flipping of the histogram below zero, coupled with bearish divergence against price, signals the time to close the position or tighten stops.

This proactive approach, grounded in histogram analysis, enables more dynamic and profitable trend following.

Final Thoughts: Elevating Your Trend Following with the MACD Histogram

The MACD histogram is often underrated in routine technical analysis. However, when properly understood and applied, it transforms trend-following trading by offering early, clearer momentum signals and better visual cues on trend strength.

By mastering histogram interpretation—spotting momentum shifts, divergences, and trend confirmations—and integrating these signals into disciplined entries and exits, traders can increase win rates and manage risk more effectively.

Remember, like any tool, the MACD histogram is most powerful when used as part of a comprehensive trading system tailored to your unique strategy and market conditions.

For traders aiming to harness the subtle yet potent nuances of momentum, the MACD histogram is not just a supplementary indicator — it is a strategic ally in navigating and profiting from market trends.
