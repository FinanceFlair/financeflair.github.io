How to Conduct Operational Due Diligence


Operational due diligence (ODD) is a critical step in evaluating businesses before mergers, acquisitions, investments, or partnerships. Unlike financial or legal due diligence, operational due diligence dives deep into the workings of a company to assess its operational strengths, weaknesses, and potential risks. Effectively conducting ODD allows investors, companies, and stakeholders to understand whether the target’s operations are sustainable, efficient, and scalable.

This comprehensive guide walks you through the key stages and best practices for conducting operational due diligence, ensuring you leave no stone unturned while gaining valuable insights into operational risks and opportunities.

What is Operational Due Diligence?

At its core, operational due diligence reviews the internal processes that drive the day-to-day functioning of a business. This typically includes examining production capabilities, supply chain management, workforce efficiency, technology infrastructure, customer service, compliance, and risk management practices.

The purpose is to confirm that the operational side can deliver consistent, high-quality results and support future growth. It also identifies operational risks that could impact financial returns or strategic goals.

Why Conduct Operational Due Diligence?

While financial due diligence can confirm the numbers, operational due diligence reveals the real engine behind those numbers. A company might have strong financials but poor operational controls that could spell trouble later.

Some reasons to emphasize operational due diligence include:

  • Validation of operational claims: Are the target’s reported capabilities and efficiencies accurate?
  • Risk identification: Spot vulnerabilities such as process bottlenecks, supply chain risks, or labor disputes.
  • Synergy assessment: Understand how operations will fit post-merger or investment.
  • Valuation support: Quantify operational assets and potential liabilities.
  • Assurance for post-close integration: Plan for smooth transitions by knowing operational nuances.

When is Operational Due Diligence Conducted?

Operational due diligence typically follows early financial and legal checks once a prospective transaction moves into detailed evaluation. It can also be ongoing, especially for investors monitoring portfolio companies or partners ensuring contractual compliance.

Step-by-Step Guide to Conduct Operational Due Diligence

1. Define Objectives and Scope

Begin by outlining the goals of your operational due diligence. Are you focused on manufacturing capabilities, IT systems, human resources, or regulatory compliance? Clarify the scope to balance thoroughness with efficiency.

Key considerations for this phase include:

  • Key operational areas relevant to your deal.
  • The timeline and deadlines.
  • Team members and expertise required.
  • Desired level of detail.

2. Assemble the Operational Due Diligence Team

An effective ODD team is multidisciplinary, combining operational experts, process engineers, supply chain analysts, IT specialists, HR professionals, and sometimes external consultants or auditors.

Make sure your team has:

  • Industry-specific knowledge.
  • Experience in operational assessments.
  • Analytical and interviewing skills.

3. Gather Preliminary Information

Request and review foundational documents to understand the business context:

  • Organizational charts.
  • Process maps and SOPs (Standard Operating Procedures).
  • Production reports and capacity utilization data.
  • Key supplier and customer contracts.
  • Quality control records.
  • Compliance and audit reports.
  • Historical downtime or incident logs.

This helps identify focus areas for the on-site investigation.

4. On-Site Operational Assessment

Visiting the company’s facilities is critical. On-site visits allow first-hand observation of operations and direct conversations with line managers and staff.

During the site visit:

  • Observe workflows on the manufacturing or service floor.
  • Inspect machinery, technology stacks, and inventory management.
  • Assess safety standards and regulatory compliance.
  • Conduct interviews with department heads impacting operations (production, supply chain, IT, HR, quality control).
  • Review key operational metrics and KPIs in action.

Look for red flags such as outdated machinery, poor maintenance, inadequate training, or inconsistent procedures.

5. Analyze Operational Processes

Mapping and analyzing core operational processes provide insights into efficiency and risk. Use process mapping techniques to identify:

  • Bottlenecks impacting throughput.
  • Redundancies increasing costs.
  • Manual processes vulnerable to errors.
  • Data gaps undermining performance visibility.

Process maturity models can help benchmark operations against industry standards.

6. Evaluate Technology and Infrastructure

Technology plays an enormous role in operational effectiveness. Evaluate:

  • IT systems’ integration level and scalability.
  • Cybersecurity posture and data protection practices.
  • Automation technologies and digital tools used in production or services.
  • ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) and other critical software systems.

Poor or outdated technology often signals operational risks and a need for further investment.

7. Assess Workforce and Human Resources

The people behind operations are just as important as the processes. Assess:

  • Workforce size, skills, and turnover rates.
  • Training programs and employee engagement.
  • Labor relations, union presence, and any ongoing disputes.
  • Health and safety compliance.
  • Leadership and management effectiveness.

Workforce issues can create hidden risks and impact continuity.

8. Review Supply Chain and Vendor Management

Strong supply chains are essential for operational stability. Examine:

  • Supplier diversification and dependency risks.
  • Contract terms, payment schedules, and negotiation leverage.
  • Inventory management, replenishment cycles, and storage facilities.
  • Contingency plans for supplier disruptions.
  • Logistics and distribution efficiency.

Supply chain weaknesses can have cascading effects on production and customer fulfillment.

9. Investigate Quality Control and Compliance

Operational excellence requires rigorous quality management and adherence to regulatory requirements.

Confirm that the target company has:

  • Documented quality assurance processes.
  • Regular audit and inspection routines.
  • Certificates and licenses for industry compliance.
  • Incident management and corrective action procedures.

Deficiencies here can result in recalls, fines, or reputational damage.

10. Identify Operational Risks and Opportunities

Compile findings to spotlight:

  • Operational risks such as process inefficiencies, outdated equipment, or labor issues.
  • Potential cost-saving or efficiency improvement opportunities.
  • Strategic operational strengths such as proprietary technology or exclusive supply agreements.
  • Scalability factors impacting growth.

Quantify risks wherever possible, estimating potential financial impact and mitigation costs.

11. Prepare the Operational Due Diligence Report

Your ODD report should clearly communicate insights to decision-makers. Structure the document with:

  • Executive summary highlighting major findings.
  • Detailed analysis by operational area.
  • Risk and opportunity matrix.
  • Recommendations and next steps.

Use data visualizations, charts, and evidence-backed conclusions. This report becomes a vital part of the overall transaction evaluation and negotiation.

12. Post-Due Diligence Follow-Up

After the report delivery:

  • Clarify any questions stakeholders have.
  • Assist in negotiating operational terms or warranties.
  • Support integration planning with operational focus.
  • Monitor agreed action items during post-close transitions.

The operational due diligence process doesn’t end with the report—its value unfolds during implementation.

Best Practices for Successful Operational Due Diligence

  • Start early: Integrate operational reviews early in the due diligence timeline.
  • Tailor to the business: Customize scope and team expertise for each industry and deal.
  • Combine quantitative and qualitative data: Numbers validate, but interviews and observations reveal hidden insights.
  • Engage with frontline employees: They offer candid views of operational realities.
  • Keep communication open: Regular updates during the due diligence process prevent surprises.
  • Leverage external experts: Specialists provide objectivity and deep industry knowledge.
  • Focus on material issues: Prioritize critical operational factors rather than aiming for exhaustive perfection.

Common Operational Due Diligence Challenges

  • Limited access to sensitive data: Target companies may hesitate to share certain operational details.
  • Time constraints: Thorough operational evaluations take time, and deal timelines can compress this.
  • Bias in self-reported metrics: Cross-verification is essential to avoid over-reliance on favorable internal reports.
  • Complexity in global operations: Multinational targets require additional cultural and regulatory considerations.
  • Rapidly evolving technologies: Staying current on technological impacts is challenging but necessary.

Addressing these challenges proactively maximizes the due diligence quality.

Conclusion

Operational due diligence is a vital component of comprehensive business evaluation that bridges the gap between financial figures and real-world operational realities. With a systematic approach encompassing site visits, process analysis, technology evaluation, human resource assessment, and risk identification, investors and acquirers can unlock valuable insights.

By understanding the nuances of operational performance, stakeholders gain confidence in their decisions, plan effectively for integration, and ultimately position their deals for long-term success. Whether you’re a private equity investor, corporate buyer, or executive exploring partnerships, mastering operational due diligence sharpens your edge in navigating complex transactions.

Conducting operational due diligence is much more than a checklist activity—it’s a strategic investment in understanding how a business truly works and where its future lies. Start with a clear plan, mobilize the right expertise, and approach the task with curiosity and rigor to uncover the real operational story behind the numbers.
