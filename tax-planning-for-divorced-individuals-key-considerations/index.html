Tax Planning for Divorced Individuals: Key Considerations


Divorce brings about significant changes not only in personal life but also in financial responsibilities, including tax obligations. Navigating this new landscape can be challenging, but with proper planning, divorced individuals can position themselves for better financial outcomes at tax time. Let’s explore some essential considerations to help you create a sound tax plan after a divorce.

Filing Status and Its Impacts

One of the first things to address post-divorce is your tax filing status. This status determines your tax bracket, standard deduction, and eligibility for credits. Your marital status on December 31 determines how you’ll file for the year.

  • Single Filing: After divorce, most individuals file as “Single.” This change can affect your tax rates and standard deduction amount.
  • Head of Household: If you’ve been assigned custody of your children for the majority of the year and meet other IRS requirements, you may qualify for the more favorable Head of Household filings, which often comes with lower tax rates and a higher standard deduction.
  • Filing Jointly (if still married for the tax year): If you divorced late in the calendar year but were married prior to December 31, you may still qualify to file jointly for the year in which the divorce was finalized. Consult with your accountant to determine whether filing jointly or separately provides the most financial benefit that year.

Understanding the difference between these filing statuses is vital to avoiding errors and maximizing potential deductions or credits.

Dependents and Tax Benefits

Custody agreements often include provisions regarding claiming dependents for tax purposes. Before filing, review your divorce decree or settlement agreement, as it may stipulate who can claim children as dependents.

  • Dependency Exemptions: Although exemptions were suspended under recent tax law changes, being able to claim dependents can make you eligible for certain benefits.
  • Child Tax Credit (CTC): Divorced parents typically negotiate which parent claims the Child Tax Credit. Usually, the custodial parent (the parent with whom the child resides for more than half the year) claims it, but with proper agreements, this can be waived to allow the non-custodial parent to claim the credit via IRS Form 8332.
  • Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC): Unlike the Child Tax Credit, the Earned Income Tax Credit is non-transferable. Only the custodial parent is eligible to claim it.
  • Tie-Breaker Test: In situations of joint custody, where both parents may be eligible to claim the same dependent, the IRS uses a tie-breaker test that considers factors like income and who provided the majority of the dependent’s support.

Clarity on dependent-related claims prevents errors and ensures you’re maximizing tax advantages.

Property Division and Tax Implications

Dividing assets during divorce can create significant tax considerations. This applies especially to the division of retirement accounts, real estate, and investments.

  • Retirement Accounts: Transfers of retirement assets incident to a divorce are generally tax-free if done under a Qualified Domestic Relations Order (QDRO). However, if this process is mishandled, taxes and penalties can apply.
  • Capital Gains on Property: When transferring real estate between spouses as part of the settlement, the capital gains tax consequences don’t arise until the property is sold. The receiving spouse assumes the original cost basis of the property, which can result in a large taxable gain when sold later.
  • Primary Residence Exclusion: If you owned a home together, selling it during the divorce may enable you to exclude up to $500,000 in gains (if filing jointly) or $250,000 (if filing separately) from your taxable income, as long as you meet the IRS’s ownership and use tests.
  • Responsibility for Taxes on Divided Income: Dividing non-physical assets like stocks or investment portfolios could have tax repercussions if one party ends up liable for any taxable gains or prior obligations.

Properly assessing the tax implications of property division is a critical component of long-term financial planning after divorce.

Spousal and Child Support

Understanding the tax treatment of spousal and child support is crucial when drafting or interpreting divorce settlements.

  • Spousal Support (Alimony): Under the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017, alimony payments are no longer deductible by the payer, nor are they considered taxable income for the recipient. This rule applies to divorce agreements executed after December 31, 2018. For older agreements, spousal support may still qualify for deductibility, depending on circumstances.
  • Child Support: Child support payments do not create a tax obligation for the recipient, nor are they deductible by the payer. Given this classification, it’s wise to structure settlements with tax benefits in mind, allocating payments where needed in ways that reduce tax burdens for both parties.

Discuss these considerations with your legal advisor and tax professional to develop a strategy that aligns with your financial goals.

Health Insurance and Taxes

Divorce affects health insurance coverage for many families. These changes can have tax implications as well, particularly if you rely on marketplace insurance or have a Health Savings Account (HSA).

  • Marketplace Insurance and the Premium Tax Credit: If you use marketplace insurance, changes to income or household size due to divorce can impact eligibility for the Premium Tax Credit, a valuable offset to health insurance costs.
  • COBRA Continuation Coverage: If you’re maintaining employer-sponsored coverage via COBRA after a divorce, these payments are not tax-deductible in most cases.
  • HSAs and Tax Implications: If you had a joint Health Savings Account, clarify its ownership and contribution limits in the divorce proceedings to avoid issues.

Include health insurance implications as part of your tax planning process, as its costs and corresponding tax considerations can affect overall financial health.

Tax Debt and Joint Liabilities

If you were married in years past and filed joint returns, any outstanding tax debt could remain an issue post-divorce. It’s important to address this in your divorce agreement.

  • Innocent Spouse Relief: If your former partner incurred tax liability without your knowledge or participation, you may qualify for Innocent Spouse Relief to shield yourself from repaying joint debts.
  • Equally Spreading Liens: If back taxes or liens exist, negotiate clearly how these liabilities will be divided to ensure you are not left bearing the burden for your ex-spouse’s share.

Stay proactive about past tax liabilities during post-divorce planning to avoid unwelcome surprises.

Estate Planning After Divorce

Estate planning often overlaps with tax planning, making it an integral part of financial strategy after divorce. Updating your estate documents to reflect new relationships, guardianship arrangements, and tax considerations is essential.

  • Update Beneficiaries: Go through contributions to retirement accounts, life insurance policies, and other investments to ensure ex-spouses aren’t inadvertently listed as beneficiaries.
  • Inheritances and Taxes: Without beneficiary updates, inheritance transfers via wills or trusts may lead to unintended tax consequences for intended heirs.
  • Future Gifting and Trust Opportunities: If gifting assets to children or other family members, review how tax rules on annual exclusions, lifetime exemptions, and trusts might fit into your post-divorce goals.

Consider working closely with a tax and estate planning professional to ensure long-term clarity and alignment.

Consulting a Professional

Tax planning after divorce involves a considerable amount of complexity due to the interwoven nature of legal and tax systems. Consulting with a financial advisor or tax professional experienced in post-divorce tax planning can help streamline the process and prevent costly mistakes. They can help you create a strategy tailored to your specific circumstances, ensuring a stable financial foundation for the future.

Conclusion

Divorce is a life-altering event that introduces profound financial changes, particularly when it comes to taxes. Understanding your obligations, rights, and opportunities is essential to protecting your financial interests. From filing status and claiming dependents to managing property division and support payments, tax planning after divorce is a comprehensive process requiring attention to detail.

Take the time to organize your financial accounts, consult with experts, and plan ahead to minimize unnecessary stress. By making informed decisions, you can successfully navigate tax season while securing a strong foundation for the next phase of your life.
