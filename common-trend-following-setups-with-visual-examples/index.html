Common Trend Following Setups With Visual Examples


Trend following remains one of the most widely used trading strategies across financial markets. By aligning positions with the prevailing market direction, traders can ride momentum and capture significant gains. Despite its seeming simplicity, success with trend following depends on recognizing reliable setups and having a clear method to enter and exit positions.

In this extensive exploration, we’ll dive into some of the most common trend following setups. These setups represent patterns and signals that appear repeatedly in price action, allowing traders to enter trades with favorable risk-to-reward ratios. Without using actual images, we’ll describe these setups in detail to help you visualize and recognize them when scanning charts yourself.

What is Trend Following?

Before dissecting specific setups, grasping the core concept of trend following is helpful. At its simplest, trend following is a trading style that buys when prices are moving up and sells (or shorts) when prices are moving down. Rather than trying to predict tops or bottoms, trend followers wait for confirmation that a new trend is underway. Their hope is to stay in these trades for as long as the trend remains intact.

Because trends can extend for days, weeks, or months, trend following requires discipline, patience, and a method to identify trend beginnings and endings accurately.

Setup 1: Moving Average Crossovers

One of the foundational trend following methods involves moving average crossovers. Moving averages smooth out price data to identify trend direction more clearly.

How It Works

  • Use two moving averages: a shorter period (e.g., 20-day) and a longer period (e.g., 50-day).
  • A buy signal occurs when the shorter moving average crosses above the longer moving average, indicating upward momentum.
  • A sell signal or short occurs when the shorter moving average crosses below the longer moving average, indicating downward momentum.

Why This Setup Works

Moving average crossovers reduce noise and confirm momentum changes. The crossover points act as signals that a trend may be starting or reversing.

Visual Example (Imagining a Chart)

Picture a stock chart with two lines: a red line for the 20-day moving average and a blue line for the 50-day moving average. When the red line crosses from below to above the blue line, the price starts an uptrend. Later, when the red line crosses below, a downtrend may begin.

Setup 2: Breakouts From Consolidation Zones

Markets often pause and “consolidate” before trending further. Identifying these consolidation zones and entering on breakouts is a core trend-following technique.

How It Works

  • Identify a price range where the market moves sideways, creating support (price floor) and resistance (price ceiling) levels.
  • Wait for the price to break above resistance for a buy signal or below support for a sell signal.
  • The breakout is confirmation that the market may trend in the breakout direction.

Why This Setup Works

Consolidations represent periods of indecision. A breakout signals that buyers or sellers have gained control, often setting the stage for a strong move.

Visual Example

Imagine price bouncing between $50 (support) and $55 (resistance) for several days. One day, the price surges above $55 with increased volume, triggering a buy signal as a new uptrend may be forming.

Setup 3: Pullback Entries in an Established Trend

Rather than buying at the breakout, some trend followers prefer to enter on pullbacks or dips within an ongoing trend. This often leads to better entry prices and lower risk.

How It Works

  • Identify a clear uptrend (higher highs and higher lows) or downtrend (lower highs and lower lows).
  • Wait for a temporary retracement or pullback against the trend.
  • Enter when price shows signs of resuming the trend after this pullback.

Why This Setup Works

Pullbacks offer chances to enter strong trends at more favorable prices. It reduces the risk of chasing overextended price moves.

Visual Example

Imagine a stock steadily climbing from $40 to $60 in a few weeks. After pushing to $60, the price temporarily pulls back to $55. When the price starts moving back up off this $55 support level, it offers an entry point within the uptrend.

Setup 4: Trendline Breaks and Retests

Trendlines are diagonal lines drawn along key swing highs or lows to signify trend direction. Their breaks and retests can serve as critical trade signals.

How It Works

  • Draw an upward trendline connecting at least two swing lows in an uptrend or a downward trendline connecting swing highs in a downtrend.
  • A break of the trendline may signal a shift or end to the existing trend.
  • After the breakout, price often retests the broken trendline level before continuing in the new direction, offering an entry opportunity.

Why This Setup Works

Trendlines act as dynamic support or resistance. Their break and subsequent retest often confirm that momentum is shifting.

Visual Example

Consider an upward sloping trendline connecting lows at $30 and $35. The price breaks below this trendline to $33 but then moves back up to test the trendline from below before continuing downward. Traders might enter a short position on the retest.

Setup 5: Moving Average Pullback with Momentum Confirmation

Combining moving averages with momentum indicators improves the reliability of pullback trades.

How It Works

  • Identify an uptrend where price is above a moving average (such as the 50-day).
  • Watch for price to pull back near the moving average.
  • Confirm a momentum shift with an indicator like the Relative Strength Index (RSI) or Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD).
  • Enter when price bounces off the moving average and momentum signals strengthen.

Why This Setup Works

The moving average acts as dynamic support, and momentum indicators help avoid false signals by confirming the trend’s strength.

Visual Example

Imagine price in a steady uptrend bouncing off the 50-day moving average at $80. RSI dips below 40 during the pullback but climbs back above 50 as price rises. This alignment confirms strong trend continuation.

Setup 6: Higher Timeframe Trend Alignment

Looking at multiple timeframes refines trend following trades.

How It Works

  • First, identify the main trend on a higher timeframe (daily or weekly).
  • Then switch to a lower timeframe (hourly or 15-minute) to find entries aligned with the higher timeframe trend.
  • This multi-timeframe approach ensures your trade is taken in the direction of the bigger picture trend.

Why This Setup Works

Trades aligned across timeframes tend to be higher probability as they follow the dominant market flow.

Visual Example

If the weekly chart shows an uptrend, a trader might look for a breakout or pullback entry on the daily or 4-hour chart aligned with that uptrend.

Setup 7: Donchian Channel Breakout

Donchian Channels plot the highest high and lowest low over a set period, highlighting volatility and breakout levels.

How It Works

  • Set the Donchian Channel to a significant period like 20 days.
  • A breakout above the upper band signals strength and potential trend start.
  • A breakdown below the lower band signals weakness.

Why This Setup Works

Donchian Channels visually capture breakout levels after periods of consolidation or compressed price action.

Visual Example

If a stock has traded between $20 and $25 for 20 days, a move above $25 signals a potential trend start.

Key Considerations When Using Trend Following Setups

While these setups offer structured ways to enter trending markets, several factors help improve their success:

  • Risk Management: Always use stops below key support or technical levels to minimize losses.
  • Trade Size: Keep position sizes small enough to endure drawdowns without serious portfolio damage.
  • Volume Confirmation: Trend moves with strong volume tend to be more sustainable.
  • Patience: Avoid jumping into trends too early or trying to catch reversals prematurely.
  • Exit Strategy: Decide beforehand how and when to exit, whether via moving average cross, trailing stop, or profit targets.

Conclusion

Mastering trend following requires a blend of art and science. The setups outlined here range from simple moving average crossovers to advanced multi-timeframe alignment and momentum confirmation. Each offers a different lens to identify and enter trends with greater confidence.

Successful trend followers maintain discipline, manage risk well, and adapt setups to their trading style and markets. Observing charts consistently and learning to mentally visualize these setups helps internalize them. Above all, staying aligned with prevailing price action rather than fighting it gives trend followers an enduring edge.

If you embrace these approaches with rigor and patience, you can tap into the powerful momentum that drives markets and increase your odds of profitable trading over time.
