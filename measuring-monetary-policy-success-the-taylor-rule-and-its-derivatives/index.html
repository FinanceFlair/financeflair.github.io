Measuring Monetary Policy Success: The Taylor Rule and Its Derivatives


Monetary policy is a crucial aspect of national economies, aiming to achieve stable economic growth and low inflation. Central banks use various tools and indicators to manage the economic stability of a country. The Taylor Rule is a formula created by economist John B. Taylor in 1993, which suggests the federal funds rate that the Federal Reserve should settle on, based on inflation and real GDP levels. This concept has had a substantial influence on the understanding of monetary policy and helps economists evaluate a central bank’s success.

The Taylor Rule calculates an optimal interest rate, which reflects the central bank’s dual mandate of maximum employment and stable prices. The formula consists of two main parts. The first part relates to the inflation rate and takes into account the difference between the inflation rate (π_t, the percentage change in an index of prices) and the target inflation rate (π 教員). The second part of the formula incorporates the gap between real GDP (y_t) and its potential output (y*_t). Here’s the basic formula:

i_t = π^e_t + β_π(π_t - π*_t) + β_y(y_t - y*_t)

Where: i_t = targeted short-term interest rate, π^e_t = expected inflation rate (typically based on over-the-counter index-linked swap rates), β_π = sensitivity of the nominal interest rate to inflation, β_y = sensitivity of the nominal interest rate to real output.

Many economists use the Taylor Rule to assess the effectiveness of the central bank’s (such as the Fed or the ECB) monetary policy decisions. By comparing the actual interest rate (set by the central bank) with the Taylor rule’s suggested rate, we can gauge whether monetary policy is too strict, or too loose. For instance, when the actual interest rate is higher than the Taylor Rule’s suggested rate, that might indicate aggressive monetary policy or an attempt to curb inflation.

John Taylor’s original rule has been updated and improved by various scholars and economists. Some modifications to the Taylor rule focus on addressing potential issues and limitations of the original formula, while others introduce new factors in the equation, such as financial stability, international considerations, and so on.

An extended version of the Taylor rule that includes financial conditions and global factors is the Global Taylor Rule. Developed by Claudio Borio and Piti Disyatat, this approach focuses on the link between monetary policy and financial cycle, taking into account the potential risks for financial stability. This may be relevant for monetary policy in transforming economies, where other financial aspects may be more significant than in developed countries.

Moreover, some researchers propose implementing the Taylor Rule in a more formalized way, setting clear targets for real GDP and inflation, along with specific thresholds or timeframes for adjustment. This kind of “mechanistic” approach helps in reducing the discretion central banks have in decision-making, increasing transparency, and accountability.

Another variation of the Taylor Rule is the Fair Taylor Rule, which argues that central banks should adjust the interest rate not only to achieve desired inflation and economic growth, but also to accommodate the social welfare of its population. In other words, the rule would consider factors beyond the basic economic indicators, such as income inequality and unemployment rate.

The Taylor Rule and its derivatives also provide useful insights into “history’s lessons”, highlighting the successes (or failures) of monetary policy decision-making. For instance, during the 2008 global financial crisis, those who applied the Taylor Rule would have suggested significant interest rate cuts. In fact, several central banks around the world enacted aggressive interest rate cuts to address the economic turmoil.

Conclusion

In summary, the Taylor Rule has been instrumental in defining and evaluating monetary policy from an economic perspective. While its original version has been refined and modified, it still remains a valuable benchmark in assessing a central bank’s effectiveness and decisions. As evolving economies continue to face new challenges and circumstances, the Taylor Rule and its derivatives will undoubtedly continue to play a vital role in shaping monetary policy strategies worldwide. With a history of eras, monetary policymakers have increasingly relied on the valuable insights derived from the Taylor Rule and its successors.
