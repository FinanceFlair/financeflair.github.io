Kickstarter vs. Indiegogo: Which Crowdfunding Platform is Best?


In the world of crowdfunding, Kickstarter and Indiegogo have become two of the most prominent platforms for launching new projects. Both offer entrepreneurs, creators, and innovators the chance to bring their ideas to life by seeking financial backing from the crowd. But how do they compare, and which one is the best choice for your project? In this article, we’ll explore the differences between Kickstarter and Indiegogo, analyzing their features, benefits, and drawbacks to help you decide which platform is best suited for your crowdfunding needs.

What is Crowdfunding?

Before diving into the specifics of Kickstarter and Indiegogo, it’s important to understand the basic concept of crowdfunding. Crowdfunding is a method of raising money for a project, business, or cause by collecting small contributions from a large number of people, typically via the internet. Crowdfunding platforms, such as Kickstarter and Indiegogo, serve as intermediaries, providing the infrastructure for creators to present their ideas and connect with potential backers.

Crowdfunding can be an effective way to test the market, secure initial funding, and generate buzz around an idea. However, it also requires significant effort in terms of marketing, communication, and delivering on promises made to backers. Choosing the right platform is essential for a successful crowdfunding campaign, which is why comparing Kickstarter and Indiegogo is critical.

Kickstarter: The Leading Crowdfunding Platform

Kickstarter is widely regarded as one of the most successful crowdfunding platforms in the world. Launched in 2009, it has helped fund thousands of creative projects, including films, music, games, technology, and design. Kickstarter’s primary focus is on creative endeavors, with a strong emphasis on innovation and artistic expression.

Key Features of Kickstarter

  1. All-or-Nothing Funding Model
    Kickstarter uses an all-or-nothing funding model, which means that if a project fails to reach its funding goal, it doesn’t receive any of the money pledged by backers. This model can be beneficial for creators, as it ensures that they only receive funding if they’ve met their target and can deliver the promised rewards. However, it also adds an element of risk for both creators and backers.

  2. Project Categories
    Kickstarter hosts a wide range of project categories, including art, technology, design, fashion, food, and more. This makes it a versatile platform for various creative and entrepreneurial endeavors. However, Kickstarter tends to attract more projects in the arts, entertainment, and technology sectors, with fewer opportunities for certain industries such as nonprofits or social causes.

  3. Large Community of Backers
    One of Kickstarter’s most significant advantages is its large and dedicated community of backers. With millions of active users, Kickstarter offers creators a wide audience to tap into for funding and support. Many backers are passionate about discovering new and innovative ideas, which can lead to increased visibility and momentum for a campaign.

  4. Rewards-Based Crowdfunding
    Kickstarter operates on a rewards-based model, meaning that backers receive non-financial incentives in exchange for their contributions. These rewards can range from early access to the product, exclusive content, and branded merchandise, to a special acknowledgment on the creator’s website. Rewards help to create a sense of involvement and excitement for backers, which can increase the likelihood of funding success.

Advantages of Kickstarter

  • Established Reputation: Kickstarter has a proven track record of success and is well-known among both creators and backers. It’s one of the most trusted crowdfunding platforms.
  • Large, Diverse Audience: With millions of backers around the world, Kickstarter offers a vast pool of potential contributors.
  • Focus on Creative Projects: Kickstarter has a specific focus on creative projects, which helps creators gain traction within their niche.
  • Built-In Marketing Tools: Kickstarter provides a range of marketing tools to help creators promote their campaigns, such as social media sharing options, email updates, and a project dashboard to track progress.

Disadvantages of Kickstarter

  • Strict Approval Process: Kickstarter has a more rigorous approval process for projects, which can be frustrating for creators who may not meet the platform’s guidelines or standards.
  • All-or-Nothing Model: While this model provides security for creators, it can be challenging for campaigns that are close to reaching their goal but fall short at the last minute.
  • Limited Flexibility: Kickstarter’s focus on creative projects means that it may not be the best option for all types of businesses or ideas, such as charitable causes or certain types of services.

Indiegogo: The Flexible Alternative

Indiegogo, founded in 2008, is another major crowdfunding platform that offers a wide range of features and flexibility for creators. Unlike Kickstarter, Indiegogo caters to a broader spectrum of projects, including not only creative endeavors but also tech innovations, health initiatives, and even personal causes. Indiegogo’s flexibility makes it an appealing option for many entrepreneurs and creators who need more options for funding and project management.

Key Features of Indiegogo

  1. Flexible Funding Options
    Indiegogo offers both fixed and flexible funding models. Fixed funding operates similarly to Kickstarter’s all-or-nothing model, where creators only receive funds if their goal is reached. However, Indiegogo’s flexible funding model allows creators to keep the funds raised, even if the campaign does not meet its target. This can be particularly useful for projects that need funding regardless of whether they reach their goal.

  2. Broader Project Categories
    Indiegogo has a broader selection of project categories than Kickstarter, including tech, health, education, gaming, and social causes. This flexibility makes it a great platform for entrepreneurs and innovators from all industries. Indiegogo also offers a category specifically for personal fundraising, which is a feature not found on Kickstarter.

  3. International Reach
    Indiegogo has a strong international presence, allowing creators to launch campaigns and backers to contribute from virtually anywhere in the world. The platform supports multiple currencies and languages, making it a popular choice for global projects.

  4. InDemand: Post-Campaign Funding
    One of Indiegogo’s standout features is its InDemand platform, which allows creators to continue raising funds even after their campaign has ended. This is a great option for creators who want to extend their campaign’s reach and secure additional contributions while they prepare to deliver the rewards or launch their product.

Advantages of Indiegogo

  • Flexible Funding Options: Indiegogo’s flexible funding model provides greater freedom for creators who may not meet their goal but still want to keep the funds they’ve raised.
  • Wide Variety of Project Types: Indiegogo supports a diverse range of projects, including not only creative works but also tech innovations, charitable initiatives, and even personal causes.
  • Global Reach: With support for multiple currencies and languages, Indiegogo is a great option for projects with international ambitions.
  • InDemand: The ability to continue raising funds after a campaign ends can be beneficial for creators looking for sustained support or ongoing contributions.

Disadvantages of Indiegogo

  • Smaller Community: Although Indiegogo has millions of users, its community is smaller than Kickstarter’s, which can lead to fewer backers for a given campaign.
  • Less Focused on Creative Projects: While Indiegogo supports a wide variety of project types, it doesn’t have the same strong creative and artistic focus as Kickstarter, which may make it harder to reach a specific niche audience.
  • Fewer Built-In Marketing Tools: Indiegogo offers fewer marketing tools than Kickstarter, which may require creators to invest more time and effort in promoting their campaigns through other channels.

Key Differences Between Kickstarter and Indiegogo

1. Funding Models

The most significant difference between Kickstarter and Indiegogo is their funding models. Kickstarter’s all-or-nothing approach ensures that creators only receive the funds if they meet their target. This can create a sense of urgency and excitement for backers, but it also carries the risk of failure if the goal isn’t met. Indiegogo, on the other hand, offers both fixed and flexible funding options. Creators who opt for flexible funding can still collect funds even if they don’t reach their target, which provides more flexibility and reduces risk.

2. Project Categories and Focus

Kickstarter has a more specific focus on creative projects, including art, design, film, and technology. This makes it a popular choice for creators in those fields. Indiegogo, however, is more versatile and supports a wider range of projects, including tech, health, education, and personal fundraising. If your project doesn’t fall within the creative domain, Indiegogo may be a better option.

3. Global Reach

Both platforms have international reach, but Indiegogo has a more robust global presence, supporting multiple currencies and languages. This can be an advantage if your project has global ambitions or requires support from international backers.

4. Community and Backer Engagement

Kickstarter has a larger, more dedicated community of backers, especially those who are passionate about creative projects. This can give creators more exposure and a better chance of finding backers who are interested in their specific niche. Indiegogo’s community is smaller but still active, and its platform allows for more diverse types of projects to gain support.

5. Post-Campaign Opportunities

Indiegogo offers the InDemand feature, which allows creators to continue raising funds after their campaign ends. This is a unique offering that Kickstarter doesn’t provide, making Indiegogo an appealing choice for creators who want to extend the life of their campaign and secure additional funding.

Conclusion: Which Platform is Best for You?

Choosing between Kickstarter and Indiegogo depends on your project’s goals, the type of campaign you want to run, and the level of flexibility you need. If you’re working on a creative project, Kickstarter is likely the better option, given its larger community and focus on creative endeavors. However, if you need more flexibility in terms of funding models or have a project that falls outside of the creative space, Indiegogo may be the better choice.

Both platforms have their strengths and weaknesses, and the best choice ultimately depends on your specific needs. Consider your funding goals, the type of project you’re working on, and your target audience before making a decision. With careful planning and the right strategy, both Kickstarter and Indiegogo can help turn your ideas into reality.
