How to Value a Stock Using Earnings Power


Valuing a stock is an essential skill for investors who want to make smart decisions and maximize returns. Among the many valuation methods, earnings power stands out as a particularly insightful approach. Unlike many models driven by speculative growth estimates or market sentiment swings, the earnings power method centers on a company’s ability to generate sustainable profits from its current operations. It provides a grounded, reality-based view of intrinsic value that can help avoid overpaying for stocks.

This comprehensive guide will walk you through the concept of earnings power, how it differs from other valuation approaches, the steps to calculate a stock’s intrinsic value using earnings power, and some practical tips for applying this method effectively.

Understanding Earnings Power

Earnings power refers to the sustainable, normalized earnings a company can generate under current operating conditions. Essentially, it asks: if the business runs steadily without extraordinary events or growth spurts, what level of profit can we reasonably expect year after year?

This approach contrasts with models that rely heavily on future growth assumptions, such as discounted cash flow (DCF) analysis that projects variable cash flows over long periods. While growth assumptions are critical in many contexts, they often introduce significant uncertainties. Earnings power valuation mitigates this risk by focusing on the company’s present earning capability.

The Core Principle

The underlying principle is simple: the true value of a company lies in its ability to produce profit not just once, but consistently. By estimating the sustainable earnings the business can deliver indefinitely, then applying a capitalization rate (or inverse of the required rate of return), an investor can calculate an intrinsic value for the stock.

Why Use Earnings Power to Value Stocks?

  • Simplicity and Objectivity: Earnings power valuation relies on historical and normalized data, reducing guesswork. It’s rooted in accounting figures adjusted for non-recurring items.
  • Stability: By focusing on consistency, it helps identify companies with reliable cash flow generation and avoid stocks priced for unrealistic growth.
  • Flexibility: This method works well across industries, especially those with mature businesses and stable profit margins.
  • Insight into Business Quality: Companies with strong earnings power typically enjoy competitive advantages like pricing power, efficient operations, or barriers to entry.

Steps to Value a Stock Using Earnings Power

Let’s break down the valuation process step-by-step.

1. Normalize Earnings

Start with the company’s reported earnings, but adjust for any irregular items that don’t reflect ongoing operations:

  • Remove one-time charges or gains: For example, restructuring costs, asset sales, or litigation settlements.
  • Adjust for cyclical fluctuations: In cyclical industries, average earnings over several years may provide a better picture than a single year.
  • Exclude extraordinary revenue or costs: Events like natural disasters or pandemic-related government grants should be factored out.

The goal is to estimate the sustainable earnings the company can generate in a typical year.

2. Determine Earnings before Interest and Taxes (EBIT)

Use EBIT as the baseline because it reflects operating profit before leverage and tax effects, giving a clearer indicator of business operations.

  • EBIT = Operating Income + Non-operating Income (if relevant and sustainable)

Example: A company reports a net income of $50 million, interest expense of $10 million, and tax expense of $12 million. Then EBIT can be roughly estimated by adding interest back to operating income, but it’s better to extract EBIT directly from the income statement to avoid confusion.

3. Calculate Adjusted Earnings Power

If the company operates with debt, interest expense distorts the picture. To value the company as a whole (enterprise value), focus on EBIT adjusted for taxes:

  • Adjusted Earnings Power = EBIT × (1 – Tax Rate)

This gives you after-tax operating earnings, assuming the company operates unleveraged.

4. Estimate Capital Employed

Determine the amount of capital the company uses to generate earnings:

  • Capital Employed = Total Assets – Current Liabilities (excluding interest-bearing debt)

Alternatively, you can use the sum of equity plus interest-bearing debt to understand total invested capital.

5. Calculate Capital Charge

Capital charge represents the cost of capital applied to the capital employed:

  • Capital Charge = Capital Employed × Cost of Capital

The cost of capital is often the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC), capturing the average return expected by both equity and debt holders.

6. Compute Earnings Power Value (EPV)

The formula for the Earnings Power Value is:

[ \text{EPV} = \frac{\text{Adjusted EBIT} \times (1 - \text{Tax Rate})}{\text{Cost of Capital}} ]

This formula assumes that the company can sustain the current earnings indefinitely with no growth.

7. Adjust for Non-Core Assets and Liabilities

Sometimes, the balance sheet contains assets or liabilities not part of core operations, like excess cash, investments, or non-operating liabilities. Adjust the EPV upward or downward to reflect these elements:

  • Add: Excess cash, marketable securities, and investments that can be monetized.
  • Subtract: Non-operating liabilities or underfunded pension obligations.

8. Derive Equity Value and Price per Share

Finally, calculate the intrinsic value of the company’s equity:

[ \text{Equity Value} = \text{EPV} - \text{Interest-bearing Debt} + \text{Excess Cash} ]

Then divide by the number of shares outstanding to get the intrinsic value per share.

Real-life Example

Imagine a company reports the following simplified metrics:

  • EBIT: $100 million
  • Tax rate: 25%
  • Cost of capital: 10%
  • Capital Employed: $1 billion
  • Debt: $300 million
  • Excess cash: $50 million
  • Shares outstanding: 50 million

Step 1: Adjust EBIT after taxes:

[ \text{Adjusted EBIT} = 100 \times (1 - 0.25) = 75 \text{ million} ]

Step 2: Calculate EPV:

[ EPV = \frac{75}{0.10} = 750 \text{ million} ]

Step 3: Calculate Equity Value:

[ 750 - 300 + 50 = 500 \text{ million} ]

Step 4: Calculate Intrinsic Price per Share:

[ \frac{500 \text{ million}}{50 \text{ million shares}} = $10 \text{ per share} ]

If the current market price is below $10, the stock might be undervalued based on earnings power.

Important Considerations and Limitations

  • Assumption of No Growth: Earnings power assumes constant earnings with no growth. If the business is growing or shrinking, adjustments are necessary.
  • Reliable Financials Needed: The model relies heavily on accurate, clean financial statements. Misleading accounting can distort valuation.
  • Capital Requirements Impact: Capital-intensive businesses require more capital to sustain earnings. Understanding how capital employed affects returns is key.
  • Ignoring Intangible Assets: Some companies’ value derives from intangible assets like brand, patents, or technology, which may not be fully captured in earnings.
  • Cyclicality and Industry Factors: Normalize earnings carefully in highly cyclical or seasonal industries.

How Earnings Power Valuation Fits With Other Methods

While earnings power offers a steady-state view of value, it does not replace growth models or relative valuation tactics but complements them. Use earnings power valuation to:

  • Check if growth assumptions in other models are reasonable.
  • Identify companies trading below their intrinsic value based on core profitability.
  • Gain a conservative baseline value during volatile markets.

For growth companies, combine earnings power with growth projections for a fuller picture. For mature firms, earnings power valuation provides a straightforward intrinsic value.

How to Improve Your Earnings Power Analysis

  • Use Multi-year Averages: Smooth out earnings variations due to economic cycles.
  • Adjust for Future Changes: Incorporate likely margin improvements or cost pressures based on management guidance or industry trends.
  • Incorporate Qualitative Analysis: Assess competitive positioning, regulatory risks, and management quality.
  • Stress Test Assumptions: Vary cost of capital and normalized earnings to understand a range of valuations.

Conclusion

Valuing a stock through earnings power is an invaluable tool for investors seeking an objective and grounded estimate of intrinsic value. It emphasizes steady cash generation ability over speculative futures, serving as a reality check against hype and irrational exuberance. By mastering this method, you will enhance your investment decision-making process, better understand company fundamentals, and uncover opportunities that others might overlook.

Keep in mind that no method is foolproof. Earnings power valuation is best used alongside a range of other tools and thoughtful qualitative judgment. When applied well, however, it provides a solid anchor in the dynamic world of stock valuation.
