Is Trend Following the Key to Achieving Consistent Gains?


The search for consistent gains in trading and investing is as old as the markets themselves. Countless strategies have promised the elusive goal of sustainable profits through bear and bull cycles, yet few have the staying power to outlast the ever-changing tides of the financial world. Among all these approaches, one stands out for its straightforward logic and empirical pedigree—trend following.

But is trend following really the answer, or just another well-cloaked mirage? Let’s take an in-depth journey beneath the surface of this methodology to discover its foundations, its strengths and weaknesses, and whether it deserves a lasting place in your trading playbook.

Understanding Trend Following

At its core, trend following is elegantly simple: buy assets that are rising, sell assets that are falling. Rather than predict market tops and bottoms, trend followers strive to participate in ongoing price moves, capturing the “middle” of major waves.

This approach eschews short-term noise. The focus is on the actual movement of price, not on economic news, analyst opinions, or macroeconomic predictions. Trend followers are agnostic about why a market is moving—they care only that it is moving.

Notably, trend following isn’t a singular, rigid system. It’s a philosophy that can be executed in myriad ways, using tools like moving averages, breakout signals, or even more esoteric technical analysis. Whether on stocks, commodities, currencies, or crypto, the principle is the same: identify directional momentum and ride it as long as it persists.

Why Trend Following Endures

Several reasons explain trend following’s enduring popularity among traders and institutional investors:

1. Survival Across Asset Classes and Decades
Trend following isn’t tied to specific markets or historical epochs. Its logic can be applied to futures, equities, currencies, and bonds. Analysis of Commodity Trading Advisor (CTA) performance, for example, shows that systematic trend following has delivered robust returns across multiple asset classes and crises, from the 1970s energy shocks to the 2008 financial crisis.

2. Built-In Risk Management
This approach inherently involves disciplined exits. Once the trend reverses or stalls, trend followers cut their positions. This means that losses are contained, while winning trades can run for longer, resulting in the “asymmetric returns” that underpin many successful systems.

3. Psychological Advantages
Humans are wired to seek comfort, often buying high and selling low out of fear and greed. Trend following applies rules, reducing the opportunity for bias and rash decisions to sabotage performance.

4. Exploitation of Market Inefficiencies
Trends exist because markets are not fully efficient. News, institutional flows, and retail herding can trigger persistent price moves. By identifying and tracking these moves, trend followers aim to turn market irrationality into profit.

The Statistical Evidence

Any strategy can sound persuasive in theory; does trend following actually deliver in the cold light of data?

Numerous academic studies have reviewed trend following returns across multiple decades and continents. The conclusion is that robust, systematic trend-following strategies generally achieve positive returns over the long haul, with relatively low correlation to equity indices.

A long-term study by Lemperiere et al. (2014), for instance, examined trend following on more than 200 years of asset price data across stocks, commodities, bonds, and currencies. They found that trend following generated persistent returns, even after accounting for transaction costs.

Further analysis by the likes of AQR Capital Management has suggested that trend-following returns are particularly pronounced during periods of market crisis—providing valuable diversification when traditional buy-and-hold portfolios need it most.

These findings are not without critics. Some argue that increased popularity of systematic investing has made trends harder to exploit, and that markets can experience extended periods where trends are absent, choppy, or abruptly reverse. However, few deny the strategy’s historical credentials.

Trend Following in Action: Famous Examples

The annals of trading history are rich with stories of those who rode trends to fame and fortune.

The Turtle Traders experiment in the 1980s, organized by Richard Dennis and William Eckhardt, serves as a legendary example. Dennis recruited a group with scant trading experience, provided clear trend-following rules, and turned several into millionaires over a period of years. Their methods relied on breakouts, fixed stop-losses, and letting profits run—a classic trend-following toolkit.

Systematic funds like Winton, Dunn Capital, and AHL have garnered billions under management by relying heavily on disciplined trend-following systems. Even today, many of the world’s biggest and oldest managed futures funds cite trend following as their core return driver.

The Anatomy of a Trend Following System

While the outer trappings may differ, most trend-following systems share a few common features.

Signal Generation
This is how the system decides when to enter a position. The simplest method uses moving averages. For example, when the 50-day moving average crosses above the 200-day, it signals an uptrend (the famous “golden cross”). More sophisticated systems use breakout rules (e.g., buying when price exceeds the highest close of the past 20 days) or momentum indicators.

Position Sizing
Trend followers typically risk a small, fixed percentage of capital on each trade to prevent any single loss from decimating the account. Volatility-based position sizing—allocating less to markets with higher volatility—is common.

Exits and Stops
Stop-losses are mandatory. These can be price-based, volatility-based, or time-based. Many systems use trailing stops to lock in profits as a trend matures.

Portfolio Diversification
Trend followers rarely focus on a single market. Diversification across many uncorrelated assets reduces the impacts of whipsaws and non-trending periods in individual markets.

Strict Discipline
Perhaps most critical is unwavering adherence to the rules. Emotional trading is the mortal enemy of systematic trend following.

Strengths of Trend Following

It’s not hard to see why many are attracted to this approach:

Profit Potential in Crisis
Trend following thrives during market chaos and dislocations. When equities tank or bonds spike, trend followers don’t freeze or panic—they follow the rules, capturing major moves that others miss or fear.

Diversification Benefits
By operating across multiple markets and timeframes, trend following reduces the reliance on any single market’s behavior. This “crisis alpha” can be a powerful addition to portfolios heavily weighted toward stocks or bonds.

Objectivity
Trend following removes much of the subjective guesswork from trading. By automating entries and exits, it bypasses common emotional traps.

Long-Term Sustainability
As long as markets occasionally trend—and history suggests they do—trend following can theoretically deliver returns, even as short-term market conditions change.

Drawbacks and Challenges

Despite its virtues, trend following is not a panacea. Would-be practitioners need to grapple with tough realities:

Low Win Rate
Most trend-following systems have a win rate between 30% and 45%. Many trades are stopped out for small losses. The strategy banks on a small number of outsized winners.

Long Periods of Drawdown
Markets can sometimes chop sideways for months or years. During these periods, trend followers may bleed capital due to “whipsaw” losses. It takes psychological fortitude to stay the course until trends re-emerge.

Delayed Entries and Exits
Because trend following waits for confirmation, entries and exits always lag market turns. This means missing absolute bottoms or tops.

Requires Discipline
Sticking to the rules during drawdowns and after strings of losses challenges the resolve of most traders. Temptation to “improve” or abandon systems is high.

No Holy Grail
Market conditions can change, and the returns of trend following can wax and wane just like any other strategy.

Is Trend Following Right for You?

Ultimately, the answer depends on your risk tolerance, investment horizon, and temperament.

Trend following might be ideal if you:

  • Prefer taking small, systematic losses for a shot at large, trend-driven gains.
  • Are comfortable with win rates under 50% and long stretches of underperformance.
  • Favor mechanical, rule-based discipline over subjective judgement.
  • Diversify across multiple markets and timeframes.

But it may not suit you if:

  • You need frequent, quick feedback or consistent monthly returns to stay motivated.
  • You’re prone to exiting strategies at the first sign of drawdown.
  • You prefer fundamentally-driven trades or more active engagement.

Implementing Trend Following: Steps for Success

For those intrigued by the trend-following approach, getting started doesn’t require a PhD or a data science team, but it does demand commitment and careful design.

  1. Pick Your Universe
    • Decide which markets you’ll trade—stocks, ETFs, commodities, currencies, or all of the above.
    • Cast a wide net for diversification.
  2. Define Your Signals
    • Select entry and exit rules, such as moving average crosses, channel breakouts, or momentum indicators.
    • Backtest these rules using historical data.
  3. Establish Your Position Sizing
    • Consider volatility-adjusted position sizing to avoid overweighting riskier assets.
  4. Set Robust Exit Criteria
    • Use stops that fit your timeframe and risk tolerance. Trailing stops can lock in profits.
  5. Automate as Much as Possible
    • Remove the potential for emotional override by automating trade entries and exits when possible.
  6. Track and Review
    • Keep detailed records and analyze results periodically.
    • Be ready for long periods when your account may stagnate or decline, and resist the urge to abandon the plan prematurely.

The Evolution of Trend Following

Trend following is not etched in stone. Just as markets evolve, so too must strategies. Today, trend followers are exploring:

  • Shorter and longer timeframes, from intraday to years.
  • Hybrid systems that blend classical trend following with machine learning or alternative data.
  • Markets from crypto to volatility futures.

Adaption and innovation remain core to surviving in a world where signals are increasingly scrutinized and exploited.

Conclusion: Consistent Gains, With A Caveat

So, is trend following the key to achieving consistent gains? The historical record suggests that, when executed with discipline and diversification, it’s one of the few enduring approaches that work across multiple markets and time periods. The appeal lies in its pragmatic embrace of uncertainty and volatility, allowing traders to thrive amid chaos.

Yet, “consistent” does not mean “constant.” There will be stretches of boredom, frustration, and drawdowns. Trend following’s greatest hurdle is not designing a profitable system, but having the patience to let it work. If you can stomach cold streaks and shun the seductive call of prediction, trend following might just be the backbone your investment strategy needs.

No method is foolproof; markets always have a way of humbling even the most seasoned participants. But for those seeking an evidence-based, resilient playbook to navigate the ever-changing market weather, trend following—warts and all—remains worthy of serious consideration.
