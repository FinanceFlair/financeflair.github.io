Trend Following in ETFs: How to Get Started


Investors are constantly seeking strategies that help them navigate the markets more effectively, aiming to capitalize on upward momentum and avoid significant drawdowns. One such approach that has garnered significant attention is trend following, particularly when applied to Exchange Traded Funds (ETFs). This strategy offers a methodical way to identify and ride market trends, potentially smoothing equity market volatility while enhancing returns.

In this comprehensive guide, we’ll explore the foundational concepts of trend following, why ETFs serve as an excellent vehicle for this approach, and provide you with actionable steps to begin your trend-following journey.

What Is Trend Following?

Trend following is an investment strategy based on the principle that assets exhibiting strong price momentum are likely to continue moving in the same direction for a period of time. Rather than attempting to predict market turns or bottoms, trend followers systematically identify ongoing market patterns and “go with the flow,” buying assets when their prices are rising and selling or avoiding them when the trend reverses.

Unlike traditional buy-and-hold investing, trend following is dynamic and responsive, aiming to capture profits during extended uptrends while minimizing losses in downtrends. The strategy is grounded in technical analysis, employing indicators like moving averages, breakouts, and relative strength measures.

Why Use ETFs for Trend Following?

ETFs have become popular financial instruments because of their liquidity, diversification, transparency, and flexibility. When combined with trend-following strategies, ETFs offer several advantages:

  • Diversification Across Asset Classes: ETFs represent baskets of securities—stocks, bonds, commodities, or mixed assets. This helps trend followers diversify exposure without having to buy individual securities directly.

  • Liquidity: Most ETFs trade like stocks throughout market hours, allowing investors to enter or exit positions quickly when trends change.

  • Lower Costs: ETFs often have lower expense ratios compared to mutual funds, making them cost-effective vehicles for active strategies.

  • Transparency: ETF holdings and prices are published and updated regularly, aiding effective trend analysis.

  • Wide Variety: Investors can employ trend following across equities, sectors, fixed-income, commodities, and international markets by choosing the relevant ETFs.

Starting trend following with ETFs allows investors to create a nimble, systematic portfolio tailored to current market conditions while managing risk efficiently.

Core Elements of a Trend Following Strategy in ETFs

Before diving into implementation, it’s important to understand the building blocks that make trend following work. These often include:

1. Defining the Trend

A trend is essentially the general direction in which an asset’s price is moving—up, down, or sideways. Identifying whether a trend exists is the first task. Common technical tools include:

  • Moving Averages: Calculated average prices over specific time windows (e.g., 50-day, 200-day). If prices remain above a moving average, the uptrend is in place.

  • Breakouts: Price moving above a previous high or resistance level can signal an emerging trend.

  • Momentum Indicators: Tools like the Relative Strength Index (RSI) gauge the velocity of price changes, helping confirm trend strength.

2. Entry and Exit Rules

Clear and systematic entry/exit rules are crucial. This eliminates guesswork and emotional decision-making. For example:

  • Entry: Buy when the ETF price closes above its 200-day moving average.

  • Exit: Sell when price closes below the 50-day moving average.

3. Position Sizing and Risk Management

Managing how much of your portfolio to allocate and defining cutoffs for acceptable losses are critical to long-term success. Common techniques:

  • Fixed Fractional Position Sizing: Allocate a fixed percentage of the portfolio per trade.

  • Volatility-Based Sizing: Invest less in more volatile ETFs to adjust overall risk.

  • Stop Losses and Trailing Stops: Automatically exit positions if prices move against you beyond predetermined levels.

4. Rebalancing Frequency

Determine how often you check trends and adjust holdings — daily, weekly, or monthly. The right interval balances responsiveness with trading costs.

Step-by-Step Guide to Getting Started With Trend Following in ETFs

Now that the core elements are outlined, here is a practical step-by-step approach to implement a trend-following strategy using ETFs:

Step 1: Choose Your ETFs Universe

Start by selecting a well-rounded group of ETFs spanning various sectors, regions, and asset classes to diversify opportunity sources. Examples include:

  • Broad market equity ETFs (e.g., S&P 500 index funds)
  • Sector-specific ETFs (technology, healthcare, energy)
  • International market ETFs
  • Bond or fixed income ETFs
  • Commodity ETFs (gold, oil)

Ensure the ETFs you select have sufficient liquidity, moderate expense ratios, and good price data history for analysis.

Step 2: Define Your Trend Detection Method

Pick technical indicators that resonate with your trading stance. Moving averages are among the simplest and most robust tools for trend identification. A common approach:

  • Use the 200-day moving average to define the primary trend.

  • Complement this with a shorter moving average (e.g., 50-day) to capture early signals and potential reversals.

Another alternative is the Dual Moving Average Crossover, where a buy signal generates when a shorter moving average crosses above the longer one and vice versa for selling.

Choose parameters suited to your investment horizon; longer timeframes filter noise but might react slower, while shorter timeframes catch trends early but may generate false signals.

Step 3: Set Clear Entry and Exit Rules

Establish precise, quantifiable triggers to open and close positions based on your trend signals. For instance:

  • Buy an ETF when its closing price rises above its 200-day moving average.

  • Sell when it dips below the 50-day average.

Alternatively, employ price breakout systems, such as buying when the price closes above the prior 20-day high and selling when it closes below the 20-day low.

Whatever method you choose, test it historically to understand performance characteristics and tweak thresholds as necessary.

Step 4: Determine Position Sizing and Risk Controls

Decide how much capital to allocate per ETF and set risk limits. Some considerations:

  • Limit individual holdings to a specific percentage of your portfolio to prevent undue exposure.

  • Use volatility-adjusted position sizing. For example, invest less in highly volatile ETFs.

  • Apply stop-loss orders to cap downside on each trade, or use trailing stops that adjust as the price moves favorably.

  • Keep cash reserves or hold a bond ETF for safety when no clear trends are present.

Always backtest your risk approach alongside your entry/exit rules to confirm resilience under different market environments.

Step 5: Implement a Rebalancing Schedule

Choose how frequently you will assess your positions and make necessary trades based on trend changes. Common intervals:

  • Weekly: More responsive to changing conditions but entails higher transaction costs.

  • Monthly: Smoother adjustments, potentially better for cost-efficiency.

  • Quarterly: Lower trading frequency ideal for longer-term investors but risks missing rapid trend shifts.

The chosen frequency should also align with your lifestyle and ability to monitor the markets regularly.

Step 6: Backtest and Paper Trade

Before committing real capital, simulate your strategy on historical data to understand:

  • Average returns and drawdowns.

  • Win/loss ratios and duration of trends.

  • Sensitivity to parameter changes.

Once comfortable with the simulated results, try trading the strategy with virtual money or small initial investments to gain confidence without risking significant funds.

Step 7: Monitor and Adjust

Markets evolve, so maintaining an ongoing review process is key. Track performance metrics and modify:

  • Indicators’ parameters if they are underperforming.

  • Rebalancing frequencies.

  • ETFs universe as new funds and sectors become accessible.

Avoid making impulsive changes based on short-term outcomes; focus on data-driven decisions.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even proven strategies can falter if mishandled. Avoid these pitfalls:

  • Ignoring Costs: Frequent rebalancing in tax-inefficient accounts can erode gains through commissions and taxes. Use low-cost brokers and tax-aware strategies.

  • Overfitting: Avoid overly complex rules tailored strictly to past data which rarely generalize well.

  • Lack of Discipline: Emotional trading or deviating from your defined rules reduces long-term success.

  • Neglecting Risk Management: Trend following doesn’t guarantee profits; risk controls are indispensable.

  • Insufficient Diversification: Concentrated bets increase vulnerability to sector-specific crashes.

  • Too Frequent Trading: Excessive turnover can increase expenses and reduce net returns.

Keeping these points in mind improves your odds considerably.

Advantages and Challenges of Trend Following in ETFs

Advantages

  • Objective and Rule-Based: Minimizes emotional decisions and biases.

  • Adaptable to Any Market: Can work across equities, bonds, commodities, and currencies.

  • Risk Management: Helps avoid large downturns by exiting declining trends.

  • Scalable: Suitable for both retail and institutional investors.

  • Transparency: Easy to monitor performance given the ETF structure.

Challenges

  • Whipsaws: Trend following can generate false signals in sideways markets.

  • Lagging Indicators: Moving averages and breakouts react to past price changes, potentially missing early trend reversals.

  • Requires Patience: Periods of drawdowns or flat performance can be discouraging.

  • Overtrading: High turnover in volatile markets can raise costs.

  • Psychological Stress: Consistent adherence to systematic rules demands discipline.

Understanding these helps prepare mentally and technically for the journey ahead.

Real-World Example: Simple Moving Average Trend Following With ETFs

Consider a portfolio universe composed of five ETFs:

  • One broad U.S. equity ETF (e.g., SPY)

  • One international equity ETF (e.g., EFA)

  • One sector ETF (e.g., XLK - Technology)

  • One bond ETF (e.g., BND)

  • One commodity ETF (e.g., GLD - Gold)

The strategy uses 200-day simple moving averages (SMA) to decide positioning:

  • If an ETF’s closing price is above its 200-SMA, allocate 20% of the portfolio to that ETF.

  • If below, move that allocation to cash or bonds.

You review signals monthly and rebalance accordingly.

Historical backtests of such a strategy often show enhanced risk-adjusted returns by avoiding prolonged drawdowns during bear markets while participating in major bull runs, demonstrating trend following’s potential when applied thoughtfully to ETFs.

Automation and Alerts

Automating signals or using alerts helps stay disciplined and responsive without constant monitoring.

Educational Material

Books like “Following the Trend” by Andreas Clenow or articles from trend following specialists offer deeper insights into mechanics and psychology.

Final Thoughts

Trend following using ETFs is a powerful strategy for investors who desire a systematic and disciplined approach to capitalizing on market momentum. By combining widely accessible ETFs with clear technical rules, proper risk management, and consistent review, individual investors can potentially improve returns while mitigating downside risks.

Starting small, thoroughly testing your method, and maintaining patience will serve you well in building an effective trend-following portfolio. As with any strategy, there are no guarantees, but trend following offers a rational and data-driven path to help navigate the complex world of financial markets.

Embarking on this journey can transform how you perceive market movements — from a guessing game to an opportunity-seizing process grounded in observable trends. Ready to get started? The markets are moving, and trends await those equipped to follow them.
