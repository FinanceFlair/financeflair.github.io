The Best Trend Following Tools for Every Type of Trader


The world of trading thrives on identifying and capitalizing on trends. Whether you’re a day trader glued to the screen or a casual investor dipping in and out of the market, mastering the art of trend following can be your edge. In a financial landscape packed with volatility, powerful tools for tracking trends have become essential. But with so many available, how do you pick the right ones?

Let’s dive into the universe of trend following tools and uncover what works best for different kinds of traders—from the lightning-fast scalp specialist to the patient positional player. Each tool brings its own strengths and weaknesses, and the goal is to create a toolkit tailored to your unique approach and time horizon.

The Essence of Trend Following

Trend following, at its core, is about identifying the prevailing direction of a market—be it stocks, forex, commodities, or crypto—and aligning trades in that direction. Rather than predicting tops and bottoms, trend followers ride waves as long as possible, exiting only when the tide turns.

A proper trend following toolkit helps traders:

  • Validate market direction across timeframes.
  • Confirm breakouts and reversals with minimal lag.
  • Filter out market noise to avoid false signals.
  • Objectively manage risk with well-defined entry and exit points.

No single tool suits everyone. Each trader’s style, risk tolerance, and preferred assets call for a customized approach. Let’s break down the most effective trend following tools, grouped by trader profiles.

1. Moving Averages: The Universal Trend Compass

Perhaps the single most recognizable tool in trend following is the humble moving average. Both simple moving averages (SMA) and exponential moving averages (EMA) smooth out price action and strip away noise. But it’s how you use them that matters.

For Day Traders:

  • 9, 20, and 50-period EMAs on the 1-minute or 5-minute chart help spot micro-trends.
  • Fast-moving averages react quickly to price, perfect for scalp traders needing rapid feedback.

For Swing Traders:

  • 20, 50, and 200-period SMAs on the 1-hour or daily timeframe reveal medium-term momentum.
  • Crossovers—especially when a short-term average slices above/below a longer-term one—signal trend shifts.

For Position Traders:

  • 100 and 200-day SMAs help filter out short-term volatility, focusing on major market trends.
  • They form the backbone of “golden cross” and “death cross” setups.

Pros and Cons

Pros:

  • Simple, visual, and widely known.
  • Highly customizable to any timeframe.

Cons:

  • Lagging by nature, sometimes late on entries/exits.
  • Choppy in range-bound conditions.

Pro Tip

Combine moving averages with other indicators to avoid whipsaws and generate more reliable entries.

2. The Power of Trendlines and Channels

While indicators are useful, the core chart still rules. Manually drawing trendlines and channels is an artform for many successful traders.

For Day Traders:

  • Draw immediate trendlines on short timeframes to catch breakouts and breakdowns during high volatility.
  • Use micro-channels to identify turning points intra-day.

For Swing Traders:

  • Broader channels can guide setups across several days or weeks.
  • Price respecting or breaking trendlines is often the first sign of a reversal.

For Position Traders:

  • Long-term trend channels can frame multi-year moves.
  • Channel midlines and extremes offer obvious, repeatable trade locations.

Pros and Cons

Pros:

  • Completely adaptable; shows support/resistance dynamically.
  • Excellent for visual traders who like hands-on analysis.

Cons:

  • Subjective—two traders may draw lines differently.
  • Prone to hindsight bias if not rigidly defined.

Pro Tip

Use trendlines in conjunction with price action patterns or indicator signals for confirmation.

3. The Relative Strength Index (RSI): Unmasking Trend Exhaustion

The RSI isn’t just for overbought and oversold conditions. Dialed-in traders use it to confirm or challenge an existing trend.

For Day Traders:

  • Watch for RSI to move above 70 or below 30 on fast timeframes, then look for divergence with price.
  • Momentum stalls often precede a swift reversal or pullback.

For Swing Traders:

  • Use RSI on the daily chart to spot when a strong uptrend enters “overheated” territory.
  • Look for “RSI bull ranges” (stays between 40 and 80) to validate sustained upward momentum.

For Position Traders:

  • Weekly or monthly RSI filters long-term exhaustion—great for adjusting position size or locking in profits.

Pros and Cons

Pros:

  • Quantifies strength or weakness of trends.
  • Easy to spot divergence—an advanced signal for reversals.

Cons:

  • Can remain overbought/oversold in strong trends—wait for confirmation.
  • Over-reliance can lead to premature exits.

Pro Tip

Combine RSI with trendlines or moving average direction for robust signals.

4. MACD: Trend Momentum with Clarity

The Moving Average Convergence Divergence indicator (MACD) brings together the strengths of two moving averages and a histogram. It’s a go-to for traders wanting to confirm momentum strength or spot new trend signals.

For Day Traders:

  • Use faster settings (e.g., 6,13,5) to speed up MACD responsiveness.
  • Focus on bullish/bearish crossovers for quick in-and-out trades.

For Swing Traders:

  • Standard MACD settings (12,26,9) help identify when a new swing is building.
  • Histogram expansion/reduction signals when momentum is accelerating or fading.

For Position Traders:

  • Use signal line crossovers on longer timeframes for major entry/exit triggers.
  • Combine with higher timeframe chart structure to avoid whipsaws.

Pros and Cons

Pros:

  • Reveals fresh momentum shifts.
  • Works well in trending climates.

Cons:

  • Can lag in sideways markets.
  • Too many signals if filter rules aren’t applied.

Pro Tip

Use MACD divergence as a “heads up” for early trend reversals before price confirms.

5. Price Action and Candlestick Patterns: The Human Touch

Trend following isn’t all about indicators—price action reigns supreme. Candlestick patterns and context clues can be your best trend following allies.

For Day Traders:

  • Track inside/outside bars and pin bars to anticipate breaks in momentum.
  • Engulfing patterns after parabolic moves hint at trend shifts.

For Swing Traders:

  • Match multi-candle patterns with support/resistance zones to confirm swing highs/lows.
  • “Three Bar Reversal” or “Morning/Evening Star” setups highlight key pivot points.

For Position Traders:

  • Monthly or weekly bullish/bearish engulfing bars often flag major moves.
  • Overlapping price action with long-term trends creates high conviction setups.

Pros and Cons

Pros:

  • Gives direct, visual feedback from live buyers and sellers.
  • Works in all markets and timeframes.

Cons:

  • Can be subjective and takes practice.
  • Must be used in context—random patterns in isolation are unreliable.

Pro Tip

Record your favorite patterns and review them to recognize high-probability setups in real time.

6. Ichimoku Cloud: All-in-One Trend System

The Ichimoku Cloud stands out as a comprehensive trend-following tool. It provides insight into direction, support/resistance, and momentum at a glance.

For Day Traders:

  • Use Tenkan-sen and Kijun-sen crossovers for quick trend signals.
  • Price above the cloud? Focus on long trades; below the cloud? Think short.

For Swing Traders:

  • The “Kumo Twist” (cloud changing color) signals potential trend reversals.
  • Chikou Span in agreement with price action reinforces trend strength.

For Position Traders:

  • Lagging span confirmation and cloud thickness help gauge the sustainability of major moves—powerful for staying in a trend.

Pros and Cons

Pros:

  • Multi-faceted, with built-in confirmations.
  • Offers predictive support/resistance.

Cons:

  • Looks complex at first glance—steep learning curve.
  • Works better in strongly trending environments.

Pro Tip

Hide all elements except the cloud itself to focus on support/resistance bias, or add them back in once you’re comfortable.

7. Average Directional Index (ADX): Measuring Trend Strength

The ADX helps you determine if the market is actually trending or just choppy—a huge advantage for picking your battles.

For Day Traders:

  • Monitor ADX spikes above 25 on the 15-minute to 1-hour chart for clean momentum.
  • Stay out when ADX is flat or below 20.

For Swing Traders:

  • Use ADX to “filter” trade setups: only trade momentum when ADX is rising.
  • DI+ and DI- crossings help clarify trend direction.

For Position Traders:

  • Plot ADX on weekly charts to judge whether strong long-term trends are underway.
  • Helps avoid getting chopped up during periods of consolidation.

Pros and Cons

Pros:

  • Defines whether trend following is likely to work.
  • Objectively measures trend intensity.

Cons:

  • Doesn’t give buy/sell signals—must be combined with other methods.
  • Sudden shifts can lag behind rapid reversals.

Pro Tip

Pair ADX with moving averages or price action to build a trend confirmation system.

8. SuperTrend Indicator: Trend Following with Built-In Stops

The SuperTrend plots a dynamic level above or below price, switching color at inflection points. It’s a favorite for traders wanting simplicity and auto-generated stop levels.

For Day Traders:

  • Apply on 5- or 15-minute charts for crisp entry and exit guidelines.
  • Quickly changes course when momentum flips.

For Swing Traders:

  • Use on hourly or daily charts, setting adjustable ATR (Average True Range) multipliers to fit market volatility.
  • Provides clear confirmation of ongoing trends and risk management.

For Position Traders:

  • Weekly SuperTrend signals help professionals ride monster trends and protect capital.
  • Ideal for trailing stops in big moves.

Pros and Cons

Pros:

  • Easy to interpret.
  • Visualizes trends and stop loss points in one tool.

Cons:

  • May whip back and forth in flat markets.
  • Not ideal for choppy conditions.

Pro Tip

Experiment with different ATR multipliers and periods to reduce false signals in sideways markets.

9. Heikin Ashi Candles: Smoother Trend Visuals

Heikin Ashi candles average price over time, reducing noise and highlighting true market momentum. Many traders rely on them as a filtering overlay for clearer direction.

For Day Traders:

  • Helps isolate micro-trends by smoothing choppy setups.
  • Fast color changes signal trend shifts without constant head-fakes.

For Swing Traders:

  • Makes extended moves simple to spot—blocks of the same color indicate confidence.
  • Useful for trailing stops—hold a position until the color flips.

For Position Traders:

  • Filters out random volatility on weekly/monthly charts for long-term clarity.
  • Works beautifully with other trend indicators.

Pros and Cons

Pros:

  • Makes trends visually obvious.
  • Helps prevent premature exits.

Cons:

  • Lags behind raw candlestick price.
  • Must use alongside real price for precise entries/exits.

Pro Tip

Overlay Heikin Ashi on traditional candles—enter on real price, manage the trade using Heikin Ashi bias.

10. Volume Analysis: Tracking Trend Conviction

No trend following toolkit is complete without a read on volume. Trends with little volume tend to fizzle, but those with growing participation can run far.

For All Traders:

  • Watch for volume spikes on breakouts, pullbacks, or key support/resistance tests.
  • Volume confirmation is critical—without it, trends are unlikely to endure.

Volume Tools:

  • On-Balance Volume (OBV): Measures cumulative volume to forecast reversals.
  • Volume Weighted Average Price (VWAP): Used by day traders as a benchmark for intraday entries/exits.
  • Accumulation/Distribution Line: Gauges buying/selling pressure—helping clarify trend strength.

Pros and Cons

Pros:

  • Volume adds conviction to price moves.
  • Many ways to visualize and interpret.

Cons:

  • Can be distorted during low liquidity periods.
  • Noisy on lower timeframes if not filtered.

Pro Tip

Combine volume spikes with a break above/below trendlines or channel boundaries for high-probability entries.

Building Your Ideal Trend Following Toolkit

No single tool will turn you into a world-class trend follower overnight. The real power comes from blending several complementary tools that fit your trading style and asset class. Here’s how to construct an effective toolkit:

  1. Choose a core trend direction indicator.
    Moving averages, Ichimoku, or SuperTrend work well.

  2. Add a momentum and strength filter.
    RSI, MACD, or ADX prevent false starts.

  3. Incorporate price action or pattern recognition.
    Trendlines, candlesticks, or Heikin Ashi for context.

  4. Use volume or volatility overlays.
    Volume analysis, VWAP, or ATR gauges risk and participation.

  5. Test and adjust on your chosen timeframe.
    What works for a five-minute chart may flop on the daily.

  6. Review your trades and learn from hits and misses.
    Journaling supports consistent improvement.

Final Thoughts

Mastering trend following tools is a journey, not a final destination. The right combination unlocks a level of confidence and composure that tilting traders dream about. Whether you’re scalping for pennies, swinging for points, or riding long-term waves, refining your trend following strategy is entirely within reach.

Begin experimenting with these tools, observe which ones enhance your natural decision-making, and gradually construct a playbook that turns trends into profits. The markets will always offer new patterns and surprises—but with your custom-built toolkit, you’ll be ready to spot and run with the next big wave.
