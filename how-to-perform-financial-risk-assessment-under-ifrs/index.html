How to Perform Financial Risk Assessment Under IFRS


Financial risk assessment is a critical component in the management of an organization’s financial health, especially under the International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS). With the increasing complexity of financial markets and regulatory environments, companies must employ robust methods to identify, measure, and manage financial risks effectively. This blog post delves into the intricacies of conducting a comprehensive financial risk assessment under IFRS, exploring the frameworks, methodologies, and best practices for businesses and financial professionals alike.

Understanding Financial Risk in the Context of IFRS

Financial risk encompasses various types of risk that can adversely affect an organization’s financial performance or stability. These typically include credit risk, market risk, liquidity risk, and operational risk, among others. Under IFRS, the focus is largely on risks that impact the measurement and presentation of financial instruments and related disclosures.

The IFRS framework, particularly the standards IFRS 9 (Financial Instruments), IFRS 7 (Financial Instruments: Disclosures), and IFRS 13 (Fair Value Measurement), provide guidance on recognizing, measuring, and disclosing financial risks. These are essential in ensuring transparency and consistency in financial reporting across global markets.

Key Financial Risks to Assess

  1. Credit Risk: The potential that a borrower or counterparty will fail to meet its obligations.
  2. Market Risk: Risks arising from changes in market prices, including interest rate risk, currency risk, and equity price risk.
  3. Liquidity Risk: The danger that a firm may be unable to meet short-term financial demands.
  4. Operational Risk: Losses resulting from inadequate or failed internal processes, systems, or external events.

Step 1: Establish the Risk Context

Before diving into specific risk measurements, it’s crucial to establish the context of the financial risk assessment.

  • Identify Business Environment and Objectives: Understand the nature of the entity’s operations, objectives, and the external environment affecting it.
  • Determine Stakeholders’ Requirements: Internal and external stakeholders, such as management, investors, and regulators, have different expectations regarding risk information.
  • Define Scope: Specify which financial instruments and transactions will be included in the assessment.

This initial step ensures the financial risk assessment is aligned with the strategic objectives of the business and is mindful of relevant regulatory requirements.

Step 2: Data Collection and Analysis

A thorough financial risk assessment relies on accurate and comprehensive data. This includes:

  • Historical financial data and transactional records.
  • Market data related to interest rates, foreign exchange rates, equity prices, and commodity prices.
  • Credit ratings and default statistics.
  • Liquidity profiles of assets and liabilities.

Analyzing this data helps in understanding past risk patterns and predicting potential future exposures.

Step 3: Risk Identification and Classification

Under IFRS, it’s vital to classify financial instruments correctly, as this classification influences how risks are recognized and measured. Instruments typically fall into categories such as:

  • Amortized Cost: Measured at original cost adjusted for principal repayments and amortization.
  • Fair Value Through Profit or Loss (FVTPL): Measured at fair value with changes recognized immediately in profit or loss.
  • Fair Value Through Other Comprehensive Income (FVOCI): Changes in fair value are recognized in other comprehensive income.

Understanding the risk implications of each category is essential. For example, instruments measured at FVTPL exhibit higher sensitivity to market risk compared to those at amortized cost.

Step 4: Measurement of Financial Risks

Meeting IFRS requirements entails employing appropriate measurement techniques for each type of financial risk.

Credit Risk Measurement

IFRS 9 introduces the Expected Credit Loss (ECL) model, which replaced the incurred loss model, requiring entities to recognize lifetime expected credit losses on financial assets. The process includes:

  • Segmentation of financial assets based on credit risk characteristics.
  • Assessment of significant increases in credit risk since initial recognition.
  • Estimation of probability of default (PD), loss given default (LGD), and exposure at default (EAD).

The incorporation of forward-looking information in the ECL model emphasizes the need for robust credit risk modeling and stress testing.

Market Risk Measurement

Market risk measurement under IFRS focuses on fair value determination and sensitivity analysis. The most common approaches include:

  • Value at Risk (VaR): Statistically estimates potential losses under normal market conditions over a given time frame.
  • Stress Testing and Scenario Analysis: Evaluates the impact of extreme but plausible market movements on the portfolio.
  • Sensitivity analysis on key risk factors such as interest rates and foreign exchange rates.

Liquidity Risk Measurement

While IFRS does not prescribe a specific model for liquidity risk measurement, disclosures require entities to evaluate the timing and uncertainty of cash flows.

  • Liquidity gap analysis: Assesses mismatches in maturities of assets and liabilities.
  • Cash flow projections: Forecast future cash inflows and outflows considering market conditions.

Operational Risk Measurement

Operational risks are less quantifiable under IFRS but are often addressed through:

  • Internal controls evaluation.
  • Loss event data collection.
  • Scenario analysis.

Although IFRS places less emphasis on operational risk from a measurement standpoint, well-documented processes help in mitigating potential financial misstatements.

Step 5: Applying Fair Value Measurement Techniques

IFRS 13 establishes a framework for fair value measurement, which is frequently utilized in financial risk assessment to measure financial instruments at fair value.

  • Market Approach: Uses prices and relevant information generated by market transactions.
  • Income Approach: Converts future amounts into a single current amount, such as discounted cash flow techniques.
  • Cost Approach: Reflects the amount that would be required to replace the service capacity of an asset.

Selecting the appropriate approach depends on instrument characteristics and market availability. Proper application of these techniques enhances the accuracy of risk modeling.

Step 6: Risk Mitigation Strategies

Identifying and measuring risks are only part of the process; effective mitigation strategies must follow.

  • Hedging Activities: IFRS 9 specifies hedge accounting principles that enable entities to align hedge instruments and hedged items in financial statements.
  • Diversification: Reduces concentration risk by spreading exposures across counterparties and markets.
  • Credit Enhancements: Such as collateral, guarantees, and netting agreements.
  • Liquidity Buffers: Maintaining readily available liquid assets.

Implementing these strategies helps organizations control risk exposures and comply with IFRS reporting.

Step 7: Disclosure and Reporting

Transparency in financial risk assessment is underscored through extensive disclosures required by IFRS standards, particularly IFRS 7.

Entities must provide detailed information on:

  • Nature and extent of risks from financial instruments.
  • Risk management objectives and policies.
  • Quantitative data such as credit exposure, aging analysis, and sensitivity analyses.
  • Information about the fair value hierarchy used for financial instruments.

Clear, comprehensive reporting not only meets regulatory needs but also supports investor confidence.

Common Challenges in Performing Financial Risk Assessment Under IFRS

  • Complexity of Models: Estimating ECL and fair value can be technically demanding.
  • Data Quality: Inaccurate or insufficient data can undermine assessment reliability.
  • Changing Regulations: Ongoing amendments to IFRS standards require constant updates to risk frameworks.
  • Integration with Risk Management Systems: Aligning accounting risk assessments with enterprise-wide risk management can be challenging.

Addressing these challenges requires cross-functional collaboration, investment in technology, and ongoing training.

Best Practices for Effective Financial Risk Assessment

  • Early and Continuous Assessment: Integrate risk assessment into financial reporting cycles.
  • Use of Technology: Leverage data analytics, AI, and automated systems to enhance accuracy.
  • Comprehensive Documentation: Maintain detailed records of assumptions, models, and methodologies.
  • Regular Training and Updates: Ensure that accounting and risk teams are proficient in the latest IFRS requirements.
  • Engage External Experts: Utilize auditors and consultants for independent validation.

Conclusion

Performing a financial risk assessment under IFRS involves a systematic approach to identifying, measuring, and managing financial risks in accordance with international accounting standards. It requires understanding the complexity of financial instruments, mastering sophisticated risk measurement models, and ensuring transparent reporting. By following a structured process from establishing context to executing disclosures, and by overcoming common challenges with best practices, organizations can safeguard their financial integrity and improve stakeholder trust.

Staying current with IFRS updates and continuously refining risk assessment methodologies will keep businesses agile in navigating the volatility of global financial markets. Financial risk assessment is not merely a compliance activity but a strategic tool that informs decision-making and fosters resilience in an ever-changing economic landscape.
