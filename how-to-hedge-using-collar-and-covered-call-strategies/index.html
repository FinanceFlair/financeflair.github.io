How to Hedge Using Collar and Covered Call Strategies


Investing in the stock market comes with risks, and the ability to manage those risks can make all the difference between a successful portfolio and a loss-heavy one. Among the various strategies used to hedge against downside risk, two strategies stand out: the collar strategy and the covered call strategy. Both are options-based strategies designed to help investors limit their losses, generate income, and protect their portfolios. While they share similarities, each strategy has distinct characteristics that cater to different risk profiles.

In this comprehensive guide, we will walk through how both strategies work, their benefits, and how you can use them to hedge your portfolio effectively.

What is a Collar Strategy?

A collar strategy involves holding a stock while simultaneously buying a protective put option and selling a call option. This strategy is used to protect against downside risk, but at the cost of limiting potential upside. The primary goal of the collar is to establish a range of acceptable loss and gain, providing peace of mind for investors during periods of volatility.

Here’s how a collar works step-by-step:

  1. Buy the underlying stock: The investor owns the stock and is concerned about its potential downside risk.
  2. Buy a protective put option: The investor buys a put option for the stock. This gives them the right to sell the stock at a predetermined price (strike price) within a specific time frame. The protective put offers downside protection if the stock price falls below the strike price.
  3. Sell a covered call option: The investor sells a call option on the stock they own. This obligates them to sell the stock at a predetermined price (strike price) if the stock price rises above that level.

By combining these three elements, the investor creates a “collar” around their stock, which limits both downside and upside potential. The protective put provides the downside protection, while the covered call generates income, which can offset the cost of the put option.

Example of a Collar Strategy

Let’s say you own 100 shares of a stock currently priced at $50 per share. You are worried about potential downside risk but want to limit the cost of hedging.

  1. Buy a protective put: You purchase a put option with a strike price of $45 for $2 per share. This ensures that if the stock falls below $45, you can sell it at $45 per share, limiting your losses.
  2. Sell a covered call: You sell a call option with a strike price of $55 for $3 per share. If the stock price rises above $55, you will have to sell the stock at $55, but you will also keep the premium from selling the call option.

In this case, the premium received from selling the call option ($3) offsets the cost of buying the put option ($2), creating a net cost of $1 for the collar strategy. If the stock price remains between $45 and $55, you will retain the stock and keep the premium. If the stock falls below $45, your loss is limited to $45 minus the initial stock price, while if it rises above $55, your upside is capped at $55, but you still make a profit from the premium received.

What is a Covered Call Strategy?

A covered call strategy is one of the most straightforward options strategies and involves holding a long position in a stock while selling call options on that same stock. This strategy is typically used by investors who expect the stock to either remain flat or increase slightly. The covered call is a popular strategy for generating income through option premiums, especially in a sideways or slightly bullish market.

Here’s how the covered call strategy works:

  1. Own the stock: The investor already owns the stock they want to hedge or generate income from.
  2. Sell a call option: The investor sells a call option on the stock they own. This obligates them to sell the stock at a specified strike price if the stock price rises above that level. In exchange for this obligation, the investor receives a premium.

Selling the call option generates immediate income from the option premium, which can be kept regardless of how the stock price moves. However, if the stock price rises above the strike price, the investor must sell the stock at that price, potentially capping their upside gains.

Example of a Covered Call Strategy

Suppose you own 100 shares of stock priced at $50 per share. You want to generate some additional income from the stock but are willing to cap your potential upside if the price rises.

  1. Own the stock: You already own the 100 shares at $50 each.
  2. Sell a call option: You sell a call option with a strike price of $55 for $2 per share.

In this case, you have received $200 ($2 per share × 100 shares) for selling the call option. If the stock price remains below $55, you keep the stock and the premium. If the stock rises above $55, you will have to sell the stock at $55, but you will still keep the $200 premium, which provides some income on top of the capital gains from selling the stock at $55.

Comparing the Collar and Covered Call Strategies

While both strategies use options to generate income and limit risk, they have different goals and structures. Below are the key differences between a collar and a covered call strategy:

1. Risk and Reward Profile

  • Collar Strategy: The collar strategy is designed to limit both downside and upside. The protective put provides downside protection, ensuring that the stock price does not fall below the put strike price. However, the upside is capped by the sold call option. As a result, the collar strategy is ideal for risk-averse investors who want to protect their stock holdings from significant losses.

  • Covered Call Strategy: The covered call strategy, on the other hand, limits only the upside potential. The investor can still benefit from price appreciation up to the strike price of the call option. If the stock rises above the call’s strike price, the stock will be called away, and the investor will have to sell the stock. However, the premium from the sold call option provides additional income, even if the stock price does not move dramatically.

2. Income Generation

  • Collar Strategy: In a collar, the premium received from selling the call option helps offset the cost of the put option. This strategy generates some income but is primarily focused on protecting against downside risk.

  • Covered Call Strategy: The primary purpose of the covered call strategy is to generate income. The call premium is the income source, and the investor gets to keep this premium regardless of how the stock price moves (as long as it doesn’t exceed the strike price of the call option).

3. Downside Protection

  • Collar Strategy: The collar strategy provides a strong downside protection due to the purchased put option. If the stock price drops significantly, the protective put guarantees that the investor can sell the stock at the put’s strike price.

  • Covered Call Strategy: The covered call strategy provides no direct downside protection. If the stock price falls, the investor still bears the full loss. The only cushion comes from the income generated by selling the call option.

4. Capital Requirement

  • Collar Strategy: The collar strategy requires purchasing both a protective put and selling a call option. While the cost of the put is often offset by the premium from the call, there can still be a net cost involved in setting up the collar.

  • Covered Call Strategy: The covered call strategy is simpler and requires less capital because it only involves owning the stock and selling a call option. However, the downside risk is not mitigated in the same way as with a collar strategy.

When to Use the Collar and Covered Call Strategies

The choice between a collar and a covered call largely depends on your investment objectives and risk tolerance.

  • Use a Collar Strategy: If you are looking for strong downside protection and are willing to cap your potential upside, the collar strategy is an excellent choice. It is best suited for investors who want to protect a significant portion of their stock holdings during volatile market conditions, especially when they are concerned about potential large declines.

  • Use a Covered Call Strategy: If you are looking to generate income and are willing to accept limited upside potential, the covered call strategy may be a better fit. It is ideal for investors who believe their stock will not rise significantly above the call’s strike price, allowing them to generate income from the call premiums while still holding the stock.

Conclusion

Both the collar and covered call strategies are effective tools for hedging, each with its unique advantages and trade-offs. The collar offers more robust downside protection but limits both gains and losses, while the covered call allows investors to generate income from their stock holdings but does not provide downside protection.

By understanding the mechanics and nuances of these strategies, investors can make informed decisions on how best to hedge their portfolios in a way that aligns with their financial goals and risk tolerance. Whether you’re a conservative investor looking to protect against large losses or a more aggressive investor seeking to generate income from your holdings, these strategies can be valuable tools in your options toolbox.
