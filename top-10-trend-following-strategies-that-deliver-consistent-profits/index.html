Top 10 Trend Following Strategies That Deliver Consistent Profits


In the world of trading, the allure of trend following lies in its simplicity and effectiveness. Unlike contrarian strategies that bet against prevailing market moves, trend following seeks to profit by riding sustained market momentum. This approach has been embraced by both retail traders and institutional investors due to its ability to cut through market noise and capture substantial gains over time.

But what exactly makes a trend following strategy successful? How can traders implement these methods to achieve consistent profitability? This comprehensive guide explores the top 10 trend following strategies driving consistent profits for traders worldwide. Each strategy blends technical analysis, risk management, and prudent timing to harness the powerful movements markets often generate.

1. Moving Average Crossovers

One of the most popular and straightforward trend following methods involves moving averages (MAs). Traders use two MAs—a short-term and a long-term—to identify the direction and strength of a trend.

  • How it works: When the short-term moving average crosses above the long-term moving average (a “golden cross”), it signals a potential bullish trend. Conversely, when the short-term MA crosses below the long-term MA (a “death cross”), it suggests a bearish trend.

  • Execution: Popular pairs include the 50-day and 200-day moving averages or a faster 20-day with a slower 50-day. Entry and exit points are triggered by the crossover events.

  • Why it works: These crossovers help filter out minor price fluctuations and confirm that a genuine trend may be forming. They work well across various asset classes, including stocks, commodities, and forex.

  • Risk management tip: Use stop-losses just below the most recent swing low (in an uptrend) or swing high (in a downtrend) to protect against false signals.

2. Donchian Channel Breakouts

Named after trader Richard Donchian, the Donchian Channel is a trend following tool that identifies breakouts from price ranges established over a fixed period.

  • How it works: The Donchian Channel plots the highest high and the lowest low over the last N periods, often 20. A buy signal triggers when the price breaks above the upper band; a sell signal when it breaks below the lower band.

  • Execution: This strategy thrives on momentum beyond consolidation zones. Traders typically enter on these breakout levels and ride the subsequent trend.

  • Why it works: Breakouts out of well-defined ranges often mark the start of strong trends as buying or selling pressure accumulates.

  • Risk management tip: To avoid false breakouts, consider filtering signals through volume confirmation or waiting for a candle close beyond the channel boundaries.

3. Average Directional Index (ADX) with Trend Confirmation

While many indicators help spot trends, the Average Directional Index (ADX) quantifies trend strength rather than direction.

  • How it works: When ADX rises above 25 (on a scale of 0-100), it suggests a strong trend. The Directional Movement Index (DMI) components +DI and -DI give directional context.

  • Execution: Trend followers buy when ADX > 25 and +DI crosses above -DI, and sell or short when ADX > 25 with -DI crossing above +DI.

  • Why it works: Using ADX helps avoid trading in choppy or sideways markets, focusing only on conditions with strong, sustained directional moves.

  • Risk management tip: Pair ADX signals with price action or moving average trends for additional confirmation and timely exits.

4. Turtle Trading System

Developed in the 1980s by Richard Dennis and William Eckhardt, the Turtle Trading System is a seminal trend following strategy that revolutionized systematic trading.

  • How it works: Turtles used two breakout levels—20-day breakouts for entries and 10-day breakouts for exits. Positions were scaled based on volatility-adjusted unit sizes (using Average True Range).

  • Execution: Buy when price closes above the 20-day high and add units over time if the trend continues. Exit when the price drops below the 10-day low.

  • Why it works: The system captures major trends with built-in risk controls and scaling, increasing exposure only in confirmed trending moves.

  • Risk management tip: Position sizing is key. Using volatility-based sizing limits risk during market drawdowns.

5. Ichimoku Cloud Trend Trading

The Ichimoku Kinko Hyo is a comprehensive indicator providing trend, momentum, and support/resistance information in a single glance.

  • How it works: The cloud (Kumo), formed by two lines (Senkou Span A and B), visually displays trend direction and strength. Price above the cloud indicates an uptrend; below it signals a downtrend.

  • Execution: Traders initiate long positions when price breaks above the cloud with supporting signals like Tenkan Sen crossing above Kijun Sen. Shorts occur on breaks below the cloud.

  • Why it works: Ichimoku offers layered confirmation, reducing noise and providing dynamic stop levels within the cloud.

  • Risk management tip: Use the cloud boundaries as trailing stops to lock in profits while allowing trends to run.

6. Relative Strength Index (RSI) Trend Following

Though RSI is often used for overbought/oversold signals, it also has strong trend following applications.

  • How it works: In a strong uptrend, RSI tends to stay above 40-50, and dips to these levels offer buying opportunities. Conversely, in downtrends, RSI often remains below 50-60, and rallies to these ranges provide selling points.

  • Execution: Combine RSI trend zone identification with moving averages for entries and exits aligning with the dominant market direction.

  • Why it works: RSI captures momentum shifts within trends and helps traders avoid premature countertrend trades.

  • Risk management tip: Avoid trading RSI extremes contrarily in strongly trending markets to reduce whipsaws.

7. Bollinger Band Trend Riding

Bollinger Bands adjust with volatility and help identify potential breakouts and trend continuations.

  • How it works: Price moving along the upper band signals a strong uptrend; hugging the lower band indicates a strong downtrend.

  • Execution: Trend followers buy when price breaks above the middle band (20-period moving average) with momentum and ride the upper band; sell or short with similar logic on the lower band.

  • Why it works: Bands expand with volatility, helping traders gauge the strength and sustainability of trends with dynamic support/resistance.

  • Risk management tip: Use band squeezes as caution signals since low volatility often precedes reversals.

8. Parabolic SAR (Stop and Reverse)

The Parabolic SAR provides dynamic stop and reversal points to aid trend following trades.

  • How it works: Dots plotted below price indicate bullish momentum; dots above indicate bearish momentum. A flip signifies a possible trend reversal.

  • Execution: Enter long trades when the indicator switches below price, and exit or short when it flips above.

  • Why it works: Parabolic SAR adapts quickly during trending periods and then signals exits before prolonged exhaustion.

  • Risk management tip: Adjust acceleration settings to avoid frequent false signals in choppy markets.

9. Volume Weighted Average Price (VWAP) Pullback Strategy

VWAP represents the average price weighted by volume and serves as a dynamic support/resistance indicator during trending sessions.

  • How it works: In strong intraday trends, price often pulls back to the VWAP before resuming the trend.

  • Execution: Trend followers buy on pullbacks to the VWAP in an uptrend and sell on rallies to VWAP in a downtrend.

  • Why it works: VWAP aligns with institutional buying/selling flows, aiding better entry timing within a trend.

  • Risk management tip: Confirm pullback strength with volume spikes or price action patterns before entering.

10. Breakout and Retest Strategy

This modern trend following approach focuses on price breaking critical levels and then retesting them for confirmation.

  • How it works: When price breaks above a resistance trendline, it often returns to retest that level as support.

  • Execution: Traders enter on successful retests, capitalizing on trend continuation with tighter stops below the retest zone.

  • Why it works: Retests filter many false breakouts by confirming demand at previous resistance levels now turned support.

  • Risk management tip: Set stops decisively below the retest low to minimize losses on failed breaks.

Final Thoughts

Consistent profitability in trend following revolves around disciplined strategy execution, robust risk management, and adaptive responses to market conditions. The strategies outlined above offer a diverse toolkit encompassing simple moving averages, volatility-based filters, momentum indicators, and breakout confirmations.

Successful traders often customize these frameworks based on their risk tolerance, preferred markets, and timeframes, combining multiple techniques to craft a synergistic approach. Experimenting with demo accounts and backtesting can help hone these trend following strategies before committing capital.

Above all, patience is paramount. Trends may take time to evolve, but when caught effectively, they provide ample opportunities for substantial and steady gains. Whether you are a novice or an experienced trader, leveraging these top 10 trend following strategies can enhance your market edge and increase your odds of consistent profits.
