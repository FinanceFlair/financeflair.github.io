Short Term Trading: Using the Stochastic Oscillator for Analysis


The Stochastic Oscillator is an efficient trading tool, widely used in short-term trading. It was developed by William W. Welch in the 1950s to compare the closing price of a stock to its price range over a specific period. This indicator helps traders in quickly determining overbought or oversold levels of a particular stock or security.

At its core, the Stochastic Oscillator is straightforward and easy to understand. It is basically a momentum indicator that shows the location of the closing price in relation to the range of prices over a specified period. The default setting for this indicator is 14 periods. Here is the formula for this captivating tool:

%K = 100 · (C – L14) / (H14 – L14)

Where C = the current closing price, L14 = the lowest low in the look-back period, and H14 = the highest high within the same period.

On top of that, Stochastic Oscillator comes with two lines, namely %K and %D, which offer a more comprehensive assessment of market momentum. The second line, %D, is a three-period moving average of %K:

%D = 3-period MA of %K

Here’s how you can use the Stochastic Oscillator effectively in your trading analysis:

Diving into the Stochastic Oscillator Levels

The Stochastic Oscillator has three main levels: the Slowdown Level, the Fast Kalma up, and the Fast Kalma Down. These levels are crucial for understanding the market trends. When the stochastic oscillator lines rise over 80, it is considered overbought and a signal to sell. When they fall below 20, it is considered oversold, signaling a potential buying opportunity.

Trading Signals based on Crossovers

The primary trading signal comes from the crossover of the slow %K line (5 periods) by the fast %K line (3 periods). A crossover from top to bottom implies a bearish market, indicating selling pressure. Conversely, an upward crossover suggests a bullish market and potential buying pressure.

Furthermore, when the fast %K line moves above or below the slow %K line, we get a credo called ’the dyeing crab.’ This moment triggers buying or selling pressure in the market of low volume reactors, denoting future price movements.

Understanding the Generic Nature of the Stochastic Oscillator

Based on our study of the Stochastic Oscillator, volatility is merely “noise” that technicians often overlook. Instead of trying to trade on every tick, focus on medium to long-term trends. Identifying breaks in support or resistance levels, crossovers, or failed attempts to continue will lead to fruitful trading opportunities.

The key takeaway from using the Stochastic Oscillator is to identify those trades with maximum probability of success while reducing errors and risks. For most novice traders, understanding trading psychology plays a crucial role in algorithmic trading too.

In conclusion, the Stochastic Oscillator allows for thorough analysis of an asset’s overbought or oversold conditions. However, it essential to integrate it with other technical indicators such as RSI, MACD, Bollinger Bands, and candlestick patterns to create a defined and effective trading strategy.

By validating signals with price action, candlestick patterns, or other indicators, you maximize your probability of success while trading short-term markets. Furthermore, you develop a sense of emotional resilience while navigating market noise, resulting in better, clearer, and disagreement decisions with possible future impacts. Regularly analyzing and interpreting these oscillations can be your key to making informed, educated trades.
