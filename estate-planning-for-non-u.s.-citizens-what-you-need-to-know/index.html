Estate Planning for Non-U.S. Citizens: What You Need to Know


Estate planning is an essential aspect of managing your wealth and securing your legacy, no matter where you are in the world. For non-U.S. citizens, however, estate planning in the United States can be a complex process with a variety of legal considerations that vary significantly from those in their home countries. Navigating U.S. laws as a non-citizen requires careful planning and a comprehensive understanding of both U.S. and international legal frameworks.

Whether you live outside the U.S., own property in the U.S., or have family members who are U.S. residents or citizens, it is crucial to take steps to ensure that your estate is handled according to your wishes. This guide will explore the key aspects of estate planning for non-U.S. citizens, covering topics such as inheritance laws, tax implications, asset protection, and how to make the best decisions for your situation.

Understanding Estate Planning for Non-U.S. Citizens

Estate planning is the process of arranging for the management and distribution of your assets after your death. In the U.S., the process involves creating legal documents such as a will, trusts, and powers of attorney. These documents ensure that your assets are distributed according to your preferences and can help avoid lengthy and costly probate procedures.

However, estate planning for non-U.S. citizens is complicated by several factors. U.S. estate and gift tax laws differ from those in other countries, and non-citizens may face higher tax rates and additional regulations when passing on assets. Furthermore, the treatment of foreign assets, such as real estate and bank accounts in other countries, requires specialized planning to avoid legal and tax pitfalls.

Key Considerations for Non-U.S. Citizens in Estate Planning

1. U.S. Estate and Gift Tax Rules

One of the most significant differences in estate planning for non-U.S. citizens is the application of U.S. estate and gift taxes. The U.S. imposes taxes on the transfer of property, whether by gift or inheritance, and the rules can be complex for non-citizens.

Estate Tax Exemption for Non-Citizens

Non-U.S. citizens are subject to U.S. estate tax on the value of their U.S. assets, such as real estate, business interests, and financial accounts located in the U.S. The estate tax exemption for non-U.S. citizens is significantly lower than that for U.S. citizens and residents. In 2025, the exemption amount for non-citizens is just $60,000, compared to the $12.92 million exemption available to U.S. citizens. This means that if your U.S.-based estate exceeds this threshold, it could be subject to estate taxes, which can be as high as 40%.

It is important to note that non-citizens are not eligible for the “marital deduction” that allows U.S. citizens to pass assets to their spouses without paying estate tax. This means that if a non-U.S. citizen leaves property to a U.S. citizen spouse, the spouse may still be subject to estate taxes.

Gift Tax for Non-Citizens

Non-U.S. citizens are also subject to U.S. gift taxes on the transfer of U.S. property during their lifetime. While U.S. citizens can make gifts of up to $17,000 per recipient (as of 2025) without triggering gift tax, non-citizens have a lower exemption amount of $15,000 per recipient. Gifts above this threshold may be subject to U.S. gift tax, which ranges from 18% to 40%.

2. The Role of U.S. Property

When it comes to estate planning for non-U.S. citizens, the treatment of U.S. property is a critical factor. U.S. estate taxes only apply to assets located within the United States, so if you own property in the U.S., such as real estate or financial accounts, these assets will be subject to U.S. estate tax.

For example, if you own a home in Florida, that property would be subject to U.S. estate tax upon your death, even if you are not a U.S. citizen. To mitigate potential estate tax liability, many non-citizens choose to structure their property ownership in a way that minimizes exposure to U.S. taxes.

3. International Estate Planning Strategies

Given the complexities of U.S. estate tax laws, international estate planning becomes crucial for non-U.S. citizens who own U.S. assets or have family members residing in the U.S. A key strategy is to establish a trust, which can offer benefits such as avoiding probate and reducing estate tax liability.

Setting Up a Trust

A trust is a legal arrangement in which a trustee holds and manages assets on behalf of a beneficiary. Non-U.S. citizens can create a trust to hold U.S. assets, which may help to avoid estate taxes. However, the effectiveness of a trust depends on how it is structured, so it is important to work with an estate planning attorney experienced in U.S. tax law to ensure that the trust meets your objectives.

4. Treaties and Estate Planning

The United States has estate tax treaties with several countries, which can provide relief from double taxation. These treaties may allow for a higher estate tax exemption or more favorable treatment of U.S. assets. If you are a non-U.S. citizen and reside in a country that has an estate tax treaty with the U.S., it is important to understand how the treaty may apply to your estate.

Working with a cross-border estate planning professional can help you navigate the complexities of these treaties and ensure that you are taking full advantage of any available benefits.

5. Planning for Foreign Assets

If you are a non-U.S. citizen with assets located outside the United States, these assets will generally not be subject to U.S. estate tax. However, foreign assets can be subject to estate taxes in the country where they are located. For example, if you own real estate in your home country, it will be subject to the estate laws of that country upon your death.

It is crucial to have a comprehensive estate plan that addresses both U.S. and foreign assets. This can include drafting separate wills for each country in which you hold assets and considering how your estate plan may be impacted by different inheritance laws.

Steps for Effective Estate Planning as a Non-U.S. Citizen

1. Consult with Professionals

Estate planning for non-U.S. citizens requires specialized knowledge of both U.S. and international laws. It is crucial to consult with professionals, including estate planning attorneys, tax advisors, and financial planners, who are well-versed in cross-border estate planning. They can help you understand your options and create a plan that minimizes tax liabilities and ensures your wishes are honored.

2. Create a Will and Trust

A well-crafted will and trust are fundamental to any estate plan. As a non-U.S. citizen, it is important to ensure that your will reflects your wishes for the distribution of U.S. and foreign assets. Additionally, a trust can be an effective tool for managing U.S. assets and reducing estate taxes.

3. Consider Life Insurance

Life insurance can be a valuable tool in estate planning, especially for non-U.S. citizens. Life insurance proceeds are generally not subject to U.S. estate tax, and they can provide liquidity to cover estate taxes and other expenses. Depending on your situation, purchasing life insurance may help offset potential tax liabilities and provide peace of mind for your beneficiaries.

4. Review and Update Your Plan Regularly

Estate planning is not a one-time event. It is important to review and update your plan regularly to account for changes in your assets, family circumstances, and applicable laws. If you move between countries or acquire new assets, you may need to adjust your estate plan to ensure it remains effective.

Conclusion

Estate planning for non-U.S. citizens involves navigating complex tax laws and legal frameworks to ensure your assets are distributed according to your wishes. By understanding U.S. estate tax rules, considering international estate planning strategies, and working with experienced professionals, you can create a plan that protects your assets and minimizes your tax liabilities.

Taking the time to develop a thoughtful estate plan is an investment in your legacy. Regardless of where you live or where your assets are located, a well-executed estate plan provides clarity and security for you and your loved ones.
