How to Value Intangible Assets in Corporate Valuation


Intangible assets form an essential part of a company’s balance sheet, representing a vital component of its overall value. Intellectual properties, patents, trademarks, copyrights, trade secrets, and other abstract assets contribute significantly to a company’s profitability, making it crucial for investors and business owners to determine their value accurately. Here’s a step-by-step guide to value intangible assets effectively:

Understanding the Types of Intangible Assets

Identifying the types of intangible assets and their value is paramount. Intellectual properties, trademarks, patents, and copyrights, while different in scope and functionality, are all valuable in their own right. Recognizing their value requires understanding the unique features that make them worthwhile investments.

  1. Intellectual Properties: Intellectual properties include trademarks, copyrights, and patents. These assets contribute immensely to a company’s long-term profitability by ensuring the company’s relevance and competitiveness are maintained in the market.

  2. Trademarks: Trademarks are distinctive logos, symbols, or names that represent a business. These are creative assets that, if maintained effectively, have long-term value and relevance in the market.

  3. Patents: Patents represent creative and inventive innovations that have a practical and applied significance. These assets carry immense value, particularly for companies in sectors with high levels of innovation.

  4. Copyrights: Copyrights refer to the ownership and control of original works (including books, music, and films). These assets have value since they protect the original creator’s rights and intellectual property.

  5. Trade Secrets: Trade secrets are unique processes, formulae, or knowledge that a company keeps confidential and protects. They possess significant value and are crucial to a company’s competitive advantage.

Evaluating the Value of Intangible Assets

Determining the value of intangible assets involves several steps. Ensure to follow this process to maximize the accuracy of your evaluation.

  1. Determine the Asset Category: Understanding the specific type of intangible asset(s) is your first step in evaluating its value. By identifying its category (IP, trademark, patent, copyright, or trade secret), you can begin to apply an appropriate valuation method tailored to that asset.

  2. Utilize an Appropriate Valuation Method: Different valuation procedures apply to different intangible assets. Royalty-based approaches and the relief-from-royalty method are popular for patents, while the multi-period excess earnings approach applies for trademarks.

  3. Analyze Market Conditions: Analyze the current market conditions when appraising intangible assets. Factors, such as industry standards, economic trends, and competitive pressures, should also be taken into account.

  4. Consider Protection Periods: Intangible assets are protected by patents, trademarks, or copyrights, depending on their type. These protection periods contribute significantly to their overall value. Ensure to consider these legal protection periods in your valuation.

  5. Factor in Growth Potentials: Intangible assets often represent long-term, sustainable growth opportunities for businesses. As such, their value is heavily influenced by the growth potentials they provide. This should also be factored into the evaluation process.

  6. Summarize the Value: Finally, aggregate the results of your evaluation. Combining the value of each intangible asset provides a comprehensive summary of your company’s intangible assets’ value.

Understanding how to value intangible assets is crucial for business owners, investors, and stakeholders alike. By recognizing the value of these assets, companies can make informed financial decisions that maximize profitability and build long-term competitiveness. Next time your business is undergoing valuation, consider the value of your intangible assets holistically to unlock their full potential.
