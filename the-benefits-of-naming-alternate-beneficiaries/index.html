The Benefits of Naming Alternate Beneficiaries


Naming alternate beneficiaries is a crucial step in estate planning that many people overlook. When we create a will or update our beneficiary designations, we often focus on naming our loved ones as primary beneficiaries. While this is understandable, it is essential to consider the potential consequences of their passing before we do. In the event that our primary beneficiary no longer passes on after we do, our estate may be subject to estate taxes, probate, or even being passed down to individuals that we may not support or trust. Naming alternate beneficiaries can help prevent these situations and ensure that our assets are distributed as we intend. Why Naming Alternate Beneficiaries Matters Having an alternate beneficiary plan in place can make a significant difference in the lives of our loved ones after we pass away. For instance, if our primary beneficiary dies before us, our estate may be distributed according to the state's intestacy laws. These laws often determine who inherits our property based on a predetermined formula, which might not align with our wishes. In some cases, our estate may even be distributed to distant relatives or others we may not have considered as beneficiaries. Nomination forms typically allow us to designate and update beneficiaries to account for various scenarios, reducing the risk of uninformed distributions. Some people may be exempt from this requirement but name an alternate beneficiary if they do. When setting up a trust fund or resolving debts left behind from a deceased loved one, naming a backup to our primary beneficiary turns out to be beneficial. Who Should Be Named Alternate Beneficiaries? The types of individuals or entities we name as alternate beneficiaries will largely depend on our personal circumstances and preferences. For example:

Children or spouses: If we have children or a spouse, they are often our top priority as designated beneficiaries. However, if our primary beneficiary is under a certain age or incapable of managing their inheritance, it may be beneficial to name an alternate beneficiary. Trust or family foundation: We may also consider naming a trust or family foundation as an alternate beneficiary to manage our estate in a more tax-efficient way or to take advantage of charitable giving opportunities. You or being solely responsible for their duty: Another option is to assign a person to act as an alternate executor for this duty and take over the administrations once you can no longer provide the responsible care and support it requires.

How to Name Alternate Beneficiaries To name alternate beneficiaries, we need to update our nomination forms or beneficiary designations. This process typically involves the following steps:

Accessing our nomination forms: We can find our nomination forms by reviewing our financial documents, such as insurance policies, retirement accounts, or trust agreements. Updating our primary beneficiary information: We need to update our primary beneficiary information, ensuring that we name the person or entity we wish to inherit our assets.
