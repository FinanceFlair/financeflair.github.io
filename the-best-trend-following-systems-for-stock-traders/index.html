The Best Trend Following Systems for Stock Traders


Trend following is one of the most popular and effective strategies for stock traders who wish to capitalize on market movements. It involves identifying and following the prevailing trend in the market, whether it’s an uptrend or a downtrend. By doing so, traders can avoid being caught in choppy or sideways markets and instead profit from sustained market moves.

But how does a trader effectively follow trends, and what are the best systems to implement this strategy? This article will explore some of the best trend-following systems available to stock traders and why these systems are valuable tools for navigating the stock market.

What Is Trend Following?

Before diving into the best trend-following systems, let’s first understand the concept of trend following itself.

Trend following is a method of investing or trading that aims to capture profit by taking advantage of sustained market moves. Traders who follow trends do not predict market movements based on fundamentals or news but instead react to the price action of stocks. The core idea is that once a trend is established, it is more likely to continue than reverse. This can be true in both upward and downward markets.

The primary goal of a trend-following system is to enter the market when a trend is just beginning and exit when it starts to lose momentum. This way, traders can lock in profits and limit losses.

Key Characteristics of Trend Following Systems

A good trend-following system has several key characteristics that make it effective:

  1. Clear Entry and Exit Signals: The system provides concrete rules that indicate when to enter or exit a trade, making it easy for traders to act without second-guessing.
  2. Risk Management: A successful trend-following system includes mechanisms to manage risk, ensuring that traders can limit their exposure in case the market turns against them.
  3. Adaptability: The best trend-following systems are adaptable to different market conditions, ensuring that they can perform well in various economic environments.
  4. Consistency: A proven trend-following system delivers consistent results over time, helping traders achieve long-term profitability.

Popular Trend Following Systems

There are many trend-following systems that traders can use, ranging from simple moving average strategies to more complex algorithms. Below are some of the most effective trend-following systems used by successful stock traders:

1. Moving Averages Crossovers

One of the most widely used trend-following strategies is the moving average crossover. Moving averages are indicators that smooth out price data over a specified period of time. Traders use moving averages to identify the direction of a trend.

A moving average crossover occurs when a shorter-term moving average crosses above or below a longer-term moving average. When the shorter-term moving average crosses above the longer-term moving average, it signals a potential uptrend. Conversely, when the shorter-term moving average crosses below the longer-term moving average, it signals a potential downtrend.

Key Steps for Moving Average Crossover:

  • Use two moving averages: a short-term moving average (e.g., 50-day) and a long-term moving average (e.g., 200-day).
  • Enter a long position when the short-term moving average crosses above the long-term moving average.
  • Enter a short position when the short-term moving average crosses below the long-term moving average.
  • Exit positions when the moving averages cross in the opposite direction.

This system is widely popular because it is simple to implement and understand. However, it works best in trending markets and may produce false signals in sideways or range-bound markets.

2. Donchian Channels

The Donchian Channel is a trend-following system that uses a price channel to help traders determine the trend’s direction. The system consists of three lines: the upper, lower, and middle bands. The upper band is the highest high over a set period, the lower band is the lowest low over the same period, and the middle band is the average of the upper and lower bands.

When the price breaks above the upper band, it signals the beginning of an uptrend. When the price breaks below the lower band, it signals the start of a downtrend. The key advantage of this system is that it allows traders to catch trends early and ride them for longer periods.

Key Steps for Donchian Channels:

  • Set a period (e.g., 20 days) for the Donchian Channel.
  • Enter a long position when the price closes above the upper band.
  • Enter a short position when the price closes below the lower band.
  • Exit positions when the price breaks back inside the channel.

The Donchian Channel system is effective in trending markets, but like other trend-following systems, it can generate false signals during sideways or choppy market conditions.

3. The Turtle Trading System

The Turtle Trading System is a famous trend-following strategy developed by Richard Dennis and William Eckhardt in the 1980s. The system involves using a set of rules to enter and exit trades based on the breakout of price levels. The idea is to enter the market when it breaks through a specific price level, signaling the start of a new trend.

The Turtle Trading System uses two primary components: a breakout strategy and a position sizing model. The breakout strategy involves entering a trade when the price moves beyond a specific threshold, while the position sizing model helps determine how much capital to allocate to each trade based on volatility.

Key Steps for the Turtle Trading System:

  • Use a 20-day breakout as the primary entry signal.
  • Exit the position when the price moves against you or after a set number of days.
  • Use the 55-day breakout for longer-term trades.
  • Determine position size using volatility measures (the average true range, or ATR).

The Turtle Trading System is known for its rules-based approach, which eliminates emotional decision-making and allows traders to focus on trend-following.

4. Average True Range (ATR) Trailing Stop

The Average True Range (ATR) is an indicator that measures market volatility. Traders use it to determine how far the market may move and set stop-loss orders accordingly. The ATR trailing stop is a dynamic stop-loss technique that adjusts as the price moves in favor of the trade.

The ATR trailing stop involves setting a stop-loss order based on the ATR value. As the price moves in the direction of the trend, the stop-loss is adjusted to lock in profits while allowing for some price fluctuations. This strategy ensures that traders can capture significant trends while protecting themselves from major reversals.

Key Steps for ATR Trailing Stop:

  • Calculate the ATR value for a given period (e.g., 14 days).
  • Set the initial stop-loss at a certain multiple of the ATR below (for long trades) or above (for short trades) the entry price.
  • Adjust the stop-loss as the price moves in favor of the trade.

The ATR trailing stop is an excellent tool for managing risk and allowing profits to run while keeping losses minimal.

5. Relative Strength Index (RSI) and Trend Confirmation

While the Relative Strength Index (RSI) is primarily known as a momentum oscillator, it can also be used in conjunction with trend-following strategies to confirm the strength of a trend. The RSI measures the magnitude of recent price changes to identify overbought or oversold conditions.

When using RSI with trend-following strategies, traders typically look for confirmation signals. For instance, during an uptrend, an RSI reading above 50 indicates that the trend is strong, while an RSI reading below 50 during a downtrend suggests the strength of the downward move.

Key Steps for Using RSI with Trend Following:

  • Use a trend-following system like moving averages or Donchian channels to identify the trend direction.
  • Confirm the trend strength using the RSI. A reading above 50 supports an uptrend, while a reading below 50 supports a downtrend.
  • Avoid trading against the prevailing trend and enter positions when the RSI supports the trend’s direction.

RSI is a helpful confirmation tool that can reduce the likelihood of false signals and improve the accuracy of trend-following strategies.

6. The 200-Day Moving Average (Long-Term Trend)

The 200-day moving average is one of the most well-known indicators used by trend-following traders to determine the long-term trend of a stock or market. A stock trading above its 200-day moving average is considered to be in an uptrend, while a stock trading below its 200-day moving average is considered to be in a downtrend.

The 200-day moving average is a lagging indicator, which means it reacts to price movements and may not capture trends at their early stages. However, it provides valuable information about the overall market direction.

Key Steps for Using the 200-Day Moving Average:

  • Monitor the price relative to the 200-day moving average.
  • Enter long positions when the price is above the 200-day moving average.
  • Enter short positions when the price is below the 200-day moving average.
  • Exit positions when the price crosses back through the 200-day moving average in the opposite direction.

While this strategy works well for capturing long-term trends, it can be slow to react to changes in the market and may not be ideal for short-term traders.

Conclusion

Trend-following is a powerful strategy that can help stock traders profit from market movements by riding trends and minimizing risk. By using the right tools and systems, traders can develop a strategy that fits their style and risk tolerance.

The systems outlined above, such as moving averages, Donchian channels, and the Turtle Trading System, are just a few of the many approaches to trend-following. Each has its strengths and weaknesses, and the best system for any individual trader depends on their goals, time horizon, and market conditions.

By understanding these trend-following systems and applying them effectively, traders can enhance their ability to navigate the stock market and increase their chances of long-term success.
