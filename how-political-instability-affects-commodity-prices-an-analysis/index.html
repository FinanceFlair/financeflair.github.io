How Political Instability Affects Commodity Prices: An Analysis


Global politics and the economy are intertwined in complex ways, and one of the most significant consequences of political instability is its impact on commodity prices. From the price of oil to the cost of wheat, the world's most essential commodities are heavily influenced by the state of the world's politics. In this post, we'll delve into the intricate relationship between politics and commodity prices, exploring how heightened tensions, conflict, and regime change can cause prices to skyrocket or plummet. A Look at the Factors Driving Political Instability Political instability can take many forms, from civil wars to elections, protests, and even shifts in government policies. When any of these events occur, they can have far-reaching effects on the global market. There are a few key factors that drive the relationship between politics and commodity prices. These include:

Supply Disruptions: Anything that disrupts the supply chain of essential commodities can cause prices to rise. Civil wars, labor disputes, and government policies can all hinder the transportation and production of many goods. Currency Fluctuations: When a country experiences political turmoil, its currency is often devalued, making imports more expensive and exports less competitive. This can lead to a significant increase in the price of essential commodities, as countries may struggle to afford these goods. Investor Sentiment: When investors become uncertain about the stability of a particular region or country, they are less likely to invest in its commodities. This reduced demand can result in lower commodity prices as supply outpaces demand. Government Policies: Changes in government policies, such as taxes, tariffs, and subsidies, can directly impact commodity prices. Sanctions and embargoes, in particular, can severely limit the supply of certain goods.

Case Study: The Syrian Civil War and Oil Prices A compelling example of the impact of political instability on commodity prices is the Syrian Civil War. The conflict that began in 2011 had far-reaching effects, including the displacement of millions of people, widespread destruction of infrastructure, and a devastating impact on the global energy market. As the conflict escalated, global oil prices rose significantly. This was due to several factors, including:

The closure of the Syrian oil pipeline, which has the capacity to produce nearly 400,000 barrels of oil per day, severely limited global supply. The decision by several Western countries to impose sanctions on Syrian oil exports, making it difficult for the country to sell its oil on the global market. Widespread destruction of oil-infrastructure, including refineries and pipelines, added to the supply shortage.

Case Study: Venezuela and Food Prices The situation in Venezuela is another prime example of how political instability can impact commodity prices. The country's economy has been in free fall since 2014, leading to a shortage of food and other essential goods. The effects on commodity prices have been devastating. For example:

Wheat, corn, and soybean prices rose sharply due to the decline in corn production, which severely limited the supply of animal feed. The introduction of price controls and rationing further limited the availability of essential goods, driving prices higher. As the crisis deepens, Venezuela has been forced to import a large quantity of food items from other countries.

Conclusion Political instability can have far-reaching effects on commodity prices, the economy, and global markets. From civil wars to election disputes and shifts in government policies, the relationship between politics and economics is complex and ever-changing. As the Syrian and Venezuelan cases demonstrate, the consequences can be severe.
