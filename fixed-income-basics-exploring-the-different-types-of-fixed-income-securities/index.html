Fixed Income Basics: Exploring the Different Types of Fixed Income Securities


Fixed income securities are a cornerstone of many investment portfolios, offering investors a reliable and stable source of income through regular interest payments. While mostly known for providing predictable cash flows, fixed income securities exhibit a wide range of features and benefits. In this post, we’ll explore the diverse landscape of fixed-income products and examine the nuances that make each type unique.

1. Treasury Securities

Treasury securities, also known as government bonds, are debt obligations issued by the U.S. Department of the Treasury. Their prominence in the investment universe is primarily due to their framework: they are backed by the full faith and credit of the U.S. government, ensuring investors receive timely interest payments and principal repayments. Some examples include:

  • Treasury Bills (T-Bills): With a maturity of one year or less, T-Bills are the shortest-term offerings in the market. They are sold at a discount and are redeemed at face value upon maturity.
  • Treasury Notes: These securities mature between one and ten years and offer semiannual interest payments.
  • Treasury Bonds: With a maturity of more than ten years, these bonds also pay interest semiannually. The longest term bond currently available is the 30-year Treasury bond.

2. Government Agency and Municipal Bonds

Issued by government agencies or municipalities to finance public projects, these bonds offer tax advantages and a strong incentive for investors, especially those in high tax brackets. These benefits arise from potential state or local tax exemptions on the income generated from municipal bonds, though federal taxes are still applicable unless the bondholder holds the bond until maturity, in which case the tax-deferred gain is realized.

3. Corporate Bonds

When corporations need to raise capital, they can issue corporate bonds. Different from government debt, these bonds carry no assurances of federal backing, but they can offer higher yields if the issuer’s creditworthiness is sufficient. Bonds are rated by credit agencies such as Standard & Poor’s, Moody’s, and Fitch to gauge the risk of default. This risk is usually mapped onto a scale, with higher ratings indicating lower risk. Conversely, lower ratings often accompany higher yields.

4. Mortgage-Backed Securities (MBS)

Mortgage-backed securities represent an innovative aspect of the fixed-income category. They are formed by pooling together individual mortgages and are typically backed by government-sponsored enterprises, such as Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac. Because mortgage-backed securities offer investors regular interest and principal payments, they are attractive for diversification goals.

5. Asset-Backed Securities (ABS)

Similar in nature to mortgage-backed securities, ABS are funded by pools of various assets, such as credit card debt, auto loans, or student loans. Investors can usually derive a steady stream of income from the coupon payments generated by the underlying assets.

6. Convertible Bonds

Convertible bonds give bondholders the right to swap their bond for a specific number of shares of the issuing company’s stock at a predetermined conversion price. This structure appeals to investors who may wish to benefit from potential share price appreciation. The fixed-income element is derived from the coupon and principal repayments.

7. Preferred Stock

Preferred stock is a hybrid of debt and equity, offering a combination of fixed-income characteristics and equity upside potential. These securities provide investors with a priority claim on the company’s earnings and assets, generally paying a fixed dividend. Additionally, preferred stockholders usually have voting rights, though these aren’t as stringent as those of common stockholders.

Fixed-Income Allocation Strategy

When deciding on a fixed-income investment, consider how much risk you’re willing to take, your tax situation, and your portfolio’s overall composition. Keep in mind that it’s not just about finding a bond with a higher yield — diversification and aligning your portfolio with your investment goals are key to long-term success.

In summary, the fixed-income universe is a complex field comprising various types of securities to intrigue or satisfy even the most discerning investor. Whether obtained through a diversified approach or more focused strategies, fixed income investments have a role in nearly every investment strategy.
