How to Use the Asset Approach for Corporate Valuation


The asset approach is a method of valuing a company by adding up the value of its individual assets and liabilities. This approach is particularly useful for companies with more tangible assets, as it provides a straightforward method for determining their overall worth. In this guide, we’ll explore the steps required to use the asset approach for corporate valuation.

Understanding the Asset Approach

Before diving into the process, it’s essential to understand the asset approach and its underlying principles. Essentially, the asset approach values a company by analyzing the worth of its assets, minus its liabilities. This method provides an indication of the enterprise’s liquidation value – what it would be worth if all its assets were sold off, and its liabilities were paid off.

The asset approach is particularly useful when the company’s intangible assets, such as patents or trademarks, play a significant role in its value. In such cases, finding a buyer for these intangible assets can be challenging, making the asset approach a more practical valuation method.

Steps for Using the Asset Approach for Corporate Valuation

Now that we’ve covered the basic principles of the asset approach, let’s delve into the process of valuation. Follow these steps to use the asset approach:

  1. Gather financial data: The first step is to gather all relevant financial data for the company, including balance sheets, income statements, and cash flow statements. This information can usually be found in the company’s annual reports or, for publicly traded companies, on financial databases.

  2. Determine the assets: Once you have the financial data, it’s time to determine the company’s total assets. Divide the assets into categories such as current assets (cash, accounts receivable, inventory) and non-current assets (property, equipment, intangible assets). Appraise each category by their market or Fair Market Value (FMV) and sum them to arrive at the Total Asset Value.

  3. Determine the liabilities: Next, evaluate the company’s liabilities, which are typically reported in its balance sheet. Include both short-term and long-term liabilities, such as accounts payable, loans, and accrued liabilities.

  4. Calculate the Net Asset Value: Subtract the total liabilities from the total assets to arrive at the company’s Net Asset Value (NAV). This figure represents the company’s worth using the asset approach.

  5. Analyze results: Compare the Net Asset Value with the company’s market capitalization or other valuation methods to determine if any discrepancies exist. Analyze the causes of these discrepancies and consider other factors when determining the company’s overall valuation.

Realizing the Benefits of the Asset Approach

Using the asset approach for corporate valuation offers several benefits:

  1. Straightforwardness: The asset approach is a relatively straightforward method for valuing a company, making it easy to comprehend and apply.

  2. Practicality: Companies with significant intangible assets can benefit from the asset approach, as it allows for more accurate valuation of these less tangible assets.

  3. Accessibility: Most financial data needed for the asset approach is publicly available, making it accessible to investors and analysts alike.

In conclusion, the asset approach for corporate valuation can provide a valuable tool for investors and company stakeholders. By following the five steps outlined above, you can determine a company’s Net Asset Value and make informed decisions about the enterprise’s worth. Remember that while the asset approach offers a sound methodology for valuation, it may not always align perfectly with market valuations, and other factors should be considered when deciding on a company’s worth.
