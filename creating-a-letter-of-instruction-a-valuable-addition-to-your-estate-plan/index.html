Creating a Letter of Instruction: A Valuable Addition to Your Estate Plan


When it comes to estate planning, creating a comprehensive strategy to ensure your assets are managed and distributed according to your wishes is essential. While many individuals focus on wills and trusts, one often overlooked but incredibly valuable document is the letter of instruction. This informal document can provide your loved ones with crucial guidance, instructions, and information that may not be appropriate for a will or a trust. Let’s explore the importance of creating a letter of instruction as part of your estate plan.

What is a Letter of Instruction?

A letter of instruction, sometimes referred to as a letter of intent, is a non-legal document that accompanies your will or trust. Unlike a will or trust, a letter of instruction does not have the legal authority to distribute assets. Instead, it serves as a detailed guide for your executor, trustee, or family members regarding your personal and financial affairs. This letter can include information about your funeral arrangements, location of important documents, passwords to online accounts, contact information for professional advisors, and any specific wishes you have for the distribution of personal items.

Why is it Important?

One of the primary benefits of a letter of instruction is its flexibility. Unlike a will, which is a public document that goes through the probate process, a letter of instruction is private and can be updated easily without the need for legal formalities. It allows you to provide personal and specific guidance to your loved ones, which can be immensely valuable during a difficult time. Additionally, a letter of instruction can include practical information that may not be suitable for a will or trust, such as the location of important documents, passwords, and other logistical details that can help streamline the administration of your estate.

What to Include

When creating a letter of instruction, it’s important to include a range of information to provide comprehensive guidance to your executor, trustee, or family members. Consider including the following:

  • Funeral and burial wishes: Detail any specific requests you have for your funeral or burial arrangements.
  • Location of important documents: Provide a list of where important documents, such as wills, trusts, insurance policies, deeds, and titles, are kept.
  • Contact information: Include the names and contact information of your professional advisors, such as attorneys, accountants, and financial advisors.
  • Digital assets: Provide instructions and passwords for accessing online accounts, including bank accounts, investment accounts, and social media.
  • Personal items: Outline any specific wishes you have for the distribution of personal items, such as family heirlooms, jewelry, or sentimental belongings.

Updating Your Letter of Instruction

As life changes, so should your letter of instruction. It’s essential to review and update this document regularly to ensure it remains relevant and reflects your current wishes. Major life events, such as marriage, divorce, the birth of a child, or the acquisition of significant assets, should prompt a review of your letter of instruction. By keeping this document up to date, you can ensure that your loved ones have the most accurate and relevant guidance when the time comes.

Conclusion

In conclusion, a letter of instruction is a valuable addition to your estate plan, providing personal, practical, and detailed guidance to your loved ones. By including this document as part of your overall estate planning strategy, you can ensure that your final wishes are communicated clearly and efficiently. Whether it’s detailing funeral arrangements, providing access to important documents, or specifying the distribution of personal items, a letter of instruction can offer peace of mind and clarity for your loved ones during a challenging time.

Remember, while a letter of instruction is not a legally binding document, its value lies in the personal guidance it provides. Consider consulting with an estate planning attorney or financial advisor to ensure that your letter of instruction complements your will and trust, providing a comprehensive and cohesive estate plan.
