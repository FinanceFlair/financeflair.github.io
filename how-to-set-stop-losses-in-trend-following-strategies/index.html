How to Set Stop Losses in Trend Following Strategies


When you embrace trend following as your trading strategy, managing risk becomes paramount. One of the most effective tools for controlling risk and protecting your capital is the use of stop losses. Setting stop losses in trend following isn’t just about preventing catastrophic losses—it’s about balancing risk with the opportunity to ride winning trends for maximum profit. This comprehensive guide will delve deep into how to properly set stop losses within trend following systems to enhance both longevity and profitability.

Understanding Trend Following and the Role of Stop Losses

Trend following is a strategy designed to capitalize on sustained movements in asset prices. It relies on the idea that “the trend is your friend,” aiming to buy in an uptrend and sell or short in a downtrend. However, trends don’t last forever, and false signals or reversals can eat into profits—or worse, cause steep losses.

Stop losses act as automatic exit points that close a position when price moves against the trader beyond a predefined threshold. They serve to limit downside, protect capital, and help maintain emotional discipline during volatile market phases. Without them, traders risk staying stuck in losing positions, which may lead to significant drawdowns.

Key Principles in Setting Stop Losses for Trend Following

Before jumping into techniques, it’s crucial to align stop loss placement with the core philosophy of trend following. Here are the guiding principles:

  1. Allow the Trend to Breathe: Too tight a stop loss leads to premature exits on normal market noise, missing out on the bigger move. Trend following requires patience and space for the trend to develop.

  2. Avoid Arbitrary Fixed Stops: Using a fixed percentage or dollar amount for all trades disregards market volatility and individual asset behavior.

  3. Adapt to Volatility: Assets and markets fluctuate differently; setting stops that account for current volatility reduces the chance of being stopped out too early.

  4. Consistency Across Trades: Having a standardized method for stop placement ensures a more objective trading system and better performance tracking.

Popular Methods to Set Stop Losses in Trend Following

1. Volatility-Based Stops

This method uses a measure of market volatility to dictate how far from the entry price the stop should be set.

  • Average True Range (ATR): The ATR indicator measures average price movement over a set period, reflecting how much an asset tends to move daily. A common approach is to place the stop a multiple of ATR away from the entry price (e.g., 1.5 or 2 times ATR).

  • Benefits: Adapts to market conditions; wider stops in volatile markets, and tighter stops in calmer markets.

  • Example: If the ATR for a stock is 1.2 points, a trader might place a stop 2.0 x 1.2 = 2.4 points away from the entry, allowing the price room to oscillate without premature stops.

2. Moving Average or Trendline-Based Stops

Using dynamic trend indicators as stop loss levels aligns well with trend following.

  • Moving Averages: Commonly, the 20-day or 50-day moving average can serve as a trailing stop, with the trade closed if price closes below the moving average.

  • Trendlines: Drawing trendlines on price charts can help identify support or resistance areas. A break below a rising trendline may be used as an exit signal.

  • Benefits: Stops adjust as the trend evolves, letting profits run while protecting against reversals.

3. Price Structure Stops

This method places stops just beyond recent price swing highs or lows to respect market structure.

  • Swing Low/High Stops: In a long trade, set the stop just below the recent swing low; in a short trade, just above the recent swing high.

  • Benefits: Utilizes logical points where, if breached, the trend may likely be reversing.

  • Consideration: Requires regular adjustment as price forms new swings to avoid stale stops.

4. Percentage-Based Stops

Setting stops based on a fixed percentage loss relative to entry price.

  • Example: A trader might decide never to risk more than 3% on a single trade, so the stop is set accordingly.

  • Drawbacks: Less adaptive to actual market volatility, so may be too tight in turbulent markets or too loose in calm ones.

Trailing Stops: Locking In Profit while Riding the Trend

A powerful tactic in trend following is the use of trailing stops that move in favor of the trade as price advances but do not move backward if the price pulls back.

  • Types of Trailing Stops:

    • Fixed Dollar/Percentage Trailing Stop: The stop moves up/down by a fixed amount or percentage when price moves favorably.
    • Volatility-Based Trailing Stop: Similar to the initial stop, but adjusted continuously using ATR or other volatility metrics.

  • Advantages: Protects unrealized gains by automatically tightening stops as the trend progresses.

  • Best Practices: Set trailing stops wide enough to avoid being stopped out on minor pullbacks but tight enough to protect gains efficiently.

Combining Stop Losses with Position Sizing for Optimal Risk Management

Stop losses should not be viewed in isolation. They work best when integrated with thoughtful position sizing to manage total trade risk.

  • Calculate Maximum Risk Per Trade: Decide on a fixed percentage of your capital to risk per trade (e.g., 1-2%).

  • Determine Stop Distance: Using your preferred stop method, find the difference between entry price and stop loss price.

  • Adjust Position Size: Size the trade so that if the stop loss triggers, it corresponds to the predetermined risk percentage of your account.

This synergy ensures that no single trade can damage your portfolio excessively while allowing stops to remain logically placed based on market conditions.

Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

1. Setting Stops Too Tight

Being stopped out repeatedly due to stop losses placed closer than normal price fluctuations can be frustrating and costly. Always account for volatility and avoid arbitrary tight stops.

2. Ignoring Market Context

Stop losses should factor in asset-specific behavior, current trend strength, and market environment (e.g., earnings announcements, economic reports).

3. Not Adjusting Stops

Markets evolve, and a stop set at trade entry shouldn’t be left untouched indefinitely. Whether using trailing stops or manually adjusting based on price action, keep stops aligned with current market conditions.

4. Emotional Interference

Ignoring stop loss discipline or adjusting stops reactively out of fear or greed can lead to larger losses. Develop a rule-based approach and stick to it.

Practical Step-by-Step Guide to Setting Stop Losses in Trend Following

  1. Identify Entry Point: Determine the price level where you will enter the trend-following trade based on your system.

  2. Measure Volatility: Use ATR or similar tools to gauge how much the price typically moves.

  3. Choose Stop Loss Method: Decide whether to use volatility-based, moving average, swing low/high, or percentage stop strategies.

  4. Set Stop Loss Price: Calculate the exact stop loss level using your chosen method.

  5. Calculate Position Size: Using your risk tolerance and stop loss distance, decide the number of units/contracts/shares to trade.

  6. Place the Trade and Set Stop Loss Order: Execute the trade entry and simultaneously place the stop loss order in your trading platform.

  7. Monitor and Adjust Trailing Stop: As the trend progresses, move the stop loss level to lock in profits according to your rules.

Real-Life Example: ATR-Based Stop Loss in Action

Imagine you enter a long trade in a currency pair at 1.3000, and the ATR (14-day) is 0.0100 (100 pips). You decide to place your stop 2 x ATR below your entry, which means your initial stop loss is at 1.2800 (1.3000 - 0.0200).

Your maximum risk per trade is 2% of your capital. Knowing the stop distance (200 pips), you calculate your position size so that if your stop loss is hit, you lose no more than 2%.

As the price moves up to 1.3200, you can trail your stop to 1.3000 (previous entry point) or use a volatility-based trailing stop to stay behind the price at a comfortable margin.

Final Thoughts on Stop Losses in Trend Following

The art of setting stop losses in trend following hinges on finding the right balance between protecting capital and giving the trend enough room to develop. By aligning stop loss placement with volatility, price structure, and logical market levels, traders can reduce false exits and maximize trend-capturing potential.

Remember that stops are an integral part of a broader risk management system including position sizing and discipline. A rule-based, adaptive approach to stop losses will serve trend followers well on their journey through the unpredictable waters of the markets.

With experience, you’ll develop a finely tuned sense of where to place stops that complement your edge and personality—transforming stop loss setting from a chore into a strategic advantage.

Feel free to experiment with these ideas in your trading plan and continuously refine your methods based on observed performance. Protecting what you earn on the winning trades is just as important as finding those trades in the first place. Trade smart, manage risk well, and let the trends carry you forward.
