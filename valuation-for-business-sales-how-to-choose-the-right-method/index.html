Valuation for Business Sales: How to Choose the Right Method


When it comes to selling your business, you’ll want to ensure you choose the right valuation method to get the best possible price. It’s essential to understand your business’ worth, which can be highly variable depending on the method used. In this blog, we’ll explore three popular methods to help you determine the best course of action for your business.

  1. Asset-based Approach

The asset-based approach is a straightforward method where a business is valued based on the total value of its assets, minus liabilities. This method can be suitable for smaller businesses without a lot of intangible assets. Valuators conduct a thorough inventory of the business’ tangible assets, including equipment, inventory, and Buildings, and estimate their value. The estimated value is reduced by any outstanding liabilities to arrive at an asset-based valuation.

Pros:

  • It’s relatively simple and straightforward, making it an excellent choice for smaller businesses.
  • It provides a clear, accurate picture of the business’ worth, considering all assets and liabilities.

Cons:

  • It may undervalue businesses with considerable intangible assets, such as patents, trademarks, or customer loyalty.
  • Does not take into account the ongoing goodwill of the company.
  1. Market-Based (Comparable Company) Approach

The market-based or comparable company approach is favored for companies with more relatable metrics. It values your business by comparing it to similar sold companies within the same industry – the more similar the companies, the more accurate the valuation. This method is helpful for businesses with well-established market benchmarks.

Pros:

  • It provides a reliable valuation by basing it on real sales of similar companies in the same industry.
  • It’s a useful method for businesses that can be easily compared to others within their industry.

Cons:

  • The comparison could be challenging for niche businesses or those situated in immature markets.
  • The method heavily relies on data availability; data could be outdated or limited.
  1. Income Approach

The income approach values your business based on the earnings the company is expected to generate now and in the future. Typically, two methods are used under this approach, namely the capitalization of earnings (P/E ratio) and discounted cash flow (DCF) method.

Pros:

  • It takes into account your business’ current and future earnings, making it more suited for valuations in today’s market.
  • It is advantageous for businesses showing a healthy and consistent stream of profits.

Cons:

  • The method’s accuracy highly depends on the accuracy of future earnings projection – a potential challenge.
  • It’s not an ideal approach for business that may not have steady earnings but rapid growth.

Choosing the Right Method

The right valuation method largely depends on your business’ unique needs and market position. Generally, you can benefit from combining multiple methods to get a more accurate picture. A professional appraiser will perform a thorough analysis for a comprehensive and well-informed valuation of your business.

As you prepare your business for sale, ensure you choose a valuation method that best aligns with your company’s particular characteristics. From tangible asset valuation to market comparison and earnings projection, knowing your worth is the first step to a successful sale. Remember, understanding your business’ worth helps you set the right expectations and makes for smoother negotiations.

In conclusion, all these methods - the asset-based, market-based, and income approach, each have their pros and cons. By combining them, or using a combination that encompasses your specific situation, you can paint a clearer picture of your business to ensure a fair and satisfactory sale.
