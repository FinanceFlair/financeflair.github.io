An Introduction to Futures Contracts: What Every Investor Should Know


Futures contracts are a sophisticated financial tool used across various sectors of the market. Frequently associated with commodities like crude oil, gold, and wheat, they also extend to financial instruments, stocks, and indexes. As with any investment, it’s crucial to understand what these contracts entail and how they work before diving into the world of futures trading.

What is a Futures Contract?

Simply put, a futures contract is an agreement between two parties to buy or sell an asset at a predetermined price for a set date in the future. These contracts are traded on exchanges and are standardized in terms of quantity, quality, and delivery timeframe.

For example, if you enter into a futures contract to buy 1,000 barrels of crude oil at $60 per barrel on September 30th, you are committing to purchase that oil no matter what the market price is at the time of delivery. If the price of oil drops sharply, you still get the oil at $60, while the seller stands to gain from the lower market prices. Just as with options contracts, without immediate action (like “buying to close”), these open contracts create future obligations which investors must manage.

Types of Futures Contracts

The range of futures contracts available on financial markets is vast and varied:

  1. Commodity Futures: These primarily deal with raw materials such as agricultural products, energy commodities, metals, and more. Key examples include corn, coffee, gold, natural gas, and oil.

  2. Currency Futures: These allow investors to speculate on the future exchange rates of different currencies.

  3. Equity Index Futures: These are based on stock market indices such as the S&P 500 or NASDAQ. They enable investors to trade the overall performance of a market without having to buy individual stocks.

  4. Interest Rate Futures: These contracts are traded on various interest rates including government bond yields, and are mainly used by institutional investors in the banking and finance sectors.

  5. Bond Futures: They allow investors to speculate on the direction of interest rates or to hedge their exposure to interest rate risk.

Why do Investors Use Futures Contracts?

  1. Hedging: One of the primary reasons why businesses and investors use futures contracts is risk management or hedging. If someone owns a commodity and is worried about a future price drop, they can buy a futures contract as insurance.

  2. Speculation: Because futures contracts are traded with leveraged positions, they allow investors to gain exposure to markets far beyond their initial investment. This can pave the way for significant profits but also carries substantial risk.

  3. Arbitrage: It’s a way to exploit the price differences for identical or similar financial instruments across various markets.

  4. Economies of Scale: They allow parties who need to frequently buy (or sell) large amounts of commodities or financial instruments to “lock-in” prices and make larger transactions more manageable.
