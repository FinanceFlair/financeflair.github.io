Top 5 Trend Following Indicators Every Trader Should Know


Spotting trends early and riding their momentum is the essence of successful trading. Whether you’re day trading forex, navigating the stock market, or diving into crypto, mastering trend following indicators can mean the difference between catching the big moves and getting left behind. While no single indicator guarantees success, combining a few—each with unique insights—can help you make precise, confident decisions.

Below, we explore five of the most powerful and widely used trend following indicators. We’ll dive into how each tool works, and how traders wield them to assess direction, confirm entries, and manage trades.

1. Moving Averages (Simple Moving Average, Exponential Moving Average)

Why Moving Averages Matter

At the very core of trend following lies the moving average. Imagine cutting through all the market noise—daily swings, false signals, and emotional whipsaws—and focusing on the underlying direction of price. That’s the moving average in action.

  • Simple Moving Average (SMA): This is the straightforward calculation—adding up the last ’n’ closing prices and dividing by ’n.’ SMAs smooth out price action, highlighting the overall direction.
  • Exponential Moving Average (EMA): EMAs place more weight on the most recent prices, making them more sensitive to shifts. That means EMAs respond to market changes faster; many traders rely on the 50 EMA and 200 EMA as benchmarks.

How Traders Use Them

  • Identifying Trend Direction: If price is consistently above a moving average, the trend is likely bullish; below indicates a bearish trend.
  • Golden Cross and Death Cross: When a short-term moving average (e.g., 50-day) crosses above a long-term (e.g., 200-day), it’s a “golden cross”—a potential strong uptrend signal. The reverse is known as a “death cross.”
  • Dynamic Support and Resistance: Price often bounces off key moving averages, offering entry or exit points.

Practical Tip

Combine two moving averages—like the 50 EMA and 200 EMA—to filter out slow-changing signals and spot fresh trends, especially on higher timeframes.

2. Average Directional Index (ADX)

Cutting Through the Chaos

Price can go up, down, or sideways. The challenge is distinguishing powerful trends from range-bound churn. This is where the Average Directional Index (ADX) shines.

The ADX is a non-directional indicator. It doesn’t show whether the trend is up or down, but it reveals how strong that trend is.

How ADX Works

  • ADX Values: Ranges from 0 to 100. Readings above 25 generally suggest a strong trend; below 20 indicates a weak or nonexistent trend.
  • +DI and –DI: The indicator comes with two lines, +DI (Directional Indicator) and –DI, giving clues about the prevailing direction.

Trading Insights

  • Confirming Trend Strength: Before entering a trade, traders check if the ADX is above the threshold, signaling momentum.
  • Avoiding Choppy Markets: If the ADX is below 20, it often means the market is dull and likely to reverse unpredictably.

Pro Tip

Pair ADX with a moving average system: only act on buy or sell signals if the ADX confirms a trend is strong enough to warrant the risk.

3. Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD)

The All-in-One Trend Follower

Some indicators try to read too much, too soon. The MACD keeps it simple and precise, measuring the gap between two exponential moving averages—typically the 12-period and the 26-period EMAs.

  • MACD Line: Difference between the two EMAs.
  • Signal Line: A 9-period EMA of the MACD Line.
  • Histogram: Graphical difference between MACD and the signal line.

Why MACD Stands Out

  • Trend Identification: When the MACD Line crosses above its Signal Line, the trend could be turning bullish; the reverse signals potential bearishness.
  • Momentum Tracking: The further apart the MACD Line and Signal Line move, the stronger the momentum.
  • Divergence Alerts: When price forms a new high but the MACD doesn’t, it could be a sign the trend is losing steam.

Using MACD in Real Trades

  • Entry Triggers: Buy when the MACD crosses over the signal line and both are above zero. Sell when the opposite happens below zero.
  • Exit Cues: Look for a reversal in the MACD’s trajectory or weakening histograms to consider scaling out of a position.

MACD’s versatility makes it a favorite across asset classes, timeframes, and trading plans.

4. Parabolic SAR (Stop and Reverse)

Catching the Wave and Knowing When to Bail

Staying in a trend until it flips can be tricky. The Parabolic SAR (Stop and Reverse) helps traders ride the wave and time their exits.

How Parabolic SAR Works

This indicator prints dots above or below the price candles:

  • Dots below price candles: Bullish trend.
  • Dots above price candles: Bearish trend.

When the dots switch sides, it signals a possible reversal and an opportunity to lock in profits or flip your position.

Why Traders Use Parabolic SAR

  • Trend Confirmation: Reinforces other indicators—if both moving averages and SAR agree, confidence increases.
  • Automated Stops: Many traders use SAR points to set trailing stop-losses, protecting gains.
  • Trade Management: The indicator responds to acceleration in movement, adapting to trends that pick up speed.

Things to Watch

While responsive, the Parabolic SAR can be ‘whipsawed’ in sideways or volatile ranges. Pair it with a trend confirmation tool like ADX for best results.

5. Ichimoku Cloud

The Powerhouse of Trend Analysis

The Ichimoku Kinko Hyo, usually called the Ichimoku Cloud, is a self-contained trend analysis system originating from Japan. At first glance, it may look intimidating—a tangle of five lines and a colorful ‘cloud’—but its clarity and depth are unrivaled.

What’s Inside the Cloud?

  • Kumo (Cloud): The shaded region forecasts future support and resistance. If price is above the cloud, the trend is bullish; below is bearish.
  • Tenkan-sen (Conversion Line): A short-term moving average, identifies minor trend changes.
  • Kijun-sen (Base Line): A medium-term moving average, guides longer moves.
  • Chikou Span (Lagging Span): Plots current closing price, shifted backward, offering confirmation and potential reversal signals.

Why Ichimoku Dominates

  • Trend Clarity: A single glance shows the overall market structure.
  • Entry and Exit Signals: Crosses of Tenkan-sen and Kijun-sen, plus cloud breakouts, provide clear cues.
  • Multiple Timeframes: Works on any market—daily, weekly, or hourly.

How Top Traders Apply It

  • Bullish Signal: Price is above the cloud, Tenkan-sen is above Kijun-sen, and the Chikou Span is above both.
  • Bearish Signal: Opposite arrangement.

For those who master its language, Ichimoku offers almost everything: trend detection, trade signals, support and resistance, and even a peek into short-term volatility.

How to Combine Trend Following Indicators

No indicator should be used in isolation. Pairing a fast-acting tool like the EMA or Parabolic SAR with a filter like ADX, or supplementing MACD signals with Ichimoku, can provide both early signals and trend validation.

Example from Real Trading

Imagine trading EUR/USD on a 4-hour chart:

  1. 50 EMA and 200 EMA: Price is above both, suggesting a bullish bias.
  2. MACD: MACD Line crosses above Signal Line, confirming new momentum.
  3. ADX: Reads 28, signaling a healthy trend.
  4. Parabolic SAR: Dots below price, providing stop guidance.
  5. Ichimoku Cloud: Price has broken above the cloud, Chikou Span is free.

Now, instead of guessing, you’re stacking the odds in your favor—aligning multiple trusted trend following indicators.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Ignoring Market Conditions: Not all environments are suited to trend following—strong trends are required.
  • Over-Reliance: Too many indicators can cause ‘analysis paralysis’—when in doubt, stick to a few you trust.
  • Repainting Indicators: Some custom indicators recalculate the past, offering false confidence.
  • Neglecting Risk Management: Even the best signals fail—always keep stops and position sizing in place.

Wrapping It All Up

Trend following indicators are essential tools in any trader’s toolkit. From the simplicity and reliability of moving averages, the momentum-confirming power of the ADX, the multi-dimensional approach of the MACD, the practical utility of the Parabolic SAR, to the all-encompassing perspective of the Ichimoku Cloud—each offers a unique lens on the market’s direction.

Choosing the right mix comes down to your trading style, preferred markets, and comfort with each tool. Backtest strategies, combine complementary indicators, and always keep your risk managed. In the world of trading, letting the trend be your friend, with help from these top indicators, is a strategy that stands the test of time.

Looking to sharpen your trend following skills? Experiment with these indicators on a demo account, compare their signals, and find the synergy that clicks with your approach. The market rewards those with patience, discipline, and the right tools at their disposal. Happy trading!
