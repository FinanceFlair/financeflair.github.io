How to Use Covered Calls for Extra Income in Your Portfolio


Investing in the stock market is often considered a long-term strategy, where gains are realized through appreciation in asset value. However, for investors seeking to boost their income without liquidating their positions, there is an often-overlooked strategy: covered calls. In this post, we will explore how to use covered calls to generate additional income, explain the mechanics behind the strategy, and walk you through the steps to implement it successfully in your portfolio.

What Is a Covered Call?

A covered call is an options trading strategy where an investor sells call options on a stock or another asset they already own. Essentially, it involves writing (selling) a call option for shares that are already in your portfolio. In return for selling this option, you collect a premium from the buyer of the option. This strategy can provide extra income while still holding onto your stock, but with some limitations on potential profit if the stock price rises sharply.

To put it simply:

  • Covered: You already own the underlying stock.
  • Call: You are selling the right for someone else to buy the stock at a predetermined price (strike price) by a certain expiration date.

The income you generate from this strategy comes from the premium you receive for selling the call option. If the stock’s price does not rise above the strike price by the option’s expiration date, you keep the premium and retain the stock. If the stock’s price rises above the strike price, you may be obligated to sell your shares at that price, but you still keep the premium.

Why Use Covered Calls for Extra Income?

Covered calls are an appealing strategy for investors looking to generate income from their existing portfolios without selling their stocks. Here are a few reasons why this strategy can be advantageous:

  1. Generate Extra Income: The primary benefit of covered calls is that they allow you to earn additional income on top of any dividends or capital gains you may already be receiving from the stock. The premium you receive from selling the call options is yours to keep.

  2. Downside Protection: While the income from covered calls won’t protect you from large drops in stock value, it does provide a cushion in the event the stock’s price falls. The premium you collect reduces your effective purchase price, which can help offset some of the losses if the stock price drops.

  3. Increase Portfolio Efficiency: By utilizing covered calls, investors can maximize the income potential of their existing assets. It’s an efficient way to make the most out of stocks that are already sitting in your portfolio, especially those with limited growth potential in the short term.

  4. Less Risk Than Naked Calls: Compared to a “naked call” strategy (where the investor sells calls without owning the underlying stock), a covered call carries less risk. Since you own the underlying stock, you are not exposed to unlimited losses if the stock price rises significantly above the strike price.

  5. Suitable for Range-Bound Stocks: Covered calls are particularly well-suited for stocks that are expected to trade within a defined range over a given time period. If you believe a stock will not rise significantly, covered calls can be an excellent way to capture income without taking on much additional risk.

How to Execute a Covered Call

Executing a covered call involves the following steps:

  1. Choose the Stock: The first step in selling a covered call is choosing a stock that you already own or are willing to own. Typically, this strategy works best for large-cap, stable companies with lower volatility, as their price movements are more predictable.

  2. Select the Right Call Option: After selecting the stock, you will need to choose the right call option to sell. When choosing the strike price and expiration date, keep the following in mind:

    • Strike Price: This is the price at which the buyer of the option has the right to buy the stock. Ideally, select a strike price above the current stock price. If the stock goes above this price, you will have to sell your shares, but you’ll still keep the premium.
    • Expiration Date: The expiration date is when the option will expire. You can choose options that expire in the short term (e.g., weekly or monthly) or longer-term options (e.g., several months out). The choice depends on your investment goals and how long you’re willing to hold the stock.

  3. Sell the Call Option: Once you’ve chosen the strike price and expiration date, you can sell the call option. When you sell the option, you receive a premium. This premium is your immediate income, and it is yours to keep regardless of what happens with the stock price.

  4. Monitor the Stock: After you’ve sold the call, you’ll need to monitor the stock. If the stock price remains below the strike price, the option will expire worthless, and you keep the premium as profit while continuing to hold your stock. If the stock price rises above the strike price, the buyer of the option may choose to exercise the call, and you’ll have to sell your shares at the strike price. However, you still keep the premium, so you benefit from both the stock appreciation (up to the strike price) and the premium.

  5. Repeat the Process: You can repeat the process of selling covered calls as long as you continue to hold the stock. Every time you sell a covered call, you have the potential to collect another premium.

Risks of Using Covered Calls

While covered calls are generally considered a conservative strategy, they are not without risks. Here are some of the key risks to be aware of:

  1. Limited Upside Potential: If the stock’s price rises sharply above the strike price, you miss out on those additional gains. Since you are obligated to sell the stock at the strike price, you won’t be able to benefit from any appreciation beyond that level.

  2. Stock Decline: If the stock price falls significantly, the premium you received for selling the call option won’t be enough to offset the decline in the stock’s value. In the worst-case scenario, you could incur significant losses if the stock drops substantially.

  3. Obligation to Sell: If the stock price rises above the strike price, the buyer of the call option can choose to exercise the option. This means you will have to sell your stock at the strike price, even if the market price is higher. This can be frustrating if you believe the stock has further growth potential.

Tips for Maximizing Income with Covered Calls

To get the most out of your covered call strategy, consider the following tips:

  1. Select Stocks with Steady, Predictable Performance: Covered calls work best with stocks that have stable and predictable price movements. Avoid volatile stocks that could see significant price swings, as this increases the risk of having to sell at an inopportune time.

  2. Choose the Right Strike Price: The strike price should be high enough that the stock has room to appreciate without triggering the call, but not so high that it severely limits your income. Strikes that are around 5-10% above the stock’s current price tend to be a good balance.

  3. Use Shorter Expiration Dates: Shorter expiration dates allow you to collect premiums more frequently and reduce your exposure to large price movements. Weekly or monthly expiration dates can provide regular income streams.

  4. Don’t Rely Solely on Covered Calls: While covered calls can enhance your income, don’t rely on them exclusively. They should be just one part of a diversified investment strategy.

  5. Monitor the Market: Even though covered calls are a more conservative strategy, it’s still important to keep an eye on market trends and your stocks. If you see signs of significant changes in the market, it may be time to adjust your strategy.

Conclusion

Covered calls offer a powerful tool for generating extra income in your portfolio, allowing you to earn premiums without selling your stocks. While the strategy comes with some limitations, such as capping potential gains and exposing you to losses if the stock price falls, it can be a great way to enhance returns, particularly for stable, predictable stocks. By carefully selecting stocks, options, and expiration dates, you can successfully implement covered calls as part of a broader investment strategy, making your portfolio work harder for you.

Whether you’re a seasoned investor or just starting, covered calls can provide a consistent and reliable way to boost your income and reduce risk in your portfolio. Keep in mind that successful covered call strategies require careful monitoring and understanding of both the market and the stocks in your portfolio. With the right approach, you can use covered calls to maximize your investment returns over time.
