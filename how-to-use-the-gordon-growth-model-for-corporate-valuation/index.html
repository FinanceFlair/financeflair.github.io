How to Use the Gordon Growth Model for Corporate Valuation


The Gordon Growth Model (GGM) is a widely used method for estimating the fair value of a company. Formulated by Myron J. Gordon and Edward J.aldo, it’s based on the idea that a company’s value is built upon its expected future growth and the rate of return the firm’s equity earns. This powerful tool can guide investors and analysts in determining a company’s intrinsic value, enabling informed decisions. Let’s delve into the salient aspects of the GGM and discuss how to use it effectively.

Key Components of the Gordon Growth Model

The basic formula for GGM is:

Valor = FV / (r - g)

Where:

  • Valor: The present value of future cash flows (i.e., the fair value of the company)
  • FV: The forecasted value of the company at a specific period, commonly infinity
  • r: The rate of return on the company’s equity
  • g: The growth rate of the company’s earnings per share

To use GGM, you should be adept in evaluating and interpreting the key components.

Step-by-Step Guide to Implementing GGM for Corporate Valuation

Step 1: Determine the Growth Rate

To start, compute the Company’s Earnings Per Share (EPS) growth rate. This could be based on historical figures, industry standards, or a combination of these. It’s critical to ensure that your growth rate is realistic, cementing in mind that over- or under-estimates could deliver misleading results.

Step 2: Choose the Infinite Horizon

The GGM traditionally adopts an infinite time horizon, suggesting that the company’s growth rate will persist indefinitely. This approach often provides a ceiling for the company’s valuation.

Step 3: Estimate the Cost of Equity

Determine the cost of equity (r) using either the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) or the hurdle rate. Account for elements like risk, inflation, and the risk-free rate while finalizing this parameter.

Step 4: Project Future Free Cash Flows

Compute Free Cash Flows (FCF) derived from earnings, capital expenditures, and changes in working capital. Project these values based on the company’s historical trends and industry standards.

Step 5: Calculate Present Value

With all the parameters determined, compute the present value of the Company’s future cash flows using the GGM formula above. This figure should deliver the Company’s intrinsic value.

Caveats and Extensions of the Gordon Growth Model

While GGM offers powerful insights, it isn’t without its limitations. For instance, the infinite timeline can be overly simplistic, overlooking possible growth plateaus or declines. Additionally, firms demonstrating a stable, slow growth might be inaccurately valued due to GGM’s assumption of stable growth. Interpret these caveats mindfully, and seek to augment or adjust the model where necessary.

Gordon Growth Model is but one tool in your valuation toolbox. Combine it with other methods, amplifying its utility and accuracy.

Conclusion

The Gordon Growth Model remains a vital tool for valuing companies. By understanding its theory and practicing its application, you can better determine a company’s intrinsic value and inform your investment decisions. Remember, like any modeling tool, its accuracy is tied to your capacity to accurately estimate its key components. Exercise prudence in your projections, and use GGM in combination with other valuation methodologies for the most robust results.
