Trend Following for Beginners: What You Need to Know


Investing and trading can often feel like navigating through a dense forest without a map. Among the numerous strategies available, trend following stands out as one that’s both accessible and potentially rewarding, especially for beginners. This approach harnesses the natural momentum of markets, aiming to ride sustained price movements rather than predict sudden reversals. Here’s a comprehensive guide to understanding trend following, how it works, and how you can apply it effectively.

Understanding the Essence of Trend Following

At its core, trend following is a trading strategy based on the idea that assets tend to move in persistent directional trends over time. Rather than trying to guess where a price will go next, trend followers identify the current direction—up, down, or sideways—and position themselves accordingly.

The philosophy is straightforward: if a stock, currency, commodity, or any other asset is trending upward, buy and ride the wave; if it’s trending downward, sell or short to capitalize on the decline. This approach embraces the market’s behavior rather than fights it.

Why Trend Following Appeals to Beginners

  • Simplicity: The concept is relatively easy to grasp. Once you understand how to spot trends, you can make trading decisions based on them.
  • Systematic Approach: It relies more on data and less on emotional decision-making. You develop a set of rules and follow them consistently.
  • Reduced Risk of Being Wrong: By waiting for confirmation of a trend before entering, you avoid trying to predict exact market bottoms or tops, which can be error-prone.

Key Concepts Behind Trend Following

Before diving into practical applications, it’s essential to understand some critical concepts.

1. Trends and Their Types

Trends can last varying durations, typically classified as:

  • Short-term trends: These last from days to weeks.
  • Intermediate-term trends: Usually weeks to months.
  • Long-term trends: Can span months to years.

Trend followers often use moving averages and other indicators to capture these time horizons.

2. Momentum

Momentum is the force behind trends. An asset with strong momentum continues moving in its direction due to factors like investor sentiment, fundamental news, or technical triggers.

3. Breakouts and Pullbacks

  • Breakouts: Occur when a price moves beyond a resistance or support level, signaling potential trend continuation.
  • Pullbacks: Temporary reversals within an ongoing trend, often seen as opportunities to enter at better prices.

Tools and Indicators for Trend Following

Certain technical tools are invaluable in spotting and confirming trends.

Moving Averages (MA)

Simple Moving Averages (SMA) and Exponential Moving Averages (EMA) smooth out price data to reveal the underlying trend. Common setups include:

  • 50-day and 200-day Moving Averages: Widely used to assess long-term trends.
  • Crossovers: When a shorter MA crosses above a longer MA (golden cross), it may signal a bullish trend; the opposite (death cross) may indicate a bearish trend.

Average Directional Index (ADX)

The ADX helps measure trend strength without indicating direction, typically ranging from 0 (no trend) to 100 (very strong trend). Values above 20 or 25 suggest a trending market.

Trendlines and Channels

Drawing straight lines along price highs or lows can help visualize and confirm trend pathways. Channels, drawn parallel to trendlines, identify potential support and resistance within the trend.

Volume

Volume confirms trends. Rising prices with increasing volume strengthen the trend’s validity, while declining volume during price advances may hint at weakness.

Step-by-Step Guide to Getting Started with Trend Following

Step 1: Choose Your Market

Trend following works across various markets, including stocks, futures, forex, and commodities. Beginners often start with stocks or ETFs due to accessibility and liquidity.

Step 2: Identify the Trend

Use moving averages or price action to assess whether the market is trending. For instance, if today’s price is above the 50-day moving average and the MA is sloping upward, the trend might be bullish.

Step 3: Establish Entry Rules

Define clear entry criteria. For example:

  • Enter a long position when the price closes above the 50-day MA.
  • Confirm with an ADX reading above 25.
  • Consider entering on pullbacks to the moving average.

Step 4: Determine Exit Rules

Exiting a trade is as important as entering. Common exit strategies include:

  • Closing the position if the price falls below the moving average.
  • Using trailing stops to lock in profits as the trend progresses.
  • Setting a maximum acceptable loss to manage risk.

Step 5: Position Sizing and Risk Management

Never risk too much on a single trade. Many trend followers risk 1-2% of their capital on each position. Proper position sizing and stop-loss placement are critical to avoid catastrophic losses.

Step 6: Practice Discipline and Patience

Trend following can sometimes involve long periods of sideways movement or minor losses. Staying disciplined according to your rules ensures you don’t exit premature winners or chase losses.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Chasing Markets: Jumping in too late after the trend has matured.
  • Ignoring Risk Management: Failing to set stops or risking too much capital.
  • Overtrading: Taking too many trades without strong trend signals.
  • Emotional Trading: Letting fear or greed override systematic rules.

Benefits and Limitations of Trend Following

Benefits

  • Works in Various Markets: Flexibility across asset classes.
  • Aligns with Market Momentum: Profits from persistent price moves.
  • Reduces Emotional Trading: Following rules helps maintain objectivity.

Limitations

  • Whipsaw Risk: Sideways markets can generate false signals causing losses.
  • Late Entries: Trends identified after they start may limit profit potential.
  • Not Suitable for All Market Conditions: Flat or highly volatile non-trending environments pose challenges.

Realistic Expectations: What Trend Following Can Do for You

Trend following isn’t a get-rich-quick strategy. It’s about capturing sustained moves while managing risk. Success depends on:

  • Consistent application of your trading plan.
  • Understanding market cycles and adapting accordingly.
  • Learning from mistakes and refining your tactics.

Many professional traders and funds build entire portfolios around trend following due to its robustness over time. For beginners, it offers a logical and methodical way to engage markets without needing to predict every price swing.

Advanced Tips for the Ambitious Trend Follower

Once comfortable with basics, you might explore:

  • Multi-Timeframe Analysis: Confirm trends across daily, weekly, and monthly charts.
  • Combining Indicators: Use multiple tools to reduce false signals.
  • Diversification: Apply the strategy to multiple assets to smooth returns.
  • Automated Systems: Some traders code their rules into software to reduce emotional bias and ensure discipline.

Final Thoughts

Trend following combines simplicity with power by riding the natural ebb and flow of markets. While it requires patience and rigorous adherence to rules, the strategy offers beginners a pathway to participate confidently in financial markets. By focusing on identifying strong trends, managing risk, and avoiding common pitfalls, you can build a foundation for systematic, disciplined trading.

Every trader’s journey is unique, and trend following offers a compass to navigate markets methodically. Start small, learn continuously, and watch how aligning with momentum can transform your perspective on trading and investing.
