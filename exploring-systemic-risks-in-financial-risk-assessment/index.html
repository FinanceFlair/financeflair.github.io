Exploring Systemic Risks in Financial Risk Assessment


Financial risk assessment plays a critical role in guiding decision-making processes for individuals and organizations alike. However, as with any interconnected system, understanding the workings of one part doesn’t necessarily extend to understanding the collective network. When traditional risk assessment fails to account for systemic risks, the potential for catastrophic consequences looms large.

Systemic risks, often known as “too big to fail” or “TBTF,” originate from interconnected parts of a financial system. These risks can cause widespread damage, leading to huge economic collapse if left unmitigated.

In this blog post, we take a closer look at systemic risks in financial risk assessment and the implications of ignoring these risks.

A Crash Course on Systemic Risks

Understanding systemic risks requires grasping the concept of interconnected systems. Financial systems include banks, insurance firms, pension funds, stock exchanges, central and commercial banks, and various other institutions. These entities are linked by numerous relationships and dependencies, such as:

  1. Regulatory and legal ties
  2. Market interdependencies
  3. Counterparties and financial contracts
  4. Shared resources, such as payment systems and information networks

Systemic risks typically arise and propagate through these connections. Even a small issue in one interconnected entity can ripple across the entirety of the system, leading to financial crises.

Of note is the 2007-2008 Global Financial Crisis; this was brought on by the U.S. housing bubble and quickly escalated due to the extensive entanglements of banks, investors, and mortgage providers. The crisis eventually led to government bailouts and continuing financial disparity around the world.

The Implications of Ignoring Systemic Risks

Ignoring systemic risks is an uphill battle against inevitability. The consequences include:

Increased Fragility

By brushing off the possibility of systemic risks, the collective financial system becomes fragile. A single event with the potential to topple a solitary bank takes on catastrophic implications when considering the potential for collapse throughout the entire system. This heightened vulnerability hinders the financial system’s ability to recover from shocks and stresses.

Enhanced Complexity

As the world’s financial system becomes increasingly complex, so too do the interconnected relationships among entities. This complexity increases the potential for situations where mitigating one risk inadvertently creates another. Conceivably, the situations become exponentially difficult to navigate, and the margin for error shrinks.

Amplified Economic Disarray

When systemic risks strike, the damage can be economy-wide, leading to a tumultuous economic climate. Entire industries can crumble, governments struggle, and individuals feel the pinch. But recovering from these widespread crises can take years—or even decades—of diligent work.

Taking Systemic Risks Seriously

The significance of systemic risks to the financial system is undeniable. To mitigate these risks effectively, regulators, institutions, and investors need to address them head-on. The vicious cycle of neglecting systemic risks must end, paving the way for a stronger, more resilient financial system.

This starts with reevaluating the approach to risk assessment. Going beyond individual risk analysis digs deeper into the interconnected fabric of the system, better preparing us for the implications of these “too big to fail” risks.

As the financial system pushes the boundaries of innovation, so must its understanding and management of systemic risks. In the end, a proactive stance against systemic risks bolsters the flourishing of the global financial landscape and spurs growth in a more secure environment.

The ball is set into motion, and the responsibility to pick it up lies with all who contribute to the financial system. Systemic risks are not “too big to fail.” Instead, they must be faced head-on, analyzed, and mitigated; after all, we may be only one connection away from a system-wide catastrophe.
