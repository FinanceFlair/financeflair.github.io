Cognitive Biases That Impact Financial Decision Making


We've all heard the phrase "don't let emotions get the best of you when it comes to financial decisions." But have you ever stopped to consider the other unconscious influences that shape our financial choices? Cognitive biases - patterns of thinking that distort our perception of reality - play a significant role in how we allocate our resources, invest our money, and plan for the future. In this article, we'll delve into the top cognitive biases that impact our financial decision-making and explore the consequences of letting them guide our financial choices. Loss Aversion: Fear of Loss Over Gains One of the most widely recognized cognitive biases is loss aversion, first introduced by psychologists Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky in their groundbreaking work on prospect theory. Simply put, people tend to fear losses more than they value corresponding gains. This bias leads us to prioritize avoiding losses over securing gains. For instance, consider the difference between the prospect of losing $100 versus gaining $100. Each option presents an equal loss in absolute terms, yet research has shown that the former option is often viewed as being more significant. As a result, we engage in risk-averse behavior to mitigate potential losses, even if it means turning down potential opportunities. In the realm of personal finance, loss aversion can lead to over-investing in low-risk assets, stymieing growth and returns on our investments. Anchoring Bias: The Power of Initial Impressions Anchoring bias occurs when we irrationally rely on the first piece of information we receive when making a decision, overweighting its importance relative to other criteria. We've all been in situations where an initial offer or starting price sets the stage for subsequent negotiations. In finance, anchoring bias can cause us to overestimate the value of a security based on its initial offering or a previous sale price. We attach way too much weight to the initial anchor, allowing it to dictate our views on future options. This can lead us into suboptimal decisions, as we may end up overpaying or missing out on more suitable alternatives that weren't directly involved in the initial perception. Mental Accounting: Separate Treating and Accounting Mental accounting is another pervasive bias that affects financial decision-making, where we assign one set of rules and values for everyday expenses versus investments. We view money spent at the grocery store very differently from money invested in stocks or bonds. Even though our minds are trying to categorize our expenses into different mental accounts, this can make us biased by matters as "retirement money" and 'personal money". This approach might lead us to treat our funds unfairly and neglect opportunities due to this disproportionate treatment of our money.

Think about buying a product that is described as 80% fat-free compared to one described as 20% fat. One option stimulates feelings of health while the other seems unhealthy.
