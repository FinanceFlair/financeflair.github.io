Best Grants for Small Business Funding This Year


Starting and growing a small business requires a steady influx of capital. While loans and investments are common routes for securing funding, grants offer an attractive alternative that doesn’t require repayment or equity sharing. Grants can significantly alleviate financial pressure and provide resources to scale operations, innovate, or simply survive challenging economic landscapes. This year, a variety of grant opportunities are available for small businesses across industries, regions, and stages of development. This comprehensive guide will walk you through some of the best grants for small business funding available now, explaining eligibility, application tips, and how to leverage these awards effectively.

Why Look for Small Business Grants?

Unlike loans, grants are essentially free money intended to foster entrepreneurship, innovation, and economic growth. These funds are usually provided by government agencies, non-profits, foundations, and private sector partners with specific objectives in mind—whether it’s promoting green technologies, supporting minority entrepreneurs, or revitalizing rural economies.

Here are some compelling reasons grants should be part of your funding strategy:

  • Non-dilutive Capital: No need to give up ownership or control.
  • Accessible to Diverse Businesses: Grants target various demographics, sectors, and regions.
  • Catalyst for Growth: Can fund research and development, marketing, hiring, or infrastructure upgrades.
  • Boost Credibility: Winning a grant often increases visibility and trust among investors and customers.

Navigating the grant landscape can seem overwhelming, but understanding the major programs and their criteria will give you a clear advantage.

Top Small Business Grants Available This Year

1. Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR) and Small Business Technology Transfer (STTR) Programs

The SBIR and STTR are two of the most prestigious and competitive federal grant programs aimed at fostering innovation in small businesses. Focused specifically on technological advancements and research, these grants provide funding during early-stage development.

  • Who is Eligible? Small businesses with fewer than 500 employees that are based in the U.S.; must be majority-owned by U.S. citizens or permanent residents. SBIR allows the business to perform research in-house, while STTR requires collaboration with a research institution.
  • Grant Size: Phase I awards typically range from $50,000 to $250,000 over 6 to 12 months; Phase II offers larger funding, often up to $1 million.
  • Sector Focus: Technology-driven fields including biotechnology, energy, defense, and information technology.
  • Application Tips: Align proposals with agency-specific priorities; focus on technical merit, commercial potential, and innovation.

The SBIR/STTR programs offer a multi-phased funding opportunity that can lead to significant growth and partnerships with federal agencies.

2. Economic Development Administration (EDA) Grants

The EDA offers grants aimed at revitalizing distressed communities by supporting small businesses that stimulate economic growth, innovation, and job creation.

  • Who Can Apply? Small businesses partnering with local or regional development agencies; nonprofits and state or local governments may also apply for community project grants.
  • Types of Grants: Public works grants for infrastructure, technical assistance grants for innovation centers, and planning grants.
  • Focus Areas: Projects generating jobs, increasing economic diversification, and fostering innovation ecosystems.
  • How to Benefit: Collaborate with regional economic development organizations for stronger application packages.

EDA grants are well-suited to businesses looking to expand operations in economically challenged areas or those that can serve as economic catalysts in their communities.

3. The Amber Grant for Women Entrepreneurs

Named in memory of a young woman entrepreneur, the Amber Grant awards monthly and annual grants to female business owners pursuing innovative ventures.

  • Eligibility: Women-owned small businesses or startups in any industry.
  • Award Amount: $10,000 monthly grant winners with a $25,000 annual grant awarded to one of the monthly recipients.
  • Unique Advantage: Simple application process focusing on business passion and vision.
  • Use of Funds: Can be used for anything to support business growth: marketing, product development, or operational costs.

This grant encourages and empowers women entrepreneurs with accessible funding and exposure opportunities, making it one of the most prominent women-focused grants.

4. USDA Rural Business Development Grants (RBDG)

For small businesses operating in rural communities, the USDA’s RBDG program offers funding to promote economic development and job creation.

  • Who Qualifies? Small businesses and nonprofit organizations in USDA-designated rural areas.
  • Grant Amounts: Typically between $10,000 and $500,000 depending on project scope.
  • Purpose: Business expansion, technical assistance, training, and infrastructure improvements.
  • Advice: Emphasize local economic impact and sustainability in your proposal.

RBDG grants help bridge funding gaps for rural enterprises that may struggle to access conventional financing models.

5. Minority Business Development Agency (MBDA) Grants

MBDA is dedicated to supporting minority-owned businesses through grants and business centers nationwide.

  • Who is Eligible? Minority-owned businesses such as African Americans, Hispanic Americans, Asian Americans, Native Americans, and Pacific Islanders.
  • Grant Programs: Capacity-building, access to capital initiatives, and market expansion grants.
  • Benefits: Assistance goes beyond funds; includes networking, mentorship, and federal contracting opportunities.
  • Maximizing Potential: Apply through local MBDA business centers for direct guidance and support.

Minority entrepreneurs can tap into MBDA resources to secure funds and build business capabilities designed to foster long-term success.

6. National Association for the Self-Employed (NASE) Growth Grants

NASE supports self-employed professionals and micro-businesses with targeted grant programs aimed at business development.

  • Eligibility: NASE members running micro or small businesses.
  • Grant Size: Up to $4,000 per award.
  • Use of Funds: Expansion, marketing, hiring, equipment purchases, or professional services.
  • How to Qualify: Must be a paid NASE member in good standing and create a convincing growth plan.

These grants are ideal for entrepreneurs who need smaller but flexible financial boosts to implement concrete growth strategies.

7. Local and State Government Grants

Many state governments and municipalities allocate funds to support local small businesses, often with various focus areas like technology, green business, or tourism.

  • Finding Opportunities: Monitor state economic development websites, local chambers of commerce, and municipal business departments.
  • Application Requirements: Often include proof of local operation, job creation plans, or community engagement.
  • Examples: New York’s Excelsior Jobs Program, California Small Business COVID-19 Relief Grants, Texas Enterprise Fund.
  • Pro Tip: Develop relationships with local officials and participate in business development workshops to stay informed.

Local grants can be less competitive than federal ones and offer a pathway to build regional partnerships.

Tips for Writing a Winning Grant Application

Securing a grant is competitive and requires strategic preparation. Here’s how to position your business proposals for success:

  • Understand the Grant’s Purpose: Tailor your application to show how your business aligns with the grantor’s mission and objectives.
  • Be Specific and Measurable: Detail how funds will be used with clear, achievable goals.
  • Highlight Community Impact: Demonstrate how your business contributes to job creation, innovation, or social good.
  • Engage Professional Help: Consider consultants or grant writers experienced in the particular grant program.
  • Prepare Detailed Budgets: Provide transparent, realistic financial plans and justify expenses.
  • Follow Instructions Precisely: Grants often have strict guidelines; missing details can disqualify your application.
  • Use Data and Stories: Combine statistics with compelling narratives to make your case memorable.

Well-prepared applications improve chances of winning and can impress future investors or partners even if the grant isn’t awarded immediately.

Beyond Grants: Complementary Funding Strategies

While grants are fantastic resources, they rarely cover all capital needs. Combining grants with other funding sources can maximize your business’s growth potential:

  • Microloans: Often more accessible and have lower interest than traditional business loans.
  • Angel Investors and Venture Capital: Suitable for high-growth startups looking for mentorship and capital.
  • Crowdfunding: Useful for product launches and community-backed projects.
  • Revenue-Based Financing: Enables flexible repayments correlated with sales.
  • Business Competitions: Many offer cash prizes and visibility.

Diversifying funding streams ensures that your business can adapt and scale efficiently.

Final Thoughts

The landscape for small business grants in 2024 is rich with opportunities for entrepreneurs who invest the time to tailor powerful applications and leverage multiple programs. From federal innovation research awards to niche local grants and minority-focused funding, there’s a potential source of capital suited to nearly every business type.

Start by assessing your business goals, identifying grants that align with your industry and mission, and preparing thorough applications. Keep abreast of deadlines and program updates, and don’t hesitate to seek advice from business development centers and grant professionals.

With persistence and strategy, securing a grant can be a transformative funding boost, paving the way for your business to thrive in today’s competitive market.

Resources for Grant Research and Application:

  • Grants.gov: Centralized portal for federal grants.
  • SBA.gov: Small Business Administration resources and funding opportunities.
  • Local Small Business Development Centers (SBDCs): Free consulting and information.
  • Minority Business Development Agency (MBDA): Support for minority entrepreneurs.
  • State and Municipal Economic Development Agencies: Regional grant listings and support.

Empowered with the right funding, your small business can tackle challenges, innovate boldly, and seize new market opportunities in 2024 and beyond.
