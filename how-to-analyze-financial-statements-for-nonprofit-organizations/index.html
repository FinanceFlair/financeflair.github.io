How to Analyze Financial Statements for Nonprofit Organizations


Understanding financial statements is essential for anyone involved with nonprofit organizations. Whether you’re a board member, executive director, donor, or volunteer, gaining insights into the organization’s financial health ensures accountability, transparency, and long-term sustainability. However, analyzing financial statements for nonprofits differs in some respects from analyzing the financials of for-profit businesses. This guide breaks down the key components and provides a step-by-step approach to interpreting nonprofit financial statements effectively.

The Unique Nature of Nonprofit Financials

Nonprofit organizations operate with a mission-driven focus rather than an emphasis on profit generation. This fundamental difference impacts the structure and terminology used in their financial statements. For instance, nonprofits do not have “owners” or “shareholders,” and their goal is often to maximize the impact of their charitable work rather than maximize revenue.

Instead of focusing on profit margins, the financial health of a nonprofit is evaluated based on how resources are being deployed to achieve its mission, how well it manages cash flow, and its ability to sustain operations in the long term. To fully understand a nonprofit’s financial standing, it’s essential to know the basics of its core financial documents.

Key Financial Statements for Nonprofits

Nonprofits use three primary financial statements to report their financial position and activities:

1. Statement of Financial Position

Often referred to as the “balance sheet” in for-profits, the statement of financial position provides a snapshot of the organization’s financial health at a specific point in time. It includes three core elements:

  • Assets: Resources the nonprofit owns, such as cash, investments, receivables, equipment, or property.
  • Liabilities: Obligations the nonprofit owes, including accounts payable, loans, and accrued expenses.
  • Net Assets: The difference between total assets and liabilities. Net assets are classified into two categories:
    • Without Donor Restrictions: Funds that can be spent on any of the organization’s operations.
    • With Donor Restrictions: Funds restricted by donors for particular purposes or time periods.

2. Statement of Activities

This document is akin to the income statement in the for-profit world. It summarizes the organization’s revenue and expenses over a particular period, providing insight into whether the nonprofit had a surplus (positive net income) or deficit (negative net income). Revenue and support are divided into categories:

  • Revenue Without Donor Restrictions: Contributions, program fees, investment income, and grants that the organization can use freely.
  • Revenue With Donor Restrictions: Funds given for a specific goal or program.
  • Expenses: Grouped into program expenses, management and general expenses, and fundraising expenses. This categorization showcases how resources are allocated to achieve the mission.

3. Statement of Cash Flows

The statement of cash flows tracks the actual movement of cash in and out of the nonprofit, separated into three activities:

  • Operating Activities: Day-to-day operating cash inflows and outflows, such as donations, salaries, and rent.
  • Investing Activities: Cash used for purchasing or selling long-term assets, such as property or equipment.
  • Financing Activities: Cash flows from borrowing or repaying loans, as well as endowment-related activities.

By analyzing this statement, you can evaluate whether the nonprofit is generating enough cash to sustain its operations and manage unexpected financial challenges.

How to Analyze Nonprofit Financial Statements

Once you’re familiar with the components of these financial statements, it’s time to dive deeper into understanding what they reveal about the organization.

1. Assess Liquidity

Liquidity is the nonprofit’s ability to meet short-term obligations. A quick way to measure liquidity is by calculating the current ratio:

Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities

A ratio above 1 indicates good short-term financial health, as the nonprofit has enough assets to cover its liabilities. You should also pay attention to unrestricted net assets, as these funds represent financial flexibility.

2. Evaluate Revenue Streams

Reviewing the statement of activities enables you to assess the diversity and reliability of revenue streams. Nonprofits should aim for a well-rounded variety of funding sources to reduce dependency on a single donor or program. Pay attention to:

  • The proportion of revenue with vs. without donor restrictions.
  • The stability of recurring revenue from memberships, grants, or contracts.
  • Significant fluctuations in funding year over year, which could signal vulnerability.

3. Examine Expenses and Allocation

A nonprofit’s expenses can reveal how effectively it uses its resources to achieve its mission. Focus on the organization’s program expense ratio, which measures how much of total expenses are allocated to program services:

Program Expense Ratio = Program Expenses / Total Expenses

GuideStar and other nonprofit evaluators recommend that this ratio exceed 65% to demonstrate efficient use of funds for mission-related activities. However, make sure to also review administrative and fundraising expenses so you can evaluate how the organization balances operational efficiency with investment in growth.

4. Review Trends and Changes Over Time

Analyzing financial statements across multiple years allows you to identify trends. For example:

  • Are total revenues and expenses showing consistent growth?
  • Is the organization maintaining surpluses, or does it show frequent deficits?
  • Is the balance of net assets with donor restrictions changing significantly?

Looking at trends helps assess whether the nonprofit is on a sustainable path or facing financial challenges that require attention.

5. Analyze Cash Flow

The statement of cash flows offers a window into the organization’s liquidity and operational efficiency. Focus on:

  • Whether operating activities generate positive cash flow. Consistent negative cash flow from operations could be a red flag.
  • Large swings in cash flow from investing or financing activities, which could reflect significant one-time events like asset purchases or loan repayments.

Red Flags to Watch For

When analyzing nonprofit financial statements, keep an eye out for potential warning signs that may indicate financial instability or poor management:

  • Negative Unrestricted Net Assets: This indicates the organization has more obligations than unrestricted resources, posing a risk to sustainability.
  • Excessive Fundraising Costs: Spending a disproportionate amount on fundraising without corresponding growth in revenue could signal inefficiency.
  • Unexplained Deficits: Repeated deficits without clear justification can indicate poor financial planning or instability.
  • Heavy Reliance on a Single Donor: Dependence on one or two major sources of funding exposes the nonprofit to risk if those sources are lost.

Tips for Accessing and Understanding Financial Information

Nonprofits are required to file Form 990 with the IRS, which provides detailed information on their finances, governance, and more. As a publicly available document, Form 990 is an invaluable resource for assessing an organization’s accountability and transparency. Additionally, many nonprofits publish annual reports or audited financial statements, offering further insights.

When diving into these documents, remember to look beyond just the numbers. Financial statements only tell part of the story—they should be interpreted alongside strategic plans and programmatic outcomes to gain a comprehensive understanding of how the nonprofit is performing.

Conclusion

Analyzing financial statements for nonprofit organizations is a valuable skill that informs decision-making and promotes transparency and stewardship. By systematically reviewing the statement of financial position, statement of activities, and statement of cash flows, you can evaluate the organization’s financial stability, resource allocation, and overall health. Always pair financial analysis with an understanding of the nonprofit’s mission and goals to form a holistic picture of its impact and sustainability.

Whether you’re looking to donate wisely, guide an organization as a board member, or ensure accountability as a staff leader, understanding nonprofit financial statements empowers you to make informed, mission-driven decisions.
