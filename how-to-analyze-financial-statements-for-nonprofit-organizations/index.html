How to Analyze Financial Statements for Nonprofit Organizations


Financial statements are essential tools for understanding the financial health of any organization, and nonprofit organizations are no exception. Whether you are a nonprofit manager, a board member, a donor, or simply someone curious about nonprofit finances, being able to analyze these documents will allow you to evaluate the transparency, accountability, and operational efficiency of the organization.

In this guide, we’ll walk through how to scrutinize the key financial statements of a nonprofit organization, interpret the data they contain, and use that information to make informed decisions.

Understanding Nonprofit Financial Statements

Nonprofit organizations use financial statements differently than for-profit businesses. While for-profit entities focus primarily on profit generation, nonprofits aim to fulfill their mission while sustainably managing their resources. As such, the main financial statements for nonprofits revolve around two core principles: accountability and stewardship.

The most common financial statements for nonprofit organizations include:

  1. Statement of Financial Position (Balance Sheet)
  2. Statement of Activities (Income Statement)
  3. Statement of Cash Flows
  4. Statement of Functional Expenses

Let’s delve into each of these statements and explore how to analyze them effectively.

Statement of Financial Position (Balance Sheet)

The statement of financial position provides a snapshot of the organization’s financial standing at a specific point in time. It highlights the nonprofit’s assets, liabilities, and net assets.

Key Components to Analyze:

  1. Assets:
    Examine both current and non-current assets. Current assets like cash, accounts receivable, and short-term investments represent resources that can be readily used. Non-current assets, such as property or long-term investments, indicate longer-term stability.

    • Look for trends: Are cash reserves healthy? Is there over-reliance on receivables or restricted funds?
    • Evaluate liquidity: Are there sufficient current assets to cover current liabilities?

  2. Liabilities:
    Liabilities are the obligations owed by the nonprofit. These could include accounts payable, loans, or deferred revenue. Pay attention to short-term vs. long-term liabilities.

    • Assess the liability structure: Does the organization have manageable debt levels?
    • Monitor trends: Is the nonprofit taking on more debt over time, and if so, is it justified by expansion or growth?

  3. Net Assets:
    Nonprofits report net assets in two categories:

    • Without donor restrictions: Funds the organization can generally use at its discretion.
    • With donor restrictions: Funds tied to specific purposes per donor instructions.

    A healthy ratio of unrestricted and restricted net assets indicates financial flexibility and the ability to respond to unexpected needs or emergencies.

Questions to Ask:

  • Does the organization have enough liquid assets to meet short-term needs?
  • Is it financially stable without excessive liabilities?
  • Are restricted funds properly segregated?

Statement of Activities (Income Statement)

The statement of activities outlines the revenues and expenses over a specific reporting period, offering insight into the nonprofit’s operational performance.

Key Components to Analyze:

  1. Revenue:
    Revenue for nonprofits comes from various sources, including contributions, grants, program service fees, investment income, and fundraising events. You’ll want to evaluate:

    • Diversity of revenue streams: Dependence on one or two funding sources poses significant risks, especially if those sources decline.
    • Reliability of funding: Are revenue sources stable, recurring, and aligned with the organization’s mission?

  2. Expenses:
    Nonprofit expenses are typically categorized as:

    • Program Expenses: Directly tied to the organization’s mission and programs.

    • Management and General Expenses: Administrative and operational costs.

    • Fundraising Expenses: Costs incurred to raise funds.

    • Assess the ratio of program expenses to total expenses: A higher percentage indicates efficient resource allocation toward fulfilling the mission.

    • Track trends in administrative and fundraising expenses: Excessive growth here may suggest operational inefficiency.

  3. Net Income (Change in Net Assets):
    This is the nonprofit equivalent of “profit.” While not the goal of nonprofits, a positive change in net assets shows financial sustainability, whereas consistent deficits may signal trouble.

Questions to Ask:

  • Are revenues diversified and aligned with the mission?
  • Is the organization allocating the majority of its funds to program expenses?
  • Does the nonprofit show a surplus or deficit at the end of the period?

Statement of Cash Flows

The statement of cash flows reveals how cash is generated and used across three key activities: operating, investing, and financing. For nonprofits, cash flow analysis is vital to understand liquidity.

Key Components to Analyze:

  1. Operating Activities:
    Cash inflows and outflows related to daily operations, such as donations received and payments for program services. Consistent positive cash flow from operating activities is a strong indicator of financial health.

  2. Investing Activities:
    Includes purchases or sales of property, equipment, or investments. Be cautious if too much cash is tied up in non-liquid assets, potentially impairing the nonprofit’s flexibility.

  3. Financing Activities:
    Relates to activities like loans or financing of restricted funds. Nonprofits should use financing activities strategically and avoid over-reliance on debt.

Questions to Ask:

  • Is operating cash flow sufficient to sustain operations?
  • Are investments being made prudently to enhance services or mission impact?
  • Is the nonprofit maintaining healthy cash reserves for emergencies?

Statement of Functional Expenses

Unique to nonprofits, the statement of functional expenses breaks down expenses by both function (program, management, fundraising) and nature (salaries, rent, supplies, etc.). This level of transparency helps stakeholders assess efficiency and accountability.

Key Components to Analyze:

  1. Program Expense Ratio:
    A common metric in nonprofit evaluation is the percentage of total expenses devoted to programs. A ratio above 75% is generally considered high-performing.

  2. Fundraising Efficiency:
    Calculate the cost to raise $1. Low fundraising costs are desirable, but extremely low costs could suggest underinvestment in revenue-generating activities.

  3. Administrative Cost Control:
    Administrative expenses should be reasonable but adequate for maintaining quality operations.

Questions to Ask:

  • Are program expenses consistently prioritized?
  • Is fundraising showing a solid return on investment?
  • Are administrative costs proportional to organizational size and complexity?

Assessing Long-Term Sustainability

Beyond individual financial statements, it’s important to interpret financial data holistically to gauge the long-term sustainability of a nonprofit. Key considerations include:

Trends Analysis:

  • Are revenues steadily growing or at least keeping pace with inflation and rising expenses?
  • Are deficits or surpluses part of a recurring pattern?

Reserves and Liquidity:

  • Does the organization have sufficient reserves to weather challenging financial periods?
  • Are liquid assets readily available to cover urgent needs?

Operating Margin and Efficiency:

  • Is the nonprofit generating a modest surplus regularly?
  • Are operational costs in proportion to revenue and mission activities?

External Risks:

Understand potential risks, such as shifts in donor priorities, changes in government funding, or economic downturns, and assess how prepared the organization is to respond.

Final Thoughts

Analyzing financial statements for nonprofit organizations may seem daunting at first, but with practice, the process becomes an invaluable tool for assessing financial health and mission alignment. When reviewing these documents, approach the data holistically—no single metric tells the full story.

Transparency, accountability, and sustainable operations are the cornerstones of a well-run nonprofit. By understanding how to read and analyze financial statements, you’ll be equipped to make informed decisions and support organizations that truly make a difference.
