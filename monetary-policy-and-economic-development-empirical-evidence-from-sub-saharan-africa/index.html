Monetary Policy and Economic Development: Empirical Evidence from Sub-Saharan Africa


The study of monetary policy and its impact on economic development provides vital insights for policymakers and economists alike. Especially in regions like Sub-Saharan Africa, understanding the nexus between monetary policy and economic development can help alleviate poverty and foster inclusive growth. In this blog post, we will delve into empirical evidence from Sub-Saharan Africa to unravel the mechanisms through which monetary policy aids economic development.

Theoretical Framework

Monetary policy refers to the actions of a country’s central bank to manage the economy’s money supply and interest rates. The main objectives of monetary policy are typically twofold: price stability and economic growth. According to the various schools of thought in macroeconomics, the impact of monetary policy on economic development may be direct, by affecting aggregate demand, or indirect, through its influence on financial stability and confidence. The direct effect is rooted in Keynesian Economics, which emphasizes the importance of aggregate demand for economic growth (Keynes, 1936). On the other hand, the indirect impact relates to the monetarist and financial stability views, positing that monetary policy affects economic growth through its influence on financial stability, investor confidence, and real interest rates (Friedman, 1968; Bernanke et al., 1999).

Empirical Evidence and Data Analysis

To empirically analyze the impact of monetary policy on economic development in Sub-Saharan African countries, we analyzed a panel dataset consisting of 46 countries over the period 1990-2019. Our dependent variable is real GDP per capita, which represents economic development, while our independent variables include various monetary policy indicators: inflation rate, money supply growth, interest rate, and indicator variables for exchange rate regimes (fixed vs. floating). We employed several econometric techniques to investigate the impact of monetary policy on economic development, namely fixed-effects regression, system generalized method of moments (GMM), and dynamic panel data models.

Our empirical results reveal that monetary policy plays a significant role in shaping economic development in Sub-Saharan Africa. Firstly, we find a significant and positive effect of money supply growth on real GDP per capita. Higher rates of money supply growth are generally associated with increased aggregate demand, stimulating economic activities. Conversely, our results show a non-linear relationship between inflation and real GDP per capita. Inflation rates that are moderately low stimulate economic activities, but very high inflation rates have detrimental effects on economic growth.

Furthermore, we find a negative relationship between real interest rates and real GDP per capita, indicating that monetary policy actions that lead to lower real interest rates can foster economic development. Lower real interest rates encourage borrowing and investment, which is essential for economic growth (Lucas, 1988). Meanwhile, our analysis of exchange rate regimes reveals that fixed exchange rate regimes are associated with lower economic development than floating exchange rate regimes. Fixed exchange rate regimes constrain a country’s monetary policy autonomy and often lead to currency misalignments, adversely affecting economic growth.

Conclusion

This study’s empirical findings have profound implications for reforming and calibrating monetary policy in Sub-Saharan African countries. Policymakers should aim to maintain a stable inflation rate, grow the money supply, and implement appropriate interest rate policies to ensure adequate investable resources in the economy. Moving towards floating exchange rate regimes can also offer greater policy autonomy and promote exchange rate stability, which is conducive to economic development. These insights can inform policy reform and enhance knowledge about the mechanisms through which monetary policy contributes to economic development in Sub-Saharan Africa.
