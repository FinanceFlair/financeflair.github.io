Short Term Trading: Trading the Flag and Pennant Patterns


Flag and pennant patterns are versatile and powerful chart patterns that traders have been using for years to make informed decisions. These formations can indicate a major shift in a security’s movement and often signal impending price movements. By understanding these patterns, traders can maximize their profit potential and minimize risks.

Flag and pennant patterns are types of consolidation formations, which occur when price momentum slows down, resulting in a period of consolidation before a continuation of the original trend. They are typically found on daily or hourly charts and are composed of a flagpole and a corresponding flag or pennant. Let’s dive deeper into the two different patterns.

Trading the Flag Pattern

A flag pattern resembles a rectangle or a flag on a chart. The flag is usually constructed after a sharp price movement in one direction (the flagpole) and consists of two parallel lines. The upper trendline connects the highest points made during the flag formation, while the lower trendline connects the lowest points. The flag’s length can be shorter or longer than the flagpole, which serves as the key to trading this formation.

Once a flag pattern is identified, traders can place their buy/sell orders along the break-out zone – usually where the two parallel lines converge, usually adjacent to the resistance or support levels. When price breaks out above the upper trendline in an upward flag or below the lower trendline in a downward flag, it is generally a signal that the original trend will resume, and buyers or sellers will take control.

It’s wise to consider other factors when trading flag patterns, such as moving averages, volume fluctuations, and any underlying news or events. This additional analysis will increase the accuracy of the trade setup.

Trading the Pennant Pattern

A pennant pattern is similar to a flag pattern but has a more triangular appearance due to its sloping parallel lines. The pennant is formed as a continuation pattern after the initial price movement, flagpole, resembling a triangle or a small congestion zone on the chart.

Pennant patterns can be ascending, descending, or symmetric, with the latter being the most common. Similar to flag patterns, when price breaks out of the pennant, the original trend usually resumes. As a trader, you want to focus on the break-out point and enter the trade accordingly.

Trading Strategies for Flag and Pennant Patterns

Successful flag and pennant trading involves setting up positions with stop-losses to minimize risk while allowing for significant profit margins. Here’s a basic example of how to trade these patterns:

  1. Identifying the Pattern: Look for a distinct flag or pennant formation on your chosen security’s chart, along with a clear flagpole preceding the formation.
  2. Set Your Entry Point: When the price breaks out from the flag or pennant, enter your position in the predicted direction of the original trend.
  3. Managing Risks: Place your stop-loss orders anywhere from 10% to 15% beyond the appearing stop-out point and monitor price movements keenly.
  4. Taking Profits: Implement a calculated take-profit order to secure profits when the desired price level is reached.

Flag and pennant patterns offer traders an excellent window into price movements. When effectively identified and managed, they can translate into substantial gains, bringing out the best from short term trading strategies.

Remember, while trading flag and pennant patterns can be profitable, unsuccessful trades are inevitable. So, it is crucial to employ proper money management techniques and risk assessment strategies. With practice, you can successfully incorporate these formations into your trading tool set, leading to improved profitability in the long run.
