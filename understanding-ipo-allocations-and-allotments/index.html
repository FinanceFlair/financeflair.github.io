Understanding IPO Allocations and Allotments


An Initial Public Offering (IPO) is an essential process for companies seeking capital for expansion or organizational growth. IPOs provide investors with the opportunity to buy newly released stocks in the company’s shares. However, one of the most critical aspects of an IPO process is the allocation and allotment of shares. This process can be complex, and many investors often have questions about how allocations and allotments work during an IPO. In this article, we will provide a comprehensive understanding of IPO allocations and allotments and explore the implications for investors.

What Are IPO Allocations and Allotments?

Before diving into the details, let’s begin by defining the terms.

Allocation in the context of an IPO refers to the distribution of shares among the various categories of investors. These categories include retail investors, institutional investors, and insiders.

Allotment, on the other hand, refers to the actual allotting or granting of shares to investors after the subscription process.

Both these terms are crucial for the complete understanding of an IPO process. The following sections will explore these concepts in more detail.

Understanding IPO Allocation

To begin, understanding IPO allocations requires first understanding the types of investors involved in the IPO process.

  • Retail Investors: These are individual or small-scale investors who usually invest in IPOs through a stockbroker. They typically provide a significant portion of the funds in an IPO.
  • Institutional Investors: These are larger, more established investors, such as mutual funds, insurance companies, pension funds, and banks. They often have a significant impact on the demand for and Pricing of IPOs.
  • Insiders: These are the company’s employees, shareholders, and promoters. They have an inside view of the company’s operations and can provide valuable insight into the company’s future performance.

During the allocation process, the underwriters or investment banks managing the IPO will determine the proportion of shares each category should receive. The primary factors determining allocation include the investor’s historical trading activity, risk profile, and market demand for the IPO.

Allotment Process in IPO

The allotment process begins after the subscription period ends. During this time, the underwriters verify the eligibility of the investors, compute the share allocation, and grant shares to successful applicants.

Demand-allotment occurs when investors have subscribed to more shares than the company offered. In this scenario, the underwriters may select investors randomly or prioritize investors based on their category or investment history.

Offer-allotment occurs when more shares have been offered than subscribed. In this scenario, the underwriters may offer shares to a particular category of investors or follow a specific policy to fulfill the offer.

Short allotment occurs when subscribers receive less than the number of shares they applied for. In this case, investors can request their money back or opt to receive shares in the secondary market.

Long allotment happens when subscribers receive more shares than they applied for. In this case, the excess shares can be sold in the secondary market or sometimes returned for a refund.

IPO Greenshoe Option

The Greenshoe option is a mechanism used by underwriters to manage fluctuations in demand and pricing after the IPO. This provision allows the underwriters to purchase and sell shares in the secondary market to meet the potential demand beyond the initial offer.

IPO Lock-in Period

A lock-in period is a time in which insiders and underwriters are restricted from selling their shares after the IPO. This provision aims to minimize short-term price volatility and provide a fair opportunity for other investors to sell their shares.

Conclusion

Understanding the allocation and allotment process is essential for investors participating in an IPO. While the process can be complex, this guide should provide a solid foundation for navigating the allocation and allotment of shares during an IPO. By keeping these concepts in mind, investors can make informed decisions about investing in IPOs and potentially reap the benefits of future company growth.
