How to Use a Spendthrift Trust to Protect Inheritances


As individuals, we often focus on accumulating wealth without considering what might happen to our assets after we're gone. An inheritance can be a blessing, but it can also be a curse if not managed properly. This is where a spendthrift trust comes in - a powerful tool that can safeguard inheritances and ensure they're used for the intended purpose. What is a Spendthrift Trust? A spendthrift trust, also known as a protective trust or discretionary trust, is a type of irrevocable trust designed to protect inheritances from being squandered or depleted by the recipient's poor financial decision-making. The trust is typically set up by the settlor (the person creating the trust) to benefit a beneficiary, such as a child or grandchild. The Benefits of a Spendthrift Trust Before we dive into the details, it's essential to understand the benefits of a spendthrift trust. Here are a few reasons why this type of trust is a valuable asset protection tool:

Protection from creditors: A spendthrift trust shields the inheritance from creditors, asset collectors, and lawsuits, ensuring the beneficiary's assets remain safe. Limited access to funds: The trust is typically set up with a specific amount or percentage that the beneficiary can access each year, preventing them from squandering the entire inheritance all at once. Tax benefits: Spendthrift trusts can be designed to minimize taxes and ensure the trust assets remain untaxed, preserving more of the inheritance for the beneficiary. Flexibility: Spendthrift trusts can be tailored to meet the specific needs of the beneficiary, including providing for their education, healthcare, or other expenses.

How a Spendthrift Trust Works To understand the mechanics of a spendthrift trust, let's break down the key components:

Trustee: The trustee is responsible for managing the trust assets, making decisions about investments, and distributing funds according to the trust's rules. Beneficiary: The beneficiary is the individual who benefits from the trust and receives the income and assets. Trust assets: The trust assets include the property, investments, and funds set aside for the beneficiary's benefit. Life estate: The life estate is the amount of time the trust exists, often tied to the settlor's life or a specific period.

Setting Up a Spendthrift Trust To establish a spendthrift trust, the settlor must follow these steps:

Consult with an attorney: Work with a qualified estate planning attorney to create a customized trust document. Fund the trust: Transfer the initial assets to the trust. Name a trustee: Appoint a trustee who will manage the trust assets. Determine the trust terms: Specify the trust's rules, including the beneficiary's rights and limitations.

Common Scenarios for a Spendthrift Trust Spendthrift trusts are particularly useful in situations where the beneficiary may not be mature enough to handle finances responsibly, such as:

Minor beneficiaries: Children or grandchildren who are still minors and need protection from financial reckless behavior. Beneficiaries with a history of financial issues: Individuals who have a history of overspending, addiction, or poor financial management. Beneficiaries with special needs: Individuals with special needs or disabilities requiring a trust to ensure their inheritance remains safe.

Alternatives to Spendthrift Trusts While a spendthrift trust is a powerful tool, it may not be the best fit for everyone. Alternative options include:

Trust Amendments: Modifying an existing trust to include spendthrift provisions. Standing Trusts: A trust with a trustor and a transfer of beneficial interest to another entity or trustee, rather like the spendthrift trust. Minor settlors' extension trust: Designed for juveniles to use money in a more flexible and liberal way for example allowing expenditure for travel or utility bill pay
