How to Use Relative Strength Index (RSI) with Fundamentals


Understanding the market feels a lot like trying to solve a puzzle. It’s not enough to recognize a single piece; the picture only emerges when multiple pieces fit together. Two of the most telling clues in this vast picture are the Relative Strength Index (RSI) and company fundamentals. In isolation, RSI signals potential price reversals, while fundamental analysis reveals the true worth of an asset. But when these powerful tools are combined, investors can gain unique insights and make sharper, more confident decisions.

Let’s dive deep into the art and science of weaving together RSI and fundamentals to unlock market-beating strategies.

Demystifying RSI: What Does It Really Tell You?

First, let’s clarify what the Relative Strength Index actually measures. RSI is a momentum oscillator that moves between 0 and 100, gauging the speed and change of price movements. Traditionally, technical analysts consider a stock overbought above 70 and oversold below 30. This signal can be useful in spotting short-term inflection points, but by itself, RSI has limitations—especially in trending markets.

A trader might spot an “overbought” condition and prepare to sell, only to watch in dismay as the price powers even higher. RSI’s strength lies in its ability to warn of froth or exhaustion, but it doesn’t distinguish between good companies and bad ones. That’s where fundamental analysis steps in.

Unpacking the Power of Fundamentals

Fundamental analysis strips away the market’s emotional noise and looks at cold, hard numbers. Think revenue growth, profitability ratios, debt levels, earnings reports, and sector trends. This approach is about understanding what the business is worth regardless of what the market says about its price. It answers: Should this company be worth this much?

Quality fundamentals allow investors to:

  • Identify sustainable business models
  • Avoid value traps (cheap stocks that stay cheap)
  • Unearth growth catalysts that technicals might overlook
  • Set realistic price targets and long-term expectations

Relying solely on technicals or momentum indicators like RSI can backfire if the underlying business is deteriorating—or outperforming for good reason.

The Magic Happens at the Intersection

So, how do you actually blend the Relative Strength Index with fundamental analysis? The secret is to let each tool do what it does best, and to have them “cross-confirm” your thesis. This combination filters out much of the market’s noise, so that only the highest probability trades or investments remain.

Let’s break down the workflow:

1. Use Fundamentals as a First Filter

Begin by screening for stocks (or assets) with solid fundamentals. For example:

  • Consistent revenue and earnings growth
  • Strong return on equity or invested capital
  • Manageable debt levels relative to cash flow
  • Positive industry or sector outlook
  • Reasonable or undervalued price-to-earnings and price-to-book ratios

This step ensures you’re focusing only on investment-worthy companies. It helps avoid getting lured by technical signals on fundamentally weak stocks—where overbought conditions can simply precede a bigger plunge.

2. Deploy RSI to Pinpoint Entries and Exits

With a shortlist of fundamentally sound stocks, turn to the Relative Strength Index for tactical decisions. For instance:

  • If a fundamentally strong stock’s RSI sinks below 30 and then turns upward, it may signal a rare buying opportunity—the company is oversold, not because it’s broken, but likely due to market panic or short-term noise.
  • Conversely, when a fundamentally weak stock flashes an “oversold” RSI, it’s often a value trap. Steer clear.

On the exit side, if RSI approaches 70 or higher for a stock you own and fundamentals haven’t changed, consider taking some profits. The overbought signal might mean the upside is limited in the short run, even though the long-term picture is intact.

3. Spotting Divergences for Added Insight

One advanced strategy involves seeking “divergences” between price movement and RSI, confirmed by fundamentals. For instance:

  • If a growth stock with strong earnings momentum is making new highs, but RSI is making lower highs (bearish divergence), it may hint at a short-term exhaustion—providing a timely pause to assess or reduce exposure.
  • If a value stock’s price makes new lows but RSI is making higher lows (bullish divergence) and fundamentals remain robust, it could set up a classic mean-reversion opportunity.

Case Study: Marrying RSI & Fundamentals in Action

Suppose you’ve screened for companies that reported above-consensus earnings, sustained double-digit revenue growth, and healthy balance sheets. You then select a company in this cohort—let’s call it “Innovatech Inc.”

After a stellar earnings report, the stock surges, and RSI enters “overbought” territory above 75. Rather than selling immediately, you examine the fundamentals:

  • Profit and revenue growth aligns with industry trends
  • Management commentary suggests growth is sustainable
  • Analyst upgrades abound

With this confirmation, you might opt to hold, betting the strong trend has legs. If, instead, you see weak forward guidance or deteriorating margins, the RSI signal (overbought) plays a bigger role in your decision to lock in profit.

Fast forward a few months: a sector-wide selloff drags all stocks lower and Innovatech’s price dips, pushing RSI under 30. Fundamentals remain intact—no negative developments—suggesting an overreaction by the market. This could be a high-quality entry point.

Practical Tips for Getting the Balance Right

Backtest your strategy. Before combining RSI and fundamentals with real capital, study historical performance on your chosen stocks or sectors. Find which blend of signals—such as combining an RSI below 35 with dividend increases—yields the best risk/return.

Avoid chasing extreme readings. RSI isn’t built for buy-and-hold strategies. If fundamentals deteriorate, don’t let an “oversold” RSI coax you into a falling knife.

Stay wary of earnings season. RSI values can swing wildly after earnings surprises. Confirm post-earnings price moves with updated fundamentals and guidance.

Mix with other indicators. RSI and fundamentals together are powerful, but not infallible. Confirm your thesis with other signals, such as volume analysis or moving averages.

Keep your thesis flexible. Markets adapt and so should you. If the narrative for a stock changes, don’t stick with a strategy just because indicators once aligned.

Real-World Examples: Winners and Warnings

Tesla in 2020-2021. TSLA’s fundamentals improved dramatically with sustained profits, growth in China, and addition to the S&P 500. RSI signaled “overbought” conditions multiple times. Investors who sold solely based on RSI missed a much larger move driven by fundamentals. However, using RSI to time partial exits likely improved returns for traders unwilling to buy and hold through volatility.

Bed Bath & Beyond during the meme stock craze. RSI screamed “overbought” as price exploded. Fundamentals were dire—declining revenue, negative earnings. Combining these factors flagged a sell or avoid signal. Those ignoring the warning from either technicals or fundamentals got burned in the sharp reversals.

Building a Repeatable Process

Develop a checklist that you consult before every trade or investment. Here’s a sample workflow:

  1. Screen for fundamentals: Only consider stocks with strong or improving fundamentals.
  2. Evaluate recent performance: Check for earnings or news that might artificially skew RSI.
  3. Analyze RSI: Look for oversold signals (<30) in strong stocks; overbought readings (>70) to consider trimming.
  4. Look for divergence: Identify bullish or bearish divergences with fundamentals as confirmation.
  5. Monitor regularly: Both RSI and fundamentals can change. Revisit positions frequently, especially after earnings or sector news.

Pros and Cons of Integrating RSI with Fundamentals

Pros:

  • Fewer false signals versus pure technical or fundamental trading
  • Higher conviction in entries and exits
  • Better ability to ride winners and cut losers
  • Filters out low-quality opportunities

Cons:

  • More complex and time-consuming than single-method approaches
  • Can lead to “analysis paralysis” if too many indicators conflict
  • In fast-moving markets, lag in fundamentals may result in missed moves

The Bottom Line

The marriage of Relative Strength Index and sound fundamental analysis forms a powerful strategy in today’s markets. RSI highlights where price momentum reaches extremes, while fundamentals anchor your decisions in real-world business results. When a fundamentally strong stock flashes an “oversold” RSI, that’s often a golden ticket. Conversely, technical warnings on weak companies can usually be trusted.

Staying flexible, continuously learning, and updating your approach as the market evolves leads to stronger results over the long haul. Experiment, backtest, and find the blend of signals that suits your style—then let the combined power of technical and fundamental tools work for you.

Happy trading!
