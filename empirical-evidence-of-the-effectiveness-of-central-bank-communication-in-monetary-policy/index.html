Empirical Evidence of the Effectiveness of Central Bank Communication in Monetary Policy


Central banks have long been the cornerstone of a country’s financial system, with their policies, decisions, and communication having far-reaching effects on the economy. Prudent and effective central bank communication can potentially tip the scale in favor of a resilient economy. But, how does one empirically measure the effectiveness of central bank communication in monetary policy?

In this day and age, information travels at the speed of light, making it imperative for central banks to project transparency and credibility in their actions and statements. The communication channels employed by central banks can range from press releases, interest rate announcements, speeches by policymakers, to collaborations with the media and social platforms.

Over the years, researchers have collectively established various indicators to assess the impact of central bank communication on the financial markets. An early work by Gera et al. (2017) established that central banks’ verbal interventions could prompt market participants to re-evaluate their expectations on prospects of future inflation and grow economic activity. Clarida et al. (2009) convened that forward guidance, by way of signaling future monetary policy decisions, can shape expectations about the terminal real interest rate and influence long-term interest rates.

Indeed, enormous attention has been given to researching the channels through which central bank communication influences macroeconomic outcomes, particularly on the expectations channel: managing inflation expectations and anchoring long-term market yields through clear and strategic communication. Various studies have investigated the impact of central bank communication on interest rate spreads, asset prices, and exchange rates.

Measuring such effects empirically requires robust methodologies to crawl beneath the surface of seemingly transparent actions and communication. Macro-finance models such as Generalized Method of Moments (GMM) regressions and Long-Run Restrictions have been employed by researchers to quantify the impact of central bank communications. By constructing models that govern interest rate dynamics and inflation expectations, researchers can estimate the effect of communication on various economic activities.

Recent findings show that the effectiveness of central bank communication varies across different communication channels, influenced by their clarity, timeliness, and precision. Mixed evidence testifies that pronouncements perceived to contain forward guidance on policy rates tend to have the most significant impact on financial markets. Importantly, research indicates that the effectiveness of communication hinge not just on what is said, but also on whose channels and by whom.

For instance, using GMM estimation techniques, Moessner (2013) evaluated the association between Fed Chairman’s press conference remarks and the results of Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) meetings. The study inferred that such announcements meaningfully moved market-based measures of Federal Funds rate expectations. Similarly, Belke et al. (2016) applied vector autoregressive (VAR) models to discern the effects of European Central Bank (ECB) President’s press releases on market participants’ perceptions of the future monetary policy path.

Nonetheless, the shifting landscape of monetary policy and evolving communication strategies of central banks continue to pose challenges for empirical research. Central banks face the double-edged sword of providing reassurance without breaching their independence or redlining current policy stances.

Therefore, in devising a framework to capture the changing horizon of central bank communication, future research should not only focus on improving empirical methods to detect subtle effects but also deepen its scope for understanding how central bank communication can be harnessed for better policy implementation.

In conclusion, while there is a consensus on the importance of central bank communication, obtaining empirical evidence to measure its full effectiveness remains a challenging endeavor. Nonetheless, empirical research evidence to-date highlights the significance of communication in executing monetary policy. Comprehending these complexities and advising the central decision-makers on communication strategies presents a golden opportunity for economists and policymakers alike to delve into the world of central bank communication with a rigorous and empirical approach.
