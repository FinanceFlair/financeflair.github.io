Setting Entry and Exit Points in Trend Following Trading


When it comes to trend following, one of the key elements that separate successful traders from the rest is the ability to identify precisely when to enter and exit the market. The art and science of timing these points can dramatically influence your profitability, risk management, and overall trading psychology. In this comprehensive guide, we’ll dive deep into setting effective entry and exit points in trend following trading, unpacking various strategies, technical indicators, and essential mindset considerations.

Understanding Trend Following: The Basics

At its core, trend following is a strategy that capitalizes on the momentum of price movements, whether upward or downward. Traders seek to ride established trends rather than attempting to predict reversals, aiming to maximize profits by staying in the trend as long as possible.

This philosophy inherently relies on a disciplined approach to identifying when a trend is strong enough to follow, and when it might be losing steam. Because trends don’t last forever, correct timing of entry and exit points is crucial.

The Importance of Well-Defined Entry Points

Jumping into a trade too late can expose you to unnecessary risk or reduce the profit potential, while entering too early can mean getting trapped in false signals. Therefore, the first step in trend following is determining the optimal moment to join a trend.

Using Moving Averages for Entry Signals

Moving averages are among the most popular tools for spotting trends and setting entry points. They smooth out price fluctuations and reveal the underlying direction.

  • Simple Moving Average (SMA) and Exponential Moving Average (EMA): When a shorter-period moving average crosses above a longer-period moving average, it often signals a new upward trend, known as a golden cross. Conversely, a downward crossover hints at a downtrend.

  • Entry Point: A common tactic is to enter a long position once the shorter moving average closes above the longer moving average, confirming upward momentum. This crossover reduces the chance of chasing a weak or short-lived trend.

  • Confirm with Volume: Higher trading volume accompanying the crossover often enhances the reliability of the signal, as it reflects stronger market conviction.

Price Action and Breakouts

Another widely used entry strategy is to go long or short when the price breaks above resistance or below support levels.

  • Identify Key Levels: These can be prior highs/lows, trendlines, or consolidation boundaries.

  • Entry Point: Place a buy order slightly above a resistance breakout or a sell order just below a support breakdown. This method takes advantage of the momentum shift that breaks out of a trading range.

  • Volume Confirmation: Breakouts accompanied by higher volume present higher confidence in the new trend direction.

Utilizing Technical Indicators for Entry Precision

Beyond moving averages, several indicators can aid in timing entries:

  • Relative Strength Index (RSI): Often used to detect entry points by indicating oversold or overbought conditions. For upward trends, look for the RSI crossing above a threshold (e.g., 30 or 40) from below, suggesting strengthening momentum.

  • MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence): Helps confirm entry when the MACD line crosses above the signal line, indicating bullish momentum.

  • Trend Strength Indicators: Tools like the Average Directional Index (ADX) help measure trend strength, advising traders to avoid entering when the trend is weak (ADX below 25 generally).

Waiting for Retracements

Jumping into a trend immediately after a breakout can sometimes lead to entries at inflated price points. Waiting for a retracement to a moving average or previous support level can provide a better risk-to-reward ratio.

  • Entry Point: For an uptrend, consider entering near a moving average line or where the price previously found support after a breakout.

  • This method assumes the trend will resume after a momentary price pullback and helps avoid chasing.

Crafting Exit Strategies to Preserve Gains and Limit Losses

Just as crucial as entering at the right moment is knowing when to exit, both to lock in profits and to protect yourself from severe losses.

Trailing Stop Loss: Riding the Trend Safely

The beauty of trailing stops is that they allow you to protect profits while giving the trade room to breathe.

  • Fixed Percentage Trailing Stop: For example, set a stop loss 3-5% below the highest price reached during the trade.

  • Moving Average Trailing Stops: Use a dynamic stop based on a moving average (e.g., exiting if the price closes below the 20-day SMA).

  • Trailing stops prevent premature exits during healthy pullbacks while automatically exiting if the trend reverses significantly.

Target-Based Exits: Setting Profit Goals

Some traders prefer pre-set price targets based on technical levels:

  • Fibonacci Extensions: Use Fibonacci retracement and extension levels to set realistic price targets at which to take partial or full profits.

  • Previous Highs or Lows: Targets can be the next resistance or support level where price might stall or reverse.

  • It is wise to combine target-based exits with trailing stops to capitalize fully on trends that extend beyond your initial expectations.

Exit Signals from Technical Indicators

Much like entries, indicators also guide when to exit:

  • MACD Crossovers: A bearish crossover (MACD line crossing below signal) can hint at weakening momentum.

  • RSI Divergences: If the price is making new highs but the RSI forms lower highs (bearish divergence), it might be time to consider an exit.

  • ADX Falling Below Thresholds: A decreasing ADX suggests the trend is weakening.

Time-Based Exits

Sometimes, minimizing risk involves time discipline:

  • Exiting trades after a set number of days if the trend hasn’t moved enough in your favor helps free capital and limits opportunity cost.

Combining Multiple Techniques for Robust Decision Making

No single method guarantees success. Synchronizing various tools and signals boosts confidence and filters false trades.

For example, a trader might:

  • Wait for a moving average crossover to establish the trend.

  • Confirm with volume surge and RSI strength.

  • Enter on a retracement to the moving average.

  • Set a trailing stop at a moving average line or percentage level.

  • Take profits near Fibonacci levels, adjusting stops progressively.

The Role of Psychology and Discipline

Beyond technicalities, emotional control defines the ability to stick to an entry or exit plan. Fear of missing out (FOMO) and panic selling can sabotage even well-planned trades.

To cultivate the right mindset:

  • Plan entries and exits before entering the trade.

  • Use alerts or orders to automate exits and avoid emotional decision-making.

  • Accept that some trades will hit stops — managing expectancy over time is key.

  • Review and learn from all trades to refine your approach.

Adapting Strategies for Different Markets and Timeframes

Trend following entry and exit tactics vary depending on asset volatility and trading horizon.

  • Futures and Forex: Often require tighter stops and faster reactions due to higher leverage and volatility.

  • Stocks and ETFs: Typically allow for wider stops and longer holds.

  • Intraday vs Swing Trading: Intraday trades rely more on momentum indicators and real-time volume spikes; swing trades emphasize daily moving averages and zone breakouts.

Testing and adapting your approach to the characteristics of your chosen market is essential for long-term success.

Practical Example: Implementing a Trend Following Entry/Exit Strategy

Imagine trading a medium-cap stock showing a steady uptrend.

  1. Identify trend: The 50-day EMA crosses above the 200-day EMA, volume is rising.

  2. Wait for pullback: Price dips to the 50-day EMA.

  3. Confirmation: RSI rebounds from oversold region; MACD crosses bullish.

  4. Entry: Buy as the price starts rebounding off the 50-day EMA.

  5. Stop loss: Place just below the recent swing low or slightly below the 50-day EMA.

  6. Trailing stop: Use a 3% trailing stop as the price climbs.

  7. Take profit: Set a target at the next resistance level or use Fibonacci extension to estimate upside.

This step-by-step method balances discipline, risk management, and trend maximization.

Conclusion: The Craft of Timing in Trend Following Trading

Mastering entry and exit points is a dynamic journey. It demands learning to read price action, understand indicator signals, and acknowledge one’s own psychological biases. While no system can predict markets with certainty, combining technical tools and sound risk management creates an edge.

Experiment with different signals, keep trading journals, and refine your tactics. With persistence, the ability to master entry and exit points in trend following trading will transform your approach from reactive to proactive, setting the stage for sustainable success in diverse market conditions.
