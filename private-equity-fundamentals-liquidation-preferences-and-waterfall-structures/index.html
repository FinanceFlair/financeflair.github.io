Private Equity Fundamentals: Liquidation Preferences and Waterfall Structures


Private equity is a complex and fascinating world, filled with intricate mechanisms and structures that drive investment decisions. Two fundamental concepts within private equity are liquidation preferences and waterfall structures. In this blog post, we will explore these concepts in detail, shedding light on how they shape the distribution of funds in private equity deals.

Liquidation Preferences

Liquidation preferences are contractual provisions that dictate the order in which investors receive proceeds from a company’s liquidation or sale. In simple terms, it determines who gets paid first and how much they receive before other stakeholders receive any distributions.

Participating Liquidation Preferences

There are two main types of liquidation preferences: participating and non-participating. Let’s start by discussing participating liquidation preferences.

Under a participating liquidation preference, investors receive their initial investment back first, along with a predetermined rate of return. After that, they also participate in the distribution of remaining proceeds on a pro-rata basis with other stakeholders. This means that investors with participating preferences have the potential to receive double-dip returns.

For example, let’s say Investor A has a participating preference with a 2x multiple and a 20% stake in a company. If the company is sold for $10 million, Investor A would first receive their initial investment of $2 million (20% of $10 million) plus an additional $2 million (2x multiple). They would then participate in the distribution of the remaining $6 million alongside other stakeholders.

Non-Participating Liquidation Preferences

Non-participating liquidation preferences, on the other hand, limit investors to receiving either their initial investment back or their share of the remaining proceeds. In non-participating preferences, investors must choose between their preference amount or converting to common shares and receiving their pro-rata share of the remaining distribution.

Using the same example as before, if Investor A has a non-participating preference and the company is sold for $10 million, they would have to choose between receiving their initial investment of $2 million or converting to common shares and receiving their pro-rata share of the remaining $8 million alongside other stakeholders.

Waterfall Structures

Waterfall structures, also known as distribution waterfalls, determine how profits are distributed among different classes of investors. They establish a hierarchy of priorities for distributing funds and outline the specific conditions that must be met before proceeds can flow to the next level.

First Tier: Return of Capital

The first tier of a waterfall structure focuses on returning investors’ capital contributions. This ensures that investors recoup their initial investments before any profits are distributed. Once the initial investment is returned, the waterfall moves on to the next tier.

Second Tier: Preferred Return

The second tier of the waterfall structure involves distributing a preferred return to investors. This preferred return is typically a fixed percentage of the initial investment or a hurdle rate that must be achieved before other stakeholders receive distributions.

Third Tier: Profit Sharing

After the preferred return has been distributed, the waterfall structure moves on to the third tier, which involves profit sharing among different classes of investors. This tier determines the allocation of profits based on the specific terms outlined in the investment agreement.

Final Tier: Carried Interest

The final tier of the waterfall structure is where the general partners or fund managers receive their share of the profits. This is known as the carried interest, and it incentivizes fund managers to generate strong returns for investors.

Conclusion

Understanding the fundamentals of private equity, such as liquidation preferences and waterfall structures, is crucial for both investors and entrepreneurs. These concepts shape the distribution of funds, determine investor returns, and provide the structure for successful private equity deals.

By delving into the intricacies of liquidation preferences and waterfall structures, investors can make informed decisions and entrepreneurs can structure their deals to align with their goals. Private equity is a dynamic and ever-evolving field, and mastering these fundamentals is key to navigating this complex landscape successfully.
