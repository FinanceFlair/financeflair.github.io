How to Teach Kids About Money: Fun Activities


As a parent or guardian, teaching your child about money is essential for their financial literacy and long-term success. However, it's not just about handing over a allowance or explaining the concept of budgets; it's about creating a dialogue and instilling a mindset that will serve them well throughout their lives. One way to achieve this is by incorporating fun activities into your money lessons, making it a hands-on and engaging experience for your kids. Set the Foundation Early On Children as young as three can start learning about money by counting out coins or bills to purchase toys or small items at a toy store or from a vending machine. This simple activity encourages an understanding of the value of money, recognizing the act of giving, taking, and keeping. Around the age of five or six, you can start discussing the concept of needs vs. wants. Play a game where your child has to decide how they want to spend their pretend money, prioritizing essential items such as food and clothing over discretionary ones like toys. Implement Real-World Scenarios Children love playing pretend, and using real-world scenarios can make money lessons incredibly engaging.

Create a mock store in your home where the kids are the customers and they have to make purchases using play money. This scenario can help them understand pricing, taxes, and making change. Explain the value of saving by creating a "piggy bank" where they deposit a portion of their allowance each week. Over time, they can see the money grow, understanding first-hand the concept of compound interest. Teach them to earn money by starting a small business, such as dog-walking, lawn-mowing, or even making handicrafts. This teaches entrepreneurship and responsibility.

Games and Activities Beyond the Classroom Leonardo da Vinci said, "Experience is the teacher of all things." Engage your child in real-life experiences where money management is a key component. This can include:

Planning a field trip and budgeting for snacks, transportation, and entry fees. Organizing a family trip by setting a budget together and making financial decisions on the go. Starting a fundraising campaign for a cause they're passionate about.

Books, Timelines, and the Future To cement the lessons learned, reading books about money management and creating timelines of financial milestones can be incredibly helpful.

Learn about different historical periods where money was introduced and how economies have evolved. Explore the ups and downs in the financial world, large corporations, and small startups. Use a timeline to mark and celebrate financial achievements, small or large, from both a personal and a global perspective.

Parental Involvement Beyond Financial Duties While teaching kids about money can sometimes feel like a burden placed on one parent, it should be a shared responsibility. Ensure that both parents are more consistent in their approach to money matters and are giving the same message. Having parents from different backgrounds or work experiences can add different perspectives, teaching kids about various financial decisions and outcomes.
