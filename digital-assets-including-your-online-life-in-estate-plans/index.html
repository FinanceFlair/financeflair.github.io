Digital Assets: Including Your Online Life in Estate Plans


In today’s connected world, our lives are as much digital as they are physical. We store valuable information, memories, and even assets online—from bank accounts and cryptocurrency wallets to photo libraries and social media profiles. Despite this, many people overlook the importance of incorporating these digital assets into their estate plans. Preparing for the management and transfer of your online belongings is a growing necessity.

This comprehensive guide explores the types of digital assets, the risks of ignoring them in estate planning, and practical steps to secure and pass them on.

What Constitutes Digital Assets?

Digital assets are broadly defined as electronic forms of information that have value. These can be personal, financial, or business-related. Some common examples include:

  • Online Financial Accounts: Bank accounts, investment portfolios, cryptocurrency wallets, PayPal accounts.
  • Email and Communication Accounts: Gmail, Outlook, Slack, or other messaging platforms.
  • Social Media Profiles: Facebook, Instagram, Twitter, LinkedIn, TikTok.
  • Digital Media: Photos, videos, music libraries stored in platforms like Google Photos, iCloud, or Spotify.
  • Blogs and Websites: Ownership of domains, hosting accounts, blogs, and content rights.
  • Subscription Services: Netflix, Audible, Amazon Prime with digital purchases.
  • Gaming Accounts: Steam, Xbox, PlayStation Network accounts with in-game currencies and items.
  • Cloud Storage: Google Drive, Dropbox, OneDrive.

The value of these assets can be both emotional and monetary, making their proper handling after death critical.

Why Is It Important to Include Digital Assets in Estate Plans?

Estate plans traditionally address tangible assets such as real estate, cars, or jewelry. However, digital assets are often left unaccounted for, which can create several problems:

1. Risk of Loss or Deletion

Many online platforms have policies that delete accounts after periods of inactivity or upon notification of death without a designated fiduciary. Without instructions, valuable files, financial holdings, and sentimental items might be lost forever.

2. Complicated Access

Digital assets are typically protected by passwords and two-factor authentication, and policies often prohibit sharing account credentials. Without proper authorization, loved ones might face legal hurdles or be unable to retrieve critical information.

3. Financial Implications

Some digital assets carry monetary value. Cryptocurrency wallets, for instance, may hold substantial funds. Social media influencer accounts or e-commerce sites tied to business revenues can continue to produce income or have resale value.

4. Preserving Online Presence

For many people, online identities represent a significant part of their legacy. Managing social media after death—whether memorializing or removing profiles—can be deeply meaningful for family and friends.

Legal Landscape Surrounding Digital Assets

The legal frameworks governing digital assets vary depending on jurisdiction and platform policies. Several laws have been enacted to tackle these issues, such as the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA) in the United States, allowing fiduciaries controlled access to digital property under certain conditions.

Still, platform terms of service often restrict account access, which complicates estate administration. For instance, platforms like Facebook have specific procedures for memorializing accounts or transferring control.

How to Incorporate Digital Assets in Estate Planning

Incorporating digital assets into an estate plan requires intentional action. Here are essential steps to consider:

Step 1: Inventory Your Digital Assets

Compile an exhaustive list of your digital property. Include:

  • Account names
  • URLs
  • Usernames
  • Passwords (securely stored)
  • Any relevant recovery information such as security questions or authentication apps

This list should be updated regularly.

Step 2: Assign a Digital Executor

Designate someone responsible for managing your digital assets after death. This person should be trustworthy, tech-savvy, and informed about the necessary legal and practical steps.

Clearly name this individual in your will or estate documents.

Step 3: Create a Digital Asset Inventory Memorandum

A memorandum can be referenced in your will but kept separate for security. This document lists accounts and access details, instructions for management, and wishes regarding the assets.

Specify roles like which accounts to delete, memorialize, or transfer ownership.

Step 4: Use Password Managers

Password managers can securely store and share access credentials. Many offer features to transfer access to trusted contacts upon death.

Step 5: Understand Platform Policies

Some platforms offer legacy contacts or special processes for account transfer. For example:

  • Facebook allows users to designate a legacy contact.
  • Google lets you set up an Inactive Account Manager to notify trusted people or delete data.

Include this knowledge in your planning to leverage existing options.

Step 6: Review Legal Documents

Ensure your will, trust, and powers of attorney explicitly mention digital assets. Work with an attorney knowledgeable in estate and digital law to draft provisions that comply with your jurisdiction’s rules.

Step 7: Update Regularly

Your digital life evolves continually. Revisit and revise your inventory, passwords, and executor designations periodically to reflect changes.

Special Considerations for Cryptocurrency

Cryptocurrencies present unique challenges:

  • Private Keys and Wallets: If private keys or seed phrases are lost, funds are irretrievable.
  • Cold Storage: Physical devices holding cryptocurrencies require specific instructions for access and transfer.
  • Regulating Ownership: Some laws still lag on cryptocurrency estate treatment.

Include clear, secure instructions regarding cryptocurrency in your estate plan. Consider using multi-signature wallets or legal instruments like trusts to safeguard these assets.

Protecting Privacy and Security

While planning, it’s critical to balance accessibility with security:

  • Do not include passwords in your will since wills become public after probate.
  • Use encrypted digital vaults or legally protected memorandums.
  • Inform your digital executor of their responsibilities and how to protect your data.

Privacy policies of service providers often prohibit password sharing, so the legal authority granted via estate documents is key.

Handling Digital Assets After Death: What Happens Next?

When someone dies, their digital executor must navigate multiple steps:

  1. Notification to Platforms: Submit death certificates and follow procedure to access or close accounts.

  2. Accessing Financial Accounts: Coordinate with banks or exchanges for funds transfer.

  3. Managing Social Media: Memorialize, delete, or modify profiles per instructions.

  4. Preserving Memories: Download photos, videos, or documents to preserve the deceased’s legacy.

  5. Dealing with Legal Hurdles: Handle disputes or background checks necessary to comply with laws.

An organized plan ahead of time dramatically reduces the burden on families.

Future Outlook: Evolving Digital Estate Planning

As technology integrates deeper into daily life, new types of digital property will emerge, such as virtual land in metaverses or NFTs (non-fungible tokens). Estate planning must adapt continuously to encompass these evolving assets.

Artificial intelligence assistants, voice data, and biometric identifiers could also require special consideration in the near future.

Conclusion: Your Online Life Deserves a Place in Your Estate Plan

Establishing a clear, actionable plan for digital assets is no longer optional. It has become an integral part of comprehensive estate planning. By taking inventory, designating responsible parties, and documenting intentions, you ensure that your online life—and its value—are respected and managed according to your wishes.

The digital world may seem intangible, but the need for careful stewardship is very real. The peace of mind that comes from knowing your digital legacy is secured is worth the effort.

If you haven’t considered your digital assets in your estate plan, now is the time to start—because your life online is just as important as the life you live offline.
