10 Questions to Ask for a Quick Working Capital Review


Working capital is the lifeblood of any business. It represents the difference between current assets and current liabilities, serving as a measure of operational liquidity. A healthy working capital ensures you can meet short-term obligations while driving long-term growth. However, managing working capital effectively requires periodic reviews to identify inefficiencies and opportunities for optimization. To make this process concise yet impactful, here are ten key questions to ask during a quick working capital review.

1. What is the Current Working Capital Ratio?

The working capital ratio, calculated as current assets divided by current liabilities, is one of the most straightforward indicators of a company’s liquidity. A ratio between 1.2 and 2 is generally considered healthy, as it suggests the business has sufficient short-term assets to cover its liabilities. A ratio lower than 1 may indicate liquidity problems, while an excessively high ratio could signify idle assets. During the review, identify the factors influencing this ratio and whether recent shifts signal potential concerns.

2. Are Accounts Receivable Turnover Rates Healthy?

Accounts receivable represent money owed to your business for goods or services rendered. Slow turnover in this area can tie up cash and impact liquidity. To assess performance, calculate the accounts receivable turnover ratio (net credit sales divided by average accounts receivable). Use this data to gauge whether your collection processes are efficient or if extended payment terms are becoming problematic. If turnover is slow, consider reviewing your customer credit policies or implementing stricter collections practices.

3. What is the Average Days Sales Outstanding (DSO)?

Closely tied to accounts receivable, the DSO metric measures how quickly your business collects payments after a sale. A high DSO means customers are taking longer to pay, reducing cash flow. By targeting a lower DSO, businesses can improve liquidity and working capital. Compare your DSO to industry benchmarks and analyze whether specific customer demographics or recurring late payments are contributing to delays.

4. How Efficient are Accounts Payable Processes?

On the flip side, accounts payable refers to the money your business owes to suppliers. Evaluating your accounts payable turnover ratio (total purchases on credit divided by average accounts payable) highlights how quickly you’re meeting obligations. A longer payment cycle might improve short-term cash flow, but delaying payments excessively risks supplier relationships and potential penalties. Ensure you’re balancing payments strategically and taking advantage of any early payment discounts offered.

5. What is the State of Inventory Management?

Inventory ties up significant chunks of working capital, and poor inventory management can create cash flow challenges. Calculate key metrics, such as inventory turnover (cost of goods sold divided by average inventory). Slow turnover might suggest overstocking, excess production, or declining demand, while high turnover could indicate potential stockouts. Consider implementing Just-In-Time (JIT) inventory practices or better forecasting to optimize this area while mitigating risks of overstocking or shortages.

6. Are Short-Term Liabilities Appropriately Managed?

A working capital review should closely examine current liabilities, especially short-term debt, payroll obligations, and taxes payable. Determine the maturity dates of these liabilities and whether your business has the cash available to cover them. Additionally, explore the possibility of restructuring high-interest short-term debt to more favorable terms if it’s impacting your cash flow. Effective management of short-term liabilities is crucial to maintaining financial stability.

7. What is the Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC)?

The cash conversion cycle measures how long it takes for a business to convert its investments in inventory and other resources into cash flows from sales. A shorter CCC indicates better working capital efficiency. The CCC consists of three parts: days inventory outstanding (DIO), days sales outstanding (DSO), and days payable outstanding (DPO). Reviewing these components helps pinpoint specific areas—inventory turnover, receivables management, or payables timing—that may need adjustment.

8. How Flexible is Your Access to Cash?

In addition to analyzing existing cash reserves, a working capital review should explore the business’s flexibility in securing additional cash, if needed. Do you have access to a revolving line of credit, or can you leverage invoice financing during cash crunches? Consider how prepared your business is to weather unexpected downturns or seize unplanned opportunities. Keep in mind that having a robust cash management strategy bolsters resilience.

9. Are Financial Projections Aligned with Current Liquidity?

Projected revenues and expenses provide crucial insights into future working capital needs. Compare prior forecasts with actual performance during the review process. Were there inaccuracies in predicting cash inflows or outflows? If so, what adjustments need to be made to refine future projections? Ensuring realistic and reliable financial forecasting can dramatically improve your ability to maintain a healthy working capital position.

10. What Internal Processes Could Be Streamlined?

Finally, assess whether internal inefficiencies are putting undue pressure on your working capital. Automation and process improvement play significant roles in operational efficiency. For example, digitizing invoicing, payments, and expense tracking boosts real-time visibility into cash flow and improves collection cycles. Streamlined operations contribute to more effective working capital management, creating a ripple effect across various aspects of the business.

Conclusion

A quick working capital review can uncover critical insights about your business’s financial health, enabling you to take proactive steps to address inefficiencies and safeguard liquidity. By asking the right questions—like the ten highlighted above—you can build a clearer picture of your company’s short-term solvency and long-term stability. Remember, while working capital might seem like a narrow area of focus, its effective management influences everything from day-to-day operations to strategic growth initiatives. Regularly dedicating time to review and optimize it is not just prudent—it’s essential for success.
