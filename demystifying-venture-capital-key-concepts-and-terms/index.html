Demystifying Venture Capital: Key Concepts and Terms


If venture capital wasn’t a matter of life and death, it would be a real blow to choosing the wrong one. - Tom Perkins

Venture capital (VC) has increasingly been at the center of attention as an essential component of the startup ecosystem. VC funds typically focus on high-growth potential, high-risk companies. If, for a moment, you think that this process would dramatically change with age (they mature), remember that Facebook was founded in 2004, while Google started in 1998. Venture capitalists typically invest in early-stage companies with the promise of a substantial monetary return.

Key Terms

  1. Limited Partners (LPs): Investors in the venture capital fund, often pension funds or institutions with large amounts of capital to invest.
  2. General Partners (GPs): Professionals who manage the fund and invest the money received from LPs. Also known as “VCs” or “venture capitalists."
  3. Portfolio company: Startups and companies in which the VC invests.
  4. Term Sheet: A short, non-binding document outlining the proposed terms of a venture capital investment.
  5. Liquidation preference: It decides the order of payment in case the company is being liquidated. Investors have their stake returned first before other shareholders.

Venture Capital in a Nutshell

Generally, VC firms pool money from high net worth individuals, institutions, pension funds, and endowments, aiming to invest in budding businesses with a compelling value proposition. VC financing customarily happens in rounds.

As companies advance through various stages, startups may seek successive rounds of funding, with each round typically requiring a “valuation” of the company’s worth, and negotiations between VCs and entrepreneurs.

Key Concepts

1. Valuation

The worth of a business is critical in determining each investor’s share and stake in the company. Nevertheless, determining a company’s valuation involves more than just the book value or tangible assets. VC’s appoint a sophisticated method, where the financial health of the startup is calculated considering various factors, including growth potential, target audience, and market size.

2. Exit Strategy

A successful exit strategy is a top priority for venture capitalists. Investment funds for a V.C to exit their investments for a significant return. Possible exit strategies include mergers and acquisitions, an Initial Public Offering (IPO) on a public stock exchange, or a secondary market acquisition.

3. Board Seats

VCs might secure themselves board seats in firms they invest in. Having a board seat provides VCs key decision-making power and oversight.

4. Liquidation

Venture capitalists invest in companies understanding that most will fail. Therefore, the possibility of liquidating assets safeguards their investments before they invest.

5. Stage-wise Investments

VCs invest throughout a company’s growth phases—seed stage, startup, early, and expansion. Each stage needed investment has a unique focus.

Understanding the dynamics of venture capital can be challenging. However, for budding entrepreneurs and those interested in investing, it is vital to delve into the world of venture capital, venture capitalists’ role is, and the associated jargon.

As William Draper, a founding partner at Draper Fisher Jurvetson, stated,

“The venture capital industry is less about the money and more about innovation, creating jobs, improving lives, and making the world a better place.”

In the world of venture capital, it’s not just about wealth creation, but innovative startups that make a difference, making the future exciting. Remember, it’s not just the money; it’s the game changer that makes all the difference.
