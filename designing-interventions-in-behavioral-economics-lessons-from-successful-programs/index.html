Designing Interventions in Behavioral Economics: Lessons from Successful Programs


Behavioral economics is a powerful tool for understanding the factors that influence decision-making, and designing interventions that help individuals make better choices. By applying insights from this exciting field, we can create programs that address a wide range of social issues, from improving financial literacy to increasing exercise and healthy eating habits.

Several successful programs have already demonstrated the ability to change behavior on a large scale. Here are some key lessons we can learn from these interventions.

  1. Nudge Theory in Action The nudge theory, developed by Nobel laureate Richard Thaler and Cass Sunstein, asserts that people’s choices can be influenced by small, non-invasive changes to the environment. Some of the most successful behavioral economics programs rely on nudging to encourage people to make more positive decisions.

For example, the UK’s savings program, “Save as You Earn,” encouraged employees to deposit a portion of their paychecks into savings accounts. By making it easy to set up regular deposits and providing clear feedback on progress, the program achieved widespread success.

  1. Default Effects One of the most powerful tools in a behavioral economist’s toolkit is the default effect. This occurs when individuals are more likely to follow the default option when faced with multiple choices. Default effects have been used in several programs to shift behavior at scale.

An excellent example is the “Energy Conservation Program” implemented in California, which automatically enrolled households in a energy-saving plan unless they opted out. By making energy-saving the default choice, the program dramatically reduced overall energy consumption.

  1. Social Norms Social norms play a significant role in shaping people’s behavior. By leveraging these norms, successful behavioral economics programs can create a positive feedback loop that reinforces the desired behavior.

Consider the “Green in 2015” campaign, an Australian initiative that aimed to reduce water consumption. The campaign used social norm marketing techniques to encourage households to use WaterPoll, a website that allowed people to compare their water usage with their neighbors’. Participants were incentivized to reduce their water use to improve their online ranking. The program significantly decreased overall water usage, demonstrating the power of social norms.

  1. Commitment Devices Commitment devices are methods used to lock individuals into a certain behavior, often with a financial incentive. These devices can be powerful tools for encouraging lasting change.

A fascinating example is “Commit-to-Exercise,” a program implemented in a large U.S. city that provided participants with a personalized workout plan and required a small monthly payment to continue. Participants were more likely to stick with their exercise routines, as they had made a financial commitment to do so.

  1. Incentivizing Change Incentives are a cornerstone of many successful behavioral economics programs, as they can provide a powerful motivator for change.

The “Incentives4Me” trial in Denmark provided individuals with a mobile-app-based incentive system to encourage more active lifestyles. By earning points for physical activity, participants could then redeem their points for a range of rewards, from shopping vouchers to date night packages. The program was a resounding success, demonstrating the potential of incentives when paired with technological innovation.

These programs are just a few examples of the many successful behavioral economics initiatives in action. By drawing on these lessons, policymakers and designers can create effective interventions that promote positive, lasting change. As we continue to refine our understanding of human behavior, the potential for these interventions is limited only by our imagination.
