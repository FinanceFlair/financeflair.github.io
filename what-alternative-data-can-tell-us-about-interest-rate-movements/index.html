What Alternative Data Can Tell Us about Interest Rate Movements


Ever since the dawn of gold and silver, currency and trade have been intrinsically linked to commodities and their value. Over time, economies have evolved, and so have the measures to capture their pulse. The Federal Reserve’s interest rates are well-known components of the financial worlds, both affecting and reflecting economic trends. However, it’s crucial to look beyond just standard data sources for a holistic understanding.

Alternative data provides a broader lens into an economy’s complex tapestry, revealing patterns that may not show up in standard statistics. These sources can highlight hidden aspects and predictive capacities in relation to interest rate movement. Some examples include social media data, geospatial data, and, as we’ll focus on in this article, patent data.

Patent Data: An Underutilized Window

Patents are issued when someone invents a new process or product to solve a problem. With the number of patents granted, we can measure creativity and innovation within an economy. This data is also a megaphone for the fertile minds that could potentially disrupt and reshape industries.

A spike in patent volume often reflects an increase in R&D within a sector. The government, recognizing these innovations and handpick the most promising, channels funds and resources into these areas. This in turn can cause the economy’s growth and productivity to pick up, leading to a more accommodative monetary policy and thus, lower interest rates.

Analyzing Interest Rates with Alternative Data

Assuming predictive capabilities, we can analyze the patent data in conjunction with interest rates to form a better consensus regarding the economy. Here’s the basic framework:

  1. Interest Rate Correlation: There is a negative correlation between patent volume and interest rates. When patent numbers surge, the innovation potential in the economy increases, resulting in accommodative monetary policies and reduced interest rates. Conversely, stagnant or reduced patent volumes may signal a slowing economy, leading to tighter monetary policies and higher interest rates.

  2. Timing: The timing of patent filings and approvals offers insight into growth. If patents are being filed and approved in a quick timeframe, this could signify efficient business operations and streamlined bureaucracy, further boosting the economy.

  3. Geographic Location: Geospatial data can give even more granularity to the analysis. Regions with the most patents being filed and approved may indicate enticing economic climates, potentially leading to future monetary policy changes.

Beyond the Numbers

While numbers can tell us a lot, alternative data sources such as patent data also offer an X-ray into the qualitative aspects of an economy. Technological advancements and innovations can represent the zeitgeist of an era, reflecting social, environmental, and governmental zeitgeists.

Understanding these massive shifts in an economy driven by alternative data can be an indispensable tool for investors, policy makers, and all stakeholders in the debt market. It can shed light on areas that traditional data might miss, leading to more informed decisions and a better understanding of the economy’s pulse.

Conclusion: Alternative Data for Alternative Answers

In a complex world, obtaining an accurate and all-encompassing view of our economies can be challenging. However, it’s with this pursuit that the concept of alternative data arises. By combining traditional and non-traditional sources, we better understand the intricate pieces that make up a prosperous economy.

Interest rate movements are often signaling mechanisms for economic health. By evaluating them alongside alternative data, we are granted a more complete picture of an economy’s past, current, and future states. Patents may be far from the first thing that comes to mind when considering the implications of interest rates, but exploring the unconventional in data can provide crucial, competitive advantages not easily replicated in the ever-changing financial arena.
