The Growing Appeal of Secondary and Tertiary Real Estate Markets


Over the past decade, the real estate landscape across the globe has experienced significant shifts. While primary markets in major metropolitan areas have traditionally drawn the most attention from investors, homeowners, and developers alike, secondary and tertiary real estate markets are rising steadily as attractive destinations for both residential and commercial investments. This shift is driven by multiple factors including evolving lifestyle preferences, affordability demands, technological advancements, and shifting economic dynamics. This blog post explores why these less conventional markets are witnessing a boom, what makes them desirable, and how they could shape the future of real estate investment.

What Defines Secondary and Tertiary Real Estate Markets?

Before diving into why these markets are booming, it’s important to clarify what exactly is meant by secondary and tertiary real estate markets.

  • Primary markets refer to large, well-known metropolitan areas such as New York, Los Angeles, London, Tokyo, and San Francisco — cities with established infrastructure, major corporate headquarters, and global economic influence.
  • Secondary markets are typically mid-sized cities or metropolitan areas that have good infrastructure and economic activity but don’t quite reach the scale or headline status of primary cities. Examples include cities like Raleigh-Durham, Nashville, or Denver in the U.S.
  • Tertiary markets are smaller cities, towns, or regions that are less densely populated and often overlooked by mainstream investors and developers. These places may have emerging economies, niche industries, or simply smaller populations.

Key Drivers Behind the Shift to Secondary and Tertiary Markets

Affordable Living and Investment Opportunities

One of the most significant catalysts for the growing appeal is affordability. Housing prices and rental rates in primary markets have skyrocketed, pricing many out of ownership or forcing renters to stretch significant portions of their income on housing. In contrast, secondary and tertiary markets offer more attainable price points for both buyers and renters.

For institutional investors and individual buyers alike, lower acquisition costs in these markets mean higher potential yields and less initial capital outlay. For renters and homeowners, secondary markets often mean access to larger living spaces, better community amenities, and a higher overall quality of life without the astronomical costs associated with major urban centers.

The Rise of Remote Work and Lifestyle Flexibility

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the adoption of remote work on an unprecedented scale, fundamentally altering how and where people can live and work. No longer tied to a daily commute or proximity to corporate hubs, many workers have the flexibility to relocate to more affordable areas with attractive amenities.

This new freedom has caused many professionals, especially millennials and Gen Z, to seek out cities and towns that offer a blend of economic opportunity and lifestyle benefits — like lower congestion, more green space, and close-knit communities — often found in secondary and tertiary markets.

Economic Diversification and Emerging Industries

Many secondary and tertiary markets have been investing heavily in economic diversification. Some of these regions have targeted burgeoning sectors such as technology, healthcare, renewable energy, or advanced manufacturing, creating new job opportunities and attracting skilled professionals.

For example, cities like Boise, Idaho, have seen substantial growth due to the tech industry’s expanding footprint outside the traditional hubs. Similarly, university towns or regions with growing healthcare industries often provide stable economic environments supportive of real estate growth.

Infrastructure Improvements and Connectivity

Improved infrastructure also plays a crucial role in making secondary and tertiary markets more attractive. Enhanced transportation networks — such as new highways, regional airports, and train services — reduce the perceived isolation of smaller cities and make them more accessible.

Additionally, investments in broadband and telecommunications infrastructure have made high-speed internet available in many previously underserved areas, supporting remote work and modern business needs.

Quality of Life and Community Amenities

Aside from economics, lifestyle considerations weigh heavily on people’s decisions to move or invest. Secondary and tertiary markets often offer appealing amenities such as:

  • Less traffic congestion and pollution
  • Access to outdoor recreational activities like hiking, fishing, and skiing
  • Lower crime rates compared to some big cities
  • A stronger sense of community and local culture

For many, these factors contribute to a better work-life balance, making these markets not only affordable but also desirable places to settle down and raise families.

Investment Potential and Risks

Attractive Returns and Growth Potential

Because these markets are less saturated, property values in secondary and tertiary cities often have more room to appreciate over time. Real estate investors who get in early can leverage rising demand to generate strong capital gains and consistent rental income.

Some investors also find opportunities in redevelopment projects or converting underutilized properties, benefiting from increasing interest in these areas.

Diversification and Portfolio Resilience

For investors heavily concentrated in primary markets, branching out into secondary and tertiary markets offers diversification benefits. Different economic drivers and demographic trends can reduce portfolio volatility, making these less conventional markets valuable from a risk management perspective.

Considerations and Challenges

Despite the appeal, investing or relocating to secondary and tertiary markets isn’t without challenges:

  • Liquidity: Properties in smaller markets may take longer to sell.
  • Economic Stability: Some of these markets are more sensitive to economic downturns or lack diversified job bases.
  • Infrastructure and Services: Even with improvements, some areas may not yet have sufficient amenities or transportation links compared to primary cities.
  • Regulatory Environment: Zoning laws, tax policies, and development regulations vary widely and can impact investment returns.

Because of these factors, thorough research and local market expertise are critical when considering secondary or tertiary market real estate.

Notable Examples of Secondary and Tertiary Market Growth

To illustrate the trend, here are some U.S. cities experiencing notable growth outside the usual primary market hubs:

  • Raleigh-Durham, North Carolina: Known for its Research Triangle Park and a strong tech presence, it balances job growth with a reasonable cost of living.
  • Boise, Idaho: An emerging tech hub with a high quality of life and affordable housing attracting remote workers and entrepreneurs.
  • Nashville, Tennessee: Combining a thriving healthcare industry with cultural attractions, its real estate market has surged in recent years.
  • Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania: A city transitioning from its industrial legacy to technology and education, enjoying revitalization and growing interest.
  • Tulsa, Oklahoma: Offering affordable housing and innovative programs to attract remote workers, it represents the increasing viability of tertiary cities.

The Future Landscape of Real Estate Markets

As we look forward, the divide between primary, secondary, and tertiary real estate markets may start to blur somewhat. Technological advances, shifting work habits, and demographic trends will continue to redistribute population and economic activity.

Developers, policy makers, and investors will likely pay more attention to smaller urban centers and emerging suburbs, creating a more balanced and inclusive real estate environment. This could lead to a virtuous cycle — infrastructure and amenities improve, attracting more residents and businesses, which in turn drives further real estate demand and development.

The growing popularity of secondary and tertiary real estate markets marks a promising evolution. It allows for more accessible homeownership, diversified investment strategies, and sustainable growth outside the traditional urban cores, reshaping the landscape of real estate for years to come.

Whether you’re a first-time homebuyer, seasoned investor, or simply curious about the future of urban development, keeping an eye on these dynamic markets can provide unique opportunities and insights in a rapidly changing world. The appeal of these once-overlooked locations is clear: they offer a compelling mix of affordability, quality of life, and growth potential that’s increasingly hard to ignore.
