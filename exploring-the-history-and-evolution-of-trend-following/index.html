Exploring the History and Evolution of Trend Following


The roots of trend following delve far deeper than many traders realize. Far from being a modern innovation born of computers and quants, trend following strategies reach back through centuries of market history, quietly shaping the fortunes of traders who recognized that markets, for all their apparent chaos, are prone to move in sustained directions for periods of time. The tale of trend following is a journey through booms and busts, across trading floors and into the digital age, where the basic premise—riding trends, cutting losses, and letting winners run—remains astoundingly relevant.

The Genesis: Early Trading and the Birth of Trends

Before books, before backtests, before ticker tapes, there were markets. As early as the 18th century, rice traders in Osaka used forward contracts and technical methods to speculate on price action. These early practitioners noticed something that would echo through centuries: sometimes, prices do not just wander aimlessly, but catch a directional momentum that persists. The famous Dojima Rice Exchange became a laboratory for nascent trend following ideas, although the term did not exist.

By the 19th century in London and New York, commodities speculators noticed a similar rhythm. Jesse Livermore, immortalized as the boy plunger, made—and lost—fortunes trusting the notion that prices traveling in one direction often persist, at least for a time. Livermore’s advice—“Follow the trend. The trend is your friend.”—still resonates with traders today. He used tape reading, a precursor of chart analysis, to spot movements, not caring much for why they happened, only that they did.

Codifying a Strategy: The 20th Century Shift

With the dawn of technical analysis in the 20th century, trend following took on firmer shape. Charles Dow, the founder of the Wall Street Journal, advocated observing market averages, stating that “markets are driven by trends which persist until definitive signals prove that they have ended.” The Dow Theory formed a bedrock for subsequent generations of traders to build upon.

The concept gained serious traction with Richard Donchian, often called the “father of trend following,” who in the 1940s and 50s applied systematic rules to trading commodity futures. Donchian’s simple breakout rule—buy when prices exceed the highest high of the past n days, sell when they drop below the lowest low—became the forerunner of mechanical trading systems. By documenting the rules and results, Donchian pointed toward what would become a revolution: trading could be process-driven, not just gut-driven, and the trend following method was repeatable and scalable.

Around the same time, Ed Seykota took Donchian’s approach and, armed with the first computer for trading analysis at a major brokerage, began backtesting historical data. Seykota’s maxim, “The trend is your friend except at the end when it bends,” succinctly captures the rule-based flexibility of trend following. His track record—turning a $5,000 account into over $15 million for one client over two decades—became legendary, inspiring a new wave of quantitative traders.

The Turtle Traders: Legendary Experiment

Perhaps no story defines the popularization of trend following like the tale of the Turtle Traders. In the early 1980s, trading icons Richard Dennis and William Eckhardt held a wager: could trading success be taught, or was it innate? Dennis took a group of students—many with no prior market experience—and, over two weeks, transmitted the basic principles of trend following using a mechanical system of entries, exits, and position sizing.

The results were staggering. Many of the Turtles went on to become independently wealthy, proliferating the idea that trend following wasn’t just for the Wall Street elite—it was repeatable and teachable. The approach Dennis taught: trade a diverse basket of markets, take trades in the direction of breakouts, cut losers quickly, and let profitable trades run, captures the core doctrine of trend following. The Turtles’ story serves to demystify trading and demarcate the shift from discretion and intuition to disciplined, systematic processes.

Adapting Through Decades: Evolving Markets, Same Principles

While the DNA of trend following remains remarkably persistent, the methods for implementing it have evolved with markets. In the 1990s and 2000s, the rise of hedge funds and commodity trading advisors (CTAs) vaulted trend following into institutional prominence. Bill Dunn, John W. Henry (who later bought the Boston Red Sox), and David Harding built empires on the back of trend based systematic trading, often using large-scale diversified portfolios of futures.

Through these decades, research, backtesting, and risk control became more sophisticated. Computers enabled faster execution and a greater diversity of instruments and markets. Timeframes shifted as some practitioners sought faster, more computationally intensive trends, while others stuck with slower, long-term cycles aiming to capture seismic moves in markets such as oil, currencies, stock indexes, and bonds.

Yet the core remained: catch large moves, survive periods of losses, and manage risk obsessively. Trend following funds displayed resilience in market crises. When tech stocks collapsed in 2000, or when the global financial crisis devastated equities in 2008, systematic trend followers—often positioned short or in haven assets—delivered outsized gains just as many other strategies faltered.

Trend Following in the Algorithmic Era

The 21st century brought new challenges and opportunities. High-frequency traders and market-makers injected complexity into market microstructure. It’s tempting to imagine that algorithmic speed or artificial intelligence might render trend following obsolete; after all, if everyone knows about trends, shouldn’t they disappear?

Yet, ironically, as more participants focus on short-term noise and liquidity provision, trend followers operating on longer horizons continue to thrive. Markets are complex adaptive systems, driven by crowd psychology, macro shifts, and persistent flows. Feedback loops, central bank policies, technological shifts, and geopolitical disruptions continue to generate new trends—though their speed and intensity might change.

Modern trend followers are often quants running complex portfolios across hundreds of markets. Their systems may optimize entries, exits, and position sizing with surgical precision, but the foundational principles echo those set in the early days of Donchian and the Turtles. Risk management—never risking too much on a single trade, always having a plan to exit—is paramount. Diversification across markets is a central tenet.

Myths, Misses, and Market Psychology

A persistent misunderstanding is that trend following works all the time, or that it is a secret key to wealth without risk. In reality, trend following can be grueling, with long stretches of small profits or drawdowns, punctuated by explosive gains during market dislocations. Patience, discipline, and the ability to do the “hard” thing—holding onto winners, cutting losers quickly, resisting the urge to anticipate reversals—separate successful practitioners from the rest.

Critics sometimes label the discipline as “simple” or “outdated,” especially after periods of underperformance. Yet, as countless studies and decades of audited results demonstrate, systematic trend followers have exhibited an enduring edge, especially when traditional asset classes move sideways or trend sharply.

Market psychology plays a pivotal role. Trends exist because human beings are prone to herding, panic, and euphoria. Fundamental narratives may come and go, but crowd behavior—fear of missing out, reluctance to cut losses, the joy of making money—remains astonishingly consistent. Trend following doesn’t try to predict the next big move; it simply follows where the crowd has already started to go, betting that more of the same will follow…until it doesn’t.

Trend Following in the Expanding Universe of Markets

The universe of tradable markets has exploded in the last two decades. Cryptocurrencies, volatility contracts, emerging market equities, and esoteric commodities provide new hunting grounds for trend followers. This diversification not only opens new sources of potential profit, it mitigates the risk of stagnation in any single sector.

Though classic trend following was often applied to futures markets—thanks to liquidity, leverage, and ease of going long or short—its principles now permeate across asset classes. Exchange traded funds (ETFs), contracts for difference (CFDs), and other instruments democratize access, permitting both institutional giants and retail traders to apply systematic trend strategies.

As data collection, analytics, and machine learning mature, trend followers constantly search for tweaks: should parameters be adaptive or fixed? Does volatility expansion offer better signal than price alone? Can sentiment data or alternative signals enhance classic trend models? The debate is ongoing, but no innovation has displaced the basic insight that directional price persistence is a feature, not a bug, of financial markets.

Key Elements That Stand the Test of Time

Despite changes in tools, technologies, markets, and participants, several elements are perennial within trend following:

  • Systematic Rules: Whether encoded in software or on a notepad, clear entry and exit processes are non-negotiable.
  • Risk Management: Limiting downside and controlling losses takes precedence over chasing returns.
  • Diversification: Not every market will trend at the same time, so spreading bets reduces reliance on any one outcome.
  • Patience and Discipline: Enduring losses and periods of uncertainty is the price for catching rare, outsized moves.
  • Emotional Detachment: Adherence to the process trumps gut feelings; emotional trading is the enemy of consistency.

Trend followers are, at heart, agnostic about market direction. They trade neither hope nor fear, only the evidence of price action. This humility—accepting that predicting the future is a futile quest—is paradoxically empowering. By focusing on response, not prediction, they turn uncertainty into a workable advantage.

Looking Forward: The Enduring Allure of Trend Following

The future of trend following is both uncertain and, oddly, assured. Trading environments will keep shifting, crowd behavior will mutate alongside technology, and periodic drawdowns will test the fortitude of practitioners. New regulations, market fragmentation, and innovations like decentralized finance will bring new puzzles.

Yet, as long as markets reflect the behavior of crowds—who collectively oscillate between fear, greed, and inertia—trend following will remain a viable path. Its success will wax and wane, but just as a river carves a path through time, so too does the logic of riding trends etch itself into the history of trading.

The lesson of trend following, distilled over centuries, is as simple as it is profound: follow where the capital flows, manage the risks, and let the market—not ego—write the end of each trade. Whether practiced by samurai rice traders, Wall Street legends, or today’s code-wielding quants, trend following endures as a testament to the power of human behavior manifest in markets. It’s not a secret formula, but a philosophy—one that rewards patience, discipline, and humility before the unpredictable dance of prices.

The history and future of trend following, then, are not merely tales of technical tricks or statistical edges. They’re a mirror of the market itself: ever-changing, often humbling, and filled with opportunities for those ready to ride the wave when it appears.
