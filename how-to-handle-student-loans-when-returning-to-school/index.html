How to Handle Student Loans When Returning to School


Returning to school can be an exciting, albeit financially challenging, decision. Whether you’re looking to finish a degree, change careers, or pursue a higher level of education, one of the major hurdles you may face is how to manage student loans. This can become especially complex if you already have outstanding student loans from a previous academic pursuit. Understanding your options, managing your finances, and planning for your future education costs is essential to navigating this phase without overwhelming stress.

In this post, we’ll explore the best strategies for managing student loans when you’re going back to school. We’ll cover the different loan types, ways to minimize debt, deferment options, repayment strategies, and other financial planning tips. By the end, you’ll have a comprehensive understanding of how to approach student loans when returning to school and how to set yourself up for financial success.

1. Assess Your Current Loan Situation

Before you can take steps toward managing your student loans effectively, you must first understand what you’re dealing with. Start by reviewing your existing loans in detail. If you have multiple loans, take note of the following:

  • Loan types: Federal loans, private loans, and institutional loans all have different terms and conditions.
  • Loan balances: How much do you owe, and what are your monthly payments?
  • Interest rates: Higher interest rates mean more money paid over time.
  • Repayment status: Are you currently in forbearance, deferment, or on a repayment plan?

You can gather this information from your loan servicer or by checking your loan balance on the official loan servicer website. Understanding these basics will allow you to plan for the future and help you make decisions about whether to consolidate or refinance your loans.

2. Consider Loan Consolidation or Refinancing

For many people returning to school, consolidating or refinancing existing loans can be a smart way to simplify their repayment process. Let’s break down the two main options:

Consolidation

Consolidating your federal loans can make managing them easier. By combining multiple loans into a single loan, you can potentially reduce the number of monthly payments you need to make. This could give you a simpler approach to repaying your loans. However, be aware of the following considerations:

  • Loan Terms: Consolidation might not always offer lower interest rates. The interest rate on your new consolidated loan will be the weighted average of your current rates.
  • Loss of Benefits: Some federal loan benefits, such as eligibility for Public Service Loan Forgiveness or income-driven repayment plans, may be lost during consolidation.

If you plan to continue working in a public service job, consolidating may affect your ability to use forgiveness programs, so it’s important to carefully evaluate whether this is the right option for you.

Refinancing

Refinancing allows you to take out a new private loan to pay off your existing student loans. This could potentially offer a lower interest rate if your credit score is good and you have stable income. However, refinancing federal loans with a private lender means you lose access to federal protections such as income-driven repayment plans or loan forgiveness programs.

Refinancing might be beneficial if you are certain you can handle the new terms, but be careful when refinancing federal loans because you’ll no longer be able to access federal repayment options and benefits.

3. Evaluate Your Eligibility for Federal Student Loan Benefits

One of the most important aspects of managing student loans when returning to school is taking advantage of the federal student loan programs available to you. Some of these options could ease the financial burden significantly, depending on your circumstances.

Income-Driven Repayment Plans

If your financial situation has changed since you first took out your loans, enrolling in an income-driven repayment (IDR) plan could reduce your monthly payments. These plans adjust your payments based on your income and family size, and they may also lead to loan forgiveness after a set period.

Deferment and Forbearance

If you’re returning to school, you may be eligible to temporarily postpone payments on your federal student loans through deferment or forbearance. Deferment allows you to temporarily stop payments without accumulating interest on subsidized loans, but interest still accrues on unsubsidized loans during this period.

Forbearance allows you to temporarily suspend payments, but interest will accrue on both subsidized and unsubsidized loans. This option can help ease financial strain while you’re in school, but keep in mind that it may increase your overall loan balance due to accumulated interest.

Public Service Loan Forgiveness

If you’re pursuing a degree with plans to work in public service (nonprofit organizations, government agencies, etc.), you may be eligible for the Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF) program. After 10 years of qualifying payments while working in public service, the remaining balance of your federal loans could be forgiven.

If you’re already enrolled in this program, be sure to continue making qualifying payments, as returning to school may not interrupt your PSLF status, provided you meet the program requirements.

4. Create a Budget for Your Education

Before you return to school, it’s crucial to create a comprehensive budget that includes all of your living expenses, tuition, and loan payments. Creating a realistic budget will help you avoid financial surprises and ensure that you’re financially prepared for school. Here’s how to set yourself up for success:

Tuition and Fees

Start by estimating your tuition and fees for the upcoming semester. Consider both in-state and out-of-state tuition, along with any additional fees for labs, books, and technology. Many schools offer scholarships or financial aid to help offset these costs, so make sure you’re applying for any relevant funding opportunities.

Living Expenses

Living expenses can be a significant part of your school budget, particularly if you’re attending school away from home. Be sure to account for rent or housing costs, utilities, groceries, transportation, and personal expenses.

Loan Repayment

Include your loan repayments in your budget. Consider how your payments will change if you take out additional student loans for your return to school. Many students take out federal Direct Unsubsidized Loans or Grad PLUS Loans, which can help finance graduate studies. You should also think about how these payments will fit into your long-term financial plan.

Creating a budget and sticking to it will help you avoid taking on unnecessary debt while keeping your finances in check during your time in school.

5. Explore Scholarship and Grant Opportunities

When returning to school, it’s important to seek out additional sources of funding to minimize your loan debt. Scholarships and grants are excellent alternatives to loans, as they don’t need to be repaid.

Scholarships

Scholarships are available for nearly every field of study, and they’re often awarded based on merit, academic achievements, or specific demographics. Be sure to search for scholarships within your school, professional organizations in your field, and through national databases like Fastweb and Scholarship.com.

Grants

Grants are another form of financial aid that you don’t have to repay. The federal government, state governments, and various private institutions offer grants to eligible students. The Pell Grant is the most common federal grant, awarded to low-income students, while there are also specific grants available for certain professions or educational programs.

By applying for scholarships and grants, you can lower the amount you need to borrow and reduce your overall student loan burden.

6. Focus on Minimizing Debt

While taking on student loans is sometimes necessary, there are steps you can take to minimize the debt you incur. These strategies can help keep your finances healthy throughout your educational journey.

Work-Study and Part-Time Jobs

If possible, consider working part-time or participating in a work-study program. Work-study programs are often available through your school and offer flexible work hours to accommodate your academic schedule. Earning money while in school can help cover living expenses and reduce the amount you need to borrow.

Live Frugally

Living within your means is essential when returning to school. If possible, try to keep your living expenses low by opting for shared accommodations, cooking meals at home, and finding low-cost transportation options.

By cutting unnecessary costs and staying frugal, you can avoid taking on additional debt and potentially finish school with less financial strain.

7. Plan for Loan Repayment After Graduation

It’s also important to plan for life after graduation. Before you return to school, research the loan repayment options available to you and determine how you’ll manage your debt once you’ve completed your degree.

Standard Repayment Plans

Most federal loans offer a standard repayment plan, where you make fixed monthly payments over a period of 10 years. This is often the default repayment plan, but if it doesn’t work for you, there are other options available.

Extended and Graduated Repayment Plans

If you find the standard repayment plan unaffordable, you can explore extended or graduated repayment plans, which provide longer repayment periods or flexible payment schedules based on your income.

Loan Forgiveness

If you’re pursuing a career in public service, you may qualify for loan forgiveness through PSLF, as discussed earlier. Other loan forgiveness programs, such as Teacher Loan Forgiveness, may be available for certain professions.

Refinancing for Better Terms

Once you’ve established your career and income, refinancing may be an option to reduce your loan interest rates or adjust your payment terms. This could allow you to pay off your loans more quickly and with lower overall costs.

Conclusion

Managing student loans while returning to school requires thoughtful planning and careful consideration of your options. By assessing your current loan situation, exploring federal benefits, minimizing debt, and utilizing financial planning strategies, you can set yourself up for success both in school and beyond. Remember that the choices you make now can have a significant impact on your financial future, so take the time to understand your options and make informed decisions. With the right approach, you can handle student loans effectively and focus on achieving your academic and career goals without the financial burden weighing you down.
