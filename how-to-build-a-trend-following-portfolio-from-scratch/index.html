How to Build a Trend Following Portfolio from Scratch


Building a trend following portfolio from scratch is a powerful investment strategy that taps into the momentum of the market to generate favorable returns. Unlike traditional buy-and-hold approaches, trend following actively seeks to capitalize on sustained price movements, whether upward or downward. This method requires a clear understanding of market trends, disciplined risk management, and a well-defined process to select assets and execute trades. In this comprehensive guide, you’ll discover how to construct a trend following portfolio step-by-step, from setting your investment objectives to implementing trading systems and managing risk effectively.

Understanding the Foundations of Trend Following

Before diving into portfolio construction, it’s essential to understand what trend following entails. At its core, trend following is a systematic approach that aims to identify and follow the prevailing direction of asset prices. It relies on the principle that markets often exhibit persistent trends driven by investor psychology, economic fundamentals, and technical momentum.

Trend followers do not try to predict market tops or bottoms. Instead, they identify when a trend is in motion and ride that wave until evidence suggests it is ending. This discipline helps avoid the pitfalls of market timing and emotional decision-making.

Key characteristics of trend following include:

  • Systematic decision-making: Using predefined rules based on price and volume data.
  • Diversification: Spreading exposure across multiple uncorrelated assets.
  • Risk management: Applying stop losses and position sizing to preserve capital.
  • Adaptability: Being able to switch between assets or markets depending on the prevailing trends.

With these principles in mind, let’s break down how to build a robust portfolio centered around trend following.

Step 1: Define Your Investment Objectives and Constraints

Every successful investment strategy begins with clear objectives and a realistic appraisal of your constraints. Ask yourself:

  • What is your target return? Are you seeking steady growth, aggressive gains, or capital preservation?
  • What is your risk tolerance? How much drawdown or volatility can you endure?
  • What is your investment horizon? Trend following works well across medium to long timeframes but can be adapted for different periods.
  • What capital is available? This determines the scale of your portfolio and trade sizes.
  • Are there tax considerations or regulatory rules?

Understanding these parameters will help you select suitable assets and trading frequencies aligned with your goals.

Step 2: Choose Markets and Asset Classes

One hallmark of a solid trend following strategy is diversification across multiple markets and asset classes. This reduces correlation risk and increases the chance of capturing trends in different economic environments. Common asset classes for trend followers include:

  • Equities: Stocks and equity indices worldwide.
  • Commodities: Energy, metals, agriculture, and other raw materials.
  • Currencies (Forex): Major and minor currency pairs.
  • Fixed Income: Government and corporate bonds (though trends may be less persistent).

Selecting markets that have sufficient liquidity and trading volume is crucial for efficient execution and tighter spreads.

Diversification Rationale

Different markets react differently to various economic factors. For example, in inflationary periods, commodities may trend robustly, while bonds might decline. By adding diverse assets, you balance returns and reduce drawdowns.

When starting, choose around 10 to 20 instruments to avoid overcomplication while ensuring diversification. You can expand later as you gain experience and confidence.

Step 3: Develop or Select a Trend Following System

A trend following portfolio relies on specific trading systems or algorithms designed to detect trends and signal entry or exit points. There are many ways to implement trend following, but the most common methods include:

Moving Average Crossovers

One of the simplest and most intuitive methods, moving average crossovers use two moving averages of different periods (e.g., 50-day and 200-day). A buy signal occurs when the shorter-term average crosses above the longer-term average, indicating an uptrend. A sell or short signal happens when the opposite crossover occurs.

Advantages:

  • Easy to implement.
  • Works well in sustained trending markets.

Disadvantages:

  • Vulnerable to whipsaws in choppy markets.
  • May lag due to averaging.

Breakout Systems

Breakout methods focus on price levels, entering trades when prices break above resistance or below support zones established over a prior period (e.g., 20-day highs or lows). These systems attempt to catch new trends as they begin.

Advantages:

  • Captures new market moves.
  • Responsive to changing conditions.

Disadvantages:

  • False breakouts can cause losses.
  • Requires filtering techniques.

Momentum Indicators

Indicators such as the Relative Strength Index (RSI) or the Average Directional Index (ADX) help confirm trend strength and reduce false signals. Combining momentum with price-based systems can refine entry and exit timings.

Trend Strength Filters

Adding filters to confirm the existence of a strong trend before entering positions improves performance. For example, volatility filters or trend strength measures help avoid whipsaw trades.

System Parameterization and Backtesting

It’s essential to parameterize your selected system based on historical data relevant to your chosen markets. For example, you might test different moving average lengths to identify the best fit for equities versus commodities. Backtesting on historical data introduces confidence and helps adjust rules to reduce overfitting.

Step 4: Position Sizing and Risk Management

No matter how effective your trend detection system is, managing risk and determining proper position sizes will dictate long-term success.

Risk Per Trade

Establish your maximum risk per trade, often expressed as a percentage of your total portfolio (e.g., 1% to 2%). This protects your capital against unexpectedly large losses.

Volatility-Based Sizing

Adjust position sizes in proportion to the volatility of each asset. For instance, if one instrument is more volatile, you may reduce exposure to keep the portfolio risk balanced.

Use of Stop Losses

Implementing stop-loss orders limits downside risk on each trade. In trend following, stops are often placed just beyond recent price swing points or volatility bands.

Portfolio-Level Risk Controls

Set rules on maximum drawdown, exposure limits per asset, and aggregate position limits to avoid concentration risk.

Step 5: Execution and Monitoring

Building the ideal portfolio means little without consistent execution and monitoring.

Automate Where Possible

Use trading platforms or software to automate entries, exits, and position adjustments to minimize emotional interference and errors.

Schedule Routine Reviews

Regularly assess system performance, market conditions, and portfolio diversification. Markets evolve, and trend behavior changes over time.

Rebalance Periodically

Trend following portfolios may naturally become unbalanced as some positions grow. Periodic rebalancing helps maintain your desired allocation.

Step 6: Psychological Discipline and Patience

Trend following requires emotional discipline to endure drawdowns, avoid premature exits, and stick to the system rules. Trends sometimes take weeks or months to develop fully, and losses are part of the process.

Recognize that consistency and confidence in your system will reward patience over time.

Example of a Simple Trend Following Portfolio Setup

To concretize these concepts, here’s an example portfolio design:

  • Markets: S&P 500 ETF, Gold Futures, Crude Oil Futures, Euro/USD Forex, 10-year Treasury Bonds.
  • System: 50/200-day moving average crossover with a 20-day breakout filter.
  • Risk per trade: 1% of portfolio.
  • Position sizes: Adjusted for volatility using Average True Range (ATR).
  • Stop loss placement: ATR distance below entry price.
  • Review frequency: Weekly.

This diversified setup offers exposure to equity, commodities, currency, and bonds, harnessing trends across sectors.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

When building and managing a trend following portfolio, watch out for:

  • Overtrading: Jumping into multiple trades without strong confirmation leads to excessive costs and losses.
  • Ignoring market regimes: Some periods are range-bound; adapting filters or pausing trading can save losses.
  • Overfitting systems: Excessive curve-fitting reduces robustness and future performance.
  • Neglecting risk management: Even the best system can blow up a portfolio without proper controls.
  • Emotional trading: Deviating from rules under stress often leads to poor results.

Advantages of a Trend Following Portfolio

  • Potential for uncorrelated returns: Many trend systems perform well when traditional investments struggle.
  • Defined risk: Rules govern when to exit losing positions.
  • Adaptability: Can be applied across assets, markets, and timeframes.
  • Systematic: Reduces emotional bias and guesswork.

Final Thoughts

Building a trend following portfolio from scratch combines art with science. It blends market insight, disciplined risk control, and rigorous systems development. Success demands continuous learning and adjusting, but the reward is a resilient investment approach attuned to market dynamics.

By carefully defining your objectives, selecting diverse assets, implementing tested trend signals, sizing positions prudently, and maintaining execution discipline, you can construct a trend following portfolio capable of navigating varied market conditions.

Whether you are a beginner or an experienced investor, trend following offers a versatile framework to grow wealth and control risk, making it an invaluable tool in any investor’s arsenal.
