How to Manage Drawdowns in Trend Following Trading


Trend following trading strategies have long been popular among traders due to their simplicity and effectiveness. By focusing on riding the wave of established market trends, trend followers aim to capture large moves and profit from sustained directional movements. However, as with all trading strategies, drawdowns are an inevitable part of the process. Effectively managing drawdowns in trend following can be the difference between long-term success and failure.

In this blog post, we’ll explore strategies and techniques for managing drawdowns in trend following trading. By understanding the psychology, risk management principles, and practical methods to limit the negative impact of drawdowns, you’ll be better equipped to handle them and keep your trading plan intact.

What is a Drawdown?

Before diving into drawdown management techniques, it’s important to understand what a drawdown is in the context of trading. A drawdown refers to a decline in the value of a trading account or portfolio from its peak to its trough. This is often expressed as a percentage of the total account balance and represents the loss experienced during a losing streak or a period of adverse market conditions.

For example, if your trading account reaches a high of $100,000 and drops to $85,000 before rebounding, the drawdown would be $15,000 or 15%. Drawdowns are a natural part of trading, especially in trend following, where profits are typically realized over long periods, and short-term losses may occur as part of the process.

While drawdowns are to be expected, excessive drawdowns can severely impact a trader’s ability to recover and grow their account over time. Therefore, managing drawdowns effectively is a key aspect of trend following success.

The Importance of Managing Drawdowns

Understanding the importance of managing drawdowns in trend following trading goes beyond merely avoiding big losses. Drawdowns have psychological, financial, and strategic implications:

1. Psychological Impact

The psychological toll of a large drawdown can be detrimental to a trader’s performance. Losing confidence in the strategy or panicking during a rough stretch can lead to poor decision-making, including abandoning the system mid-way through a trend. Such actions are often the result of emotional reactions rather than logical assessments.

2. Capital Preservation

From a financial perspective, a drawdown represents a temporary reduction in capital. A large drawdown can hinder a trader’s ability to capitalize on future opportunities and recover losses. To ensure that your capital is protected and ready to take advantage of future trends, managing drawdowns effectively is critical.

3. Sustainability of the Strategy

Consistent drawdowns can signal that the current strategy might not be well-suited for current market conditions. If drawdowns consistently exceed acceptable levels, it may indicate that the trend-following system requires adjustments to remain profitable in the long run.

Key Strategies for Managing Drawdowns in Trend Following

Managing drawdowns effectively is a blend of sound risk management practices, psychological resilience, and strategic flexibility. The following strategies are proven methods to help traders mitigate the effects of drawdowns in trend following systems.

1. Position Sizing and Risk Management

The most important method of mitigating drawdowns is using proper position sizing. Effective position sizing limits the risk of any single trade or series of trades affecting the overall account balance too drastically.

How to Implement Proper Position Sizing

  • Fixed Fractional Method: This is one of the most common approaches, where traders risk a fixed percentage of their capital on each trade. For example, risking 1% of your trading capital per trade ensures that a string of losses will only impact a small portion of your overall capital.

  • Volatility-Based Position Sizing: This technique adjusts the position size based on the volatility of the market. In highly volatile markets, the position size is reduced to accommodate higher risk, while in less volatile markets, the position size is increased. This approach ensures that risk remains proportional to market conditions.

  • Kelly Criterion: A more sophisticated approach that aims to maximize the long-term growth of capital by adjusting position size based on the edge of a trading system. The Kelly Criterion optimizes the trade-off between risk and reward, aiming for a balance that allows traders to grow their accounts without taking on excessive risk.

The idea behind position sizing is to ensure that the account can weather the storm of a series of losing trades without jeopardizing the trader’s overall success. Proper risk management techniques allow trend followers to stay in the game long enough to benefit from the larger trends that follow short-term drawdowns.

2. Diversification of Markets and Systems

Diversification is another key principle for managing drawdowns. Instead of relying on a single market or asset class, trend followers can diversify across various markets to smooth out the impact of drawdowns.

Diversification Techniques

  • Asset Class Diversification: Trend followers often spread their trades across different asset classes such as stocks, bonds, commodities, and currencies. This reduces the risk that a drawdown in one asset class will severely affect the overall portfolio.

  • Market Diversification: Traders can diversify by trading across different markets within the same asset class. For example, diversifying within commodities by trading oil, gold, and agricultural products, or diversifying within stocks by investing in both large-cap and small-cap companies.

  • System Diversification: Some traders use multiple trend-following systems or trading strategies to reduce the likelihood of a prolonged drawdown. By utilizing different systems that perform well in different market conditions, traders can balance out the overall performance of their portfolio.

Diversification reduces the risk that a single losing streak will significantly impact your overall capital. By spreading your risk, you are less likely to experience a large drawdown that derails your strategy.

3. Cutting Losses and Utilizing Stop-Losses

While trend-following strategies are typically based on letting profits run, it’s still crucial to have a disciplined approach to cutting losses when they occur. A well-defined stop-loss strategy can prevent small losses from turning into significant drawdowns.

Types of Stop-Loss Orders

  • Fixed Stop-Loss: This is a traditional method where a predetermined percentage of the market price is set to exit the trade if the price moves against you. For example, a 2% stop-loss could be set for every position you take.

  • Trailing Stop-Loss: A trailing stop moves with the market price in the direction of your trend but will lock in profits if the market reverses. This allows you to capture profits while minimizing losses in case the market turns against you.

  • Volatility-Based Stop-Loss: This method uses the volatility of the market to set stop-loss levels. When volatility is high, the stop-loss is set wider to account for larger price swings, while in low volatility conditions, the stop-loss is set tighter to protect against smaller adverse movements.

While using stop-losses in trend-following systems can be counterintuitive, especially when the strategy focuses on capturing large trends, they are essential tools for preserving capital during periods of market uncertainty or when the trend may not develop as expected.

4. Avoiding Overtrading

Overtrading is another issue that can exacerbate drawdowns, especially when traders become overly eager after a series of losses. It’s important to stick to your strategy and not attempt to recover losses too quickly by taking on excessive risk.

Overtrading often happens as a result of the “fear of missing out” (FOMO), where traders feel pressured to take additional positions after a losing streak to recoup losses. This behavior leads to larger drawdowns and can permanently damage a trader’s confidence and account balance.

How to Avoid Overtrading

  • Predefined Trade Rules: Establish clear rules for entering and exiting trades and stick to them. This removes the emotional element from trading decisions and helps you avoid impulsive moves.

  • Use a Trading Plan: A detailed trading plan includes rules for risk management, position sizing, and trade selection. Adhering to this plan helps you stay focused and prevents emotional trading decisions.

  • Accepting Losses as Part of the Process: It’s essential to view losses as an inevitable part of the process rather than a sign of failure. Acknowledging that drawdowns are temporary helps traders stay calm and avoid making rash decisions to chase losses.

5. Psychological Resilience and Long-Term Focus

Lastly, managing drawdowns is as much about mental fortitude as it is about technical strategies. Trend following requires patience, discipline, and the ability to endure periods of drawdown without abandoning your system or second-guessing your decisions.

During drawdowns, traders are often tempted to abandon their strategy in favor of a more aggressive or emotionally driven approach. However, the key to long-term success in trend following is the ability to stay focused on the bigger picture and trust the process.

Developing Psychological Resilience

  • Mindfulness and Emotional Control: Developing mindfulness techniques and emotional control strategies can help traders manage the stress of drawdowns. Meditation, journaling, and taking breaks can all contribute to maintaining a calm and rational mindset.

  • Building Confidence Through Backtesting and Analysis: Confidence in your trading strategy is essential. Backtesting your trend-following system on historical data and analyzing past performance can help reinforce your belief in the strategy, making it easier to endure periods of drawdown.

  • Focus on the Long-Term: Trend following is a marathon, not a sprint. While short-term losses can be painful, focusing on long-term goals and understanding that drawdowns are part of the cycle helps maintain a long-term perspective.

Conclusion

Managing drawdowns in trend-following trading is essential to maintaining profitability and psychological resilience in the face of inevitable market fluctuations. By implementing proper position sizing, diversifying your trades, using effective risk management strategies, and building psychological resilience, you can navigate the ups and downs of trend following and come out ahead in the long run. The key is to stay disciplined, trust your strategy, and always focus on the bigger picture.

Whether you’re a beginner or an experienced trader, these principles will help you build a robust and sustainable trend-following trading system that can withstand the inevitable drawdowns while allowing you to capitalize on profitable trends when they arise.
