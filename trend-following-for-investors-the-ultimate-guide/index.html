Trend Following for Investors: The Ultimate Guide


Trend following has become one of the most talked-about approaches in the world of investing. Investors, both retail and professional, are drawn by its promise of riding market momentum and capturing profit during sustained price movements. But what exactly is trend following? Why does it work? How can you implement it in your own portfolio? This guide covers every essential you need to know, from the core concepts to execution and advanced strategies.

What Is Trend Following?

Trend following is a systematic investment strategy that seeks to capitalize on the persistence of market trends. Rather than attempting to predict market tops or bottoms, trend followers endeavor to enter positions when a price trend has already established itself—upwards or downwards—and exit as soon as the trend appears to reverse.

This approach rests on the historical observation that financial markets often exhibit prolonged and persistent price moves. Trend followers believe “the trend is your friend,” leveraging momentum rather than fighting it.

Why Trend Following Works

At first glance, the idea of buying high and selling higher (or selling low and covering lower) seems counterintuitive, especially to value investors. Yet, trend following works for several reasons:

  1. Human Psychology: Markets are driven by collective human behavior, which often results in emotional overreactions, herding, and momentum.
  2. Delayed Information Flow: News and major events cause gradual shifts as large investors digest information and progressively adjust positions.
  3. Market Inefficiencies: No market is perfectly efficient. Trends emerge from inefficiencies, and systematic strategies can exploit these.
  4. Cutting Losses, Riding Winners: Trend following inherently lets profits run and cuts losses short, dealing with the exact opposite of most investor behavior.

Historical Performance of Trend Following Strategies

Trend following has a storied history. Some of the most successful hedge funds deploy this approach at scale, often under labels like “managed futures” or “CTA” (Commodity Trading Advisor) strategies.

Legendary Trend Followers:

  • Richard Dennis & The Turtles: Famous for taking novices and turning them into successful traders using a rules-based trend following system.
  • Bill Dunn & Dunn Capital: Dedicated to systematic trend approaches, showing strong risk-adjusted returns over decades.
  • John W. Henry: His trend following funds preceded his famed involvement with the Boston Red Sox.

Long-term studies suggest that trend following can outperform in volatile or directional markets and provides portfolio diversification, especially during crisis periods.

The Fundamentals: Core Principles of Trend Following

  1. Price Is King: The system cares only about price action. Fundamentals, news, and rumors are ignored.
  2. Rules-Based Trading: Entries, exits, and risk management are governed by clear, objective rules.
  3. No Prediction: Trend followers do not attempt to anticipate market moves—entries occur only after trends establish.
  4. Diverse Markets: Success is found through broad diversification—stocks, bonds, commodities, and currencies.
  5. Long & Short: The strategy works in both directions, profiting from bullish and bearish trends alike.

Simple Trend Following Strategies

The beauty of trend following lies in its simplicity. Here are foundational strategies anyone can implement:

1. Moving Average Crossover

This is one of the oldest and most reliable trend following signals:

  • How it works: Buy when a short-term moving average (e.g., 10-day) crosses above a longer-term moving average (e.g., 50-day); sell or go short when the reverse occurs.

Example:

If the 50-day MA crosses above the 200-day MA: Go Long.  
If the 50-day MA crosses below the 200-day MA: Sell or Go Short.

2. Breakout Systems

Price breaks can signal the beginning of new trends.

  • How it works: Enter a buy when the price exceeds the high of the previous ’n’ days (such as 20, 50, or 100 days). Exit or reverse when the price drops below the low of the past ’n’ days.

Example:

If the price reaches a 20-day high: Go Long.  
If the price falls to a 20-day low: Exit or Go Short.

3. Donchian Channels

Richard Donchian’s system focuses on defining upper and lower bands (channels) based on recent highs and lows.

  • How it works: Buy when the price breaks above its highest high over X days; sell when it breaks below its lowest low over X days.

Risk Management in Trend Following

Success hinges less on the entry method and far more on risk management. Here’s the mantra: survive first, thrive later.

Key Risk Management Techniques:

  • Position Sizing: Don’t risk more than a small percentage (often 1-2%) of your capital per trade. Use the volatility of the asset (e.g., Average True Range) to size positions.
  • Stop Losses: Systematic stops, often trailing stops, keep losses small and profits running.
  • Portfolio Diversification: Spread risk across unrelated markets—if one trend fails, others can balance the portfolio.

The Impact of Drawdowns

Drawdowns are normal and can be deep. Trend followers live with periods of underperformance, knowing that outsized gains during trending markets can more than compensate.

Advanced Trend Following Concepts

After mastering the basics, these techniques enhance returns and manage risk:

1. Volatility Scaling

Adjust position sizes dynamically based on market volatility. This preserves risk exposure across different asset conditions.

2. Multiple Timeframe Analysis

Combine long-term trend signals with shorter-term trend indicators, enabling traders to refine entries and exits or scale positions.

3. Portfolio Optimization

Advanced practitioners use algorithms to allocate capital efficiently across assets, balancing correlation and volatility for smoother returns.

4. Adaptive Indicators

Adaptive moving averages or algorithms like the Kalman filter enable systems to adjust sensitivity based on changing market conditions.

How to Implement Trend Following: Step-by-Step Guide

1. Choose Your Markets

Diversification is your best friend. Consider including indices, sectors, commodities, currencies, and bonds. Many trend following systems perform better with more markets.

2. Define Your Rules

Use a transparent, systematic approach. Example rules might include moving average crossovers, breakouts, and fixed stop-losses.

3. Backtest Your Strategy

Use historical data to see how your approach performs over years or even decades. Pay close attention to drawdowns, losses, and win/loss ratios.

4. Refine and Optimize

Tweak your system. Test variations in your signal periods, position sizing, and stop-loss parameters. Beware of overfitting—avoid making your system too specific to past data.

5. Execute Religiously

Discipline is everything. Execute without emotion, following your rules as written. The biggest risk to trend followers is giving in to fear or greed.

6. Monitor and Adjust

While systematic, trend following strategies need oversight. Markets evolve. Periodically review your strategy, market universe, and correlations.

Common Pitfalls in Trend Following

No strategy is without challenges. Avoid these common missteps:

  • Chasing After Backtests: Too easily, investors fall into the trap of curve fitting—designing a strategy that looks perfect in hindsight but fails in live markets.
  • Abandoning the System: Emotional swings can cause investors to quit on a strategy after a string of losses—usually just before a big win.
  • Overconcentration: Focusing on too few markets or correlated assets can increase risk.
  • Ignoring Costs and Slippage: Fees, commissions, and slippage can erode returns, especially on frequent trading systems. Always factor in trading costs when evaluating performance.

Trend Following in Different Asset Classes

The principles of trend following apply across asset classes, but each carries unique nuances.

1. Stocks and Equity Indices

Equities tend to trend strongly in bull markets. Trend following systems here often filter for higher liquidity and employ longer timeframes to minimize noise and transaction costs.

2. Commodities

Commodities like gold, oil, corn, and copper are naturally cyclical. Strong push-pull dynamics (supply and demand imbalances) create profit opportunities for trend followers.

3. Currencies (Forex)

Currencies are influenced by macroeconomic trends, rates, and capital flows. High leverage and liquidity make Forex a popular choice, but trends can be choppier and whipsaws more common.

4. Bonds

Interest rate cycles generate strong trends in government bonds. Diversification between various maturities and countries enhances performance.

Integrating Trend Following Into a Portfolio

One of the most powerful attributes of trend following is diversification. Because trend following has little correlation with typical buy-and-hold or value strategies, it adds resilience to portfolios.

Examples of Integration

  • As a Core Trading Strategy: Dedicate a percentage of the portfolio to a diversified, systematic trend following fund or your own system.
  • As a Hedge: Use long/short trend following to hedge equity risks, particularly effective during market downturns.
  • Blended with Other Factors: Combine trend following with value, momentum, or carry strategies for a balanced approach.

Tools and Platforms for Trend Following

Software & Services

  • Backtesting Platforms: Amibroker, Tradestation, MetaTrader, QuantConnect.
  • Execution Brokers: Interactive Brokers, Tradestation, IG.
  • Data Providers: Yahoo Finance, Quandl, Bloomberg.

Books & Learning Resources

  • “Trend Following” by Michael Covel: A well-known book on the philosophy and practice of trend following.
  • “Following the Trend” by Andreas Clenow: Focuses on systematic trend strategies across futures markets.
  • CTAs and Hedge Fund Letters: Many professional trend followers publish insights or performance data that can be educational.

Final Thoughts: The Enduring Appeal of Trend Following

Trend following endures as a cornerstone of systematic investing for a reason. It is objective, adaptable, risk-aware, and deeply rooted in decades of market experience. Seasoned investors appreciate that predicting markets is difficult—if not impossible—but following trends can be systematically exploited for profit over the long run.

While drawdowns are inevitable and market conditions can shift, a carefully constructed and executed trend following strategy remains a robust toolkit for investors looking to diversify, reduce risk, and potentially achieve long-term returns. The journey to mastering trend following isn’t about having a crystal ball; it’s about having a well-tuned compass and the discipline to follow it.

If you’re searching for a strategy that’s stood the test of time, keeps emotion out of investing, and thrives on adaptability, trend following deserves a place in your arsenal. With persistence and a sound plan, you can turn market trends from a source of uncertainty into an engine of opportunity.
