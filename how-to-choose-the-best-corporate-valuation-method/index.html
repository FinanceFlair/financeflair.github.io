How to Choose the Best Corporate Valuation Method


As a business owner, figuring out the value of your company can be quite a daunting task. With numerous methods claiming to provide the most accurate results, it’s essential to take a step back and analyze each option thoroughly before making a decision. In this article, we’ll delve into the most prevalent corporate valuation methods, highlighting all their unique aspects and ultimately guiding you in selecting the best one for your business.

1. Asset-Based Method

The asset-based method is one of the more straightforward techniques for determining the value of a company. This approach evaluates your business based on the fair market value of the assets it owns, less any debts or liabilities.

Pros:

  • It offers a clear snapshot of your company’s financial standing by showcasing all physical, intangible, and invested assets.
  • It’s relatively easy to calculate and doesn’t require extensive analysis, making it ideal for smaller businesses.
  • Convenient for avoiding overvaluation that might occur with other methods.

Cons:

  • Doesn’t factor in earnings potential or goodwill, which might grossly undervalue the company.
  • Only considers tangible assets, thus disregarding potential value contributed by intangible assets like trademarks, patents, or brand reputation.

2. Earnings-Based Method

The earnings-based method operates on the principle that a company’s worth is ultimately tied to its capacity to generate profits. Two common approaches here are the Capitalization of Earnings (BOE) and Gordon Dividend Growth Model (WACC).

Pros:

  • Incorporates the all-important question of profitability, making it closely aligned with an investor’s perspective.
  • Adjusts for risk by accounting for debt and differences in risks across industries.
  • Tends to deliver an accurate representation of a company’s current reserves, plus the anticipated future profitability.

Cons:

  • May undervalue a company relying heavily on intangible assets.
  • Requires extensive financial data analysis, rendering it time-consuming for small companies.

3. Market-Based Method

The market-based method is an oh-so-popular approach since it reflects real-world buying-and-selling dynamics. It heavily relies on the comparable company analysis (CCA) and the precedent transactions method (PTM).

Pros:

  • Represents a realistic picture by relying on current market trends and previous transactions.
  • It’s dynamic and adaptable, giving consideration to an industry’s specific traits.
  • It’s more representative of a company’s actual worth, considering recent industry trends and similar ventures.

Cons:

  • Highly influenced by stock market fluctuations, leading to unpredictable outcomes.
  • Property-heavy or younger industries may struggle to find similar companies or previous transactions, skewing results.

The Final Verdict

Every business is different, and so are the challenges and opportunities. So, it’s crucial to understand and weigh the advantages and drawbacks of each method before finalizing. The asset-based method offers a straightforward, easy-to-compute valuation but may overlook the unquantifiable benefits. Earnings-based methods offer an accurate picture of a company’s earnings capacity, though it might undervalue asset-rich companies. Market-based methods, finally, rely on real-world data but are impacted by market erraticness.

A perfect valuation method is a unicorn in the whirling world of business. Yet, understanding these techniques and assessing your business in light of these could aid you in discerning the best one for your company’s unique valuation needs. Remember, knowledge unlocks opportunities – and that’s precisely the door this exploration opens for you. Happy valuing!
