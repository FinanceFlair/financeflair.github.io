A Step-by-Step Guide to Backtesting Trend Following Strategies


Backtesting is a crucial step in the development of any trading strategy, and trend following is no exception. In this comprehensive guide, we will walk you through the process of backtesting trend following strategies, highlighting the key steps and considerations to keep in mind. Step 1: Define Your Strategy Before diving into backtesting, it's essential to define your trend following strategy. This involves deciding on the types of markets you will trade (e.g., FX, stocks, futures), the time frames you will focus on (e.g., short-term, medium-term, long-term), and the signals you will use to enter and exit trades. For example, a simple trend following strategy might involve buying an asset when its price crosses above a moving average (MA) and selling it when the price crosses below the MA. Alternatively, you might use a more complex rule-based approach that involves multiple indicators and decision trees. Step 2: Gather Historical Data Once you have defined your strategy, you will need to gather historical data to backtest it. This involves collecting price and volume data for the assets you will trade, as well as any relevant market indicators. There are several sources for historical data, including:

Exchange databases: Many exchanges provide historical data for free or at a low cost. Market data providers: Companies like Quandl, Alpha Vantage, and Xignite offer comprehensive market data feeds. Open-source databases: Websites like Quandl and Alpha Vantage also offer open-source databases for free.

Step 3: Choose a Backtesting Platform Next, you will need to choose a backtesting platform to execute your strategy. Popular options include:

QuantConnect: An open-source platform for algorithmic trading that supports backtesting, paper trading, and live trading. Zulutrade: A social trading platform that allows users to backtest, trade, and earn rewards. MatLab: A programming tool primarily used by engineers, geeks, and mathematicians for a lot of computation and mathematical simulations

Each platform has its strengths and weaknesses, and you will need to choose the one that best aligns with your needs and goals. Step 4: Implement Your Strategy Once you have chosen a backtesting platform, it's time to implement your strategy. This involves writing code, selecting indicators, and setting parameters. When implementing your strategy, it's essential to keep in mind the following:

Risk management: Your backtesting platform should be able to assess the risk of your strategy and provide alerts when positions are at risk. Stop-loss: Your strategy should have a stop-loss in place to minimize losses. Profit targets

Step 5: Backtesting and Refining With your strategy implemented, it's now time to conduct the backtesting process. This involves running your strategy on historical data to evaluate its performance. There are several metrics to keep in mind when backtesting:

Return on Investment (ROI): The percentage return of your strategy relative to the initial investment. Sharpe Ratio: A measure of the risk-adjusted return of your strategy. Compounding return: The return on investment expressed in terms of the initial investment.

When running your backtest, you will likely want to vary multiple parameters to determine which parameter combination yields the best results. Step 6: Refine and Iteration The final stage in the process is to refine and iterate your strategy based on the results of the backtest. Refining your strategy may involve:

Checking assumptions: Validate the assumptions underlying your strategy. Handling slippage and commissions: Ensure your backtesting reflects realistic slippage and commissions. Considering alternative trade durations: Trade execution and choice of execution timing.

In terms of iteration, you will likely need to follow a testing, refining and more testing iterate strategy later in this process. Conclusion Backtesting is a complex and nuanced process that requires a deep understanding of the strategy, the market, and the tools used to execute trades.
