The Link Between Capital Structure and Cost of Capital


Capital structure and cost of capital are two pivotal concepts in corporate finance. While capital structure refers to the composition of a company’s long-term financing, the cost of capital denotes the rate of return required by investors to invest in the company’s assets. The connection between these two aspects plays a critical role in determining a company’s financial strategy. Let’s delve deeper into this link to fully grasp its significance.

Factors Influencing Capital Structure

Capital structure is determined by a company’s mix of debt, preferred stock, and common equity. Various factors influence a company’s capital structure, such as industry norms, tax considerations, business life cycle, and company-specific factors. A well-thought-out capital structure optimizes the balance sheet and helps in minimizing the cost of capital.

Cost of Capital: A Multi-Faceted Concept

The cost of capital primarily depends on the riskiness of a company’s operations, market demand, and investor preferences. Key elements contributing to the cost of capital include:

  1. Cost of Debt: This consists of the interest payments on borrowed funds. Interest rates vary depending on the market conditions, credit rating, and time horizon.
  2. Cost of Equity: This denotes the return required by shareholders. It is primarily determined by the company’s beta (a measure of volatility) and the prevailing equity market returns.
  3. Cost of Preferred Stock: This reflects the return expectations of preferred stockholders.

The cost of capital is an integral component of the capital budgeting process, which evaluates potential investments and their contribution to the overall corporation’s functioning.

The Link: How Does Capital Structure Affect Cost of Capital?

A company’s capital structure significantly impacts its cost of capital, as each form of financing carries its own risk and return characteristics. Here are some ways in which the two are connected:

Debt and Equity

Companies often opt for debt and equity financing because of their distinct advantages. However, a company that relies heavily on debt financing may have a higher cost of capital due to increased financial risk posed by the growing debt burden.

Conversely, a company that opts for extensive equity financing may enjoy a lower cost of capital but may risk diluting the control of current shareholders and enduring fluctuations in the stock market.

Lehman’s Pecking Order Theory

As per the Pecking Order Theory proposed by Steven A. Lehman, companies prefer issuing securities in the following order: (1) internal financing, (2) debt, (3) preferred stock, and (4) common stock.

This theory emphasizes that companies aim to maintain an optimal capital structure to minimize costs while considering the above-order preference for various financing options. Thus, a company’s capital structure decision-making process impacts the cost of capital.

Balancing the DuPont Triangle

The DuPont analysis segregates return on investment (ROI) into two parts: asset turnover and profit margin. These two elements give rise to the DuPont formula, which calculates the cost of capital:

Cost of Capital = Economic Value Added (EVA) / Total Invested Capital

Optimizing the use of the DuPont analysis alongside a suitable capital structure formula may lead companies to make more informed and strategic decisions that reduce their cost of capital and boost profitability.

To capitalize on this vital link, corporations should strive for a balanced capital structure, utilizing options such as internal financing, debt, and equity strategically. A well-thought-out financial plan that considers the inherent risks and returns associated with each financing method leads to more significant growth opportunities and higher shareholder returns.

In conclusion, capital structure and the cost of capital are two linchpins of corporate finance. By carefully navigating these interconnected concepts, companies can build a robust financial foundation that fosters growth, attracts investors, and bolsters long-term success.
