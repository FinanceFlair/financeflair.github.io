How to Use Leverage in Equity Research for Better Insights


Equity research is one of the most important pillars of investment decision-making. It involves analyzing stocks, companies, industries, and the market at large to provide recommendations on investment strategies. To make well-informed recommendations, analysts rely on various tools and techniques that help them assess the potential risks and rewards of an investment. One such tool is leverage.

Leverage is often discussed in financial markets, but its use in equity research can be a bit more complex. When applied correctly, leverage in equity research can provide deeper insights into a company’s financial health, profitability, and long-term sustainability. However, when used improperly, it can lead to distorted views and skewed analysis.

In this post, we will explore how leverage works in the context of equity research and how to effectively use it for better insights.

What is Leverage?

In a financial context, leverage refers to the use of borrowed capital to finance the purchase of an asset, with the expectation that the income generated from the asset will exceed the cost of the borrowing. In other words, leverage involves using debt to amplify potential returns.

In equity research, leverage often refers to a company’s use of debt to finance its operations. A company with high leverage has a significant amount of debt relative to its equity, while a company with low leverage uses less debt in its operations. This financial structure plays a crucial role in determining a company’s risk profile, its ability to meet obligations, and the potential return for investors.

Types of Leverage in Equity Research

When we talk about leverage in equity research, there are two main forms to consider:

  1. Operating Leverage: This refers to the proportion of fixed costs in a company’s total cost structure. Companies with high operating leverage have a higher proportion of fixed costs, meaning that any increase in revenue can lead to a more significant increase in profit. On the other hand, if revenue falls, the impact on profit can be severe.

  2. Financial Leverage: This relates to the amount of debt a company has taken on to finance its operations. Companies with high financial leverage have more debt relative to equity. While this can lead to higher returns when things go well, it also increases the financial risk of the company because it has fixed obligations to meet, regardless of its profitability.

Both types of leverage provide important insights into a company’s operations and overall financial health. Equity analysts must carefully evaluate these aspects to assess the risks and rewards of investing in a company.

How Leverage Enhances Equity Research Insights

Leverage can significantly enhance the insights equity researchers gain from their analysis. Here’s how:

1. Assessing Profitability and Risk

When assessing a company’s profitability, leverage plays a crucial role in determining how much of that profitability is attributable to the company’s own resources versus borrowed funds. A high level of financial leverage means that a company is borrowing significant amounts to finance its operations. This can magnify both profits and losses. If the company’s investments yield returns that exceed the cost of debt, the returns for equity holders can be significant.

On the flip side, if the company’s performance deteriorates, the risks of high leverage become apparent. A highly leveraged company may struggle to meet its debt obligations if revenue drops or costs rise unexpectedly. This scenario highlights the importance of understanding leverage in equity research, as it provides key insights into a company’s risk profile.

2. Identifying Undervalued or Overvalued Stocks

Leverage can help analysts identify stocks that are either undervalued or overvalued. By using leverage metrics such as the debt-to-equity ratio or interest coverage ratio, equity researchers can determine if a stock is trading at an appropriate value relative to its debt levels.

For example, companies with low debt may appear undervalued because they have less financial risk. Conversely, companies with high levels of debt might be overvalued if their leverage exceeds what is sustainable in the long run. This can present a profitable investment opportunity if the market is not correctly pricing in the risks associated with the company’s leverage.

3. Evaluating a Company’s Long-Term Viability

The level of leverage a company uses can be an indicator of its long-term viability. Companies with high leverage may face more difficulties in the long run, especially if the economic environment becomes challenging or if interest rates rise. A highly leveraged company must generate enough revenue to cover both operating expenses and debt obligations.

On the other hand, companies with lower leverage have greater financial flexibility and may be better positioned to weather downturns. Equity analysts often use leverage to assess a company’s risk of bankruptcy or financial distress, which is crucial in determining its long-term viability.

4. Understanding Financial Performance Trends

Leverage helps equity researchers understand how a company’s financial performance trends over time. By analyzing the effects of operating and financial leverage, analysts can identify whether a company is relying too heavily on debt to boost its profits or if it has the right balance between equity and debt financing.

For instance, if a company’s operating profits are growing but its debt levels are also increasing, it could indicate that the company is becoming more reliant on borrowed money to maintain profitability. Conversely, a company that reduces its debt while maintaining profitability might be seen as making a strategic move to lower risk and improve long-term stability.

5. Understanding the Impact of Interest Rates

The cost of debt is another factor where leverage plays a significant role in equity research. A company’s interest payments are tied to the amount of debt it carries, and as interest rates rise, the cost of debt increases. Companies with high leverage may see their profitability squeezed by rising interest rates, as more of their revenue goes toward paying off debt.

Equity researchers must account for these factors when using leverage to analyze stocks. They need to assess how sensitive a company is to interest rate changes and whether its current leverage structure will allow it to thrive in a rising rate environment. By doing so, they can forecast how external factors, such as changes in the broader economy, could affect a company’s financial performance.

How to Incorporate Leverage into Equity Research

Now that we understand how leverage can improve insights, let’s explore how to incorporate leverage into your equity research effectively.

1. Use Leverage Ratios

Leverage ratios are crucial tools in equity research. These ratios allow analysts to assess a company’s debt levels relative to its equity, earnings, and assets. The most commonly used leverage ratios include:

  • Debt-to-Equity Ratio (D/E): Measures the proportion of debt used to finance a company’s assets relative to its equity.
  • Debt-to-Assets Ratio: Shows the proportion of a company’s assets that are financed through debt.
  • Interest Coverage Ratio: Measures a company’s ability to meet its interest obligations with its earnings before interest and taxes (EBIT).

By calculating and analyzing these ratios, equity analysts can gain valuable insights into how a company is using leverage to finance its operations and whether it has a manageable level of debt.

2. Assess the Company’s Industry and Peer Comparison

Leverage varies across industries. Some sectors, such as utilities and real estate, tend to have higher levels of debt, while others, like technology or consumer goods, often operate with lower leverage. When conducting equity research, it is important to compare a company’s leverage to that of its peers and industry averages.

This industry comparison helps determine whether a company’s leverage is above or below the norm for its sector and whether it is operating within a reasonable range of debt. Analysts can use this information to assess the relative risk of a stock compared to its competitors.

3. Analyze Financial Statements

Financial statements are the cornerstone of equity research. When analyzing a company’s financial statements, analysts should closely examine its balance sheet, income statement, and cash flow statement to assess how leverage is impacting the company’s financial position.

On the balance sheet, analysts can look at the company’s debt levels and compare them to equity. In the income statement, analysts can examine how interest payments are affecting profitability, and in the cash flow statement, they can assess how debt repayment is impacting cash flows.

4. Look for Leverage-Related Risks

Leverage introduces several risks that equity researchers should keep in mind. These include:

  • Liquidity Risk: High leverage can lead to liquidity issues, especially if a company is unable to generate sufficient cash flow to meet debt obligations.
  • Solvency Risk: Excessive leverage can lead to solvency problems, where a company’s liabilities exceed its assets, making it unable to meet its financial obligations.
  • Volatility Risk: Highly leveraged companies are often more sensitive to changes in market conditions, making their stock prices more volatile.

Analysts should look for signs of these risks in the company’s financials and consider them when making investment recommendations.

Conclusion

Leverage is a powerful tool in equity research that can provide analysts with a deeper understanding of a company’s financial structure, profitability, and risks. By incorporating leverage into their analysis, equity researchers can better assess the sustainability of a company’s performance and make more informed investment decisions.

However, leverage also comes with risks. Analysts must carefully consider both the potential rewards and the risks associated with high levels of leverage, ensuring that their analysis is balanced and objective. By effectively using leverage in equity research, analysts can gain better insights into the market and identify the most promising investment opportunities.
