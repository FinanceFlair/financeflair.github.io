The Role of Anchoring and Adjustment Bias in Option Pricing


Option pricing is a complex field that requires a deep understanding of various factors and biases that can influence the valuation process. One such bias that plays a significant role in option pricing is the anchoring and adjustment bias. In this blog post, we will explore the concept of anchoring and adjustment bias and its implications for option pricing. Anchoring bias refers to the tendency of individuals to rely heavily on the initial piece of information presented to them when making decisions or judgments. This initial piece of information, known as the anchor, serves as a reference point that influences subsequent judgments and decisions. Anchoring bias can have a profound impact on option pricing, as it can lead to distorted valuations based on the initial reference point.

The Impact on Option Pricing

In the context of option pricing, anchoring bias can manifest in several ways. Traders and investors often anchor their valuations to the current market price of the underlying asset or to recent historical prices. This anchoring can lead to mispricing of options, as it fails to take into account other relevant factors such as market volatility, time to expiration, and intrinsic value.

For example, if an investor is considering the purchase of a call option on a stock that is currently trading at $100, they may anchor their valuation to this price. This anchoring bias may cause them to overvalue the option, as they fail to consider factors such as the potential for the stock price to decrease or the time value of the option.

The Role of Adjustment Bias

Adjustment bias refers to the tendency of individuals to make insufficient adjustments from an initial anchor when incorporating new information into their decision-making process. This bias can further exacerbate the impact of anchoring bias in option pricing. Traders and investors may fail to adequately adjust their valuations based on new market information, leading to mispriced options.

For example, if the investor mentioned earlier anchors their valuation to the current stock price of $100 and receives news of a negative earnings report, they may make insufficient adjustments to their valuation. This adjustment bias may cause them to underestimate the potential impact of this new information on the option’s value.

Mitigating Anchoring and Adjustment Bias

To mitigate the impact of anchoring and adjustment bias in option pricing, traders and investors should adopt a systematic and disciplined approach. This approach should involve a thorough analysis of all relevant factors, including market conditions, volatility, time to expiration, and intrinsic value. By avoiding overreliance on initial anchors and making appropriate adjustments based on new information, traders and investors can make more accurate valuations of options.

Additionally, being aware of one’s own biases and actively seeking alternative perspectives can also help mitigate the influence of anchoring and adjustment bias. Engaging in open discussions with colleagues, conducting thorough research, and considering multiple scenarios can provide a more holistic view of option pricing and reduce the impact of biases.

Conclusion

Anchoring and adjustment bias plays a significant role in option pricing. By understanding the impact of these biases and adopting a systematic approach, traders and investors can make more accurate valuations of options. Mitigating the influence of anchoring and adjustment bias requires discipline, awareness of biases, and a willingness to consider alternative perspectives. By doing so, market participants can enhance their decision-making process and improve their overall performance in option trading.
