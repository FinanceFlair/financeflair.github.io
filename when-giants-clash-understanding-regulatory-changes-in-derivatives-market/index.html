When Giants Clash: Understanding Regulatory Changes in Derivatives Market


The derivatives market, often described as the $600 trillion shadow banking system, is a world unto itself. An instrument used as a hedge, a speculative tool, or even a means to transfer risk, derivatives have long been shrouded in mystery, especially in the face of regulatory changes. This post sheds light on regulatory changes and how they continue to shape the derivatives market.

The Genesis of Derivatives Regulation

Derivatives became popular in the 1970s but faced their first major fall out in the late 1980s when Barings Bank collapsed due to unhedged derivative positions taken by a single trader. This collapse brought about the first wave of regulatory changes, making it mandatory for investment banks and brokerage firms to centralize risk management.

In 1994, the Group of Thirty, a think-tank comprising global leaders from finance, industry, government, and academia, recommended a comprehensive set of guidelines for derivatives regulation. Many countries incorporated these recommendations within their regulatory frameworks, but it wasn’t enough.

The Dot Com Bubble and Subprime Mortgage Crisis

The dot com bubble burst and the derivatives market was once again in the spotlight. Derivatives, specifically credit derivatives, played a significant role in the 2008 financial crisis, and it subsequently accelerated the pace of regulatory developments. It convinced governments and regulatory bodies that a new, more robust regulatory structure was essential to protect the market from such crises.

The Dodd-Frank Act

In 2010, the US passed the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act, aimed at increasing transparency in the derivatives market. Central to this act was the creation of a central clearinghouse for standardised derivatives contracts and a new agency to regulate over-the-counter (OTC) derivatives. These changes aimed to reduce the systemic market risks caused by the opaque nature of OTC derivatives.

EMIR and MiFID II

The European Markets Infrastructure Regulation (EMIR), introduced in 2012, shares the same objectives as Dodd-Frank. It applies to both EU residents and non-EU firms trading with EU clients and mandates the clearing and reporting of derivatives transactions, aiming to increase transparency and reduce systemic risk. Since its implementation, numerous infrastructural changes have taken place to facilitate these regulatory requirements.

The Markets in Financial Instruments Directive II (MiFID II), enacted in 2018, expands on these principles. It redefined the range of financial instruments and tightened pre- and post-trade transparency requirements. It aimed to improve investor protection by standardizing trading practices, and greatly impacted the derivatives market.

Open Questions Remain

Despite these changes, fundamental questions about the derivatives market’s role in systemic risk continue to be raised, and there are ongoing debates about its regulation. For instance, does central clearing and mandatory reporting provide enough transparency? Are there other areas that need to be addressed?

Concluding Thoughts

Regulatory changes in the derivatives market, since the 1990s, have been geared towards improving market transparency and reducing systemic risks. Post the 2008 financial crisis, the pace of change has accelerated, with significant legislation like the Dodd-Frank Act, EMIR, and MiFID II aiming to restructure and integrate the market in a way that promotes stability.

As the derivatives market continues to evolve, it is crucial to keep an eye out for further shifts in regulatory trends, as they could signal significant changes not just in the derivatives market, but in the global financial system at large. The answer to the question posed in the title, “When Giants Clash”, is always evolving, making it a story worth following.
