Trend Following vs. Contrarian Investing: What Works Best?


In the vast landscape of investment strategies, two approaches often stand out: Trend Following and Contrarian Investing. Each carries its own philosophy, risk profile, and appeal to different types of investors. But which one truly works best?

Let’s embark on a detailed exploration of these two strategies, their fundamental principles, historical performance, psychological underpinnings, and practical considerations to help you understand which might suit your investing style and financial goals.

Understanding Trend Following

Trend following is an investment strategy that capitalizes on the momentum of an asset’s price movement. The basic idea is simple: “The trend is your friend.” Investors who follow trends buy assets that have been rising in price and sell (or avoid buying) those that are declining.

Key Characteristics of Trend Following

  • Momentum-Based: The strategy is rooted in the belief that assets that have performed well recently will continue to do so, thanks to market psychology and behavioral biases.
  • Rule-Based System: Many trend followers use technical indicators, such as moving averages or breakouts, to signal when to enter or exit positions.
  • Relies on Market Efficiency Exceptions: Rather than attempting to predict future directions based on fundamental data, trend followers react to price action, assuming that markets exhibit predictable momentum patterns.
  • Risk Management Focused: Because trends can reverse suddenly, effective trend following includes strict stop-loss strategies.

Examples of Trend Following

  • Moving Average Crossovers: Buying when the short-term moving average crosses above the long-term average and selling when the opposite occurs.
  • Breakout Strategies: Entering trades when a price breaches recent highs or lows, signaling a new trend.

Historical Performance

Trend following has a strong track record across various asset classes, including stocks, commodities, and currencies. It tends to perform well during strong trending markets, such as bull runs or persistent bear markets, by riding waves of momentum.

However, during choppy or sideways markets, trend following strategies often suffer whipsaws—entering and exiting positions frequently with small losses.

Demystifying Contrarian Investing

Contrarian investing takes the opposite approach: it involves going against prevailing market trends or sentiment. Contrarians buy when others are fearful and sell when others are greedy, operating on the belief that markets often overreact to good or bad news.

Core Principles of Contrarian Investing

  • Market Sentiment as a Guide: Contrarians pay close attention to investor psychology, sentiment indicators (e.g., volatility indices, survey data), and market extremes.
  • Value-Oriented: Many contrarian strategies look for undervalued assets that the market has unjustly penalized.
  • Discipline to Resist Herd Mentality: Contrarians must have the conviction to act in defiance of popular opinion.
  • Long-Term Horizon: Because market overreactions can persist, patience is crucial for this strategy to pay off.

Practical Manifestations

  • Buying After a Market Crash: When prices plunge due to panic selling, contrarians see opportunity.
  • Selling Overhyped Stocks: When certain assets become popular due to hype or speculative bubbles, contrarians may reduce exposure.

Historical Performance

Contrarian investing has proven effective in capitalizing on market corrections and reversals. It particularly excels in identifying opportunities during phases of irrational exuberance or fear.

However, contrarian investments can remain out of favor for extended periods, necessitating a strong mental framework and risk tolerance.

Psychological Foundations: Why Do These Strategies Work?

Both trend following and contrarian investing build on behavioral finance insights. Market participants are not perfectly rational; emotions, biases, and crowd behavior profoundly influence markets.

Trend Following & Herd Behavior

Trend followers benefit from market momentum driven by herd behavior. When many investors buy into a rising asset, it pushes prices higher, attracting more buyers—a reinforcing loop that extends trends.

Contrarian Investing & Overreaction

Contrarians exploit human tendencies toward overreaction. Panic and greed cause prices to overshoot fundamentals, offering bargains or signals to sell.

In essence, these strategies play off different facets of investor psychology: trend following rides the wave, while contrarian investing looks for the turning points.

Which Strategy Works Best?

The answer depends on multiple factors, including your risk tolerance, investment horizon, expertise, and personality.

For the Rule-Oriented Investor

If you prefer a defined system with clear entry and exit signals, trend following provides a framework that can be backtested and automated. It suits those who can stick to rules and handle losses occurring during range-bound markets.

For the Patient and Independent Thinker

If you’re comfortable acting against prevailing norms and can endure periods of underperformance, contrarian investing offers chances to find undervalued gems and profit from market overreactions. Emotional resilience is key to this approach.

Blending Both Approaches

Many successful investors combine elements of both strategies to diversify risk and capture different market dynamics. For example:

  • Use trend following for tactical allocation during strong market phases.
  • Apply contrarian insights for long-term value investing and identifying cyclical turnarounds.

Practical Tips to Implement These Strategies

Starting Trend Following

  1. Pick Your Markets: Trend following works well in futures, forex, equities, and ETFs.
  2. Set Clear Rules: Define entry/exit criteria like moving average crosses or breakout levels.
  3. Risk Management: Use stop losses and position sizing to control drawdowns.
  4. Backtest and Track: Rigorously test strategies on historical data before committing capital.

Getting Into Contrarian Investing

  1. Study Sentiment Indicators: Track surveys, volatility indices, and market breadth.
  2. Identify Overreactions: Compare valuation metrics and fundamentals to prices.
  3. Be Patient: Prepare for possibly long wait times before market sentiment shifts.
  4. Stay Informed: Contrarian investing requires up-to-date market knowledge.

Real-World Examples

  • Trend Following Success: The “Turtle Traders” of the 1980s famously profited from trend-following strategies in commodities and currencies, proving that systematic methods can yield strong returns.

  • Contrarian Investing Wins: Legendary investors like Warren Buffett have often taken contrarian positions, buying when markets were down and fearful.

Final Thoughts

Both trend following and contrarian investing offer distinct pathways with documented merits. Neither is inherently superior—it comes down to your personality, market conditions, and investment goals.

Rather than choosing one exclusively, being fluent in both approaches can strengthen your overall strategy, allowing you to adapt to varying market cycles.

Investment success is less about picking “the best” strategy and more about understanding when and how to apply different tools wisely, while maintaining discipline and emotional balance.

Whether you’re drawn to ride the momentum or swim against the tide, mastering these investment philosophies can empower you to navigate markets with greater confidence and resilience.
