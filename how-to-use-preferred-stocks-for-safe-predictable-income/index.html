How to Use Preferred Stocks for Safe, Predictable Income


Investing for consistent income can be challenging, especially in a market that is constantly shifting. While bonds and dividend-paying stocks are traditional options for stable returns, there is another often-overlooked asset class that can provide a reliable income stream: preferred stocks. These securities can offer a unique blend of income potential, stability, and lower risk compared to common stocks, making them a valuable tool for income-focused investors. In this post, we will explore how preferred stocks work, their benefits, and how to effectively incorporate them into your investment strategy to secure predictable income.

What Are Preferred Stocks?

Preferred stocks are a type of equity security that sits between common stocks and bonds in terms of risk and return. Like common stocks, preferred stocks represent ownership in a company. However, preferred stockholders have a higher claim on the company’s assets and earnings than common stockholders. This preference extends to dividends, which are paid to preferred shareholders before common shareholders receive any.

While preferred stocks don’t have voting rights, they typically offer a fixed dividend, which makes them a stable income-producing asset. The dividends are usually paid quarterly or monthly, depending on the company’s policy, and are often more predictable than those paid on common stocks. This characteristic makes preferred stocks an attractive choice for income-focused investors.

The Benefits of Preferred Stocks for Income Investors

1. Steady and Predictable Income

One of the primary reasons investors turn to preferred stocks is their ability to provide a consistent and predictable income stream. The fixed dividend payments are established at the time of issuance, which means investors can rely on a set amount of income, typically paid quarterly. For individuals looking to build a reliable cash flow, this can be especially appealing.

For example, many preferred stocks offer dividend yields that range between 4% to 7%, significantly higher than the average dividend yield of common stocks or bond interest rates. With this consistent payout, preferred stocks are often favored by retirees or those in need of regular income.

2. Priority Over Common Stock in Dividend Payments

Preferred shareholders enjoy priority when it comes to dividend payments. In the event that a company experiences financial difficulties and cannot pay its dividends to both common and preferred shareholders, preferred stockholders are paid first. This security provides a higher level of protection for income investors in times of economic downturns.

3. Capital Appreciation Potential

While preferred stocks are primarily purchased for their income-generating potential, they also have some room for capital appreciation. When interest rates fall or a company improves its financial standing, the market value of preferred stocks can rise. Though preferred stocks generally don’t experience the same level of price volatility as common stocks, they can still offer some price appreciation over time, adding an extra benefit for investors.

4. Less Volatility Compared to Common Stocks

One of the biggest challenges when investing in common stocks is volatility. The stock market can fluctuate wildly due to market sentiment, economic data, and global events. Preferred stocks tend to be less volatile because their value is primarily driven by interest rates and the company’s ability to pay dividends, rather than market sentiment alone. For investors seeking a safer alternative to common stocks, preferred stocks are often a better choice, offering more stability without sacrificing income potential.

5. Tax Advantages

Depending on the jurisdiction, preferred dividends may receive more favorable tax treatment compared to interest income from bonds or ordinary income from common stocks. In the United States, for example, qualified dividends paid by preferred stocks may be taxed at a lower rate than interest income, which can make a significant difference in your after-tax income. However, it’s essential to consult with a tax professional to understand the specific tax implications based on your individual circumstances.

Types of Preferred Stocks

Not all preferred stocks are created equal, and it’s essential to understand the different types available before making an investment. Here are the most common types:

1. Cumulative Preferred Stock

Cumulative preferred stockholders have the right to receive any missed dividend payments in the future before any dividends can be paid to common stockholders. This feature adds a layer of security for investors, as the company is obligated to make up for any skipped dividend payments.

2. Non-Cumulative Preferred Stock

In contrast to cumulative preferred stock, non-cumulative preferred stock does not guarantee missed dividends will be paid in the future. If the company skips a dividend payment, the stockholder has no recourse to receive those dividends. This type of preferred stock carries more risk and is less attractive to income-focused investors.

3. Convertible Preferred Stock

Convertible preferred stocks can be converted into a predetermined number of common shares, typically at the discretion of the investor. This feature gives the investor the potential for upside capital appreciation if the company’s stock price rises significantly. However, these stocks may offer lower dividend yields than non-convertible preferred stocks due to their added potential for capital gains.

4. Callable Preferred Stock

Callable preferred stocks can be redeemed by the issuer before the maturity date, usually at a premium above the par value. While this offers flexibility for the issuing company, it introduces some risk for investors. If interest rates decline or the company’s financial situation improves, it may choose to call the stock, leaving investors to reinvest their capital in lower-yielding securities.

5. Adjustable-Rate Preferred Stock

These preferred stocks have a dividend rate that adjusts periodically based on a reference interest rate, such as LIBOR (London Interbank Offered Rate). This type of preferred stock offers some protection against rising interest rates, as the dividend yield increases along with the reference rate. Adjustable-rate preferred stocks are more attractive in environments where interest rates are expected to rise.

How to Choose Preferred Stocks for Income

When selecting preferred stocks for income, investors should consider several factors to ensure that the investment meets their income goals and risk tolerance. Here are some key aspects to look for:

1. Dividend Yield and Payment Frequency

The dividend yield is one of the most important factors to consider when evaluating a preferred stock. This yield indicates how much income you can expect to receive based on the current price of the stock. Investors should look for a yield that is attractive relative to other income-generating assets, such as bonds and dividend-paying common stocks. Additionally, the frequency of dividend payments—monthly, quarterly, or semi-annually—should align with your income needs.

2. Creditworthiness of the Issuer

Just like bonds, preferred stocks are issued by companies, and the financial health of the issuing company is a critical factor in the safety of the investment. It’s essential to assess the company’s credit rating and financial strength before purchasing its preferred stock. Companies with higher credit ratings are less likely to default on dividend payments, which can provide greater security for income investors.

3. Call Risk

Callable preferred stocks may be redeemed by the issuing company, which could limit your ability to earn income if the stock is called early. Be sure to assess the likelihood of the stock being called based on the company’s current financial position and the prevailing interest rate environment. If you are concerned about call risk, it may be better to focus on non-callable preferred stocks.

4. Par Value and Price

The par value of preferred stock is the amount that the issuing company agrees to pay the shareholder in the event the stock is redeemed. Preferred stocks are typically issued at par value, but their market price can fluctuate based on interest rates, the issuing company’s financial health, and other market factors. Investors should be mindful of the price they pay for preferred stocks, as buying at a premium can reduce the overall yield.

5. Dividend Coverage Ratio

The dividend coverage ratio is a measure of a company’s ability to pay its dividends, both on preferred and common shares. A high dividend coverage ratio suggests that the company generates enough earnings to comfortably meet its dividend obligations. A low ratio, on the other hand, could signal potential risks to the sustainability of dividend payments.

Risks of Investing in Preferred Stocks

While preferred stocks can provide a steady income, they come with risks that investors should be aware of:

1. Interest Rate Risk

Preferred stocks are sensitive to changes in interest rates. When interest rates rise, the value of preferred stocks tends to fall. This is because newer issues will offer higher yields, making older preferred stocks with lower yields less attractive. Conversely, when interest rates fall, the value of preferred stocks can increase, as their fixed dividends become more attractive.

2. Credit Risk

If the issuer of the preferred stock experiences financial difficulties or goes bankrupt, the dividends may be suspended, or the stock may become worthless. This risk can be mitigated by investing in preferred stocks from companies with strong credit ratings, but it’s still a factor to consider.

3. Call Risk

As mentioned earlier, callable preferred stocks may be redeemed early by the issuer. If interest rates decline or the company’s financial situation improves, the stock may be called, and investors may be forced to reinvest their capital in lower-yielding securities.

4. Liquidity Risk

Preferred stocks tend to be less liquid than common stocks, meaning they may be more difficult to buy or sell at favorable prices. This can make it harder for investors to exit their positions if necessary, especially during periods of market volatility.

Conclusion

Preferred stocks are an excellent tool for investors seeking safe, predictable income. They offer the potential for higher yields compared to bonds, and their priority over common stock dividends adds an extra layer of security. While they come with risks such as interest rate risk, credit risk, and call risk, careful selection and a focus on high-quality issuers can mitigate these concerns. By understanding how preferred stocks work and the different types available, you can build a portfolio that generates reliable income while maintaining a lower level of risk than traditional equity investments. Whether you’re building a retirement income strategy or simply seeking to enhance your cash flow, preferred stocks offer a valuable addition to any income-focused investment plan.
