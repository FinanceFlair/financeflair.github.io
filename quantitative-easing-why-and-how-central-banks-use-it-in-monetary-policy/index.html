Quantitative Easing: Why and How Central Banks Use It in Monetary Policy


Quantitative Easing (QE) is a monetary policy implemented by central banks in order to stimulate their respective economies. This powerful tool has gained significant attention in recent years, specifically in 2008 following the global financial crisis. Through QE, central banks purchase large quantities of securities from commercial banks and other financial institutions in the open market. This action not only injects cash into the financial system, but also aims to lower interest rates and encourage lending, investment, and economic growth. While QE is by no means a new concept, its prominence has led to much speculation about its effectiveness, legitimacy, and potential risks.

Understanding Quantitative Easing

At its core, QE is designed to combat severe financial crises, specifically periods of high unemployment and deflation, or even in worse cases, potential deflationary spirals. By purchasing significant amounts of securities like government bonds from commercial banks and financial institutions, central banks create new money to finance these purchases. This instantly increases the money supply and drives up the prices of those bonds, thus increasing their value in the market. In the process, it increases the reserves of commercial banks, which promotes lending and monetary growth.

Why Central Banks Use Quantitative Easing

QE is not arbitrary and is primarily used in response to specific signs of economic distress. The primary reasons behind central banks adopting this policy are:

  1. Low interest rates: By purchasing large amounts of bonds, central banks effectively remove them from the market. When fewer bonds are available, their prices increase, and interest rates decrease, incentivizing borrowing and encouraging economic activities.

  2. Economic Growth: Central banks use QE to stimulate the economy by providing capital to banks that can then lend to businesses and individuals. This increased access to financing fosters investments, boosts economic growth, and increases the overall employment rate.

  3. Avoiding Deflation: The risk of deflation - a decrease in the general price level of goods and services - often leads to liquidation (spending less and saving more because prices are expected to decrease even further). This, in turn, causes reduced economic activities, resulting in a deflationary spiral. QE prevents this by stimulating demand through lower interest rates.

How Central Banks Execute Quantitative Easing

The actual implementation of QE is conducted through the Open Market Operations (OMO), which is a central bank’s buying and selling of government securities in the open market. Here’s a step-by-step explanation of how central banks execute QE:

  1. Determine the Target: Central banks identify economic indicators, like inflation rate, unemployment rate, and Gross Domestic Product (GDP) growth rate, to determine whether QE is necessary.

  2. Transmission Mechanisms: Once the decision to implement QE is made, central banks must select specific transmission mechanisms to inject money into the economy effectively. Common transmission mechanisms include reducing interest rates, increasing the reserves of commercial banks, and increasing their ability to lend.

  3. Execution: Central banks execute QE by purchasing government bonds, foreign currencies, or other financial instruments in the open market. These securities could either be short-term bonds (like Treasury Bills) or long-term bonds (like Government Bonds).

  4. Monitoring effects: Central banks continuously monitor changes in the economy and respond by adjusting the intensity and scale of QE as necessary to ensure the desired effects.

Criticisms and Concerns

Despite central banks’ best efforts to stimulate economic growth through QE, there are significant concerns and criticisms about its effectiveness and potential risks.

  1. Risk of Hyperinflation: By increasing the money supply, QE could potentially lead to inflation, and in extreme cases, hyperinflation. Critics argue that continually printing money to stimulate the economy often results in the devaluation of currency, hurting consumers, and exacerbating income inequality.

  2. Unpredictability: The long-term effects and risks of QE remain unclear, making it a risky and unpredictable strategy for economic recovery.

  3. Asset Bubbles: Some economists argue that QE could lead to the creation of asset bubbles in the financial market, which may burst and trigger another financial crisis.

Conclusion

Quantitative Easing is a powerful yet controversial tool central banks employ to stimulate economic growth during times of economic distress. While it may provide temporary relief for struggling economies by increasing the money supply and lowering interest rates, it’s crucial for central banks to carefully execute this policy and monitor its effects rigorously. Only time and ongoing research will reveal the long-term effectiveness and potential risks of central banks’ extensive use of QE in response to contemporary economic challenges.
