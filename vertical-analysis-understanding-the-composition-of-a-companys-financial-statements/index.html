Vertical Analysis: Understanding the Composition of a Company's Financial Statements


Financial statements are vital tools for understanding the financial health and performance of a company. They provide valuable insights into a company’s operations, profitability, and overall financial status. One method that can help in analyzing these statements is vertical analysis. In this blog post, we will explore vertical analysis and how it can enhance your understanding of a company’s financial statements.

What is Vertical Analysis?

Vertical analysis, also known as common-size analysis, is a technique used to evaluate the composition of a company’s financial statements. It involves expressing each item on the financial statement as a percentage of a relevant base figure. This allows for a more comprehensive understanding of the relative importance of each component within the statement.

Vertical analysis is typically performed on the income statement and the balance sheet. By expressing each line item as a percentage, it becomes easier to compare and analyze the financial statements of different companies or track changes within a single company over time.

Performing Vertical Analysis

To perform vertical analysis, you need to choose a relevant base figure against which each line item will be expressed as a percentage. The base figure is usually the total revenue for the income statement and the total assets for the balance sheet.

Let’s take an example to illustrate how vertical analysis works. Suppose we have a company’s income statement with the following figures:

Revenue: $1,000,000
Cost of Goods Sold: $600,000
Gross Profit: $400,000
Operating Expenses: $200,000
Net Income: $200,000

To perform vertical analysis on this income statement, we will express each line item as a percentage of the total revenue.

Revenue: 100%
Cost of Goods Sold: 60%
Gross Profit: 40%
Operating Expenses: 20%
Net Income: 20%

By expressing each line item as a percentage, we can easily see the proportion of revenue allocated to different components of the income statement. This provides valuable insights into the company’s cost structure, profitability, and operational efficiency.

Interpreting Vertical Analysis

Vertical analysis allows for meaningful comparisons and trend analysis. By comparing the vertical analysis of multiple companies or tracking changes in a single company’s financial statements over time, you can gain insights into their financial performance and identify potential areas of concern.

For example, if the cost of goods sold as a percentage of revenue is significantly higher than industry peers, it may indicate inefficiencies in the company’s supply chain or production process. Similarly, a decreasing trend in operating expenses as a percentage of revenue over time can suggest improving operational efficiency.

Vertical analysis also helps in identifying the impact of key events or decisions on a company’s financial statements. For instance, if there is a sudden increase in the percentage of operating expenses, it may be due to a new marketing campaign or expansion into new markets.

Conclusion

Vertical analysis is a powerful tool for understanding the composition of a company’s financial statements. By expressing each item as a percentage of a relevant base figure, it provides valuable insights into the relative importance of different components within the statements. This analysis allows for meaningful comparisons, trend analysis, and identification of potential areas of concern or improvement.

Next time you come across a company’s financial statements, consider performing vertical analysis to gain a deeper understanding of their financial health and performance. It’s a simple yet effective technique that can greatly enhance your ability to evaluate and interpret financial information.
