Trend Following and Scalping: Can They Be Combined?


In the world of trading, there are various strategies employed by traders in their quest to profit from market movements. Among these strategies, trend following and scalping are two of the most widely discussed. Each method has its own set of principles and techniques, attracting traders for different reasons. But what happens when you try to combine these two approaches? Can they coexist, or do they conflict with one another?

This post explores the fundamental concepts of trend following and scalping, their core differences, and whether they can be merged effectively. By the end, you’ll have a clearer understanding of how these strategies might work together, or if one should be abandoned in favor of the other.

Understanding Trend Following

Trend following is a long-term trading strategy that aims to capitalize on sustained market movements. The basic idea is simple: once a trader identifies a trend—whether upward or downward—they take positions that align with the direction of that trend. The goal is to stay in the trade as long as the trend persists, riding it for as long as possible.

Key Features of Trend Following:

  • Patience: Trend followers are often in trades for a longer duration, ranging from hours to days or even weeks.
  • Risk Management: Trend followers use various tools such as stop losses and trailing stops to manage risk and protect profits while riding the trend.
  • Technical Indicators: Many trend-following traders use indicators like moving averages, the Relative Strength Index (RSI), and the Average Directional Index (ADX) to identify the presence and strength of a trend.

Trend following is rooted in the belief that markets often exhibit long-lasting trends, and by capturing these movements, traders can achieve substantial profits. However, it requires a deep understanding of market dynamics and the ability to remain patient during periods of low volatility.

The Nature of Scalping

On the other side of the spectrum lies scalping, a strategy that focuses on making quick profits from small price movements. Scalpers hold positions for a very short time—sometimes just seconds or minutes—before closing them out. The idea is to accumulate small profits over a large number of trades, ultimately leading to substantial gains.

Key Features of Scalping:

  • Speed: Scalpers thrive in high-frequency environments, executing numerous trades throughout the day.
  • Small Profit Margins: Unlike trend followers, scalpers are not looking for large price moves. Instead, they aim for small, incremental profits from tiny price shifts.
  • Leverage: Scalpers often use leverage to amplify their small gains and increase their trading volume.
  • Volume: Scalpers require access to liquid markets, as they need to enter and exit positions rapidly without significantly moving the price.

Scalping requires quick decision-making, exceptional market awareness, and the ability to handle stress. It is often performed in highly liquid markets like forex, where price movements can be predicted with relative accuracy on very short timeframes.

The Fundamental Difference Between Trend Following and Scalping

While both strategies aim to profit from price movements, their approaches are drastically different:

  • Time Horizon: Trend following focuses on the long-term, while scalping is all about short-term movements. Trend followers typically hold positions for longer periods, looking for sustained price shifts. Scalpers, in contrast, aim to capitalize on tiny fluctuations within minutes or seconds.

  • Profit Targets: A trend follower may look for large profits from a single trade, holding out for a big price move. Scalpers, however, look for numerous small profits that accumulate over time, requiring multiple trades within a day.

  • Risk Tolerance: Trend following generally involves a higher risk tolerance, as positions can be exposed to market fluctuations for longer periods. Scalping, while involving smaller risk per trade, can accumulate risk over many trades, especially when leverage is involved.

  • Market Analysis: Trend followers typically analyze the broader market trend using indicators and charts. Scalpers, on the other hand, often rely on very short-term technical analysis, such as tick charts and order book analysis, to identify micro-level price movements.

Given these differences, it seems like combining trend following with scalping could lead to a blend of contradictory tactics. However, there may be ways to use both strategies together, depending on the trader’s preferences and market conditions.

Can Trend Following and Scalping Be Combined?

The short answer is yes—but with some important considerations. Combining trend following with scalping requires adapting both strategies to fit within a cohesive framework that makes sense for the individual trader. It’s about finding the right balance between short-term and long-term techniques. Here are some potential ways these strategies might be merged:

1. Scalping Within a Trend

One of the most practical ways to combine trend following and scalping is to apply the scalping strategy during periods when a clear trend is in place. Here’s how it works:

  • Identify the Trend: First, a trend-following strategy is employed to identify the current market trend using tools like moving averages, trendlines, or momentum indicators.

  • Look for Small Opportunities Within the Trend: Once the trend is confirmed, the scalper can zoom in on smaller price movements within the broader trend. For example, while the trend is moving upward, scalpers can enter short-term trades to take advantage of minor pullbacks or consolidations within the larger trend.

  • Exit the Market Quickly: Scalpers can exit their positions quickly once they’ve achieved their small profit target, continuing to ride the broader trend. This allows them to capitalize on smaller movements without counteracting the main trend.

2. Scalping the Pullbacks in a Trend

Another approach is to scalp during temporary pullbacks or retracements within a dominant trend. In many cases, trends do not move in a straight line; they exhibit periods of consolidation or correction before resuming their primary direction.

  • Spot the Pullback: Traders can use retracement tools like Fibonacci or simple trendline analysis to identify when a pullback is likely to occur.

  • Scalp the Pullback: Once the pullback is identified, scalpers can enter trades in the direction of the trend, expecting the market to continue in the prevailing trend after the pullback has ended.

This approach allows traders to maximize profit from both short-term price movements and the longer-term trend without being overly exposed to the risks of holding positions too long.

3. Timing and Market Conditions

Successful blending of trend following and scalping also depends on market conditions. In volatile markets with clear trends, scalpers may find it easier to find small opportunities within the broader trend. However, in choppy or range-bound markets, scalping may not align with trend-following strategies, as trends will be less predictable and harder to identify.

  • Adaptability: A trader looking to combine these strategies must be adaptable, switching between scalping and trend-following depending on the market context. For example, in a strong trending market, a trader may focus on scalping smaller moves in the direction of the trend, while in a sideways market, they might stick to trend-following.

4. Different Timeframes for Each Strategy

One of the most challenging aspects of combining these strategies is managing different timeframes. Trend followers often work with longer timeframes—such as hourly, daily, or even weekly charts—while scalpers focus on much shorter timeframes like one-minute or five-minute charts.

A trader combining both approaches may need to monitor multiple timeframes simultaneously:

  • Longer Timeframes for Trend Analysis: Use daily or hourly charts to identify the broader market trend.

  • Shorter Timeframes for Scalping: Zoom into shorter timeframes (1-minute or 5-minute charts) for quick scalp trades during minor fluctuations within the trend.

This approach requires excellent time management and quick execution, as the trader must juggle multiple charts and trade signals.

Risks and Challenges

While combining trend following and scalping can provide a broader range of opportunities, it also introduces unique challenges:

  • Increased Complexity: Combining two different strategies requires a deep understanding of both approaches. It can be overwhelming for a trader to balance both types of analysis and decision-making processes.

  • Execution Risk: Scalping demands quick execution, and combining it with a trend-following approach can make it difficult to execute trades at the right time, especially if the trader is distracted by multiple positions and timeframes.

  • Overtrading: A common issue when using multiple strategies is the temptation to overtrade. By trying to implement both trend-following and scalping tactics, traders may end up taking more positions than they should, leading to poor risk management and possible losses.

Conclusion

Trend following and scalping are both valuable strategies, each with its own set of advantages and disadvantages. While they operate on different timeframes and trading philosophies, it is possible to combine them effectively if done with proper planning and discipline. By using scalping tactics within the context of a broader trend, traders can enhance their profits while staying in alignment with the market’s primary direction.

The key to success lies in adaptability, proper risk management, and understanding when each strategy is appropriate. As with any trading method, experimentation and continuous learning will help traders refine their approach and improve their overall performance. Whether you choose to pursue trend following, scalping, or a combination of both, the most important factor is to stay consistent and disciplined in your trading approach.
