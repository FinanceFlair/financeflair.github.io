What's Your Number? Understanding Valuation Methods in Entrepreneurial Finance


Valuation is a critical aspect of entrepreneurial finance. It’s the process of estimating the worth of a company or business opportunity. Determining the right value can help entrepreneurs secure investments, settle mergers and acquisitions, and make informed decisions regarding their startup or small business. In this post, we’ll explore various methods to value a company, keeping in mind that no single technique is universally applicable. Different methods suit different purposes and situations. Let’s dive in!

1. Asset-Based Approach

The asset-based approach calculates a company’s value by summing up its current assets minus its liabilities. Here, we consider the actual worth of a business’ assets, such as inventory, equipment, buildings, and other belongings:

Value = (Current Assets) - (Liabilities)

This method is popular for valuing companies when they have significant assets and limited intangible elements.

2. Income Approach

The income approach estimates the worth of a business based on the future earnings capabilities. It values the company based on the present value of its estimated earnings and benefits. The most popular methods under this approach are Discounted Cash Flow (DCF),Capitalized Earnings, and Multiplier Methods:

  • DCF Method: This involves estimating the cash inflows and outflows, discounting them to their present value, and summing up these values to obtain the company’s worth.

  • Capitalized Earnings: It’s the valuation formula used for companies with stable, consistent earnings. The earnings are split by a capitalization rate to arrive at the value.

  • Multiplier Method: The price-to-sales (P/S) or price-to-earnings (P/E) ratios help determine a company’s value under this method.

This approach is favored when we seek to value the business based on its current or future earnings potential.

3. Market Approach

The market approach values a company based on similar, recently sold businesses. Also known as the ‘comparison method,’ it considers factors like industry, size, and geographical location. Popular techniques in this method include:

  • Public Company Method: using similar public companies’ share price.
  • Asset Sale Method: estimates a business’ value by supposing it to be sold as a set of assets.
  • Industry Standards Method: assesses the company value based on industry-wide benchmarks.

The market approach is handy for establishing a company’s value when similar businesses have been sold recently or when comparables can be found.

4. Book Value Approach

This is the simplest and least complicated way to value a business. It considers the accounting value of a company’s assets minus liabilities. Book value equals the net worth of the company as calculated on the balance sheet. Though it couldn’t be the most accurate valuation method, it’s beneficial for a quick assessment when financial statements are available.

In conclusion, different valuation methods cater to specific needs, businesses, and circumstances. Understanding these different methods can help entrepreneurs determine their company’s worth accurately. Remember that while no single technique provides the absolute value, combining various methods gives a more comprehensive and reliable approximation of a business’ worth.

As you navigate the waters of entrepreneurial finance, understanding the different valuation methods can guide your decision-making process. So, the next time you find yourself asking ‘what’s your number?’ remember these four approaches, and which one is the most suitable for your circumstances.

Now, it’s time to set sail with your business value in your pocket, ready to face the challenges of entrepreneurial finance!
