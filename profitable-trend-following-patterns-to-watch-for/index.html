Profitable Trend Following Patterns to Watch For


Trend following has long been one of the most popular and profitable strategies in trading and investing. It is built on the principle that assets that have been moving in a particular direction tend to continue moving in that direction for some time. Identifying and following these trends early on can lead to significant profits for traders who know how to spot the right patterns. In this post, we’ll dive deep into some of the most profitable trend-following patterns that can help you unlock success in your trading strategies.

Understanding Trend Following

Before we explore the specific patterns, it’s essential to understand what trend following is all about. Trend following is a strategy where traders look to capitalize on the continuation of an existing trend rather than trying to predict reversals. In simple terms, the trader enters a position when an asset is trending upward or downward and exits when the trend shows signs of losing momentum.

The key to successful trend following lies in patience, discipline, and using the right tools to identify trends. The patterns we will discuss today are tools that can help you spot trends in the early stages, providing the best opportunities for profit.

1. Moving Average Crossover

One of the most common and profitable trend-following patterns is the Moving Average Crossover. This pattern occurs when two different moving averages (MA) cross each other, signaling a potential trend reversal or continuation. Moving averages smooth out price data to help traders see the underlying trend more clearly.

  • Golden Cross: The golden cross happens when a short-term moving average, like the 50-day MA, crosses above a long-term moving average, such as the 200-day MA. This signals the start of an uptrend and is often seen as a strong buy signal.

  • Death Cross: The death cross occurs when a short-term moving average crosses below a long-term moving average. It indicates a potential downtrend and is seen as a sell signal.

The moving average crossover is one of the simplest yet most reliable patterns to follow. However, traders should exercise caution, as false crossovers can sometimes occur, especially in volatile or sideways markets.

2. Breakouts and Breakdowns

Breakouts and breakdowns are another essential trend-following pattern to watch for. These occur when an asset’s price breaks above a significant resistance level (breakout) or below a major support level (breakdown). These patterns often indicate the start of a new trend, as price action has confirmed a breakout or breakdown from a range.

  • Breakout: When the price moves above a resistance level, it signals that the asset has broken free from its previous range and may continue higher. Traders often buy the asset as soon as the breakout occurs or wait for a pullback to enter at a better price.

  • Breakdown: On the flip side, when an asset’s price moves below a support level, it signals that the downtrend may continue. Traders looking for short opportunities may enter positions as the breakdown occurs, often expecting the price to move lower.

The key to successful trading using breakouts and breakdowns is waiting for confirmation, often through a strong candlestick close above resistance or below support, and managing risk with stop-loss orders to avoid false breakouts.

3. Channel Patterns

Channel patterns are formed when price movements consistently bounce between two parallel trendlines, creating a defined upper and lower boundary. Channels are particularly useful in trend-following strategies because they help identify the current trendâ€™s direction while providing clear levels of support and resistance.

  • Ascending Channel: An ascending channel occurs when the price is making higher highs and higher lows, and the trendline rises in a diagonal upward direction. This indicates a strong uptrend. Traders may go long when the price bounces off the lower trendline and sell when it reaches the upper trendline.

  • Descending Channel: A descending channel forms when the price is making lower highs and lower lows, and the trendline moves downward. This suggests a strong downtrend, and traders might consider shorting the asset when the price touches the upper trendline.

Channels are great tools for identifying profitable trend-following opportunities, as they allow traders to enter at key levels within the ongoing trend. The main risk in channel trading is the potential for a breakout, which might signal a reversal of the trend.

4. Flags and Pennants

Flags and pennants are continuation patterns that indicate the price is likely to continue in the direction of the prevailing trend after a brief consolidation. These patterns typically form after a strong price movement, followed by a short consolidation or period of sideways price action.

  • Flag: A flag is a rectangular-shaped pattern that slopes against the prevailing trend. In an uptrend, a flag typically appears as a small consolidation area where the price moves sideways or slightly downward. Once the price breaks out of the flag, the trend is likely to continue in the same direction.

  • Pennant: A pennant is a smaller, symmetrical triangle that forms after a strong price move. Like the flag, the pennant represents a consolidation before the trend continues. Once the price breaks out of the pennant, it often resumes the previous trend.

Flags and pennants are reliable trend-following patterns because they are often formed during brief periods of consolidation, allowing traders to catch the continuation of the primary trend. The key is to wait for the breakout from the pattern, confirming that the trend is resuming.

5. Cup and Handle

The cup and handle pattern is a classic chart formation that resembles the shape of a tea cup. This pattern occurs when a price makes a rounded bottom (the cup) followed by a small consolidation (the handle), and then breaks out to the upside. The cup and handle is a bullish continuation pattern, signaling that the asset is likely to continue higher after the consolidation.

To identify a cup and handle pattern, traders look for the following:

  • The price should form a rounded bottom, ideally with a gradual decline and recovery.
  • The handle should form after the cup, with a slight downward or sideways movement in price.
  • The breakout occurs when the price moves above the handleâ€™s resistance level, confirming the bullish trend.

The cup and handle is particularly powerful in longer time frames, such as weekly or monthly charts. It is often seen as a strong signal for a bullish trend to continue, and many traders rely on this pattern to enter long positions.

6. Head and Shoulders

The head and shoulders pattern is one of the most widely recognized trend reversal patterns. It typically signals a change in trend direction, from bullish to bearish. The head and shoulders pattern consists of three peaks: a larger peak (the head) between two smaller peaks (the shoulders). The pattern is completed when the price breaks below the neckline, which is drawn by connecting the lows between the head and shoulders.

  • Head and Shoulders Top: This pattern signals a reversal from an uptrend to a downtrend. The first peak is the left shoulder, followed by a higher peak (the head), and then a smaller peak for the right shoulder. Once the price breaks below the neckline, the trend is expected to reverse, and traders can enter short positions.

  • Inverse Head and Shoulders: This is the reverse of the head and shoulders pattern and signals a reversal from a downtrend to an uptrend. It forms when the price makes a low (left shoulder), followed by a lower low (head), and then another higher low (right shoulder). A breakout above the neckline after the right shoulder forms signals the beginning of an uptrend.

The head and shoulders pattern is one of the most reliable reversal patterns and can be used by traders to identify potential trend changes. The key is confirming the pattern with a breakout and following the trendâ€™s new direction.

7. Trend Lines and Support/Resistance Levels

Drawing trend lines and identifying key support and resistance levels is an essential technique in trend following. Trend lines are drawn by connecting the highs or lows of price movements, while support and resistance levels are horizontal lines where price has historically bounced or reversed.

  • Uptrend: In an uptrend, traders will draw an ascending trendline by connecting the lows. A break of the trendline could signal the end of the uptrend.

  • Downtrend: In a downtrend, a descending trendline is drawn by connecting the highs. If the price breaks above the trendline, it may indicate the end of the downtrend.

Support and resistance levels are areas where the price tends to reverse. Identifying these levels helps traders know where to expect pullbacks or trend continuation.

Conclusion

By practicing patience, using risk management techniques, and staying disciplined in following these patterns, traders can unlock the potential of trend following to achieve long-term success.
