What Are the Different Types of Fixed Income Securities?


Navigating the world of investing can be a whirlwind, with a variety of asset classes offering different risks, returns, and purposes. Among the foundational pillars of investment portfolios lies the reliable world of fixed income securities. These investments have earned their reputation for providing steady returns and a certain degree of predictability, making them a cornerstone for conservative investors and institutions alike.

But what exactly are fixed income securities? Broadly speaking, they are financial instruments that pay a fixed or variable interest (also known as a coupon) over a specified period, culminating in the return of principal at maturity. While the archetype is the government bond, this asset class branches off in a multitude of directions, each with its own profile, use case, and subtleties worth exploring.

Let’s delve into the landscape of fixed income instruments, highlighting their distinguishing features and helping you better understand how they might fit into your investment strategy.

1. Government Bonds

When investors think of fixed income, government bonds are usually the first to come to mind—and for good reason. Governments, both at the national and local levels, are among the most active issuers of these securities.

a. Treasury Securities

Issued by national governments, treasury securities are often considered the gold standard of reliability. In the United States, these include Treasury bills (T-bills), Treasury notes (T-notes), and Treasury bonds (T-bonds):

  • T-bills: Short-term securities that mature in a year or less. They’re sold at a discount, and investors receive the full face value at maturity, with the difference representing the return.
  • T-notes: Intermediate-term investments, maturing between 2 and 10 years, paying interest semi-annually.
  • T-bonds: Long-term securities, with maturities of 20 or 30 years, also paying semi-annual interest.

b. Municipal Bonds

State and municipal governments issue their own bonds to fund local projects like schools, hospitals, and infrastructure. These are often grouped into:

  • General Obligation Bonds: Backed by the full faith and credit of the issuer, often repaid through taxation.
  • Revenue Bonds: Supported by the revenue generated from the specific projects they fund, such as toll roads or utility projects.

Municipal bonds are particularly attractive to investors in higher tax brackets due to their tax-advantaged status in certain jurisdictions.

c. Sovereign Bonds

Outside the U.S., other countries issue their own government bonds—such as the UK’s gilts or Japan’s government bonds (JGBs). These, too, come with varying maturities and structures but generally serve the same purpose: funding government needs and providing a predictable income stream to investors.

2. Corporate Bonds

Corporations of all sizes issue bonds as a way to raise capital for expansions, acquisitions, or operational needs. Unlike government-issued securities, corporate bonds carry credit risk—the risk that the issuer might default.

a. Investment-Grade Corporate Bonds

Bonds rated ‘BBB-’ (or the equivalent) or better by credit rating agencies are believed to have a relatively low risk of default. Blue-chip companies with stable cash flows typically issue these, making them a popular choice for risk-averse investors seeking higher yields than government offerings.

b. High-Yield (Junk) Bonds

Issued by companies with lower credit ratings, these bonds pay higher interest rates to compensate for their greater risk. While they can add income and diversification, they also carry a higher probability of issuer default.

c. Convertible Bonds

These unique securities offer the right—the option—to convert the bond into a predetermined number of shares of the issuing company’s stock. Initially, they pay a fixed interest rate, blending characteristics of both bonds and equities. This hybrid nature may appeal to adventurous investors seeking a balance between income and potential capital appreciation.

3. Agency and Supranational Bonds

Government-affiliated organizations and international entities also tap into fixed income markets.

a. Agency Bonds

In the U.S., several agencies—such as the Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corporation (Freddie Mac), the Federal National Mortgage Association (Fannie Mae), and the Government National Mortgage Association (Ginnie Mae)—issue bonds to support specific sectors, like housing. Some agency bonds are explicitly backed by the U.S. government (Ginnie Mae), while others have implicit backing.

b. Supranational Bonds

These are issued by entities formed by two or more governments, such as the World Bank or the European Investment Bank. They’re designed to fund global projects—ranging from development initiatives to infrastructure improvements—and offer a degree of diversification and creditworthiness above many corporate issuers.

4. Asset-Backed Securities (ABS)

Asset-backed securities group together pools of underlying assets—like auto loans, credit card receivables, or leases—and issue bonds backed by these pools. Investors receive payments from the cash flows generated by the underlying assets.

a. Mortgage-Backed Securities (MBS)

By far the best-known type of asset-backed security, mortgage-backed securities aggregate thousands of home loans into a single tradable instrument. As homeowners repay their mortgages, investors receive portions of these payments, factoring in the risk of early repayments or defaults. MBS can be issued by government agencies or private financial institutions, with differing risk and return profiles.

b. Collateralized Debt Obligations (CDOs) and More

CDOs bundle various types of debt—like corporate loans or other ABS—into a single vehicle, divided into tranches with differing priorities for payments. While these instruments can boost yields, they also introduce layers of complexity and risk, a fact made clear during the 2008 financial crisis.

5. Structured Notes

Structured notes are a flexible, custom-made type of fixed income security. They blend characteristics of traditional bonds with derivatives, allowing issuers to tailor the risk and return profile to specific investor needs.

A structured note’s return may link to the performance of a market index, a basket of stocks, or even interest rates—providing upside beyond a simple coupon payment, sometimes at the expense of full protection of capital.

6. Certificates of Deposit (CDs) and Time Deposits

Often overlooked, certificates of deposit and their global cousins—time deposits—are fixed income instruments issued by banks. Investors lock up funds for a specified period and earn a pre-determined rate of interest.

Although generally considered extremely safe, they do come with interest-rate risk: if rates rise during the term, investors may find themselves locked into a below-market yield. In the U.S., CDs are usually insured up to certain limits by the FDIC, adding a layer of security for retail investors.

7. Inflation-Protected Securities

Rising prices can erode the purchasing power of fixed interest payments. Inflation-protected securities are designed to remedy this, adjusting both principal and coupon payments in line with changes to a specific inflation index (like the Consumer Price Index in the U.S.).

a. Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS)

TIPS are a U.S. government product that regularly adjusts the bond’s principal for inflation, so while the real yield may be modest, the investor maintains purchasing power over time.

b. Similar Global Offerings

Other countries offer their own inflation-linked bonds, such as UK Index-Linked Gilts or Canadian Real Return Bonds, with operational details varying by country.

8. Perpetual Bonds

Perpetual bonds, or “perps,” are unusual in that they pay a steady coupon indefinitely, without ever maturing. While rare among governments, they’re issued by certain financial institutions and corporations looking to raise long-term capital without a repayment date hanging over them. The downside for investors is that the return of principal is not guaranteed—though they may trade their bonds in the secondary market.

9. Callable and Puttable Bonds

Customizability defines these instruments. Callable bonds grant issuers the right to redeem the bond before maturity—usually after an initial lockout period—often when interest rates have fallen and the issuer can refinance more cheaply. In exchange, these bonds typically offer higher yield to investors.

Conversely, puttable bonds hand the power to the investor, allowing them to “put” (sell) the bond back to the issuer at specified times. This enables investors to exit early if rates rise, and typically comes at the cost of a lower yield.

10. Foreign Currency Bonds

While most fixed income securities in developed markets are issued in the local currency, some issuers offer bonds denominated in foreign currencies, giving investors access to potential currency appreciation or diversification. However, these introduce currency risk—returns may be amplified or diminished by fluctuations in exchange rates.

11. Savings Bonds

Available in many countries, savings bonds are government-issued securities tailored to individual investors. For example, U.S. Series I Bonds and Series EE Bonds are geared towards retail savers, often featuring flexible purchase amounts and incremental compounding.

These can be ideal for conservative savers who prefer holding bonds directly or for educational purposes, such as funding a college account for a child.

Why Fixed Income Securities Matter in a Portfolio

Understanding the array of fixed income securities on the market is more than an academic exercise—it’s a practical necessity for building a resilient, diversified portfolio.

  • Stability and Predictability: The regular income and return of principal at maturity can offer much-needed stability amid equity market swings.
  • Diversification: Fixed income typically has different performance drivers compared to stocks, helping mitigate portfolio volatility.
  • Customization of Risk and Reward: From the highest-rated government bonds to high-yield corporate issues and innovative structured products, there’s a fixed income security for nearly every need and risk tolerance.

Risks You Need to Know

Fixed income investments aren’t risk-free. While many investors gravitate towards this asset class for safety, several key risks deserve attention:

  • Interest Rate Risk: Bond prices fall when rates rise, and vice versa. The longer the maturity, the more sensitive a bond’s price will be.
  • Credit Risk: Especially relevant for corporate, high-yield, or emerging market bonds; the issuer might default.
  • Reinvestment Risk: If interest rates fall, it may be challenging to reinvest coupon payments at similar rates.
  • Liquidity Risk: Some bonds trade infrequently, making it harder to sell at a fair price.

Understanding these risks, along with the structural nuances of each type of fixed income security, results in more informed choices and less unwelcome surprise down the road.

The Bottom Line

The fixed income universe is both broad and deep, offering a rich toolkit for investors from all walks of life. Whether the objective is securing steady income, diversifying risk, preserving capital, or even speculating on interest rates or currency movements, there’s a fixed income security calibrated for the job.

Smart investors build their knowledge of this asset class, looking beyond the basics of government bonds and exploring the attributes, advantages, and pitfalls of each type of security. With tools ranging from straightforward CDs to exotic structured notes and social-impacting supranational bonds, the possibilities are as varied as investor goals themselves.

So, as you chart your financial course, consider the role that fixed income securities play. Explore the full menu, weigh the trade-offs, and discover how these often underappreciated investments can support your broader objectives—now and in the years to come.
