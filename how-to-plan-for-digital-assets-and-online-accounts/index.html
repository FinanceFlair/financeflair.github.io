How to Plan for Digital Assets and Online Accounts


In today’s digital age, many aspects of our lives are intertwined with online platforms. From social media profiles to online banking, digital assets have become a vital part of our personal and professional identities. With the ever-increasing dependence on digital tools and platforms, it’s crucial to have a plan for managing your digital assets and online accounts, especially in the event of an unexpected situation.

This guide will take you through the necessary steps to safeguard your online presence, ensuring that your digital assets are properly managed and passed on according to your wishes.

What Are Digital Assets?

Digital assets are any online account, file, or piece of data that holds value and is stored digitally. These assets include everything from social media accounts (like Facebook, Instagram, Twitter) to cloud storage, digital currencies, websites, and intellectual property stored online.

Common Types of Digital Assets:

  1. Social Media Accounts: Profiles on platforms such as Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn, and Twitter.
  2. Financial Accounts: Online banking accounts, investment portfolios, and digital wallets such as PayPal or cryptocurrency exchanges.
  3. Websites and Blogs: Websites, domains, and blogs that you own and manage.
  4. Email Accounts: Personal and professional email accounts.
  5. Cloud Storage: Files stored on cloud platforms like Google Drive, Dropbox, or iCloud.
  6. Intellectual Property: Digital artwork, photos, or writings that hold monetary value.
  7. Digital Subscriptions: Accounts for subscriptions like Netflix, Spotify, or any other paid service.

Having a clear understanding of the digital assets you hold will help you take the right steps to plan for their management.

Why Planning for Digital Assets is Important

Planning for your digital assets is important for several reasons:

  1. Security: Your digital assets could contain sensitive information that needs protection. Without proper planning, these accounts could be vulnerable to hacking, identity theft, or unauthorized access.
  2. Ease of Transfer: In case of an emergency or when you are no longer able to manage your accounts, planning ensures that your loved ones or trusted individuals can access and manage your digital assets.
  3. Legacy and Closure: For many, digital assets hold sentimental value. Planning ahead can ensure that your online presence is closed or passed on in the way you intend after you are gone.
  4. Legal Compliance: Many countries have laws and regulations that dictate what happens to your digital assets in case of death or incapacity. Having a clear plan in place will ensure compliance with legal requirements.

Steps to Plan for Your Digital Assets and Online Accounts

1. Create an Inventory of All Your Digital Assets

The first step in planning for your digital assets is to create a comprehensive list of everything you own. This inventory will include all your online accounts, websites, and any other assets stored digitally. It’s essential to categorize these assets to ensure nothing is overlooked.

  • Social Media Accounts: Make a list of all the social media accounts you use, including personal and professional platforms.
  • Financial Accounts: Include online banking, cryptocurrency accounts, PayPal, investment platforms, etc.
  • Cloud Services: Record your Google Drive, Dropbox, or other cloud storage services.
  • Subscriptions: Include any ongoing digital subscriptions like Netflix, Spotify, or software licenses.
  • Emails: Document all the email accounts you use.
  • Websites/Blogs: If you own any websites or blogs, include login details and administrative access information.

It’s also helpful to note the usernames, passwords, and security questions associated with each account.

2. Decide Who Will Manage Your Digital Assets

Once you have a list of your digital assets, it’s crucial to decide who will manage them in case you’re no longer able to do so. This could be a family member, friend, or trusted individual. You may want to designate multiple people for different aspects of your digital assets.

Consider the following roles:

  • Digital Executor: This person will be responsible for handling the transfer, closure, or deletion of your online accounts and digital assets. They will manage your digital legacy after you’re gone.
  • Backup Contacts: These individuals will have access to certain accounts or assets, such as your financial information or email, in the event of an emergency.

Be sure to choose people who are responsible, tech-savvy, and trustworthy.

3. Use a Password Manager

One of the biggest challenges in managing digital assets is remembering and securely storing passwords. Using a password manager can significantly simplify the process of managing your online accounts. These tools store your usernames, passwords, and other sensitive information in an encrypted vault.

A password manager allows you to:

  • Safely store and manage login credentials for all of your accounts.
  • Generate strong, unique passwords for each account, reducing the risk of hacking or unauthorized access.
  • Share access to accounts with trusted individuals in case of emergency.

Some popular password managers include LastPass, Dashlane, and 1Password.

4. Set Up a Digital Will

A digital will is a legal document that outlines how your digital assets should be handled after your death or incapacitation. In many cases, online services and platforms allow you to specify who can access and manage your accounts upon your passing. A digital will ensures that your wishes are honored, and that there is no confusion over what should happen to your online accounts and assets.

5. Ensure Legal Access to Your Accounts

Certain online platforms, such as email providers and social media platforms, have their own procedures for granting access to a deceased person’s account. It’s important to ensure that the digital executor or your designated contact can access these accounts legally.

  • Facebook: Allows you to designate a legacy contact who can manage your account after you pass away.
  • Google: Offers an Inactive Account Manager that allows you to select trusted contacts to access your account when it becomes inactive.
  • Apple: Has a similar feature called Digital Legacy, which allows you to assign someone to access your Apple ID and personal information.

Make sure to familiarize yourself with the terms of each platform, as policies vary.

6. Update Your Plan Regularly

Your digital assets and online accounts may change over time, so it’s essential to update your plan regularly. As you add or remove accounts, you should adjust your inventory, password manager, and digital will accordingly. Additionally, ensure that your designated contacts are kept up to date and informed about your plans.

A regular review ensures that your digital estate plan remains current and effective.

7. Understand Digital Asset Laws

The laws surrounding digital assets vary by jurisdiction, so it’s essential to understand the legal framework that applies to your digital property. Some countries have specific laws regarding digital estates, such as the ability to transfer or close digital accounts after death. Other countries have less clear policies, and platforms may have their own terms of service that dictate what happens to your accounts.

Consulting with an estate planning lawyer can help ensure that your digital assets are handled in accordance with local laws.

The Importance of Communication

Having open communication with your loved ones about your digital asset plan is crucial. Make sure that your designated contacts are aware of your digital estate plan and know where to find your passwords, login details, and any important documents. This will help prevent confusion in the event that something happens to you unexpectedly.

8. Backup and Protect Your Data

Lastly, it’s important to back up your critical digital assets, especially important files and documents. Whether it’s through cloud storage or physical external hard drives, having a backup ensures that your data is protected in case of a system failure, hacking, or any unforeseen event.

Encryption is an essential part of securing sensitive data, so make sure that your backup files are encrypted and stored securely.

Conclusion

Planning for your digital assets and online accounts is a crucial step in safeguarding your online identity and ensuring that your wishes are carried out in the event of an emergency or death. By following the steps outlined in this guide, you can protect your online presence, transfer valuable assets to trusted individuals, and provide peace of mind to your loved ones.

Remember that planning for digital assets is not a one-time task; it requires ongoing maintenance and updates. By staying proactive and organized, you can ensure that your digital estate is handled in a secure and efficient manner.
