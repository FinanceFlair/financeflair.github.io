Exploring Trade Wars: The Ripple Effects of Geopolitical Events on Derivatives Market


Global trade has always been a dynamic and complex entity, influenced by various political and economic factors. The most recent and notorious player in this field has been the notorious trade war between the United States and China. Taking place since 2018, this ongoing war has caught the attention of businesses, governments, and economists worldwide. One sector that has been particularly affected is the derivatives market. In this blog post, we will explore the consequences of the trade war on the derivatives market and the ripple effects on the global economy.

Understanding the Trade War

Before we dive into the consequences, let us first get a grasp on what the trade war between the US and China actually is. The trade war is a conflict between the world’s two largest economies, sparked by several factors, including protectionist sentiments, technological disagreements, and China’s non-reciprocal trade policies. The situation has led to the imposition of tariffs on a wide range of goods, ranging from industrial machinery to agricultural products. This has caused a ripple effect in the global supply chain, influencing various sectors, including but not limited to, the derivatives market.

The Impact on Derivatives Market

The derivatives market is fundamentally a market where the contractual obligations between parties are based upon an agreed-upon value of an item or asset, with one party long and the other short. Thus, various financial instruments, which are derived from the value of an underlying asset, are bought, sold, and traded. In the case of the trade war, these instruments have been heavily impacted.

  1. Volatility: Firstly, trade tensions have led to increased market volatility. As companies brace themselves for the uncertainties of tariffs and retaliatory measures, investors become more cautious, leading to the increased fluctuation of stock prices and significant spikes in derivative contracts. This volatility directly affects the risk and return profiles, ultimately leading to losses for parties on the wrong side of the contract.

  2. Currency Impact: The trade war has had significant implications on currency markets, with alternative asset classes becoming more attractive to investors seeking safe-haven assets. This has also had indirect effects on currency forward contracts, which are a significant component of the derivatives market.

  3. Industrial Conglomeration: Secondarily, trade wars can lead to changes in industrial strategies, as businesses are compelled to alter their supply chains and manufacturing bases to mitigate risks. Alternative manufacturing hubs, such as Vietnam, are benefiting from the relocation of certain activities, sparking demand for derivatives that hedge exposure to different currencies and economies.

  4. Global Growth Concerns: Finally, the overall climate of uncertainty has provoked not only greater fluctuations for market participants but also concerns about the growth outlook for the global economy. This, in turn, has an impact on interest rates, inflation expectations, and expectations for economic policy uncertainty. These macroeconomic effects figure prominently in derivative pricing models, which have had to adjust as circumstances and assumptions continue to shift.

The Larger Picture: Ripple Effects on the Global Economy

While the direct consequences of the trade war on the derivatives market are meaningful, the economic effects extend far beyond this single sector. The very essence of the trade war is fundamentally a question of confidence – will US and Chinese leaders forge a sustainable resolution, or will the current impasse drag on indefinitely?

Additionally, supply chain adjustments due to the trade war have prompted debates about globalization, economic nationalism, and the role of international institutions, further amplifying uncertainty in the global economy.

In conclusion, the 2018 trade war between the US and China has, without a doubt, had a significant impact on the derivatives market. The ripple effects of this event have extended across various sectors, inflicting adjustment processes that exhibit the intricate relationship between the political and the economic. The trade war is a stark example of how geopolitical events can profoundly influence markets and force participants to recalibrate their risk management strategies.
