Advanced Charting Techniques for Technical Analysis


When it comes to technical analysis, charting techniques play a crucial role in understanding market trends and making informed decisions. These techniques are vital for both novice and experienced traders as they provide a visual representation of price movements and help in identifying trends and potentials. In this article, we will explore some advanced charting methods that can enrich your technical analysis toolkit.

1. Point and Figure Charting

Point and Figure (P&F) charting techniques are unique in that they focus more on price movements than time. This style of charting eliminates the concept of time factors, making it ideal for longer-term stock market investors. The chart’s key elements include the open-close charts and the three-point reversal.

Open-High-Low-Close (OHLC) Charts: In OHLC charts, each bar represents a specific time period, and four critical data points are plotted - high, low, open, and close prices. This format provides a clear view of trends and price action over extended periods.

Three-Point Reversal: This technique uses an upside-down triangle to indicate a bullish reversal and a downside-downward triangle to signify a bearish reversal. Upon the completion of a reversal, a target price is derived from the P&F chart.

2. Japanese Candlestick Charting

Candlestick charting originates from Japan, with each candlestick representing an interval of time. Its distinctiveness lies in representing the high, low, open, and close prices of a security within a specified period.

Candle Bodies: The elongated boxes or lines represent the difference between the open and close prices. When the close is higher than the open, the line is green, denoting a bullish day, and the body filled. Conversely, when the open is higher than the close, the line is red, signifying a bearish day and the body unfilled.

Key Terms: The shadow above and below the body represents the high-low volatility and their length aids in identifying potential supports and resistance levels. Pinocchio lines can be used to identify a reversal of trend.

3. Drawing Trend Lines and Channels

Both trend lines and price channels help to identify and track trends in the market. Trend lines represent a direct line connecting applicable price points. In contrast, price channels consist of a top and bottom trend line parallel to one another, hence the name ‘channels.’

Trend Lines: These can be applied to both upward and downward trending markets. In an uptrend, a rising trend line implies support, while a decline in prices breaks the line signaling support lost. For downtrend analysis, a falling trend line signifies resistance.

Price Channels: Channels help to visualize trends. The upper line represents resistance, while the lower line signifies support. Breaches in the channel could signal trend changes, displaying a shift in momentum.

4. Fibonacci Retracements & Extensions

Fibonacci ratios help to predict future price levels using historical price data. Named after the mathematician, these ratios are derived from the convergence of a ‘retroflection’ of a line with a spiral. Notably, the ratios of 0.382, 0.618, and 1.618 are paramount in technical analysis.

Retracements: Market prices often retrace a specified percentage of the previous move, mostly as measured in a swing or wave. For instance, should the price of a security fall from $100 to $75, the $100 level may provide support as a .382 retracement of the $25 decline is at $87.50.

Extensions: These go beyond the 100% Fibonacci level. The 161.8% and 618.0% levels are notable for the extent to which an outgoing move is likely to continue.

In conclusion, advanced charting techniques can enhance the technical analysis process, making traders prescient to prevailing market trends. Coupled with experience, initiative, and passion, such techniques can significantly contribute to successful trading decisions. As an up-and-coming trader, it’s essential to know and implement these techniques effectively.
