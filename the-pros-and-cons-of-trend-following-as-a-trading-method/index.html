The Pros and Cons of Trend Following as a Trading Method


Trend following. It’s a phrase that sparks heated debate among traders, a philosophy that has created empires, ruined accounts, and inspired countless books, podcasts, and heated YouTube comment threads. Many approach it with reverence or skepticism, and most traders at least flirt with trend following at some point on their journey. But what does it offer, and what pitfalls does it conceal? To answer that, let’s dive deep into the pros and cons of trend following as a trading method.

Understanding Trend Following

Before weighing the positives and negatives, it’s worth establishing what trend following actually is. At its core, trend following is a trading strategy designed to capitalize on the momentum of price movements. Instead of predicting when a market will reverse, trend followers aim to buy when prices are rising and sell (or short) when prices are falling, riding the wave for as long as possible.

Classic trend following assumes that prices move in sustained directions for periods of time, interrupted by shorter, less predictable reversals or corrections. This stands in contrast to mean reversion, where the expectation is that values revert to a defined “normal” or average.

Systems can be simple—think moving-average crossovers—or involve sophisticated algorithms that analyze volatility, price channels, and more. The unifying idea: Let winners run, cut losers short.

Trend Following: The Allure

1. Simplicity and Robustness

One of the big pluses of trend following is conceptual simplicity. The basic logic—“the trend is your friend”—translates easily into systematic rules. Novices and veterans alike find comfort in clear, repeatable strategies. Most trend following rules do not require a PhD in statistics to implement; you can run basic strategies with just price data, reducing reliance on subjective interpretation or ambiguous signals.

2. Limited Need for Prediction

Unlike discretionary trading or fundamental analysis, trend following strategies don’t obsess over predicting tops, bottoms, or fair valuations. Their edge comes from reacting to what the market is already doing, not from trying to predict what it should do. In markets rife with unpredictable geopolitical shocks or sudden economic changes, removing the need to “call the future” is a practical advantage.

3. Emotional Discipline

Trading is notorious for triggering emotional whiplash: greed in rallies, fear in crashes, flinching at noise. Trend following mitigates some of these issues by relying on mechanical rules, taking significant discretion out of the equation. Once the entry and exit criteria are set, the system runs without panicking over short-term noise. For traders vulnerable to FOMO and panic-selling, trend following can impose a helpful layer of discipline.

4. The Power of Fat Tails

Traditional financial models often assume that price moves are neatly distributed—most returns fall within a certain band around the mean. But real markets are messier; they generate “fat tails” with sudden, outsized moves. Trend followers are uniquely positioned to profit from these rare-but-powerful events. When a true trend emerges—be it the dot-com boom or the 2008 crash—trend-following systems have the potential to capture enormous profits that most other methods miss.

5. Applicability Across Markets

Trend following isn’t limited to stocks. Futures, forex, commodities, and even cryptocurrencies can all be fertile ground for trend-based strategies. As long as price data exists, the mechanics translate. This helps with diversification—a key to avoiding catastrophic losses.

6. Historical Performance

While past performance isn’t a guarantee, there are legendary stories: the Turtles, Richard Dennis, the rise of CTAs (Commodity Trading Advisors). These practitioners demonstrated that well-executed trend following can deliver solid returns, especially in high-volatility periods. There’s a track record that, while not always consistent, is hard to ignore.

The Downsides: Where Trend Following Falters

Of course, for every trader extolling the virtues of trend following, there’s another cursing the method’s frustrations. Let’s dig into the cons.

1. Drawdown Pain

Perhaps the greatest challenge in trend following is enduring the deep, often prolonged drawdowns. Since the method works by capturing relatively infrequent large moves, it’s normal for the strategy to lose multiple trades in a row when markets chop sideways. This results in extended periods of underperformance, during which it’s easy to question the system or abandon it altogether. Emotional resilience is essential.

2. Whipsaw Hell: Sideways Markets

Trends don’t happen all the time. Markets often meander without clear direction, especially in periods of low volatility or uncertainty. Trend-following signals in these environments generate “whipsaws”: repeated buy-and-sell trades that rack up losses and transaction costs. The frustration mounts as trend followers get chopped to pieces before the next big move (if it comes at all).

3. Late to the Party, Late to Leave

Trend followers typically wait for confirmation—a breakout above resistance, a moving average crossover—before acting. This guarantees that entries are never at the exact bottom, and exits are rarely at the top. Catching the “middle” of a trend means missing both the juiciest start and hanging on as it reverses. While this is baked into the risk/return profile of the strategy, it can be irksome to see profits evaporate on the backend of a trade.

4. Frequent Small Losses vs Infrequent Gains

The psychology here matters: trend following often wins by tolerating lots of small, annoying losses for the occasional big win. Winning rate might be strikingly low—some famous systems have winning percentages below 40%. For traders wired to seek frequent validation, this can be demoralizing. The method demands a long-term mindset.

5. No Insider Edge

Because trend following is widely understood and straightforward to implement, there’s no “information edge.” Many participants use similar signals, which can increase crowding and degrade performance during certain regimes. When too many traders chase the same signals, entries/exits can become self-fulfilling but also prone to sudden, violent reversals.

6. Overfitting and Parameter Optimism

While trend following can be as simple as a moving average crossover, temptation abounds to tweak rules for historical perfection. Optimizing entry/exit criteria or signal filters on past data—without considering changing market regimes—can lead to disastrous out-of-sample results. Even simple strategies need to be robust—resistant to overfitting and sensitive to changing volatility and liquidity.

7. Transaction Costs and Slippage

Trend following can involve frequent position adjustments, especially in turbulent or rangebound markets. If the underlying assets are illiquid, or if bid-ask spreads are wide, these costs eat into already modest winning trades. High-frequency trend systems in particular must be meticulously designed to account for real-world execution costs.

Trend Following Myths and Misconceptions

Over years of trading forums, books, and podcasts, several myths about trend following have sprouted:

  • Myth: Trend following always works. It doesn’t. The method can go years underperforming benchmarks or even losing money in the wrong environment.
  • Myth: It’s only for large funds. Retail traders can and do use trend following, though they may need to adapt position sizing and risk controls for their capital base.
  • Myth: Trends are obvious. In hindsight, absolutely. In real time, defining and trading trends is much harder—not least because markets are noisy, and real breakouts often reverse.
  • Myth: Requires no thinking. Mechanical rules help, but crafting robust rules, position sizing, and staying disciplined takes mental work and ongoing refinement.

Keys to Successful Trend Following

Given the pros and cons, what separates the long-term winners from the frustrated masses? A few recurring themes:

1. Robust Risk Management

Trend following is more about managing risk than “winning” a high percentage of trades. Use position sizing, stops, and diversification rigorously. The famous “let your winners run” advice is incomplete without clear guidelines for cutting losers quickly.

2. Patience and Timeframe Alignment

Micro-trends exist, but most money is made on the big, sustained moves. Strategies that try to capture every wiggle get chewed up in noise. A larger timeframe (daily, weekly) reduces noise and whipsaws, though it may require more patience and capital commitment.

3. Diversification Across Markets and Systems

Don’t just run a single-market, single-rule system—trend following often works best when signals are spread across dozens of uncorrelated markets. Mixing different approaches (longer-term, breakout, volatility-adjusted, etc.) can further dampen drawdowns.

4. Emotional Fortitude

There will be months, quarters, or even years where nothing seems to work. The historical return data for major trend following funds is littered with barren periods. Those who stay the course reap the fat-tail benefits; those who switch strategies out of frustration tend to lose.

5. Vigilant System Review Without Chasing Perfection

Tweak, but don’t tinker endlessly. Rigorously review performance, but be wary of curve fitting and over-optimization. Understand that markets evolve; some parameter drift is inevitable, but wholesale abandonment rarely ends well.

Is Trend Following Right for You?

Given all of this, is trend following suitable for all traders? Absolutely not. It’s ideal for systematic thinkers, those able to withstand long stretches of lackluster performance and avoid succumbing to fear or boredom. For traders who obsess over why a market is moving, who crave quick wins, or hate the idea of sitting on losses, trend following might prove maddening.

Yet, even for discretionary traders, understanding trend following is worthwhile. Markets are complex ecosystems, and price trends—whether caused by herd psychology, economic cycles, or money flows—are facts of life. Knowing how trend followers think can help anticipate where crowd behavior might create opportunity or risk.

Conclusion: Riding the Wave, With Eyes Open

Trend following is neither a holy grail nor a relic of the past. It’s a disciplined, battle-tested approach with real strengths—and real challenges—that have played out across decades and asset classes. Its simplicity, if respected, can engender robust systems that clip the highs and survive the lows. Yet it requires ironclad risk management, emotional grit, and a relentless commitment to stick to the plan through dry spells.

For those who can stomach the ride, trend following offers a unique way to harness the wild, untamed beast that is the marketplace. For the rest, it at least offers valuable lessons: cut losses, let winners run, and never, ever believe the game is easy. The market rewards patience and punishes hubris, and trend following sits at the crossroads of both.

Whether you choose to incorporate trend following as your primary method or simply add elements of its philosophy to your toolkit, understanding the pros and cons is a rite of passage in the ongoing adventure that is trading. So, as the market’s waves keep rolling, the choice is yours: catch the swell, or let it pass by. The trend, as ever, may be your friend—but only if you know how to ride it.
