The Brexit Effect on Pound Sterling's Exchange Rates


The decision of the United Kingdom to leave the European Union, widely referred to as Brexit, has been one of the most seismic political and economic events in recent history. Beyond its impact on trade, governance, and social policy, Brexit has also significantly affected the financial markets, with the pound sterling (GBP) experiencing dramatic fluctuations since the referendum in 2016. To understand the far-reaching implications of Brexit on pound sterling’s exchange rates, we must delve into the timeline, underlying economic factors, and evolving market sentiment.

The Initial Shock: The 2016 Referendum

On June 23, 2016, the United Kingdom voted to leave the European Union in a historic referendum. This result came as a surprise to many investors and financial institutions, leading to immediate turbulence in the currency markets. Overnight, the pound sterling plummeted, losing over 10% of its value against the US dollar. This marked one of the sharpest one-day declines in the history of GBP/USD trading, with the currency falling from approximately $1.50 to $1.33 shortly after the results were announced.

The drop in value wasn’t confined to the US dollar alone; similar devaluations were seen against other major currencies such as the euro (EUR), the Japanese yen (JPY), and the Swiss franc (CHF). The explanation for this sharp decline lies in the uncertainty sparked by the referendum outcome. Markets react to uncertainty with caution, and Brexit raised numerous questions: What would trade relationships between the UK and EU look like? Would businesses relocate? What would happen to financial markets and investments? The lack of clarity shook confidence and led investors to redirect funds toward safer assets.

Long-Term Volatility: The Pre-Exit Years

Following the initial shock of the referendum, the value of the pound sterling remained volatile in the years leading up to the UK’s formal departure from the EU in January 2020. Ongoing political developments, negotiations over trade agreements, and fluctuating public sentiment played a pivotal role in the currency’s performance.

One of the defining moments during this period was when the UK government invoked Article 50 in March 2017, formally beginning the Brexit process. The pound remained under pressure, trading within a relatively narrow but weak range. Investors anticipated economic disruptions, given that the EU accounted for a significant portion of the UK’s total trade. As a result, every major political announcement—whether it was the resignation of a Prime Minister, the rejection of a negotiated deal, or shifts in parliamentary votes—had a pronounced impact on GBP exchange rates.

For example, during the latter half of 2019, the pound experienced sharp movements tied to the uncertainty of whether the UK would leave the EU with or without a deal. The possibility of a “no-deal Brexit” brought with it higher risks of economic disruption, which weighed heavily on the currency. On the other hand, confirmations of a more orderly exit sparked short-term rallies in the pound’s value as investor confidence was temporarily bolstered.

Post-Brexit Reality: Economic Adjustments and Ongoing Impact

When the UK finally left the European Union on January 31, 2020, the Brexit narrative did not conclude. Contrary to some expectations of stabilization, Brexit’s ramifications on the British economy and its currency became ongoing challenges. With new trade rules, financial services adjustments, and economic friction becoming apparent, the pound sterling faced continued pressures in the foreign exchange market.

Trade Imbalances and Weakening Fundamentals

One of the reasons the pound struggled post-Brexit was the increasing concern over trade imbalances. As part of the Single Market and Customs Union, UK businesses previously had frictionless trade with EU member states. After Brexit, customs checks, regulatory barriers, and tariffs introduced new costs and delays for exports and imports. These changes disproportionately affected small to medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) and industries heavily reliant on European supply chains, such as manufacturing, agriculture, and automotive sectors.

Such trade constrictions led investors to view the UK as less competitive globally, reducing appetite for holding assets denominated in GBP. Additionally, as the UK transitioned away from its main trading bloc, diversification efforts to establish new trade relationships with countries outside the EU proved slow to materialize. This economic drag continued to weaken the currency’s performance in global markets.

Financial Services and Investor Confidence

The UK’s financial services industry, which accounted for a substantial portion of GDP and contributed significantly to foreign exchange inflows, also felt the effects of Brexit acutely. London, long considered a financial hub, experienced competition from other European cities such as Amsterdam and Frankfurt—destinations that were eager to absorb businesses seeking seamless EU market access.

This exodus of segments of the financial ecosystem applied downward pressure on the pound as global investors reassessed the UK’s positioning in global finance. Although efforts were made to outline an equivalence framework for financial regulations between the UK and EU, uncertainties surrounding regulatory divergence limited investor confidence in long-term stability.

Covid-19 as a Complicating Factor

It’s impossible to analyze the post-Brexit performance of the pound sterling without acknowledging the impact of the Covid-19 pandemic. While the pandemic brought economic strains upon nearly every country, the UK faced twin challenges: navigating a global health crisis and adjusting to its new relationship with the EU. The pound saw sharp declines at the onset of the pandemic in early 2020, with further weakening exacerbated by the ongoing uncertainties around Brexit trade negotiations later in the year.

Fiscal and monetary stimulus measures rolled out by the Bank of England (BoE) and the UK government attempted to counteract the dual challenges, but these often exerted additional pressure on the currency. Low interest rates, quantitative easing, and government borrowing all factored into the pound’s relatively weaker footing during this period.

The Broader Implications for Exchange Rate Dynamics

Brexit’s impact on the pound sterling extends beyond bilateral currency pairs like GBP/USD or GBP/EUR. It has reshaped exchange rate dynamics by influencing how investors perceive the UK as an investment destination. So-called “safe-haven currencies” such as the US dollar, yen, and Swiss franc have grown more attractive amid Brexit uncertainty, while currencies in emerging markets often mirrored risk sentiment tied to Britain’s broader economic trajectory.

Further compounding the situation, sterling’s performance in the forex market is now tied not only to domestic economic policies but also to the success (or lack thereof) of diversification efforts, new trade agreements, and technological innovation.

Looking Ahead: What’s Next for the Pound?

The story of Brexit and the pound sterling’s exchange rates is not yet over. Over the longer term, the UK economy’s ability to adapt to the new global landscape will play a large role in determining the strength of its currency. Key factors influencing future movements include:

  1. Trade Agreements: Securing favorable trade deals with countries outside the European Union—such as the United States, Australia, or those within the Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership (CPTPP)—could help support economic growth and trade-driven demand for sterling.

  2. Monetary Policy: The policies adopted by the BoE to manage inflation, growth, and financial stability will remain a key driver of exchange rates. Divergent monetary policies between the BoE and other central banks often lead to exchange rate volatility.

  3. Investor Sentiment: As the UK pursues policy measures to remain competitive and rebuild investor confidence, the re-establishment of trust in the UK market will be a critical determinant of the currency’s performance.

In conclusion, Brexit has been an inflection point for the pound sterling, reshaping its exchange rate trajectory and altering the UK’s perception in global currency markets. Moving forward, the interplay between political strategy, economic adaptability, and market sentiment will define the next chapter in the pound’s evolution. As businesses and investors continue to navigate the post-Brexit landscape, keeping an eye on both policy changes and broader market trends will be essential for making informed decisions.
