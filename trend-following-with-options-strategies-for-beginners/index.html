Trend Following with Options: Strategies for Beginners


Options trading can feel like venturing into a labyrinth for many beginners. With complex terminologies, variable payoff structures, and the fast pace of markets, it’s easy to get overwhelmed. However, integrating trend following principles with options trading offers a systematic, strategic pathway that can help newcomers navigate these complexities more confidently. This comprehensive guide dives into how beginners can adopt trend-following techniques when trading options, empowering you with strategies, insights, and practical advice to kickstart your journey.

Understanding Trend Following in the Context of Options

Before diving into the actual strategies, it’s important to grasp what trend following means in trading and how it applies particularly to options.

Trend following is a strategy that attempts to capitalize on the momentum of an asset’s price. Instead of trying to predict exact price reversals or market tops and bottoms, trend followers look to identify and ride sustained directional moves. In simple terms, if a stock or index has been rising consistently, trend following suggests continuing to go long until there are clear signs that the trend has weakened or reversed.

Options add an extra layer of flexibility and risk management to this approach. Unlike holding the underlying asset, options allow traders to control larger positions with limited capital and defined risk, while still profiting from directional moves.

Why Use Options for Trend Following?

  1. Leverage: Options let you gain exposure to sizable moves with less initial investment compared to stock trading, making them attractive when identifying strong trends.

  2. Risk Control: Buying options provides a known maximum loss (the premium paid), which is crucial for beginners learning to manage risk.

  3. Flexibility to Express Views: Options allow for diverse positions — buying calls or puts, spreads, or other combos — depending on market conditions and risk tolerance.

  4. Profit From Both Uptrends and Downtrends: Calls work well in uptrends, while puts offer ways to benefit from downtrends or market corrections.

Key Components of Trend Following Strategies with Options

Before implementing strategies, mastering key elements of trend following and how they intersect with options trading is essential:

1. Identifying the Trend

Accurately spotting and confirming trends is central. Some common tools and techniques:

  • Moving Averages (MAs): The 50-day and 200-day moving averages are frequently used. A simple example: when the price crosses above the 50-day MA and sustains, it’s often a bullish sign.

  • Trendlines: Drawing support and resistance lines can help visualize upward or downward momentum.

  • Average Directional Index (ADX): ADX values above 25 typically indicate a strong trend, useful for avoiding choppy sideways markets.

  • Price Action & Volume: Confirm the trend with rising volumes during upward moves or increasing selling interest in downtrends.

2. Selection of Underlying Assets

Trend-following strategies generally work best with liquid, volatile assets where sustained moves are more common and options spreads tighter. Popular choices include:

  • Large-cap stocks (e.g., AAPL, MSFT)
  • Major indices (e.g., SPX, QQQ)
  • Highly traded ETFs

3. Timeframe Alignment

Options have expiration dates, so your trend-following timeframe and option duration must align. For trends expected to last weeks or months, consider options with 1–3 months until expiration to avoid rapid time decay.

4. Position Sizing and Money Management

Keeping risk per trade limited (typically 1–3% of the trading capital) helps preserve your portfolio against unexpected reversals.

Beginner Trend Following Options Strategies

Now let’s explore actionable strategies suitable for beginners that combine trend following with options trading.

Strategy 1: Buying Call Options in an Uptrend

Scenario: You identify an uptrend in a stock or index via moving averages and volume confirmation.

Action: Buy a call option that expires in 1–3 months with a strike price near or slightly out-of-the-money.

Rationale: This setup allows participation in the upside move with limited risk (premium paid). If the trend continues, the call’s value increases, offering leveraged gains.

Example: Apple (AAPL) is trading at $150. The 50-day MA has just crossed above the 200-day MA, and volume is rising. Buying a call with a $155 strike expiring in 2 months could capitalize on further gains.

Risks: Time decay will erode premium if the trend stalls. If the price stalls or reverses, the call may expire worthless.

Strategy 2: Buying Put Options in a Downtrend

Scenario: The market shows signs of a bearish trend, confirmed by price below moving averages and rising selling pressure.

Action: Purchase put options with a strike at or slightly out-of-the-money, expiring within 1–3 months.

Rationale: Puts increase in value as the underlying price drops, letting you benefit from downtrends without short-selling the stock directly.

Example: The SPY ETF has dropped below its 50-day and 200-day moving averages, with the ADX indicating a strong downward trend. Buying a put option expiring in 2 months allows you to profit if the decline continues.

Risks: If the decline reverses or the trend weakens, puts lose value due to decay and adverse price moves.

Strategy 3: Using Vertical Spreads to Reduce Risk

To moderate the cost and reduce the risk of buying naked calls or puts, consider using vertical spreads, which involve buying one option and selling another option at a different strike but the same expiration.

  • Bull Call Spread: Buy a call at a lower strike and sell a call at a higher strike to limit maximum loss and profit.

  • Bear Put Spread: Buy a put at a higher strike and sell a put at a lower strike for the same effect during downtrends.

Benefits:

  • Lower upfront cost compared to naked options.
  • Defined risk and reward.
  • Smoother profits if the trend continues but capped gains.

Example: In the bullish Apple scenario, instead of buying a $155 call outright, you buy the $155 call and sell a $165 call, which reduces premium paid but caps maximum profit.

Strategy 4: Rolling Option Positions as Trends Evolve

Trend following requires flexibility. If a trend remains strong beyond the initial option expiration, beginners can consider rolling their positions:

  • Close the near-expiry option.
  • Open a similar option with a later expiration to maintain exposure.

This approach helps avoid losing capital to time decay and leverages long-lasting trends.

Managing Risk and Avoiding Common Pitfalls

Trend following with options carries risks, especially for beginners. Avoid these mistakes to improve your chances of success:

  • Ignoring Time Decay: Options lose value as expiration approaches. Buying options too close to expiry without clear trend confirmation can be costly.

  • Trading in Sideways Markets: Trend following works poorly in choppy conditions. Wait for clear trend signals, indicated by price action, moving averages, and volume.

  • Overleveraging: Using too many contracts can lead to outsized losses quickly. Stick to small position sizes.

  • Neglecting Plan Discipline: Have entry, exit, and risk limits defined before trading. Avoid chasing losses or doubling down irrationally.

Developing a Routine for Trend Following with Options

Consistency and discipline help beginners build skill and confidence. Here’s a sample routine:

  1. Identify Trending Assets Daily/Weekly: Use your favorite trend indicators to compile a watchlist.

  2. Analyze Options Chains for Liquidity and Pricing: Select strikes and expirations aligning with the expected trend duration.

  3. Set Entry Rules: For example, wait until price closes above the 50-day MA and volume confirms uptrend before buying calls.

  4. Define Stop-Loss or Exit Conditions: For instance, if price falls below the 50-day MA or a certain percentage loss of premium occurs.

  5. Track and Journal Trades: Note entry, rationale, outcome, and lessons learned to refine your approach.

Conclusion

Trend following with options offers an appealing framework for beginners looking to combine straightforward trading philosophy with the leverage and risk control options provide. By focusing on identifying clear trends, aligning option expirations and strike choices, managing risk intelligently, and maintaining discipline, novice traders can participate more confidently in market momentum.

While no strategy guarantees success, integrating trend following offers a systematic method to navigate market uncertainty, turning complex options trading into a structured opportunity for growth. Start small, stay patient, and build your skills — the market rewards those who ride the wave, not those who swim against it.
