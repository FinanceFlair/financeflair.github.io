How to Use Divergence in Technical Analysis: Tools and Examples


Divergence is a powerful concept in technical analysis that can provide traders with early signals of potential trend reversals or trend continuation. It occurs when the price of an asset moves in the opposite direction to an indicator, most commonly oscillators like the Relative Strength Index (RSI), Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD), or Stochastic Oscillator. Understanding how to identify and interpret divergence can significantly improve your decision-making process and increase your chances of success in the markets.

In this post, we will dive deep into the concept of divergence, its types, and how you can use it in your trading strategy with the help of various tools and examples. Whether you’re a beginner or an experienced trader, this guide will provide valuable insights into harnessing the power of divergence in your technical analysis.

What Is Divergence?

Divergence refers to a discrepancy between the price of an asset and a technical indicator, signaling a potential change in the direction of the market. When the price moves in one direction while the indicator moves in the opposite direction, it may indicate weakening momentum in the current trend. This divergence can be an early warning sign of a reversal or an indication of trend continuation.

There are two main types of divergence:

  1. Bullish Divergence: This occurs when the price makes lower lows, but the oscillator (such as the RSI or MACD) forms higher lows. This suggests that although the price is still dropping, the momentum behind the downward move is weakening, which could indicate a reversal to the upside.

  2. Bearish Divergence: This happens when the price forms higher highs, but the oscillator makes lower highs. This suggests that even though the price is increasing, the momentum behind the upward movement is waning, signaling the possibility of a reversal to the downside.

Types of Divergence

Divergence can be classified into two types based on the behavior of the price and the indicator:

Regular Divergence

Regular divergence is the most common type and occurs when the price makes new highs or new lows, but the indicator fails to follow suit. This type of divergence typically signals a reversal in the market.

  • Bullish Regular Divergence: This type of divergence occurs when the price makes lower lows, but the indicator (like the RSI or MACD) forms higher lows. It indicates that the downward momentum is weakening, and a potential reversal to the upside could be imminent.

  • Bearish Regular Divergence: This happens when the price forms higher highs, but the indicator makes lower highs. It suggests that the upward momentum is fading, and a potential reversal to the downside is possible.

Hidden Divergence

Hidden divergence is often seen as a continuation signal. It occurs when the price makes a correction within the prevailing trend, but the indicator continues to follow the direction of the trend. Hidden divergence signals that the existing trend is likely to continue rather than reverse.

  • Bullish Hidden Divergence: This occurs when the price forms higher lows during a bullish trend, but the indicator forms lower lows. It suggests that the bullish trend is likely to continue as the correction is merely temporary.

  • Bearish Hidden Divergence: This happens when the price forms lower highs in a bearish trend, but the indicator forms higher highs. It indicates that the bearish trend is likely to continue despite a brief price retracement.

Common Tools to Spot Divergence

To identify divergence effectively, traders use various technical indicators. These tools help to gauge the momentum of an asset and detect discrepancies between price and momentum. Below are some of the most commonly used indicators to spot divergence:

1. Relative Strength Index (RSI)

The RSI is one of the most popular momentum indicators and is used to identify overbought and oversold conditions in a market. RSI values range from 0 to 100, with values above 70 indicating overbought conditions and values below 30 indicating oversold conditions. RSI is particularly useful for spotting divergence.

How to Use RSI for Divergence:

  • Bullish divergence occurs when the price is making lower lows, but the RSI is making higher lows, suggesting that the downward momentum is weakening.
  • Bearish divergence occurs when the price is making higher highs, but the RSI is making lower highs, indicating that the upward momentum is losing strength.

2. Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD)

The MACD is a popular trend-following momentum indicator that shows the relationship between two moving averages of a securityâ€™s price. It consists of two lines: the MACD line (difference between the 12-day and 26-day exponential moving averages) and the signal line (9-day EMA of the MACD line). The MACD is highly effective in spotting divergence, especially in trending markets.

How to Use MACD for Divergence:

  • Bullish divergence occurs when the price forms lower lows, but the MACD forms higher lows, suggesting a weakening downtrend.
  • Bearish divergence occurs when the price forms higher highs, but the MACD forms lower highs, signaling a weakening uptrend.

3. Stochastic Oscillator

The Stochastic Oscillator is another momentum indicator that compares a specific closing price of an asset to a range of its prices over a certain period. It consists of two lines: %K (the main line) and %D (the signal line). The Stochastic Oscillator is particularly useful for spotting divergence in overbought and oversold conditions.

How to Use Stochastic Oscillator for Divergence:

  • Bullish divergence occurs when the price makes lower lows, but the stochastic oscillator forms higher lows, suggesting that the downward momentum is fading.
  • Bearish divergence occurs when the price forms higher highs, but the stochastic oscillator forms lower highs, signaling that the upward momentum is weakening.

4. Commodity Channel Index (CCI)

The Commodity Channel Index (CCI) is a versatile indicator that can help identify overbought and oversold conditions, as well as potential trend reversals. The CCI measures the deviation of the price from its average price over a specific period. It is particularly useful for identifying divergence in markets with strong trends.

How to Use CCI for Divergence:

  • Bullish divergence occurs when the price is making lower lows, but the CCI is making higher lows, indicating that the downtrend may be weakening.
  • Bearish divergence occurs when the price is making higher highs, but the CCI is making lower highs, suggesting that the uptrend is losing momentum.

How to Trade Using Divergence

Once you identify a divergence on the chart, the next step is to incorporate it into your trading strategy. Here are a few general guidelines on how to trade using divergence:

1. Look for Confluence

Divergence should not be used in isolation. It is always better to combine divergence with other technical analysis tools, such as trendlines, support and resistance levels, candlestick patterns, or moving averages. This will increase the likelihood that the divergence signal is valid and not a false alarm.

For example, if you spot bullish divergence on the RSI, but the price is approaching a significant support level, this could increase the probability of a reversal to the upside.

2. Confirm the Divergence with Volume

Volume is another critical factor to consider when trading divergence. A divergence accompanied by strong volume may indicate a more significant price move. Conversely, low volume may suggest that the divergence is weak and the price may not follow through as expected.

3. Wait for the Reversal

While divergence can provide early signals of a trend reversal, it is essential to wait for confirmation before entering a trade. This could be in the form of a break of a trendline, a candlestick reversal pattern, or a cross of the MACD line over the signal line. Avoid entering a trade prematurely based solely on the divergence.

4. Use Proper Risk Management

Divergence is not foolproof, and false signals can occur. Therefore, always use appropriate risk management strategies, such as setting stop-loss orders and managing position sizes, to protect your capital.

Examples of Divergence in Action

Letâ€™s take a look at some real-world examples of divergence to better understand how it works in practice.

Example 1: Bullish Divergence with RSI

Suppose you are looking at the daily chart of a stock that has been in a downtrend for several weeks. The price makes a new low, but the RSI forms a higher low, indicating bullish divergence. This suggests that although the price is still falling, the downward momentum is weakening. After a few more days, the price begins to rise, confirming the reversal.

Example 2: Bearish Divergence with MACD

Imagine a scenario where a cryptocurrency is in a strong uptrend, making higher highs. However, the MACD starts to form lower highs, signaling bearish divergence. This indicates that the upward momentum is fading. Eventually, the price starts to decline, confirming the bearish reversal.

Example 3: Hidden Bullish Divergence with Stochastic Oscillator

In a strong uptrend, the price of a commodity forms a minor correction, making a higher low. However, the Stochastic Oscillator forms a lower low. This is an example of hidden bullish divergence, indicating that the uptrend is likely to continue after the correction.

Conclusion

Divergence is a powerful tool in technical analysis that can provide early signals of potential trend reversals or trend continuation. By understanding how to spot and interpret divergence using various technical indicators such as the RSI, MACD, Stochastic Oscillator, and CCI, traders can gain an edge in the market.
