How to Use Value Investing Principles to Invest in ETFs


The world of investing can feel overwhelmingly complex, filled with an alphabet soup of jargon and an ever-changing buffet of market opinions. Yet beneath the noise, timeless strategies endure. Value investing—the art of finding quality securities trading below their intrinsic worth—has long promised a path to steady, long-term growth. But while value investing often conjures images of poring over individual company balance sheets à la Benjamin Graham, the principles extend far beyond single stocks. With exchange-traded funds (ETFs) now central in many portfolios, applying value investing tactics to ETF selection can combine the wisdom of value investing with the versatility of diversified funds.

This guide explores how to use value-focused frameworks to sift through ETFs, identify bargains, and build a portfolio that can withstand market froth. If you’re keen on harnessing value investing discipline for your ETF picks, let’s dig in.

Understanding Value Investing: The Foundation

Before drilling into the specifics of using value investing tactics with ETFs, let’s clarify the essence of value investing itself.

Value investing is about finding investments trading for less than their fundamental worth. Instead of chasing trends, value investors seek mispriced assets, aiming to buy when prevailing pessimism outweighs a security’s true prospects.

Key hallmarks of value investing include:

  • Intrinsic value estimation: Assessing what a security should be worth based on earnings, assets, cash flows, and growth prospects.
  • Margin of safety: Requiring a buffer between current price and estimated value to cushion against errors or market surprises.
  • Long-term focus: Valuing patience, often holding assets for years to let the thesis play out.
  • Contrarian mindset: Embracing opportunities when others are pessimistic or ignoring unloved investments.

Traditional value investing involves combing through company financials. However, the logic behind these strategies can absolutely translate to baskets of stocks—like those found in ETFs.

Why Apply Value Investing Principles to ETFs?

ETFs deliver instant diversification, trading flexibility, and generally low fees. They mirror indices, track sectors, or even replicate factor-based strategies. But “set it and forget it” ETF investing isn’t the only game in town. Many investors fill portfolios with broad-market ETFs, never looking under the hood.

However, just like stocks, ETFs can become overvalued or undervalued relative to their underlying holdings or strategy execution. Chasing the most popular, trending ETFs can expose investors to the same risks as overpaying for a single hyped-up stock. At the same time, overlooked, “boring” funds running on value-driven methodologies can slip into the bargain bin.

By assessing ETFs using value frameworks, investors can seek out funds that deliver underlying value, minimize downside, and sidestep frothy market emotions.

Key Steps: Using Value Investing Principles to Evaluate ETFs

Applying value investing to ETFs isn’t about sniping price dips with clever timing. It’s about building a repeatable process to identify, assess, and invest in funds with a better risk-reward balance. Here’s how to go about it:

1. Define Your Investment Universe

Begin by narrowing your ETF choices. There are thousands of ETFs on the market, spanning every asset class, sector, region, strategy, and factor. Filter based on:

  • Asset class: Equities, fixed income, real assets, alternative strategies.
  • Geography: U.S. only? Developed international? Emerging markets?
  • Sector focus: Broad market, sector-specific, thematic, or factor-based?

Stick with funds offering significant trading liquidity and assets under management—niche ETFs with scant assets or low volume may carry higher risks.

2. Analyze the ETF’s Underlying Holdings

Apply the same value lens you’d use for individual stocks to the ETF’s holdings.

  • Value orientation: Does the ETF’s investment style focus on undervalued securities? For example, funds tracking value-weighted indices or screening for low price-to-earnings (P/E) or price-to-book (P/B) ratios.
  • Weighting methodology: Market-cap weighted? Equal-weight? Factor-based? The weighting formula can impact value exposure.
  • Sector allocation: Is the ETF loaded with cyclical sectors or defensive names? Sector slant affects potential risk and opportunity.
  • Country or region bias: Are holdings geographically diversified, or is there hidden concentration risk?

Many ETFs publish full transparency into their holdings—reviewing major positions, sector splits, and style exposures is crucial.

3. Evaluate Valuation Metrics at the Fund Level

Think of the ETF itself as a “stock” composed of many components. Examine aggregate valuation metrics:

  • Aggregate price-to-earnings (P/E): Lower relative to benchmarks may suggest undervaluation.
  • Price-to-book (P/B): Lower P/B can indicate the ETF holds more asset-rich, potentially undervalued companies.
  • Dividend yield: Higher yields can signal lower-priced constituents or strong cash flow.
  • Price-to-cash flow: Useful for funds heavy in cash-generative businesses.

Compare these figures against peers and the overall market. For example, a U.S. value ETF with a P/E of 13 versus the S&P 500’s 20 could be a sign of relative value.

Major ETF providers usually report these data. Alternatively, financial data aggregators and investment research platforms list comparison metrics.

4. Assess Performance Through a Value Lens

Don’t just glance at total returns. Value investing recognizes that patiently holding undervalued assets can require weathering dull or underwhelming periods.

  • Relative performance: How has the ETF done compared to broad-market or growth rivals during different market cycles?
  • Drawdown resilience: Value strategies often hold up better in downturns, even if they lag in surging bull markets.
  • Return consistency: Steady, compounded returns are preferable to wild swings.

A value ETF may lag during speculative surges but outperform when markets correct or focus on fundamentals.

5. Investigate Cost Structure

Lower costs boost net investor returns, which is a pillar of value investing’s caution about fees eating into compounding.

  • Expense ratio: Compare the ETF’s annual fee to alternatives with similar strategies.
  • Trading spread: High bid/ask spreads can eat into returns, particularly for illiquid funds.
  • Tracking error: Some ETFs are less faithful trackers of their benchmarks—wider tracking error can create opportunity for—or against—investors.

While fees matter, the lowest cost ETF isn’t always the “best value” if it lacks authenticity in its value tilt or has hidden risks.

6. Consider Forward-Looking Opportunity

Value investing is about looking beyond recent winners to anticipate where undervalued assets may cycle back into favor.

  • Market sentiment: Are value funds generally unloved? Historically, periods of value underperformance have been followed by strong catch-ups.
  • Economic backdrop: Value stocks (and value-tilted ETFs) can excel in different environments: early-stage recoveries, rising rate regimes, and inflationary times.
  • Rebalancing discipline: Some value ETFs systematically rebalance to maintain value exposure, while others can inadvertently drift toward growth.

Screen for funds with a repeatable process that maintains value orientation, not just those that happened to look like value recently.

Value ETF Types: Knowing the Landscape

Not all value-focused ETFs are created equal—understanding the types of value funds helps align your approach.

1. Broad Market Value ETFs

These typically slice core indices (like the S&P 500 or Russell 1000) into value and growth sleeves. Examples include S&P 500 Value Index-based ETFs or MSCI World Value funds.

  • Pros: Diversified, easy to access, generally low-cost.
  • Cons: Can include large, slow-moving names and financial-sector heavyweights.

2. Factor or Smart Beta Value ETFs

These deploy algorithms to select or weight stocks based on value factors: low P/E, P/B, dividend yield, etc.

  • Pros: More targeted exposure to value characteristics, systematic methodology.
  • Cons: May tilt heavily to certain industries or geographies, performance varies by factor definition.

3. Sector and Industry Value ETFs

Some focus on unloved sectors—financials, energy, materials—that tend to carry value attributes.

  • Pros: Sharper bets on reversion potential, more sensitivity to economic cycles.
  • Cons: Concentration risk, higher volatility.

4. International and Emerging Market Value ETFs

Non-U.S. value ETFs may offer richer opportunity when domestic valuations are high.

  • Pros: Geographic diversification, potential to harvest “value premium” internationally.
  • Cons: Currency risk, sometimes less transparent holdings.

Building a Value-Oriented ETF Portfolio

With knowledge of how to analyze and choose value-oriented ETFs, constructing a resilient, returns-focused portfolio involves thoughtful assembly:

Strategic Blend

Combine core broad-market value ETFs with targeted factor or sector funds to tilt toward where value is most underappreciated.

Periodic Review and Rebalancing

Stay disciplined about reviewing portfolio weights and rebalancing, as market shifts can dilute value exposure over time.

Diversification Without Diworsification

Diversification reduces risk, but loading up on countless similar ETFs can dilute conviction and drive up costs. Focus on differentiated holdings.

Patience and Process Orientation

Consistently applying a value framework often means enduring periods of underperformance. The reward comes not from chasing rallying funds, but sticking to a process rooted in fundamentals.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Applying value investing to ETFs isn’t foolproof. Watch out for these traps:

  • Style drift: Some “value” ETFs gradually morph into growth-leaning portfolios as certain stocks rise in price, diluting original strategy.
  • Chasing high yields: ETFs with huge dividend payouts may be value traps if underlying businesses are in secular decline.
  • Ignoring risk and quality: Some value screens favor companies with poor fundamentals or flawed business models. Review not just price multiples, but profitability, cash flows, and balance sheets.
  • Overemphasis on recent returns: Evaluating solely based on short-term performance can miss when value is set to turn.

Real-World Example: Applying the Value Framework

Consider two hypothetical U.S. equity ETFs:

  • ETF Alpha: Tracks a value-oriented index, 12x P/E ratio, diversified sector exposure, consistent dividend yields of 3%, expense ratio of 0.09%.
  • ETF Beta: Chases “hot stocks”, 28x P/E ratio, concentrated in technology, minimal dividends, expense ratio of 0.25%.

At first blush, ETF Beta’s five-year chart dazzles, but fundamentals suggest exuberance is built into its price. ETF Alpha, while currently lagging, boasts a cheaper valuation, more stable payouts, and a lower fee.

Applying the value investing playbook pushes you toward ETF Alpha for its margin of safety, lower speculation, and better long-term prospects. Combine with patience and time, and the value approach may deliver when sentiment resets.

Future-Proofing Your ETF Value Strategy

Value investing grows more nuanced in a world of algorithmic trading and rapid market shifts, but human psychology—greed, fear, herd behavior—remains.

Adapting timeless value investing principles to ETFs means:

  • Staying curious and informed on ETF methodologies and value definitions.
  • Avoiding complacency; even “passive” ETFs require active research.
  • Embracing process over prediction—letting the strategy play out across cycles.

With automation enabling ever more complex ETF products, disciplined value frameworks can help cut through the noise.

Conclusion

Harnessing value investing principles in ETF investing isn’t about echoing the past by blindly buying low-multiple funds. It’s about building a process: defining your universe, interrogating holdings, weighing valuation metrics, and sticking to the plan even when the crowd races in the other direction.

As ETFs proliferate and markets drift between euphoria and pessimism, the value approach offers a time-tested compass. Coupling diversification, research, cost discipline, and a contrarian streak in ETF selection can pave the way to durable, compounding returns—without the forensics demanded by single-stock selection.

In a landscape flooded with passive investing choices, thoughtful, value-driven ETF selection stands apart. Mix patience, skepticism, and discipline—and let the market work for you, not against you.
