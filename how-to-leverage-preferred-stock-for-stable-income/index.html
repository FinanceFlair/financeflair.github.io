How to Leverage Preferred Stock for Stable Income


For investors seeking a balance between income generation and relative security, preferred stock offers a compelling option often overlooked in favor of common shares or bonds. Preferred stock occupies a unique position in the financial markets, combining equity-like features with fixed-income characteristics. By understanding how preferred stock works and deploying it strategically within a portfolio, you can create a steady stream of income and reduce volatility.

What Is Preferred Stock?

Preferred stock represents ownership in a company but comes with preferential rights regarding dividends and claims on assets. Unlike common stockholders, preferred shareholders receive dividend payments before any dividends are distributed to common shareholders. They also usually have priority if the company undergoes liquidation, although they generally do not carry voting rights.

Preferred shares often pay fixed or adjustable dividends, resembling bond coupon payments, but with the added benefit of potentially appreciating in value. This dual nature makes them an attractive middle ground between common equity and debt instruments.

Advantages of Preferred Stock for Income Investors

  1. Predictable Dividends
    Many preferred stocks pay fixed dividends quarterly or monthly. This regular income can be more dependable than the dividends from common stock, which fluctuate with company profits.

  2. Priority Dividend Payments
    Preferred shareholders have a higher claim on dividends than common stockholders. If a company experiences financial distress, preferred dividends must be paid before common dividends, adding a layer of security.

  3. Potential for Capital Appreciation
    While primarily income-focused, preferred shares can also appreciate in price with declines in interest rates or improvements in company fundamentals, offering potential upside.

  4. Diversification
    Including preferred stock in a portfolio diversifies sources of income and reduces sensitivity to common stock market volatility.

  5. Tax Advantages
    Depending on jurisdiction and specific instruments, preferred dividends might receive favorable tax treatment compared to bond interest.

Understanding Types of Preferred Stock

Preferred stock isn’t a monolith; it comes in various forms, each with unique characteristics affecting income stability and risk.

  • Cumulative Preferred Stock
    Dividends accumulate if unpaid. If a company skips dividend payments, it owes those amounts to cumulative preferred shareholders before any dividends can be paid to common shareholders. This feature strengthens income reliability.

  • Non-Cumulative Preferred Stock
    Dividends do not accumulate. If skipped, the investor loses those dividend payments forever. While riskier from an income perspective, these may offer higher initial yields.

  • Convertible Preferred Stock
    Can be converted into a specified number of common shares. This offers potential upside while securing income through preferred dividends.

  • Callable Preferred Stock
    The issuing company can redeem these shares at a set price after a certain date. This feature limits price appreciation potential but may offer a higher yield as compensation.

  • Adjustable-Rate Preferred Stock
    Dividend payments fluctuate with a benchmark interest rate, reducing interest rate risk and stabilizing income in rising rate environments.

Selecting Preferred Stocks for Stable Income

When aiming for steady income, several factors demand careful evaluation:

  • Dividend Yield and Payment History
    Look for preferred stocks with attractive dividend yields but scrutinize companies’ histories for consistent dividend payments. A high yield that looks too good to be true might indicate higher risk.

  • Credit Quality
    Preferred stocks issued by companies with strong credit ratings and stable cash flows tend to have more reliable dividend payments.

  • Call Features and Maturity
    Callable preferreds can be redeemed early, truncating expected income. Understanding call provisions helps anticipate potential income disruptions.

  • Sector and Industry
    Some sectors, like utilities and financial institutions, traditionally issue preferred stocks offering stable dividends. Diversify across sectors to mitigate industry-specific risks.

Managing Risk Associated with Preferred Stocks

Though preferred stocks offer income and some security features, they also carry risks:

  • Interest Rate Risk
    Like bonds, preferred stock prices fall when interest rates rise, since fixed dividends become less attractive. Adjustable-rate preferreds mitigate this to some extent.

  • Credit Risk
    If a company’s financial health deteriorates, preferred dividends could be suspended or eliminated, affecting income stability. Financial strength and creditworthiness remain paramount.

  • Liquidity Risk
    Compared to common shares and bonds, preferred stock markets are less liquid, potentially causing price volatility and making timely sales difficult.

  • Call Risk
    Companies may redeem callable preferreds when interest rates fall, replacing them with cheaper debt or preferreds, reducing income opportunities.

Mitigation strategies include diversifying your holdings across various preferred stocks, focusing on higher credit-quality issuers, and understanding each preferred stock’s terms.

How to Incorporate Preferred Stock into Your Portfolio

  1. Income-Focused Allocation
    Designate a portion of your income-generating portfolio specifically for preferred stocks. Their predictable dividends can complement bond and dividend-paying common stock holdings.

  2. Complement to Bonds
    Preferred stock dividends often exceed bond yields, but with higher risk. Use preferred shares to boost yield while balancing overall portfolio risk.

  3. Tax Considerations
    Consult a tax advisor to structure preferred stock investments in a tax-efficient manner, especially if investing in tax-advantaged accounts or across jurisdictions.

  4. Ladder Preferred Stocks
    Similar to bond ladders, stagger investing across preferred stocks with varying call dates and features to spread call and reinvestment risk.

  5. Use Specialized ETFs or Mutual Funds
    If selecting individual preferred stocks seems complex or risky, consider funds specializing in preferred shares. These provide instant diversification and professional management, albeit with management fees.

Case Study: Building a Stable Income Portfolio with Preferred Stock

Imagine an investor named Sarah seeking $30,000 annual income from an investment portfolio. She allocates 40% of her $750,000 portfolio to high-quality cumulative preferred stocks yielding around 6%. This allocation alone generates $18,000 per year, forming the backbone of her income strategy.

She complements this with bond funds and dividend-paying common stocks to add diversification and capital growth. By maintaining a diversified mix and focusing on cumulative and adjustable-rate preferred stocks, Sarah balances income reliability with growth potential and risk mitigation.

Monitoring and Adjusting Your Preferred Stock Holdings

Once invested, ongoing monitoring is vital:

  • Dividend Announcements
    Track declared dividends and any suspensions or cuts which could signal financial trouble.

  • Credit Rating Changes
    Downgrades in creditworthiness often precede dividend cuts and price declines.

  • Interest Rate Environment
    Rising rates generally depress preferred stock prices; be prepared to adjust holdings accordingly.

  • Call Notices
    Stay alert for call announcements to plan reallocation or reinvestment strategies.

Regular portfolio reviews help maintain income stability and preserve capital value.

Conclusion

Preferred stocks provide a compelling avenue for investors seeking stable, predictable income with an equity-like position in companies. Their priority dividend rights, fixed or adjustable income features, and potential for capital appreciation create an attractive blend for income-focused portfolios.

By understanding the nuances of preferred stock types, evaluating issuer credit quality, and balancing risks with diversification, you can effectively harness preferred shares to generate steady income. Incorporating preferred stocks thoughtfully can enhance portfolio income stability and offer a valuable complement to conventional bonds and common stocks.

Leverage preferred stock as more than a niche asset—make it a cornerstone of your income strategy to achieve both stability and potential growth.
