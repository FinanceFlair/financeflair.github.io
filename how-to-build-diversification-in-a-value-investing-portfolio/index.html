How to Build Diversification in a Value Investing Portfolio


When it comes to investing, value investing has long been heralded as a strategy focused on finding undervalued stocks that have strong fundamentals but are currently overlooked by the broader market. The goal is to buy these stocks at a discount, hold them over the long term, and reap gains as their true value gets recognized.

However, like any investment strategy, value investing carries risks, and one of the most effective ways to manage those risks is through diversification. In this post, we’ll explore how to build diversification in a value investing portfolio without diluting the core principles of value investing. We’ll dive into strategies, considerations, and practical steps that investors can follow to balance risk and reward while capitalizing on value opportunities.

Understanding the Importance of Diversification in Value Investing

Value investing fundamentally relies on identifying companies that are trading below their intrinsic worth, often due to temporary issues or market sentiment. While patience and conviction are key, no one can predict the timing or outcome with complete certainty. Even the most meticulously researched stock can underperform due to unforeseen business or macroeconomic challenges.

Diversification helps mitigate these risks by spreading investments across a broader range of companies, industries, and sectors. This doesn’t just minimize downside—it also smooths out the portfolio’s overall performance, reducing the impact of any single stock’s poor performance.

Risk Concentration vs. Risk Spreading

A concentrated portfolio where a few positions represent a large portion of capital might lead to outsized gains but can also result in significant losses if those companies falter. On the other hand, an overly diversified portfolio with dozens or hundreds of holdings may dilute the value investor’s edge and lead to average market returns.

The challenge lies in finding the balance: enough diversification to protect against idiosyncratic risk (company-specific risk), but not so much that the portfolio becomes unfocused or indistinguishable from a broad index.

Setting a Diversification Framework for Your Value Portfolio

Before you start buying stocks, it’s crucial to establish guidelines for diversification tailored to your investment philosophy:

1. Decide on the Number of Holdings

Historically, many successful value investors have ranged from holding 20-30 stocks to about 50 in their portfolios. Holding fewer than 15 can increase risk, while more than 50 can make it hard to keep track of positions and conduct meaningful research.

Striking a middle ground where you can closely monitor each holding while managing risk effectively is ideal. This also depends on how much time and resources you have for research.

2. Diversify Across Industries and Sectors

Avoid overweighting any single industry or sector. Value traps or structural issues may derail companies in a specific sector, so exposure across different areas—such as consumer goods, technology, healthcare, financials, and industrials—can reduce vulnerability to sector-specific headwinds.

Aim to spread your positions among at least 5-7 sectors, while keeping in mind how cyclical or defensive these sectors are.

3. Diversify by Market Capitalization

Though value investing often gravitates toward large-cap stocks due to liquidity and stability, mid-cap and select small-cap companies can also present excellent value opportunities.

Smaller companies tend to have higher growth potential but come with increased volatility and risk. A blend of large-cap and small/mid-cap holdings can balance growth prospects with stability.

4. Incorporate Geographic Diversification

Depending on your mandate and comfort level, consider exposure to foreign markets and emerging economies, where valuations might be more attractive or where value investing is underrepresented.

International diversification adds complexity—such as currency risk and political uncertainties—but it can also enhance returns and reduce correlation with domestic markets.

5. Balance Between Value Styles

Value investing itself is multifaceted. You might identify value through traditional metrics like low price-to-earnings (P/E) or price-to-book (P/B) ratios, or through deep value metrics like net-net assets or liquidations. Alternatively, some investors look for quality value stocks—companies trading cheaply but with solid financial health and growth prospects.

Diversifying the types of value styles you invest in can create a more robust portfolio less dependent on a single valuation approach.

Practical Steps to Implement Diversification in a Value Portfolio

Building a diversified value portfolio requires discipline and an ongoing process, not just a one-time allocation decision.

Step 1: Conduct Thorough and Standardized Research

Establish a checklist or framework for evaluating value stocks systematically. Analyze financial health, cash flow, earnings quality, competitive advantages, and management integrity. Consistency ensures you apply the same value criteria across all sectors and market caps.

Step 2: Screen for Underpriced Stocks Across Sectors

Use stock screeners with value criteria but filter by sector to identify undervalued candidates in multiple industries. This helps uncover opportunities that may be hidden in less-followed sectors.

Step 3: Assess Correlation and Beta of Stocks

Review how potential stocks move relative to each other and to the market. Low or negative correlation among holdings can improve the portfolio’s risk profile by dampening volatility.

Step 4: Allocate Capital Based on Conviction and Risk

Within your targeted number of holdings, allocate more capital to your highest conviction ideas, but set limits to avoid over-concentration. For instance, no single position should exceed 10% of the portfolio. This maintains a balance between focus and diversification.

Step 5: Regularly Review and Rebalance

Investment environments change, and so do company fundamentals. Periodic reviews allow you to trim winners that become fairly valued or overvalued, and add new undervalued opportunities. Rebalancing also ensures your portfolio doesn’t become unintentionally concentrated or skewed towards certain sectors.

Step 6: Utilize Dividend-Paying Value Stocks

Dividend-paying companies often have stable cash flows and established businesses. Incorporating dividend-paying value stocks can provide steady income and reduce portfolio volatility.

Addressing Common Concerns about Diversification in Value Investing

Will Diversification Dilute Returns?

It’s true that owning dozens of stocks lowers the chance of hitting a home run with any single stock compared to a highly concentrated portfolio. But focusing singularly on a few names increases risk and may cause large drawdowns.

Diversification smooths returns and reduces the emotional stress of large swings, enabling investors to stick to their value disciplines.

How to Avoid Value Traps While Diversifying?

Diversification doesn’t mean buying any cheap stock indiscriminately. Each candidate still needs rigorous due diligence to ensure it’s undervalued for the right reasons—not because of deteriorating business fundamentals or industry decline.

How Often Should a Value Portfolio be Rebalanced?

There is no universally perfect schedule. Semi-annual or annual rebalancing is common. Reacting too frequently to market noise can be detrimental, but ignoring changes in fundamentals could expose you to prolonged losses.

The Role of Alternative Investments in a Value Portfolio

While stocks form the core of a value portfolio, some investors look beyond equities to build diversification:

  • Value-Oriented Bonds: Investing in distressed or underpriced corporate bonds can complement equity holdings.
  • Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs): Some value-focused REITs trade below their net asset value, providing income and diversification.
  • Alternative Assets: Private equity, commodities, or hedge fund strategies with a value tilt can add diversification but require access and due diligence.

These additions should align with your goals and risk tolerance.

Staying Patient and Disciplined Amid Market Movements

Value investing is inherently a long-term game, requiring patience as undervalued stocks appreciate over time. Diversification helps cushion the journey since some stocks will lag while others lead.

Maintaining discipline, not chasing hot sectors or fads, and sticking to your diversification framework helps navigate the inevitable volatility.

Summary: Building a Diversified Value Investing Portfolio

  • Select a manageable number of holdings (20-50) to balance risk spreading and focused research.
  • Diversify across sectors, industries, and market caps to reduce exposure to concentrated risks.
  • Consider geographic diversification for additional opportunities and reduced domestic correlation.
  • Blend different value styles—traditional, deep value, and quality value—to capture broader opportunities.
  • Allocate capital wisely, avoiding excessive concentration in any one stock or sector.
  • Conduct thorough, consistent fundamental analysis to avoid value traps.
  • Regularly monitor, review, and rebalance your portfolio to maintain diversification and adapt to changing conditions.
  • Stay patient and disciplined, leveraging diversification as a buffer against uncertainty.

Building a diversified value investing portfolio is not just a risk management tactic—it also positions investors to harness the full spectrum of undervalued opportunities across industries and geographies, ultimately enhancing the probability of solid, consistent long-term gains. With thoughtful planning and discipline, diversification becomes a powerful tool that complements the core philosophy of value investing.

Investing wisely is a journey, so take the time to design and nurture your portfolio with an eye toward balance, resilience, and value.
