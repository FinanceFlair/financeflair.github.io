Trend Following Myths Debunked


Trend following is one of the oldest investment strategies in existence, rooted in the basic idea of capitalizing on sustained market movements. Yet, despite its long history and proven track record, trend following remains widely misunderstood. Countless myths have sprung up around this approach, clouding the judgment of novice investors and even seasoned traders. It’s time to clear the air and explore what trend following truly entails—beyond the misconceptions.

In this comprehensive post, we will dismantle the common myths surrounding trend following, explain how the strategy operates in real markets, and uncover why it remains a powerful tool for portfolio diversification and risk management.

What is Trend Following? A Quick Primer

At its core, trend following is a strategy aiming to capture profits by identifying and riding price trends. Traders buy assets that are trending upward and sell or short those trending downward. Unlike contrarian strategies that bet on reversals, trend following assumes that market momentum tends to persist.

Trend following typically relies on technical indicators, such as moving averages or breakout systems, to identify entry and exit points. The goal is to align one’s positions with the prevailing market direction rather than guessing market tops or bottoms.

Myth 1: Trend Following Is Purely Luck

One of the strongest myths about trend following is that it’s just gambling on chance or a random “hit or miss” game. The reality is far from that. Trend following employs a systematic, rules-based methodology grounded in statistical evidence.

Backtested data over decades suggest that certain trend-following setups have positive expected returns across a variety of asset classes and markets. This isn’t coincidental; it stems from momentum persistence—a well-documented phenomenon where assets that have performed well in the past continue to do so in the near future.

While luck cannot be entirely eliminated from trading, successful trend followers reduce randomness by sticking to objective criteria, minimizing emotional biases, and following disciplined risk management.

Myth 2: Trend Following Works Only in Bull Markets

It’s a common belief that trend following shines only during long bull markets when prices are rising steadily. Conversely, skeptics think it fails miserably during sideways or declining markets.

In truth, trend following strategies are designed to profit in both up and down trends. Skilled trend followers short assets on persistent downward trends, making money even in bear markets. Because trend following adapts to prevailing market conditions, it offers tactical flexibility.

What tends to hurt trend following is sideways or choppy markets characterized by lack of clear direction and frequent false signals. These periods can generate frequent small losses, often testing the trader’s patience. However, over the long term, the wins during significant trending phases outweigh these smaller pullbacks.

Myth 3: Trend Following Means Holding Positions Forever

Another misconception is that trend followers blindly hold onto trades once entered, regardless of market changes. This idea paints trend following as stubborn and inflexible.

In practice, trend following involves clearly defined exit rules and risk controls. Stops or trailing stops are placed to protect against reversals that invalidate the original trend signal. If the market shifts or momentum fades, the position is exited promptly.

This dynamic management ensures that losses are capped and profits protected. Trend followers might hold positions ranging from a few days to a few months, depending on the strategy and market conditions—not indefinitely.

Myth 4: Trend Following Is Only for Experts and Algorithms

While sophisticated quantitative funds have embraced trend following algorithms, the strategy itself is neither exclusive to experts nor reliant on high-frequency trading. The principles behind trend following are accessible to retail investors with a basic understanding of market charts and a commitment to discipline.

Numerous simple indicators, such as moving average crossovers or breakout levels, can form the basis of a trend following system without complex programming or expensive software. The key challenge lies in consistency, emotional control, and sticking to the plan.

Successful trend following boils down to patience and systematic execution rather than insider knowledge or advanced technology.

Myth 5: Trend Following Guarantees Easy Profits

No trading strategy—trend following included—comes with a guarantee of easy money. It requires effort, discipline, and the acceptance of losses. The myth that one can effortlessly “ride trends” and make quick fortunes creates unrealistic expectations.

Trend following often involves enduring several losing trades in a row, especially during range-bound markets. It demands a long-term perspective and a belief in the statistical edge of momentum persistence rather than instant gratification.

Understanding that losses are an inherent part of the game helps traders maintain resilience and stay committed to the strategy, which is ultimately what drives success.

Myth 6: Trend Following Only Works in Stocks

Many assume trend following applies solely to stock markets, but this couldn’t be farther from the truth. Trend following strategies have proven effective across various asset classes—commodities, currencies, bonds, futures, and even cryptocurrencies.

Global markets exhibit momentum traits, and trend followers diversify by applying their systems to multiple markets simultaneously. This multi-asset approach not only broadens opportunities but also reduces overall portfolio risk through diversification.

Hence, limiting trend following to a single asset type misses its full potential.

Myth 7: You Must Catch Trends at the Very Beginning

Beginners sometimes believe successful trend following means pinpointing the exact start of a trend. This expectation often leads to frustration as markets rarely signal perfect entry points in hindsight.

True trend followers recognize that catching the initial move perfectly is impossible consistently. Instead, they focus on confirming trends based on objective criteria and managing risk carefully. Entering after a trend has established itself—even partially—still offers lucrative upside potential.

Trying to forecast or chase the very first tick usually leads to whipsaws and losses, defeating the purpose of a systematic, evidence-based approach.

Myth 8: Trend Following Is Contradictory to Value Investing

Some investors view trend following and value investing as diametrically opposed. While value investing is based on fundamentals and intrinsic worth, trend following is primarily technical and momentum-driven.

These philosophies can, however, coexist and complement each other within a diversified portfolio. Trend following often excels at capturing shorter- to medium-term price moves, while value strategies focus on longer-term appreciation. Many institutional investors combine the two to smooth returns and enhance risk-adjusted performance.

Understanding their different time horizons and goals helps clarify that they are distinct tools rather than mutually exclusive approaches.

Why Does Trend Following Work?

Now that we’ve debunked myths, it’s worth briefly exploring why momentum and trend following tend to work across markets:

  • Behavioral biases: Investors tend to underreact to new information initially and overreact later, creating momentum.

  • Herding: Market participants follow each other, causing price trends to persist.

  • Risk premiums: Trending prices may reflect compensation for bearing certain risks that play out over time.

The persistence of trends emerges from both psychological and structural market factors, making trend following a rational strategy to exploit these inefficiencies.

Practical Tips for Trend Following Success

If you’re considering implementing trend following, here are some pragmatic guidelines:

  1. Define clear entry and exit rules — Use technical indicators suited to your time horizon.
  2. Incorporate risk management — Set stops to limit losses and size positions appropriately.
  3. Diversify across asset classes — Avoid overconcentration in one market.
  4. Maintain discipline — Stick to your system through drawdowns and choppy markets.
  5. Review performance regularly — Adapt and optimize without emotional interference.
  6. Be patient — Trends take time to develop; don’t expect immediate gains.

Conclusion: Trend Following—A Strategy with Substance

Trend following is far more than the myths suggest. It’s a methodical strategy grounded in well-understood market behaviors, adaptable to different market environments and asset classes. While it’s not risk-free or foolproof, it offers a systematic way to engage with market momentum, applying clear rules and robust risk control.

By separating fact from fiction, investors can approach trend following with realistic expectations, better preparation, and greater confidence. As an enduring approach with a rich history, trend following deserves its place at the table alongside other investment strategies—not shrouded in misconceptions but illuminated by understanding.
