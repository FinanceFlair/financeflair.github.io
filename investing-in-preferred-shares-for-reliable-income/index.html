Investing in Preferred Shares for Reliable Income


Investing in preferred shares can be an attractive strategy for those looking to generate steady, reliable income from their investment portfolio. Preferred shares, also known as preference stocks, occupy a unique position in the world of financial markets, offering investors the potential for higher yields compared to common stocks while providing more stability than fixed-income assets like bonds. However, before diving into preferred shares, it’s crucial to understand how they work, their benefits, risks, and the role they can play in a well-rounded investment strategy.

What Are Preferred Shares?

Preferred shares are a type of equity security that provides holders with a fixed dividend, typically paid before dividends are distributed to common shareholders. Unlike common stocks, preferred shares do not come with voting rights, but they offer investors a more predictable income stream. These shares are often seen as a hybrid between common equity and debt instruments, combining characteristics of both.

Key Features of Preferred Shares:

  • Dividend Priority: Preferred shareholders receive dividends before common shareholders. In fact, if a company is financially struggling, preferred dividends must be paid before any common dividends can be issued.

  • Fixed Dividend: The dividends on preferred shares are generally fixed, providing more stability and predictability for investors. This fixed payout structure makes them appealing to those who rely on investment income, such as retirees.

  • Convertible Option: Some preferred shares are convertible, meaning they can be exchanged for a predetermined number of common shares. This option can provide the investor with the opportunity to participate in the upside potential of the company’s stock price.

  • Callability: Many preferred shares are callable, meaning the issuing company can buy them back at a predetermined price, typically after a certain period.

  • Cumulative Dividends: Many preferred shares come with cumulative dividends, which means if the company misses a dividend payment, it must pay the missed dividends before any common dividends can be distributed.

Types of Preferred Shares

Preferred shares come in different varieties, and understanding these different types can help investors decide which suits their financial goals and risk tolerance:

  1. Cumulative Preferred Shares: These shares allow investors to accumulate missed dividend payments. If a company skips a dividend, it must pay the missed amount in the future before any dividends can be issued to common shareholders.

  2. Non-Cumulative Preferred Shares: Unlike cumulative preferred shares, non-cumulative preferred shares do not allow for the accumulation of missed dividends. If a company skips a payment, the investor loses out on that income.

  3. Convertible Preferred Shares: These shares can be converted into a predetermined number of common shares at the option of the shareholder. This conversion option offers potential upside if the company’s stock price appreciates significantly.

  4. Participating Preferred Shares: These shares offer an additional benefit where shareholders can receive extra dividends if the company performs exceptionally well. This can be an attractive option if you’re looking for potential upside in addition to regular income.

  5. Perpetual Preferred Shares: These shares do not have a maturity date and may continue to pay dividends indefinitely, as long as the issuing company remains solvent and continues to pay out dividends.

  6. Redeemable Preferred Shares: These shares can be called back by the issuing company at a specific price after a certain period. Investors should be aware of the potential for the shares to be redeemed early, which could disrupt income expectations.

Why Invest in Preferred Shares?

1. Steady and Predictable Income

For investors seeking reliable income, preferred shares can be an excellent option. The fixed dividend payments are often higher than those of bonds and provide a level of income certainty that common stocks do not. This predictability makes preferred shares an appealing choice for those looking to supplement their income, such as retirees or income-focused investors.

2. Lower Volatility than Common Stocks

While common stocks can experience significant price fluctuations, preferred shares tend to be more stable. This stability comes from the fixed dividend payments, which make preferred shares less sensitive to market movements. As a result, they can offer a buffer during times of economic uncertainty or market downturns.

3. Priority Over Common Stockholders in Bankruptcy

In the event of a company’s liquidation, preferred shareholders have priority over common shareholders when it comes to receiving payouts. While preferred shareholders are not guaranteed to recover their full investment in a bankruptcy scenario, they are ahead of common shareholders in the priority order, making them less risky in this regard.

4. Potential for Capital Appreciation

While preferred shares are generally less volatile than common stocks, they still have the potential for capital appreciation. Convertible preferred shares, in particular, offer the possibility of converting into common shares, which can lead to capital gains if the company’s stock price rises significantly.

5. Diversification Benefits

Preferred shares can be a great way to diversify your investment portfolio. Since they offer a different risk-return profile compared to bonds and common stocks, they can help balance out a portfolio that may be overly exposed to either bonds or equities. By adding preferred shares to your portfolio, you can achieve greater diversification, which may lower overall portfolio risk.

Risks of Investing in Preferred Shares

1. Interest Rate Sensitivity

Preferred shares are sensitive to interest rates. When interest rates rise, the fixed dividend yield on preferred shares may become less attractive compared to newer investments that offer higher returns. This can lead to a decline in the price of preferred shares as investors demand higher yields.

2. Credit Risk

Preferred shares are issued by companies, and if the company faces financial trouble, it may cut or suspend its dividend payments. This is a particular risk for non-cumulative preferred shares, where missed payments are not accumulated. Therefore, assessing the creditworthiness of the issuing company is critical when considering an investment in preferred shares.

3. Call Risk

Many preferred shares are callable, meaning the issuing company can redeem the shares before their maturity date. If interest rates decline, the company may call back its preferred shares to reissue them at a lower rate. This can limit the upside potential for investors, as they might be forced to reinvest at a lower yield.

4. Lack of Voting Rights

Preferred shareholders typically do not have voting rights in the company. This means that while they receive dividends and have a priority claim on assets in the event of liquidation, they do not have any say in the management or direction of the company. For some investors, this lack of control may be a drawback.

5. Limited Capital Gains Potential

Although preferred shares can provide a reliable income stream, they generally offer less potential for capital gains than common stocks. Preferred shares are designed primarily for income, and their price appreciation potential is typically limited to the performance of the issuing company and broader market conditions.

How to Invest in Preferred Shares

1. Direct Purchase of Preferred Shares

One way to invest in preferred shares is by purchasing them directly from the issuing company or through the secondary market. This method allows investors to handpick specific preferred shares based on their dividend yields, credit ratings, and other factors. However, direct investment in individual preferred shares requires knowledge of the company’s financial health and the ability to assess the potential risks involved.

2. Preferred Share ETFs and Mutual Funds

For those who prefer diversification and professional management, preferred share exchange-traded funds (ETFs) and mutual funds provide a simple way to gain exposure to a broad range of preferred shares. These funds pool investors’ money to buy a diverse set of preferred shares, offering exposure to different companies, sectors, and types of preferred stock. This approach is ideal for those who prefer a hands-off investment strategy or who may not have the time or expertise to analyze individual securities.

3. Investment Through Brokerage Accounts

Preferred shares can typically be bought through brokerage accounts, both online and through traditional financial institutions. Investors should ensure that they have a brokerage account that supports the purchase of preferred shares and is familiar with the mechanics of the market. Many brokers offer commission-free trading on ETFs and stocks, including preferred shares, making it more accessible to retail investors.

Conclusion

Investing in preferred shares can be a compelling strategy for generating reliable, predictable income. With their fixed dividends, priority in bankruptcy, and potential for capital appreciation, preferred shares offer a unique combination of stability and growth potential. However, investors should be aware of the risks involved, including interest rate sensitivity, credit risk, and call risk, and should carefully evaluate their investment options before diving in.

For those seeking a reliable income stream or looking to diversify their investment portfolio, preferred shares can be an excellent choice. Whether through direct investments or preferred share ETFs, these securities offer a unique opportunity to balance risk and return in a well-constructed portfolio. Understanding how preferred shares work and the potential benefits they provide can help investors make informed decisions that align with their financial goals.
