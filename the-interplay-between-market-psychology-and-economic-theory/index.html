The Interplay Between Market Psychology and Economic Theory


The world of finance is often viewed as a battlefield where rational and irrational forces clash in an eternal struggle. On one side, we have the stalwarts of economic theory, armed with their cases full of historical data and statistical models. On the other, we have the market shoeshine boys and girls, armed with emotional intelligence and an uncanny ability to read the mood of the masses. At the heart of this tug-of-war lies the intersection of market psychology and economic theory. While economic theory seeks to explain the underlying mechanisms of human behavior in financial markets, market psychology delves into the messy world of emotions and human intuition. The two cannot be separated, as market participants draw upon a rich tapestry of emotions, biases, and predilections when making investment decisions. From Sentiment to Systematics The role of market psychology is often dismissed as a fleeting aberration, an intrusion of emotions that muddles the clear and rational waters of economic theory. However, it is this willful misunderstanding that illuminated the grand mistakes of past financial colloquies. Witness the trouncing of respected models in the 1929 stock market crash, 1987′s crash, and even the recent carnage of 2008.
