How to Trade with Trend Following in the Options Market


Trend following is a time-honored trading approach: spot a directional move in a market and ride it as long as the trend persists. While trend following has deep roots in the world of stocks and futures, applying this strategy in the options market introduces a layer of complexity—and opportunity. Options offer unique risk profiles, dynamic leverage, and the ability to profit from both rising and falling markets. But integrating trend-following principles with options trading requires a specific set of tools, disciplined risk management, and an appreciation for volatility’s double-edged sword.

Below, let’s dive into how trend following works in the options universe, from theory through practical implementation, including trade selection, risk management, and real-world tips to stack the odds in your favor.

Understanding Trend Following

Classic trend following leans on a simple maxim: “The trend is your friend until it ends.” Technical traders use indicators, momentum studies, and price action analysis to determine whether an asset is trending up (bullish), down (bearish), or sideways (range-bound).

Trend following seeks to:

  • Identify existing, sustained moves in price.
  • Enter positions in the direction of the prevailing trend.
  • Ride the trend as long as it persists.
  • Exit when clear signs of trend reversal or exhaustion appear.

The power of trend following is that it can capture large directional moves and, when done right, can be more forgiving of imperfect timing than mean-reversion or counter-trend trading.

When it comes to options, trend following benefits traders by providing directional edge, and combined with the right options strategy, it allows for fine-tuned risk management.

Why Trade Options with a Trend Following Approach?

Most options traders focus on short-term speculation or hedging, but trend following creates an edge grounded in the market’s most persistent feature—momentum. There are clear benefits to combining the two:

  • Leverage: Options enable greater exposure to trend moves with less capital outlay.
  • Defined risk: By buying options, losses are capped at the premium paid.
  • Profit potential: Calls or puts can multiply in value during strong trends.
  • Flexibility: You can structure positions to benefit not only from direction but also from volatility and time decay.
  • Adaptability: Both bull and bear trends present opportunities via call and put options.

However, options traders must be aware of unique risks, especially time decay (theta), changes in volatility (vega), and the possibly unforgiving nature of moves against the trend.

Building a Trend Following Toolkit for Options

Trading options with a trend following plan isn’t as simple as buying a call every time a stock ticks higher. The toolkit consists of three vital components:

1. Trend Identification

Before entering any options trade, you need a robust method for identifying trends.

  • Moving Averages: Use short-, medium-, and long-term moving averages (e.g., 21, 50, 100, and 200-day) to gauge trend direction and strength.
  • Price Structure: Look for higher highs and higher lows in uptrends, lower highs and lower lows in downtrends.
  • Momentum Indicators: RSI, MACD, and other momentum tools help confirm that a trend exists and has sufficient strength.
  • Volume Analysis: Expanding volume on moves in the trend direction signals conviction.

For intermediate-term trend following, daily and weekly charts are favored. Swing traders may opt for 4-hour or daily charts, while position traders might rely on weekly timeframes.

2. Trade Selection and Options Strategies

Selecting the right options strategy is crucial because options have many moving parts. The optimal choice depends on the trader’s risk tolerance, account size, conviction about trend strength, and market conditions.

Bullish Trends

  • Long Calls: Pure directional play; profit from sustained upward moves. Out-of-the-money (OTM) calls offer high leverage, while in-the-money (ITM) calls provide higher delta and lower theta decay.
  • Call Verticals (Bull Call Spreads): Buying a call and selling a higher strike call reduces cost and theta exposure, while capping upside.
  • Call Diagonals/Calendars: Buy a long-dated ITM call and sell a shorter-term OTM call against it to capture income while retaining trend exposure.
  • Put Credit Spreads: Collect premium by selling puts below the trend, betting that the uptrend will hold above a certain price.

Bearish Trends

  • Long Puts: Directional play on a downtrend; profit from sharp price slides. ITM puts move more closely with the underlying.
  • Put Verticals (Bear Put Spreads): Buying a put and selling a lower-strike put reduces premium outlay, which is critical during high-volatility periods.
  • Put Diagonals/Calendars: Similar construction as call versions for downward-trending stocks.
  • Call Credit Spreads: Sell OTM calls above resistance or trend lines in persistent downtrends.

Neutral or Weak Trends

  • Iron Condors and Butterflies: When trend momentum fades, switch to strategies that profit from consolidation.

Selecting which strategy to use depends on volatility, time horizon, and how far you expect the trend to travel.

3. Risk Management and Position Sizing

Options trading, even with the wind of the trend at your back, requires strict risk management.

  • Maximum Risk: Define the maximum acceptable loss per trade, often 1–2% of portfolio capital.
  • Position Sizing: Adjust position size to reflect risk, volatility, and distance to your stop.
  • Stop Losses for Options: Since options decay over time, use price stops on the underlying rather than waiting for the option to expire worthless.
  • Profit Targets: Exit whole or partial positions into strength; bank profits as trend reaches measured objectives or key resistance/support.

Many successful trend followers stagger exits—taking some profits as price surges and letting a “runner” ride as far as possible via trailing stops.

Step-by-Step Guide: Executing Trend Following Options Trades

Applying the above principles, here’s a “real-world” trend-following process, broken into digestible steps:

Step 1: Scan for Trending Stocks

Screen for assets demonstrating:

  • Sustained higher highs (uptrend) or lower lows (downtrend).
  • Price above/below key moving averages.
  • Strong volume on trend-confirming moves.
  • Confirm with sector and market context.

Step 2: Confirm the Trend

Dig deeper with:

  • Momentum oscillators (RSI > 60 or < 40, MACD slope).
  • Breakouts from consolidations or continuation patterns (flags, pennants).
  • Relative strength versus the market or sector.

Step 3: Analyze Options Chains

Look for:

  • Sufficient liquidity (tight bid-ask spreads, robust volume, and open interest).
  • Reasonable implied volatility—lower premium for buyers in uptrends, higher for sellers in range-bound phases.
  • Favorable risk-reward profiles.

Step 4: Choose Your Strategy

Based on:

  • Conviction in trend (long vs. spread).
  • Risk appetite (outright buys for aggressive, spreads for conservative).
  • Timeframe (short-dated for quick moves, long-dated for bigger swings).
  • Volatility environment (neutral/cheap options for buying, rich premiums for selling).

Step 5: Plan Entry, Stops, and Exits

  • Trigger entries on breakout or pullback setups.
  • Place stop losses based on key technical levels.
  • Set profit targets using resistance/support or implied move objectives.

Step 6: Execute and Manage

  • Enter trades with limit orders to control slippage.
  • Monitor delta (trend exposure), theta (decay), gamma (rate of P/L change), and vega (volatility sensitivity).
  • Roll positions to protect profits or extend exposure if trend persists.
  • Take partial profits as predefined targets hit.

Practical Insights: The Nuances of Options Trend Following

Let’s explore a few subtleties options trend followers need to internalize:

Dealing with Time Decay

Trend following means holding positions for days or weeks. The passage of time erodes an option’s value (theta). This is why in-the-money options (higher delta, lower theta) or calendar spreads (which offset decay) can be advantageous during sustained trends.

Volatility’s Double Edge

Implied volatility (IV) surges during breakouts or strong trends. Buying options when IV is low and selling when it’s high helps edge the odds. In roaring uptrends or panicked selloffs, vertical spreads may offset inflated premiums.

The Art of the Roll

Staying with the trend sometimes demands rolling positions—closing soon-to-expire options and opening fresh contracts further out in time or further down the strike chain—to lock in gains and maintain exposure as the trend matures.

Trade Less, Win More

Chasing every wiggle or minor swing dilutes the power of trend following. Focus on high-conviction setups, stick with your plan, and avoid overtrading.

Advanced Strategies for Seasoned Trend Followers

Once you’ve built up some experience and confidence, consider integrating these tactics:

Leveraging Synthetic Positions

A synthetic long (buying a call and selling a put at the same strike) mimics holding the underlying, enhancing exposure to trends for a fraction of the capital.

Ratio Spreads in Accelerating Trends

Ratio spreads—buying 1 call and selling 2 higher-strike calls—can generate profits in parabolic trends. Beware of excessive risk if the underlying overshoots your short strike.

Using LEAPS for Long-Duration Trends

Long-dated options (LEAPS) are powerful in major bull or bear markets. They offer significant trend exposure and low theta decay, suitable for patient traders targeting multi-month swings.

Real-World Example: Trend Following with Options

Suppose you spot a tech stock breaking out above long-term resistance, with all key moving averages stacked bullishly and solid volume behind the move.

Trade Setup:

  • Enter near-the-money call option, expiring in 2–3 months.
  • Stop loss if the stock closes below the breakout level.
  • Scale out: sell half if price hits next resistance, let the remainder ride with a trailing stop.
  • Alternatively, enter a long call vertical to reduce net cost if IV is elevated.

You monitor the position as the trend matures, ready to roll or close as profit targets are reached or momentum flags.

Result: Capturing the meat of the trend, while strictly limiting losses if the breakout fails.

Psychological Edge: Staying the Course with Trends

Perhaps the biggest challenge in trend following—especially with options—is holding onto winners. Human tendency is to grab quick profits and avoid pain. But the seminal profits are in sticking around for the body of the move.

The more you can automate entries and exits via rules, the less you’ll be plagued by hesitation or regret. Options’ defined risk structure helps, as you know upfront what you can lose and can leave the position alone to do its job.

Common Pitfalls (And How to Avoid Them)

Even savvy traders stumble when trend following with options. Here are the classics:

  • Overpaying for premium: Buying options with rich implied volatility eats up gains. Favor debit spreads or wait for IV to contract.
  • Forgetting the trend’s time frame: Options expiration and the trend’s duration must match. Don’t buy short-dated gamma for a move that may take weeks.
  • No exit plan: The trend seldom ends with a polite reversal—watch for exhaustion signals, major market news, or technical breakouts.
  • Ignoring position sizing: Leverage cuts both ways. Size positions so a streak of losses won’t wipe out your account.

Wrapping Up: Embracing Trend Following in Options

Trading with a trend-following focus in the options market requires synthesis of sound technical analysis, mastery of options strategies, and ironclad risk discipline. The appeal is clear: enormous asymmetry as trends unfold, using well-structured positions with defined risk.

The process combines methodical research, thoughtful trade structuring, and dogged psychological stamina. When these come together—backed by a robust trading plan—you put yourself in the select company of traders who let the market’s natural momentum do much of the heavy lifting.

Whether you’re buying calls in a runaway bull or profiting from puts in a spiraling bear, trend following breathes new potential into the options trader’s arsenal. Use the tools, mind the risks, and let big waves work for you. Markets always change, but trends—and the opportunities they present—remain a constant source of trading excitement and potential.

So the next time you pull up your trading platform, remember: spot the trend, pick your options play, and let the market run. You might be surprised how far a strong tailwind takes you.
