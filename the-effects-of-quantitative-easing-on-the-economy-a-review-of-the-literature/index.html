The Effects of Quantitative Easing on the Economy: A Review of the Literature


Quantitative Easing (QE) has become a prominent tool for central banks in addressing economic downturns and stabilizing financial systems. In response to crises, central banks, especially those of advanced economies like the Federal Reserve, the European Central Bank, and the Bank of Japan, have implemented QE to stimulate growth and prevent deflation. But what are the long-term effects of QE on the economy? This post aims to review the literature surrounding the effects of QE, focusing on both its intended and unintended consequences.

Understanding Quantitative Easing

At its core, quantitative easing is a non-traditional monetary policy tool used by central banks when short-term interest rates are already near zero and cannot be lowered further. It involves the central bank purchasing long-term securities, such as government bonds or mortgage-backed securities, to inject liquidity into the financial system. The goal is to lower long-term interest rates, encourage lending, boost asset prices, and stimulate economic activity.

While QE has been used in various forms across different countries, its main premise remains the same: to provide stimulus to an economy that is struggling with low growth, high unemployment, and low inflation.

The Transmission Mechanisms of QE

Before diving into the effects of QE, it is essential to understand how it works through the economy. Several transmission mechanisms explain the path through which QE influences economic variables.

  1. Asset Prices and Wealth Effect: By purchasing financial assets, central banks increase the demand for these securities, thereby raising their prices. This leads to a lower yield (interest rate), which makes borrowing cheaper and encourages investment. Additionally, the increase in asset prices can boost household wealth, leading to higher consumer spending.

  2. Interest Rates: QE directly lowers long-term interest rates by buying long-term government bonds and other securities. This reduction in long-term borrowing costs incentivizes businesses and consumers to take loans for investment and consumption, thus stimulating economic activity.

  3. Bank Lending: With an abundance of reserves in the banking system, banks have more capital to lend to businesses and consumers. The expectation is that the increased liquidity in the financial system will make it easier for banks to extend credit, leading to higher investment and consumption.

  4. Currency Depreciation: QE can also lead to a depreciation of the domestic currency. As the central bank buys large amounts of assets, it increases the supply of its currency in the global market, which may reduce the value of the currency relative to others. A weaker currency makes exports cheaper and more attractive to foreign buyers, potentially boosting the export sector.

  5. Expectations: Finally, QE can influence expectations about future economic conditions. If businesses and consumers believe that the central bank is committed to economic stimulus and will act to prevent deflation, it can lead to increased confidence, higher spending, and investment.

Short-Term Economic Effects of QE

Boosting Economic Growth

One of the primary reasons for implementing QE is to stimulate economic growth during times of stagnation or recession. The idea is that by lowering interest rates, increasing asset prices, and encouraging lending, QE can help spur economic activity. Several studies have suggested that QE has a positive short-term effect on GDP growth. For instance, research conducted by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York found that QE could add between 1% and 2% to GDP in the years following its implementation.

In Japan, where QE has been a policy tool since the early 2000s, the Bank of Japan has argued that its QE programs have supported economic growth during periods of stagnation. Similarly, the Federal Reserve’s QE programs after the 2008 financial crisis played a crucial role in stabilizing the U.S. economy, leading to a recovery in GDP growth and employment levels.

Lowering Unemployment

Another key objective of QE is to lower unemployment. By stimulating economic growth and increasing investment, QE can indirectly lead to job creation. The increased demand for goods and services resulting from lower interest rates and higher asset prices can encourage firms to expand their operations and hire more workers.

Research by the Bank of England suggests that QE has been associated with a reduction in unemployment rates in the UK, though the effects are often gradual and depend on the broader economic context. However, it is worth noting that the effects of QE on employment are not always uniform across different sectors of the economy.

Fighting Deflation

During periods of low inflation or deflation, central banks implement QE as a means to boost price levels. Deflation can be harmful because it increases the real burden of debt and can lead to a vicious cycle of reduced spending and investment. QE aims to push up inflation expectations and prevent deflation from becoming entrenched.

The European Central Bank (ECB) faced deflationary pressures in the Eurozone following the financial crisis and implemented QE to prevent falling into a deflationary trap. While inflation did not immediately rise to target levels, the ECB’s QE programs are credited with stabilizing the Eurozone and preventing a prolonged deflationary period.

Long-Term Economic Effects of QE

While the short-term benefits of QE are more apparent, its long-term effects are more complex and less certain. Some of the potential long-term impacts of QE on the economy include the following:

Income and Wealth Inequality

One of the most discussed side effects of QE is its potential to exacerbate income and wealth inequality. By boosting the prices of financial assets such as stocks, bonds, and real estate, QE disproportionately benefits wealthier individuals who hold significant assets. Lower-income households, who typically hold fewer financial assets, do not directly benefit from rising asset prices. This can lead to a widening gap between the rich and poor.

Several studies, including those by the Bank of England and the Federal Reserve, suggest that QE has contributed to increasing wealth inequality. While QE has raised asset prices, the benefits have been skewed towards those who are already wealthy, as they have the means to invest in these assets. This inequality could have long-term social and economic consequences, particularly in terms of consumer spending and political stability.

Risk-Taking and Financial Instability

Another potential concern with QE is that it encourages excessive risk-taking in financial markets. As central banks purchase a significant amount of financial assets, the prices of these assets rise, and the yields fall. Investors, in search of higher returns, may begin to take on more risk by investing in assets with higher yields but also higher risk, such as junk bonds or equities.

This search for yield can lead to financial market distortions, bubbles, and increased instability. The International Monetary Fund (IMF) has warned that the prolonged use of QE could lead to an overvaluation of certain assets and increase financial market volatility. In the long run, if these asset bubbles burst, the economy could face significant negative repercussions.

Impact on Pension Funds and Savers

Another often-overlooked consequence of QE is its impact on pension funds and savers. The low interest rates resulting from QE reduce the returns on safer assets like government bonds, which are typically favored by pension funds and individuals saving for retirement. As a result, pension funds may find it harder to meet their long-term obligations, potentially leading to higher pension deficits or increased contributions from workers and employers.

For individual savers, the low-interest-rate environment caused by QE may discourage saving and make it more difficult to generate returns on savings accounts, certificates of deposit, and other low-risk investments. This could alter consumption patterns and affect long-term wealth accumulation for many households.

Distortion of Capital Allocation

Another criticism of QE is that it can distort the allocation of capital in the economy. By suppressing interest rates and inflating asset prices, QE can encourage investment in riskier and less productive ventures. Instead of encouraging investments that would increase the economy’s productive capacity, QE may incentivize speculative investments that offer quick returns but do not necessarily contribute to long-term growth.

The Economist has highlighted that QE can lead to a mispricing of risk and may encourage “zombie” companies to remain in business despite their inability to generate adequate returns. This distortion of capital allocation can reduce the overall efficiency of the economy and hinder long-term growth prospects.

Conclusion

The effects of quantitative easing on the economy are multi-faceted and depend on the broader economic context in which it is implemented. While QE has undoubtedly provided short-term relief to economies facing stagnation or recession, its long-term effects are more ambiguous and raise concerns about income inequality, financial stability, and capital misallocation.

There is no one-size-fits-all answer when it comes to the effectiveness of QE. While it has helped central banks stabilize financial markets, boost asset prices, and support economic recovery, its unintended side effects require careful consideration. As economies continue to navigate challenges such as low interest rates and sluggish growth, understanding the full implications of QE will be crucial for policymakers to ensure that the benefits outweigh the costs.

In the end, the debate over QE is far from settled. The literature offers valuable insights into both its positive and negative consequences, and ongoing research will be critical to refining our understanding of this important monetary policy tool.
