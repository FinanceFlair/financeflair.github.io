How to Find High-Quality Trades for Position Trading


Position trading calls for patience, vision, and a systematic approach—no wild day-to-day moves or scalp-induced adrenaline here. For those with the temperament and strategy for weeks-to-months-long plays, the question becomes: among the storm of daily price action and news, how can you spot high-quality trades that offer both strong upside potential and manageable risk?

Let’s dig into the nuances, tools, and thought processes that experienced position traders use to filter the noise and zero in on reliable setups.

Understanding What Makes a Position Trade “High-Quality”

Not every chart with a sharp move is worth holding for weeks or months. The best position trades share certain characteristics:

  • A well-defined trend or range breakout
  • A strong fundamental catalyst (earnings, sector rotation, macro momentum)
  • A favorable risk/reward profile with natural, logical stops
  • Decent liquidity so you’re not stuck in a backwater stock
  • Clear technical confluence: several signals aligning, not just one

Let’s break down the actionable process that will help you consistently uncover these setups.

1. Scan Broad Markets Efficiently

Before diving into single names, begin with the broader markets. If major indices are stuck in a sideways churn or topping out, most position trades will be fighting against difficult macro tides.

Sector and Industry Trends

Focus your initial scan on sectors and industries outperforming major indices over several weeks or months. Tools like sector ETFs or industry indexes show you where the institutional money’s flowing, and where strong trends are emerging.

  • Pull a 6-to-12-month performance chart for sectors and industries
  • Note which have recently broken out, or consistently outperformed
  • Within those, dive deeper into specific stocks or ETFs

2. Fundamental Screens to Narrow the Field

Solid fundamentals reinforce the case for a longer-term trade. You’re not hunting a quick pop; you want to ride multi-week or multi-month moves, often driven by real business changes.

Screen for fundamentals such as:

  • Consistent revenue and earnings growth
  • Expanding profit margins
  • Healthy balance sheets
  • Analyst upgrades or positive estimate revisions

Web-based screeners can crunch these metrics fast. Combine a sector/industry filter from Step 1 with basic value factors (P/E, PEG, ROE), or growth metrics, to shrink your focus to a manageable watchlist.

3. Technical Analysis for Entry Timing

Markets are never perfectly rational; technical analysis lets you spot when sentiment matches the underlying story.

Trends, Breakouts, and Reversals

  • Classic Position Entry: Seek out clear uptrends with higher highs and higher lows on the daily and weekly chart
  • Pullbacks Within Uptrends: Alternatively, look for well-behaved retracements to moving averages (50DMA or 200DMA) with volume declines on the pullback and pick-up on the bounce
  • Breakouts from Bases: Long consolidations followed by a price busting through resistance—with volume—set up explosive moves.

Chart scanning software is your friend. Filter charts for:

  • New 52-week highs/lows
  • Strong relative strength over the past 3-6 months
  • Classic chart patterns (flags, cups-with-handle, etc.)

Volume and Price Action

Look for:

  • Volume clustering near breakout points
  • Reduced volatility before breakouts (a sign of accumulation)
  • “Accumulation” bars: wide range strong closes on higher volume

4. Risk and Reward: Define Your Game Plan

A high-quality trade is never just about upside. You need a clear plan to manage risk and lock in gains.

Position trading shines when:

  • The logical stop-loss is well below support (or above resistance, if shorting) and the potential reward versus risk is at least 2:1, preferably more.
  • Position sizing reflects the wider stop-loss typical in swing or position trading—don’t go oversized!

Train yourself to identify natural chart-based levels: swing lows, major moving averages, breakout points. These become your guide for stop placement. Project targets using prior swing highs, Fibonacci extensions, or longer-term resistance.

5. News and Catalysts: Add an Edge

The market has a memory for certain events—earnings beats, product launches, industry-wide tailwinds or government policy changes.

  • Filter your final watchlist for stocks with upcoming catalysts or those that have recently surprised to the upside.
  • Keep an eye on news flow, management commentary, and sector trends that can accelerate your trade’s momentum.

Remember: in position trading, you’re not just trading charts. The bigger story can sustain a move even when day traders lose interest.

6. Liquidity and Slippage

  • Avoid thinly traded stocks where wide bid/ask spreads and low liquidity can eat impact your real returns. Ideally, you want average daily volume well above 500,000 shares.
  • Position traders sometimes build positions over days or weeks to avoid moving the price—especially in smaller names.

7. Review Historical Performance

While past performance is no guarantee, you can glean a lot about a stock’s tendency to trend or chop by studying its history. Is the name prone to sharp reversals? How did it handle previous breakouts or breakdowns? Has it maintained rallies following key catalysts in the past?

Journaling your trades, reviewing the “near misses” and the big winners, will sharpen your pattern recognition and help you improve over time.

8. Combining Multiple Factors: The “Confluence” Approach

No single indicator or news event makes a great position trade on its own. Layering multiple factors boosts your confidence and success rate.

For example, a top position trade might feature:

  • Breakout from a six-month consolidation base
  • Rising earnings estimates
  • Clear sector tailwinds
  • Big volume confirmation
  • Tight logical stop placement and a favorable risk/reward

If several elements line up, you’ve probably struck gold.

9. The Psychological Edge

It’s not just the trade setup; it’s sticking with your thesis. Position trading requires emotional resilience, because many of the best setups mean sitting tight through short-term noise.

Prepare yourself mentally:

  • You will watch some profits evaporate before the move resumes.
  • The big wins often feel uncomfortable at first. If your process is robust, trust it!

10. Execution & Monitoring

Once the trade is on, don’t just set and forget. Monitor price action every few days:

  • Adjust your stop-loss to breakeven (or better) as the trade goes your way.
  • Watch for signs of notable distribution (high-volume selling, topping tails, breakdowns on volume).
  • Consider scaling out partial profits into strength, especially if the move becomes extended.

Bonus: Case Study—Putting it All Together

Let’s say a sector ETF for semiconductor stocks ($SMH) has blasted past its six-month highs, with broad strength in chipmakers following an industry-wide demand surge. You run a screener for semis showing above-average earnings growth. One name, for example, Nvidia, is building a new base just above its rising 200DMA after blowout earnings.

  • Volume uptick on up-days
  • Analysts are hiking estimates and price targets
  • No major resistance seen for 15%
  • Logical stop just under the bottom of the base
  • Risk/Reward? 20% upside, 7% risk

That’s a textbook position trade candidate.

Building and Updating Your Watchlist

Strong position traders are constantly updating their watchlists. Sectors change, leadership rotates, and new catalysts emerge. Set aside weekly time to:

  • Refresh your screens and re-rank your ideas
  • Scan charts for evolving bases or new breakouts
  • Read earnings reports, analyst commentary, and macro news

Pitfalls to Avoid

Don’t:

  • Chase extended moves after massive runs
  • Ignore liquidity (tight spreads matter)
  • Rely on a single indicator or opinion
  • Hold stubbornly after a clear break of trend—stick to your plan
  • Overtrade; patience for the right setups pays off

Resources for Ongoing Improvement

If you want to take your position trading game to the next level:

  • Use professional charting platforms that allow custom screens for technical and fundamental conditions
  • Track economic and company news with customizable alerts
  • Study successful position trades (and failures) in your journal
  • Stay plugged into the broader markets; sector money flow shifts often

Concluding Thoughts

High-quality trades for position trading don’t appear from thin air. They’re the result of disciplined screening, detailed analysis, and the willingness to act when everything lines up. By blending top-down sector reviews, razor-sharp fundamental and technical screening, solid risk management, and a big-picture view, you’ll greatly increase your odds of catching—and holding—those meaningful, multi-week winners.

Embrace the process: Scan. Analyze. Plan. Execute. Review.

That’s how you go from chasing noise to riding real trends.
