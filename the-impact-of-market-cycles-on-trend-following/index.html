The Impact of Market Cycles on Trend Following


Trend following has long been a staple strategy in both professional and retail trading circles. While the core concept is simple—identify and follow sustained price movements—its effectiveness is anything but static. In reality, the broader market cycles play a profound role in shaping the success and challenges of trend-following systems. Understanding this relationship is vital for traders and investors seeking to optimize their returns and sidestep common pitfalls.

Defining Market Cycles and Trend Following

Before delving into the nuanced interplay, it’s helpful to clarify our key terms.

Market cycles refer to the recurring patterns or phases that financial markets experience over time. These can be boom and bust periods, bullish and bearish swings, or more granular shifts like periods of high volatility followed by quiet consolidation. While the exact nature and duration of cycles can vary, the presence of cyclical behavior is widely accepted in financial theory and practice.

Trend following, meanwhile, is a strategy rooted in technical analysis. Traders aim to capitalize on persistent price directions—either upward or downward—by entering trades in the direction of the established trend and exiting when signs of a reversal appear. Its enduring appeal lies in its straightforward logic and the promise of capturing major market moves.

With these definitions in place, we can start to examine how market cycles—in all their forms—affect the performance and psychology of trend-following approaches.

The Four Phases of Market Cycles

Nearly every market, from commodities to equities, tends to move through a cycle with four broad phases:

  1. Accumulation: Following downturns, contrarian investors begin buying, though the overall sentiment remains bearish. Prices stabilize and volume is subdued.
  2. Markup: Optimism returns, and the majority recognize the emerging uptrend. Prices rise rapidly, often attracting trend followers.
  3. Distribution: After a sustained climb, the market peaks. Smart money quietly sells into elevated demand while prices move sideways or see volatile swings.
  4. Markdown: As the trend reverses, bearish sentiment takes hold. Prices fall, sometimes precipitously, until capitulation signals a potential bottom.

Trend-following strategies tend to thrive in strongly directional phases (markup and markdown) but struggle during accumulation and distribution, where whipsaws and choppiness increase.

Why Trend Following Excels in Certain Cycles

During markup and markdown phases, price action delivers the very conditions trend followers seek: sustained movement in one direction. In such cycles:

  • Momentum is self-reinforcing: Positive feedback loops, often driven by crowd psychology, fundamentals, or macro catalysts, underpin strong trends.
  • Technical tools are reliable: Moving averages, breakout systems, and momentum oscillators provide clear signals, giving trend followers a quantitative edge.
  • Volatility works in favor: As prices accelerate, trend followers capture large portions of the move rather than being whittled down by constant reversals.

Some of the most famous trend-following funds and legendary traders—think Richard Dennis and the original Turtle Traders—built their reputations during protracted bull markets or commodity super-cycles. Their results reinforce the notion that robust market trends unlock outsized returns for those able to identify and ride them.

The Perils of Non-Trending Cycles for Trend Followers

Market cycles are rarely composed solely of clean, directional segments. Sideways corrections, choppy advancements, and false breakouts all challenge the assumptions underpinning trend-following systems.

During accumulation and distribution phases:

  • False signals proliferate: Price may repeatedly threaten to break out in one direction, only to reverse, leading to multiple small losses.
  • Drawdowns accumulate: The absence of sustained trends means many trades begin with promise but fizzle out, eroding capital and confidence.
  • Systemic whipsawing: Automated strategies especially are vulnerable to frequent, rapid reversals where stop-losses repeatedly trigger.

For example, a classic moving average crossover system may endure dozens of losing trades in a flat market while waiting for a genuine trend to emerge. During prolonged periods of range-bound action, trend followers must either tighten their filters to avoid whipsaw or accept frequent small losses.

Adaptive Trend Following: Navigating the Full Cycle

To remain viable across diverse market backdrops, experienced trend followers often adapt their strategies to the prevailing cycle. Key adaptations include:

1. Dynamic Position Sizing

Rather than adopting a fixed leverage or notional exposure, traders may use volatility-based position sizing. During low-volatility or sideways periods, positions shrink, limiting drawdowns from false signals. When trending price action returns, larger positions capitalize on renewed opportunities.

2. Regime Detection Filters

Incorporating filters to gauge the underlying market regime can help sidestep flat or volatile environments. For example:

  • Volatility measures (ATR, VIX) to detect when the market is too quiet or wild.
  • Trend strength indicators (ADX, R-squared) to confirm genuine momentum.
  • Volume analysis to avoid breakouts lacking confirmation.

When such filters identify non-trending conditions, systems can temporarily reduce trading frequency or switch to mean-reversion strategies.

3. Multiple Timeframe Confirmation

Overlaying trend signals from several timeframes increases the reliability of entries. When daily, weekly, and monthly charts align in the same direction, traders enjoy greater confidence that a real trend is underway—and can often avoid the noise inherent in shorter cycles.

4. Hybrid Strategies

Some traders combine trend following with alternative approaches, such as value investing or mean reversion. These multi-strategy frameworks smooth returns during adverse cycles and exploit a wider range of market behavior.

Psychological Implications of Cycle-Driven Performance

Even the most robust system is only as strong as its practitioner’s conviction through lean times. The variability of trend-following performance over market cycles presents unique psychological challenges:

  • Drawdowns can be prolonged: It’s not uncommon for trend followers to endure months or years of subpar results during non-trending cycles. This tests both discipline and faith in the system.
  • Trend blindness: After long periods without meaningful trends, traders may become gun-shy and fail to act decisively when trends do return—a classic behavioral bias.
  • Chasing hot strategies: When trend following underperforms, the allure of chasing alternative, recently successful strategies grows. This can result in abandoning systems just before they revive in the next trending phase.

Understanding the cyclical nature of markets, with their alternating stretches of trending and non-trending behavior, is critical for managing expectations and maintaining adherence to a proven process.

Historical Case Studies: Trend Following Through Market Cycles

Looking at actual markets over time illustrates the cyclical fortunes of trend-following systems:

The Commodity Supercycles

Throughout the 1970s, commodity trends persisted for years—think oil, gold, and agricultural futures. Trend-following strategies delivered windfall gains as relentless market momentum created a favorable backdrop.

Contrast this with the 1980s and 1990s, where prolonged periods of range-bound action and unexpected reversals (sometimes due to geopolitical intervention) led to a string of challenges. Many funds shuttered or adapted as prolonged whipsaw eroded returns.

Equities in the 21st Century

After the bursting of the dot-com bubble, a new bull market took hold, benefiting trend followers until the 2008 financial crisis. The rapid, deep markdown of 2008-2009 offered lucrative opportunities for systematic short-selling.

Rise back to the 2010s: Central bank intervention and quantitative easing dampened volatility, leading to stretches of unbroken equity uptrends as well as quiescent periods, both of which influenced trend-following profitability.

The Post-2020 Landscape

COVID-induced market volatility brought backswings and sharp trends, providing fodder for adaptive trend-following models. Yet, as cycles have compressed and headlines move markets in unpredictable ways, the need for logistical flexibility has only grown.

The Role of Diversification in Countering Cycle Risk

No single market or asset class trends indefinitely. Consequently, trend followers often diversify across:

  • Geographies: US, Europe, Asia, emerging markets
  • Asset Classes: Equities, commodities, currencies, bonds, crypto
  • Systems: Multiple, uncorrelated trading algorithms

Diversification smooths overall returns, as different assets trend at different times. For instance, while equities might languish in a range, commodities or forex markets may produce strong moves. This approach mitigates the single-cycle risk and sustains portfolio returns even when specific segments stagnate.

Cycles and the Longevity of Trend Following

If there’s a lesson in the historical record, it’s that trend following is not a “set and forget” endeavor but one that requires a keen awareness of the ever-changing market landscape. Success hinges on the ability to:

  • Distinguish trending phases from choppy ones
  • Adapt position sizing, filters, and even core methodology as market cycles evolve
  • Manage psychological stresses unique to the ebb and flow of trend-following results

While it may seem alluringly simple, real-world trend following is an intricate dance with market cycles, requiring both flexibility and discipline.

Embracing Cyclical Variation: A Closing Perspective

Market cycles are as integral to trading as tides are to the sea. For the trend follower, resisting the temptation to believe in endless trending or permanent stagnation is key. By recognizing the impact of cycles, adapting methods accordingly, and diversifying exposures, traders can position themselves to not only survive but thrive over the long run.

The impact of market cycles on trend following cannot be overstated. It is the crucible in which techniques are tested, refined, and sometimes discarded. Those who navigate this relationship skillfully will always have a fighting chance—no matter what cycle the market has in store next.
