Risk vs Reward: How to Calculate Your Swing Trading Profits


Swing trading is one of the most popular trading strategies in the financial markets. It involves capturing short- to medium-term gains by holding assets for a period of days or weeks. As with any trading strategy, understanding the balance between risk and reward is crucial for long-term success. The goal is not to take excessive risks, but to position oneself in a way that maximizes potential returns while minimizing losses.

This post dives into the critical concept of risk vs reward, explaining how to calculate it and how to apply it effectively to maximize swing trading profits.

Understanding Risk vs Reward in Swing Trading

Risk vs reward refers to the relationship between the potential loss (risk) and the potential gain (reward) from a trade. It is an essential principle to evaluate before entering any position. In swing trading, this relationship helps traders make informed decisions about whether to enter or exit a position and by how much to allocate their capital.

What is Risk?

Risk is the amount of capital you stand to lose if a trade does not go as planned. In swing trading, this loss is often calculated using stop losses or the maximum amount of a trade you’re willing to lose. The risk can be measured in dollar terms or percentage terms relative to your trading account balance.

For example, if you’re willing to risk $200 on a trade, and the current price of the asset is $50, your position size would need to be such that, if the price moves against you, the loss doesn’t exceed your maximum risk tolerance.

What is Reward?

Reward, on the other hand, is the potential profit you can earn if the trade goes in your favor. This is often calculated by looking at the target price of an asset based on your analysis and determining how much the price can potentially increase. In swing trading, this reward is typically captured over a period of days or weeks.

For instance, if you enter a trade at $50 and your target price is $60, your reward would be $10 per share. The reward should always be evaluated in conjunction with risk to ensure that the trade offers a favorable risk-to-reward ratio.

Why is the Risk vs Reward Ratio Important?

The risk-to-reward ratio helps traders assess whether the potential reward justifies the risk taken. A good ratio means that you are willing to risk a small amount of capital for the chance of making a much larger return.

Consider a swing trade with a risk-to-reward ratio of 1:3. This means that for every dollar you risk, you stand to make three dollars in profit. In contrast, a 1:1 ratio means you’re risking one dollar to make one dollar. The latter may be acceptable, but generally, traders aim for a higher ratio to optimize profits and improve their win rates.

How to Calculate Risk vs Reward for Swing Trades

To calculate the risk vs reward ratio for your swing trades, follow these steps:

Step 1: Determine Your Entry and Exit Points

The first step is to identify the entry point of your trade, where you plan to buy the asset, and your target exit point, where you aim to sell the asset. This can be based on technical analysis, such as support and resistance levels, or fundamental analysis, such as the value of a company or an asset in relation to its market.

Step 2: Set Your Stop Loss

A stop loss is a predetermined price point at which you will exit the trade if it moves against you. The stop loss should be set at a level where the asset’s price breakage indicates the trade idea is no longer valid. This helps prevent large losses that can occur if the market moves significantly in the wrong direction.

For example, if you buy a stock at $100 and set a stop loss at $95, your maximum loss per share would be $5.

Step 3: Identify Your Target Price

Your target price is where you expect the price of the asset to move in your favor. This is where you will take profits. Your target should be based on technical indicators like resistance levels or chart patterns that suggest a price movement.

For instance, if the stock is trading at $100 and your target price is $120, your reward per share would be $20.

Step 4: Calculate the Risk vs Reward Ratio

Once you have your entry, stop loss, and target price, you can calculate your risk vs reward ratio using this simple formula:

[ \text{Risk vs Reward Ratio} = \frac{\text{Target Price} - \text{Entry Price}}{\text{Entry Price} - \text{Stop Loss Price}} ]

Let’s look at an example:

  • Entry price: $100
  • Stop loss price: $95
  • Target price: $120

The calculation would be:

[ \text{Risk vs Reward Ratio} = \frac{120 - 100}{100 - 95} = \frac{20}{5} = 4 ]

This means for every dollar you risk, you stand to make four dollars in profit. This would be considered an excellent risk-to-reward ratio.

Step 5: Adjusting Position Size

Your position size is how many shares or contracts you will buy in a trade. It is crucial to calculate the proper position size to ensure you stay within your risk tolerance.

To calculate the position size, use the formula:

[ \text{Position Size} = \frac{\text{Risk Amount}}{\text{Risk per Share}} ]

For example, if you are willing to risk $500 on a trade and your risk per share is $5 (based on your stop loss), your position size would be:

[ \text{Position Size} = \frac{500}{5} = 100 \text{ shares} ]

This means you would buy 100 shares to risk $500 on this trade.

Best Practices for Swing Trading: Managing Risk vs Reward

Now that you understand how to calculate the risk vs reward ratio, let’s look at some best practices for managing your risk and reward effectively in swing trading.

1. Use a Favorable Risk-to-Reward Ratio

While the optimal risk-to-reward ratio can vary based on individual risk tolerance and trading style, many experienced traders aim for a minimum ratio of 1:2 or 1:3. This means that the potential reward should be at least double or triple the potential risk.

A favorable risk-to-reward ratio gives you the flexibility to have a lower win rate while still being profitable in the long run. For example, if you have a 1:2 risk-to-reward ratio and win 50% of your trades, you would still be making a profit overall.

2. Be Disciplined with Stop Losses

Stop losses are essential to controlling your risk. It’s tempting to widen your stop loss to give a trade more room to move, but doing so increases your risk and may lead to unnecessary losses. Stick to your predetermined stop loss and avoid adjusting it unless new information suggests otherwise.

3. Adjust Position Size According to Market Volatility

Market conditions can affect the risk you take on any given trade. In volatile markets, you may want to decrease your position size or widen your stop loss to account for price swings. Conversely, in stable markets, you may choose to increase your position size to maximize potential profits.

4. Review and Adjust Your Strategy Regularly

Your swing trading strategy should not remain static. Regularly review your trades to evaluate your risk vs reward ratio. Look for patterns in both your successful and unsuccessful trades to refine your approach. By continually improving your strategy, you’ll be better positioned to take advantage of the market.

5. Use Risk Management Tools

There are various tools available to help manage risk, such as trailing stops, which automatically adjust the stop loss to lock in profits as the price moves in your favor. Risk management tools can be particularly useful in swing trading to ensure that profits are protected if the market reverses.

Final Thoughts on Risk vs Reward in Swing Trading

Calculating risk vs reward is a fundamental aspect of swing trading, and understanding it thoroughly will help you make smarter trading decisions. The key is to evaluate each trade’s risk in relation to its potential reward and ensure that your trades align with your risk tolerance and overall trading strategy.

Remember, successful trading is not about winning every trade. It’s about managing risk effectively and taking calculated risks that offer a favorable reward. By doing so, you increase your chances of being consistently profitable over the long term.

Incorporating a disciplined approach to risk management, sticking to a favorable risk-to-reward ratio, and continuously refining your strategy will significantly improve your chances of success in the world of swing trading.
