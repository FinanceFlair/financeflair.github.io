Robo-Advisor Fees: What You Should Expect in 2023


The financial landscape has seen rapid changes over the past decade, and one of the most significant shifts has been the rise of robo-advisors. These automated investment platforms have become popular among individual investors, especially those looking for low-cost, easy-to-use alternatives to traditional financial advisors. Robo-advisors promise a more hands-off approach to investing, using algorithms and technology to manage portfolios based on a person’s financial goals and risk tolerance.

However, while robo-advisors offer several advantages, one factor that can be a bit tricky to navigate for new users is understanding the associated fees. Knowing what to expect when it comes to robo-advisor fees can significantly impact your investment returns. Let’s break down everything you need to know about these costs in 2023.

What Are Robo-Advisors?

Before diving into the fees, it’s essential to have a basic understanding of what robo-advisors are and how they work. Robo-advisors are digital platforms that use algorithms to create and manage a diversified portfolio based on a set of questions about your financial situation, goals, and risk tolerance. They typically use a mix of exchange-traded funds (ETFs) to build portfolios and perform regular rebalancing to ensure they stay aligned with your financial objectives.

Robo-advisors have become increasingly popular because they offer a way to invest without requiring the high fees and personal interactions associated with traditional financial advisors. The platforms are also often more accessible, with low minimum investment requirements, making them ideal for young investors or those just starting out in the world of investing.

Types of Fees Associated with Robo-Advisors

While robo-advisors are generally cheaper than traditional financial advisors, they still come with certain fees. Understanding these costs will help you make an informed decision about which platform to use and how much you can expect to pay.

1. Management Fees

The primary fee associated with any robo-advisor is the management fee, which is typically charged as an annual percentage of your assets under management (AUM). This fee compensates the robo-advisor for the services it provides, such as portfolio creation, asset allocation, and rebalancing.

Management fees for robo-advisors generally range from 0.25% to 0.50% annually. This is far lower than the 1% to 2% that traditional financial advisors typically charge, making robo-advisors an appealing option for investors looking to save on fees.

Some robo-advisors may offer tiered pricing, where the management fee decreases as your portfolio grows. For example, you might pay a higher fee if you have a smaller portfolio and a lower fee if your investment grows beyond a certain threshold.

2. Fund Fees (Expense Ratios)

In addition to the management fee, robo-advisors charge fund fees. These fees are levied by the mutual funds or ETFs that robo-advisors use to build and manage your portfolio. These funds charge an expense ratio, which is the cost of managing the fund. Typically, expense ratios range from 0.03% to 0.10% for the ETFs that robo-advisors use.

Though these fees may seem small, they can add up over time, especially if you’re investing a substantial amount of money. It’s essential to factor in these fees when calculating the total cost of using a robo-advisor.

For instance, if you invest $100,000 in a robo-advisor with a management fee of 0.25% and an average ETF expense ratio of 0.05%, your total annual fees would be $350. While this is still a relatively low cost compared to traditional financial advisors, it’s important to understand how each fee adds up to the overall expense.

3. Account Fees

Some robo-advisors charge additional account maintenance fees, though this is becoming less common. These fees are typically levied to cover the costs of maintaining your account, processing transactions, or providing customer support.

Account fees are usually charged as a flat annual fee, which can range from $25 to $50 or more. However, many robo-advisors have moved toward eliminating these fees or offering them for free on accounts above a certain balance. It’s important to carefully review the fee structure of each platform before committing to avoid unexpected charges.

4. Trading Fees

While most robo-advisors do not charge a fee for making trades within your portfolio, some platforms might charge a fee if you want to make a custom trade, such as buying a specific security outside of the recommended ETFs or funds. This fee, typically a flat charge per trade, can range from $0 to $10 or more, depending on the platform.

However, it’s worth noting that these trading fees are not usually a significant concern for most robo-advisor users. The primary focus of robo-advisors is on passive investing through ETFs, and you likely won’t need to make frequent trades to keep your portfolio in line with your goals.

5. Premium Services or Human Advisor Fees

While most robo-advisors operate without direct human interaction, some platforms offer premium services where you can access a human financial advisor for personalized guidance. These services may be available for an additional fee or as part of a higher-tier account. The cost for human advisor services can range from $100 to $300 per hour, depending on the advisor’s expertise and the platform’s pricing structure.

Additionally, some robo-advisors provide features like tax-loss harvesting, financial planning tools, and estate planning, which might carry additional costs. If you opt for these premium services, it’s important to fully understand the fees involved before committing.

How to Minimize Robo-Advisor Fees

While robo-advisors are already cost-effective alternatives to traditional financial advisors, there are still ways to minimize the fees you pay. Here are a few strategies:

1. Choose a Platform with Low Fees

The most straightforward way to minimize robo-advisor fees is to select a platform with low management fees. Some robo-advisors charge as little as 0.25% annually, which is a significant saving compared to traditional advisors. Do your research to find platforms with competitive fees while still offering the features and services that align with your financial goals.

2. Take Advantage of Fee Waivers

Many robo-advisors offer fee waivers or discounts for certain account sizes or for customers who meet specific criteria. For example, some platforms will waive account maintenance fees if you maintain a balance above a certain threshold or for new customers during their first year. Be sure to check if such discounts apply to you.

3. Consider the Expense Ratios of Funds

When choosing a robo-advisor, take the time to look into the expense ratios of the ETFs and funds the platform uses to build your portfolio. Funds with lower expense ratios can save you money in the long run. Though the expense ratio might seem small at first, it’s an ongoing cost that can add up over time, particularly if your portfolio grows.

4. Use Additional Services Sparingly

If a robo-advisor offers premium services like access to human advisors or specialized financial planning, use these services sparingly or only when absolutely necessary. These features can be valuable, but they come at a cost, and it’s essential to weigh the benefits against the fees.

Robo-Advisor Fees in 2023: Trends and Predictions

As we move into 2023, the landscape for robo-advisor fees is continuing to evolve. Robo-advisors are becoming more competitive, with many platforms lowering their fees or offering more value-added services to attract new customers. Here are some key trends to look out for in 2023:

1. Increasing Use of Tax-Loss Harvesting

More robo-advisors are incorporating tax-loss harvesting as a standard feature. Tax-loss harvesting involves selling investments that have declined in value to offset gains elsewhere in your portfolio. While tax-loss harvesting can help you reduce your taxable income, it often comes with additional fees for the service. In 2023, you can expect many robo-advisors to include tax-loss harvesting as part of their core offering, especially on higher-tier accounts.

2. More Customization Options

As robo-advisors strive to offer more personalized services, many are introducing additional customization options that allow investors to tailor their portfolios based on specific goals, ethical preferences, or risk tolerance. While some of these services may come with additional fees, they are likely to become more prevalent as robo-advisors aim to attract a wider variety of investors.

3. Decreasing Fees

Competition in the robo-advisor market is fierce, and many platforms are lowering their fees to remain competitive. We can expect more robo-advisors to offer management fees at or below 0.25% in 2023, as they strive to capture market share. Lower fees will continue to benefit investors, particularly those with smaller portfolios who may have previously been priced out of using robo-advisors.

Final Thoughts

Robo-advisors offer an accessible, low-cost way for individuals to invest and grow their wealth. However, understanding the fees associated with these platforms is crucial to making the most of your investment. The combination of management fees, fund fees, account fees, and any premium services can add up, but with the right approach, you can minimize these costs and make smart financial decisions.

As you explore robo-advisor options in 2023, be sure to evaluate the fees and services offered by each platform. Consider how those fees align with your investment goals and whether the features provided justify the cost. With careful planning, robo-advisors can be an effective tool for building a diversified investment portfolio without breaking the bank.
