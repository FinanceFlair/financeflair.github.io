Anchoring Effect: How Initial Information Influences Our Choices


Our decisions often feel like they are the product of careful consideration and objective reasoning. However, numerous psychological phenomena influence the way we make choices, often in subtle and surprising ways. One of the most potent of these is the anchoring effect, a cognitive bias where the first piece of information we encounter—whether accurate or not—heavily influences our subsequent judgments and decisions. Understanding the anchoring effect is essential not just for improving personal decision-making but also for recognizing how external factors shape our thoughts and actions.

What Is the Anchoring Effect?

The anchoring effect occurs when an initial value, number, or reference point influences how we perceive and process information. This anchor sets the tone for comparisons, shaping our judgment of what follows. Regardless of whether the anchor is significant or even logical, it establishes a mental benchmark from which we adjust our thinking.

For example, when negotiating the price of a car, the starting offer—whether from the dealer or the buyer—acts as an anchor. Even if this amount is far from the car’s true value, it biases the negotiation process. Future counteroffers and discussions are often pulled unconsciously toward the initial figure.

This phenomenon is not limited to numbers. It can manifest in a wide variety of contexts, from how we assess the quality of a product to predictions about future events. Once the anchor is planted, it exerts a persistent influence, often leading us to make decisions that lean closer to the anchor rather than evaluating evidence independently.

The Science Behind Anchoring

Psychologists Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman first introduced the concept of the anchoring effect in the 1970s as part of their foundational work on cognitive biases and heuristics. Their research revealed that people rely disproportionately on the first piece of information they receive when tasked with making estimates, decisions, or evaluations. This reliance often occurs even in cases where the anchor is arbitrary or irrelevant.

One classic study involved participants spinning a wheel of fortune with numbers ranging from 1 to 100 before being asked to estimate the percentage of African nations in the United Nations. Participants who spun lower numbers consistently provided smaller estimates, while those who spun higher numbers gave larger guesses. The random numbers on the wheel should have had no bearing on their judgments—yet they did, vividly demonstrating how anchors operate.

Neurological studies further support these findings. Anchors engage areas of the brain associated with comparison and value assignment, such as the prefrontal cortex. This underscores the instinctive nature of anchoring: it is not a conscious choice but an embedded feature of human cognition.

Real-Life Examples of Anchoring

The anchoring effect pervades an astonishing range of real-life situations. By understanding its applications, we can recognize when decisions may be unfairly influenced.

1. Retail Pricing Strategies

Ever noticed how items are often displayed alongside their original price, marked down with a discount? That $400 handbag marked down to $250 suddenly seems like a steal, even though you may never have considered spending that much in the first place. The original price anchors your perception, making the discounted amount appear more reasonable in comparison.

2. Negotiations

Whether in real estate, hiring, or business transactions, the anchoring effect frequently dictates negotiation outcomes. When one party proposes an initial offer, it creates a reference point, shifting the expectations of both sides. For example, if a seller lists a house at $500,000, potential buyers—even if they think the home is overpriced—will frame their counteroffers relative to this amount.

3. Medical Decisions

Even medical professionals are not immune to the anchoring effect. Studies have shown that an initial diagnosis or piece of information can unduly influence a doctor’s thought process. For example, a test result suggesting one possible condition can anchor a doctor’s deliberation, causing alternative diagnoses to be deprioritized, even if subsequent evidence points in a different direction.

4. Job Interviews

Anchoring also plays a role in salary negotiations. Suppose an applicant gives an expected salary figure early in the process. This number often colors the employer’s ultimate offer, even if the figure was arbitrary or premature. Similarly, when employers include a salary range in job postings, candidates often tailor their expectations to align with that anchor.

Why Are We So Vulnerable?

The anchoring effect stems from a combination of mental shortcuts and the limitations of human data processing. In an uncertain world filled with incomplete information, anchors act as starting points that simplify complex decisions. Unfortunately, this reliance can backfire, particularly when anchors are misleading.

1. Cognitive Load

Our brains are wired to conserve energy by simplifying decision-making processes. Anchors serve as mental shortcuts, saving us from reevaluating every option from scratch. However, the tendency to fixate on anchors means our judgments often adjust insufficiently, leaving us biased.

2. Effortful Adjustment

Adjusting away from an anchor requires cognitive effort. We often stop refining our judgments prematurely, a phenomenon called insufficient adjustment. For instance, after seeing a high-priced TV, you may attempt to determine if it’s worth it, but your final judgment will still be skewed toward the anchor because you didn’t fully evaluate its true value.

3. Priming

Anchors are a form of psychological priming. Once an anchor is introduced, it triggers associated ideas and assumptions in our minds, subtly influencing our perception even if we believe we are rationally deliberating.

How To Minimize the Influence of Anchoring

The anchoring effect is a deep-seated cognitive bias, but it is not insurmountable. Awareness and deliberate strategies can help mitigate its influence and lead to more balanced decision-making.

1. Recognize Anchors

The first, and perhaps most critical, step is to recognize when an anchor is being set. Whether it’s a price, a reference point, or a suggestion, identifying it as an anchor gives you the mental distance needed to question its validity.

2. Question the Relevance

Ask yourself: is the anchor relevant to the situation? If not, discard it and actively seek additional information. For instance, if someone proposes an initial price that seems arbitrary, do independent research to arrive at a more accurate starting point.

3. Deliberate Adjustment

Take the time to consciously adjust away from anchors. Practice thinking beyond the provided reference point by considering opposing viewpoints, alternative scenarios, or external benchmarks.

4. Avoid Premature Anchors

Where possible, avoid being the first to offer an anchor in negotiations or new situations. Conversely, if you find yourself setting the first anchor, use it strategically and ensure it is well-informed.

5. Diversify Perspectives

Consulting multiple sources or involving other people in discussions can help override personal biases. Different viewpoints can counteract the influence of an initial anchor and lead to more balanced outcomes.

The Ethical Implications of Anchoring

While the anchoring effect is a fascinating psychological phenomenon, it also raises important ethical questions. Many industries, particularly marketing and sales, exploit this bias to manipulate consumer behavior. By setting inflated anchors, businesses can lead people to make decisions that may not align with their true interests or financial resources.

At the same time, understanding anchoring can be a force for good. Transparency in pricing, clear communication, and consumer education can empower individuals to resist undue influence. Ethical businesses and leaders have a responsibility to minimize manipulative practices and promote more informed decision-making processes.

Final Thoughts

The anchoring effect is an unavoidable part of human psychology, shaping how we view the world and make choices. While it can be a double-edged sword—capable of both aiding and misleading us—awareness and mindfulness can help tip the scales in our favor. Whether negotiating a contract, shopping for a deal, or diagnosing a problem, training ourselves to question initial reference points and seek additional evidence allows us to make smarter, more deliberate decisions.

Anchors may be powerful, but armed with knowledge and critical thinking, we can prevent them from steering us astray. By doing so, we gain a clearer path to achieving our personal, financial, and professional goals.
