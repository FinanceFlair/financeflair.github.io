The History of Inflation: Lessons for Today's Investors


Inflation, the gradual escalation in price levels over time, has perplexed not just investors but also economists for centuries. Despite fluctuations throughout historical epochs, inflation remains a critical force shaping global economies. This blog explores the history of inflation to impart industry insights aiding modern investors in navigating potential challenges.

The Phoenicians, circa 1200 BCE, introduced the concept of inflation via a collapse in commodity markets. Silver, their standard currency, initially lost value leading to overvaluation of grain. As a contingency measure, they mandated and enforced fixed prices on daily use items. The episode emphasized the pivotal role governments play in controlling or mitigating inflation.

In the 16th century, an epic case of hyperinflation gripped the rather unfortunate nation of Germany. When World War I took its economic toll, this inflation skyrocketed. The Weimar Republic, set up to stabilize, lapsed under this financial strain, giving birth to more extreme crises.

In the more familiar 20th-century USA, inflation experienced quiet growth during post-war reconstruction. The Vietnam War and civil rights movements ushered in faster price escalation. Later, the OPEC oil shocks in the ’70s took inflation ratios stratospheric. This surge led to monumental monetary policy shifts, including Winston Churchill’s expression of the ‘balance between inflation and unemployment,’ and the ‘Monetarist’ theories propounded by Milton Friedman.

Fast forward to the 21st century, and our lens falls upon China, a recent inflation-story contender. Through market-oriented reforms, the country grew economically, yet faced unveiled inflation pressure in 2007-2008. Recognizing its influence, China adopted measures to curb inflating prices, demonstrating a geopolitical significance of managing inflation.

The inflation-history montage reflects how erratic market behavior can be, with inflation as an unpredictable dance partner. But there are lessons for today’s investors. Firstly, diversify your investment strategies to align with your risk appetite. Combining different types of investments (bonds, stocks, real estate) can adjust volatility risks. Secondly, monitor the inflation-adjusted rate of return (real return), allowing for more accurate forecasting of investment performance. Lastly, maintain an understanding of the interplay between inflation, economic policy, and your portfolio to make effective decisions.

Mastering inflation extends beyond the historical narrative. By applying timeless fundamentals alongside modern innovations, investors can steer through the inflationary minefield. The past provides substantial insights into the forces propelling our economy, priming investors to respond proactively to future shifts in inflation rates. Utilizing this knowledge ensures higher adaptability and resilience, defining characteristics of successful investors. Future-proofing your investment strategy warrants understanding the history of inflation and the ongoing role it plays in guiding financial decisions today.
