The Evolution of Crowdfunding: From Kickstarter to Today


In the early 2000s, the concept of crowdfunding was still in its infancy, largely unheard of by the general public and mainly associated with niche platforms focused on arts and creative projects. Fast forward to the present day, and crowdfunding has evolved into a multi-billion-dollar industry, diversifying into various sectors and influencing the way businesses and individuals raise funds. This transformative journey is marked by a series of milestones and innovations that have elevated crowdfunding to a mainstream phenomenon. The Birth of Crowdfunding The seeds of modern crowdfunding were sown in 2002 with the launch of ArtistShare, one of the first crowdfunding platforms, which allowed artists to receive support from fans and investors for their projects. The beginning of the 2000s also saw the rise of platforms like Kicktraq and DonorsChoose, focusing on non-profit ventures and kickstarters for creative projects. However, it was Kickstarter that changed the game in 2009 by launching a full-fledged Kickstarter platform. Founded by Perry Chen, Yancey Strickler, and Charles Adler, Kickstarter would go on to become the standard-bearer for crowdfunding, allowing creators to set funding goals and timelines for their projects, secured by a tangible reward in return for monetary contributions. This pledged-based model motivated backers to participate in projects they believed in, similar to the concept of publishing and film production where investors fund production in exchange for a share of the final product. Pioneers in the Field Indiegogo, launched in 2008 but initially gaining momentum around 2010, expanded crowdfunding to a broader audience. Realizing the success of crowdfunding platforms, it set out to differentiate itself by opening its platform to a wider scope of projects and focused on rewarding flexibility through flexible funding options, offering options to either fund through the fixed funding option, which charges fees only when a funding goal is met, or the flexible funding option which charges even when goals aren't met, but don't charge any fees in the hope that it will return to the funders. Equally visible in the rise to prominence were sites that focused on rewards to backers, such as the Starbucks versus fund Blue Bottle Coffee. the idea is that the more you request, the larger your reward will be. Another parallel development has occurred on indiegogo, with the increase in crowdfunded products, with this industry pooling millions through the likes of cellular tether kits that link weapon sights with smartphone cameras for just as amazing components of emergent options made both discoverable across a bread and butter product. Evaluating the Effectiveness Crowdfunding is not just about raising money; it's also about nurturing relationships, building a community around a project, and in many cases, fostering product development. Engagement is key - creators who take the time to respond to comments and feedback often end up motivated by relating to the people who have budgeted towards their fund raising campaigns.
