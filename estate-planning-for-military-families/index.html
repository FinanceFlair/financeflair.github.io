Estate Planning for Military Families


Estate planning is vital for everyone, but military families face unique challenges and opportunities due to their distinct lifestyles, deployments, benefits, and legal frameworks. When you serve or are closely connected to someone in the armed forces, the importance of comprehensive and adaptable estate planning grows significantly. This guide will take you through the key considerations, resources, and strategies to build a robust estate plan tailored for military families.

Understanding Estate Planning in the Military Context

Estate planning involves preparing for the management and distribution of your assets in the event of incapacity or death. For military families, the stakes are inherently higher and timelines potentially uncertain, due to deployments, frequent relocations, and risks associated with active duty.

It’s more than just drafting a will; the process encompasses appointing guardians for minor children, designating powers of attorney, preparing advance medical directives, and planning finances to accommodate military benefits and survivor stipends.

Unique Military Considerations

  • Frequent Relocations: Military families often move across state lines, so estate planning should comply with laws in potential states of residence.
  • Deployment Risks: Deployments heighten concerns over incapacity or death, emphasizing the need for clear legal authorizations and support.
  • Military Benefits: Understanding how military pay, pensions, life insurance, and Survivor Benefit Plans affect estate distribution is critical.
  • Custody Concerns: If you have minor children, guardianship designations become a crucial element, ensuring care continuity.
  • Tax Implications: Military retirement pay and survivor benefits often bring specific tax treatments that must be integrated into the plan.

Core Components of Estate Planning for Military Families

Estate planning doesn’t have a one-size-fits-all solution. However, some foundational documents and considerations recur, especially within the military framework.

1. Last Will and Testament

A will specifies how your assets will be distributed after death and appoints executors to manage your estate. For military members:

  • Ensure the will is compliant with the laws of your current state of residence, but also consider potential future residencies.
  • Your will should explicitly state how military benefits and pensions are to be handled.
  • Without a valid will, intestate succession laws could lead to unintended allocation of military benefits or property.

2. Durable Power of Attorney

This legal document grants someone authority to handle your financial and legal affairs if you become incapacitated.

  • Military families must consider giving durable power of attorney to a trusted family member or friend, as deployments may render you temporarily unavailable.
  • The scope of the power should be clearly defined to include military-specific accounts and benefits.
  • Be sure to check if the bank or institution requires military-specific language or powers.

3. Medical Power of Attorney and Advance Directives

Deployment or injury could lead to medical incapacity, making these documents crucial.

  • Medical power of attorney designates a person who can make healthcare decisions on your behalf.
  • Advance directives clarify your wishes regarding life support and other medical interventions.
  • These ensure your healthcare preferences are honored, even when you are far from your usual support system.

4. Beneficiary Designations

Do not overlook beneficiary designations on:

  • Servicemembers’ Group Life Insurance (SGLI)
  • Thrift Savings Plan (TSP)
  • Retirement pensions
  • Health savings accounts (HSA)

These override wills, so it is essential to keep them up to date, especially after life changes like marriage, divorce, or the birth of a child.

5. Guardianship Designations

Military families with minor children must explicitly name guardians in the event both parents are unable to care for them. This guards against court involvement that could delay or complicate guardianship assignments.

Addressing Military Benefits Within Your Estate Plan

Military benefits can add complexity but also provide substantial protections for your family. Here are key components you should consider integrating into your plan:

Servicemembers’ Group Life Insurance (SGLI) and VGLI

  • SGLI is a low-cost life insurance policy automatically provided to active-duty military personnel.
  • You can convert it to Veterans’ Group Life Insurance (VGLI) after separation.
  • Ensure beneficiaries are kept current, and align these policies with your estate plan.

Survivor Benefit Plan (SBP)

The SBP allows retired service members to provide ongoing income to survivors after death.

  • Premiums are deducted from retirement pay.
  • It can supplement the loss of military retirement benefits, which otherwise may cease upon death.
  • Consider coordinating SBP with other life insurance to avoid over-insurance or duplicative benefits.

Thrift Savings Plan (TSP)

Comparable to civilian 401(k) plans, TSP accounts grow tax-deferred.

  • Designate beneficiaries properly to avoid probate.
  • Understand required minimum distributions (RMDs) rules and how they interact with other retirement accounts.

Military Retirement Pay

Military pensions may have unique survivorship and taxation rules.

  • Survivors may be eligible for Dependency and Indemnity Compensation (DIC) from the Department of Veterans Affairs.
  • Coordinating all sources of income through your estate plan provides clarity and maximizes benefits for your heirs.

Practical Tips for Military Families When Planning Estates

Crafting an estate plan as a military family poses challenges due to mobility and changing circumstances. Here are actionable insights:

Keep Documents Accessible

  • Maintain digital and physical copies of wills, powers of attorney, medical directives, and beneficiary forms.
  • Use secure online storage options tailored for legal documents, making access easy wherever duty calls.

Update Your Estate Plan Regularly

  • Changes in deployment status, marital status, children, or state residency can affect your plan.
  • Review your plan annually or after any major life event.

Consult Military Legal Assistance Offices

  • Many bases have legal assistance offices staffed by attorneys familiar with estate planning intricacies specific to active-duty personnel.
  • These advisors can help navigate the intersection of civilian and military laws.

Coordinate with Civilian Legal Counsel

  • Especially when dealing with complex estates, or if planning for eventual retirement off-base, civilian estate attorneys can provide valuable second opinions or insights.

Inform Important Parties

  • Share the existence and location of your estate planning documents with key family members and executors.
  • Educate them on your intentions and the practical steps needed if something happens.

Addressing Special Situations in Military Estate Planning

Military families encounter unique scenarios that can influence estate planning strategies:

Blended Families and Remarriage

  • Custody, inheritance rights, and benefit eligibility can become intricate with multiple marriages or stepchildren.
  • Address these explicitly in your estate plan to prevent disputes.

Overseas Deployments

  • Different countries have varying laws about powers of attorney and estate management.
  • You might need additional documentation or embassy consular assistance to ensure your wishes are enforceable abroad.

Disability and VA Benefits

  • Disabilities related to military service can trigger special benefit programs.
  • It may affect eligibility for Medicaid, Supplemental Security Income (SSI), or Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI).
  • Consult a VA benefits counselor to maximize benefit integration without jeopardizing eligibility.

Planning for the Emotional and Psychological Well-being of Your Family

Estate planning isn’t just filling out legal paperwork; it’s also about peace of mind for you and your loved ones. In military families, where separation and uncertainty are frequent, making these arrangements clear can alleviate stress during already difficult times.

  • Discuss your wishes proactively with your spouse or family.
  • Consider counseling or support groups to help process complex feelings surrounding loss and contingency planning.
  • Use estate planning as an opportunity to communicate values and priorities, reinforcing family bonds despite physical distances.

Final Thoughts: Proactive Estate Planning as an Act of Service to Your Family

While no one likes to dwell on the worst-case scenarios, military families understand the importance of readiness better than most. An effective estate plan is a vital part of that preparedness, safeguarding your assets, your family’s future, and your peace of mind.

By understanding the distinct challenges that military life poses and actively engaging resources and legal tools available, military families can build estate plans that are resilient, adaptable, and aligned with their unique needs.

If you’re a service member or family member looking to start or update your estate plan, begin with local legal assistance, and make it a priority to revisit your plan regularly. Your duty to your family extends beyond your service—estate planning is a key part of that lifelong commitment.
