Understanding the Anchoring and Adjustment Bias in Financial Planning


The Anchoring and Adjustment Bias is a cognitive bias that affects our decision-making process in financial planning. It refers to our tendency to rely too heavily on an initial piece of information (the anchor) when making decisions, and then adjust our subsequent judgments based on that initial anchor.

This bias can have significant implications for financial planning, as it can lead us to make decisions based on arbitrary or irrelevant information, rather than objective financial analysis. It can also cause us to underestimate or ignore other relevant information that should be taken into account.

How the Anchoring and Adjustment Bias Works

To understand how the Anchoring and Adjustment Bias works, let’s consider an example. Imagine you are planning for your retirement and you meet with a financial advisor. The advisor presents you with two retirement plans: Plan A and Plan B.

Plan A offers a guaranteed return of 4% per year, while Plan B offers a variable return that could range from -2% to 10% per year, depending on market conditions. The advisor mentions that historically, the average return on Plan B has been 8%.

In this scenario, the initial information you receive about the average return of Plan B (8%) becomes the anchor. As a result, you may anchor your decision-making process around this number, even though it may not be the most relevant or accurate piece of information.

For instance, you may be more likely to choose Plan B, even though it carries a higher level of risk, simply because the 8% return seems attractive compared to the guaranteed 4% return of Plan A. This decision is influenced by the anchoring effect, as you are adjusting your judgment based on the initial anchor of 8%.

Limitations and Risks of the Anchoring and Adjustment Bias

The Anchoring and Adjustment Bias can lead to suboptimal financial decisions. By fixating on an initial anchor, we may overlook other important factors such as risk tolerance, investment time horizon, and individual financial goals.

One of the dangers of this bias is that it can lead to excessive risk-taking or unrealistic expectations. For example, if you anchor on a high historical average return of a particular investment, you may underestimate the potential downside and overestimate the potential upside.

Additionally, the Anchoring and Adjustment Bias can be manipulated by others, such as salespeople or financial advisors, who may use anchoring techniques to influence your decision-making process. By presenting you with a high anchor, they can make other options seem more appealing in comparison.

Overcoming the Anchoring and Adjustment Bias

Awareness is the first step in overcoming the Anchoring and Adjustment Bias. By recognizing this bias, you can actively challenge and question the initial anchor presented to you. It’s important to gather and consider multiple sources of information before making any financial decisions.

Seeking advice from multiple financial advisors or conducting thorough research can help you avoid being influenced solely by one anchor. By diversifying your sources of information, you can make more informed and rational decisions that align with your individual financial goals and risk tolerance.

Conclusion

The Anchoring and Adjustment Bias is a cognitive bias that can significantly impact financial planning. By understanding how this bias works and its potential limitations, you can become more aware of its influence on your decision-making process.

Remember to critically evaluate the initial anchor presented to you and consider multiple sources of information before making any financial decisions. By doing so, you can ensure that your financial planning is based on a comprehensive analysis rather than arbitrary or irrelevant information.

Financial planning is a complex process, and it’s important to approach it with an open mind and a willingness to challenge assumptions. By overcoming the Anchoring and Adjustment Bias, you can make more informed decisions that align with your long-term financial goals and aspirations.
