How to Create a Financial Statement Analysis Report


A financial statement analysis report is a crucial document that helps businesses, investors, and analysts assess the financial health of a company. It offers an in-depth examination of a company’s financial records and presents a clear picture of its financial stability, performance, and future outlook. In this guide, we will walk through the essential steps of creating an effective financial statement analysis report, breaking down the process and focusing on the key components and methods that will help you craft a comprehensive and insightful document.

Understanding the Financial Statements

Before diving into the analysis, it is important to understand the three core financial statements that you will be reviewing:

  1. Balance Sheet – This statement provides a snapshot of a company’s assets, liabilities, and shareholders’ equity at a particular point in time. It is divided into two sections: assets (what the company owns) and liabilities (what the company owes). The balance sheet follows the fundamental accounting equation:

    Assets = Liabilities + Equity

  2. Income Statement – Also known as the profit and loss statement, it shows the company’s revenues, expenses, and profits over a specific period. This document is key to understanding the company’s profitability, as it highlights how much money the company made and spent during a given time frame.

  3. Cash Flow Statement – This statement tracks the inflows and outflows of cash, showing how well a company manages its cash to fund its obligations and growth. It is divided into three sections: operating activities, investing activities, and financing activities.

These three statements work together to give you a comprehensive view of the company’s financial health. They are interrelated, and an in-depth analysis requires reviewing all of them in context with each other.

Step 1: Collect the Financial Statements

To begin your analysis, the first step is to collect the latest financial statements of the company. These are typically available in annual reports, quarterly filings, or directly from the company’s investor relations page. The more recent the statements, the more relevant your analysis will be. You should ensure that you have the following documents:

  • The most recent balance sheet
  • The latest income statement (also known as the profit and loss statement)
  • The most recent cash flow statement

Make sure that you’re looking at consistent time periods when collecting the data, especially when comparing multiple periods.

Step 2: Perform a Horizontal Analysis

Horizontal analysis, also known as trend analysis, involves comparing financial data over multiple periods. This method helps identify growth trends and patterns in a company’s financial performance. Here’s how to conduct horizontal analysis:

  1. Select a time frame: Choose at least two periods for comparison (e.g., quarterly or annual data).
  2. Calculate the change: For each line item on the financial statements, subtract the previous period’s value from the current period’s value.
  3. Determine the percentage change: Divide the difference by the previous period’s value and multiply by 100 to determine the percentage change.

Example:

  • If the revenue in Year 1 is $500,000 and in Year 2 it is $600,000, the percentage change would be:

    ((600,000 - 500,000) / 500,000) * 100 = 20% increase

This allows you to see trends in key areas like revenue growth, profitability, and expenses.

Step 3: Conduct Vertical Analysis

Vertical analysis is a method used to analyze each line item on a financial statement as a percentage of a base item within the same statement. This analysis helps to assess how different components contribute to the overall performance of the company. It’s commonly used on both the income statement and balance sheet:

  • Income Statement: The base item is usually total revenue or sales. For example, if your cost of goods sold (COGS) is $300,000 and your total revenue is $1,000,000, the COGS would represent 30% of revenue.

    Formula:

    (COGS / Total Revenue) * 100

  • Balance Sheet: The base item is typically total assets. For example, if total liabilities are $200,000 and total assets are $500,000, liabilities would make up 40% of total assets.

    Formula:

    (Liabilities / Total Assets) * 100

Vertical analysis is beneficial for understanding the proportional relationship between financial items, which provides valuable insights into areas like cost control and capital structure.

Step 4: Key Ratios to Include in the Report

Financial ratios are essential tools in financial statement analysis. They offer a quick snapshot of various aspects of a company’s financial performance and health. Below are some of the most important ratios to include in your analysis:

  1. Liquidity Ratios: These ratios measure a company’s ability to meet short-term obligations.

    • Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities
    • Quick Ratio = (Current Assets - Inventories) / Current Liabilities

  2. Profitability Ratios: These ratios assess a company’s ability to generate profits relative to its revenue, assets, and equity.

    • Gross Profit Margin = (Revenue - Cost of Goods Sold) / Revenue
    • Operating Profit Margin = Operating Income / Revenue
    • Net Profit Margin = Net Income / Revenue

  3. Efficiency Ratios: These ratios measure how effectively a company uses its resources to generate sales and maximize profits.

    • Asset Turnover Ratio = Revenue / Average Total Assets
    • Inventory Turnover Ratio = Cost of Goods Sold / Average Inventory

  4. Leverage Ratios: These ratios indicate the extent of a company’s reliance on debt for financing.

    • Debt-to-Equity Ratio = Total Debt / Shareholders’ Equity
    • Debt Ratio = Total Debt / Total Assets

  5. Market Value Ratios: These ratios are important for understanding how the market views the company.

    • Earnings Per Share (EPS) = Net Income / Number of Outstanding Shares
    • Price-to-Earnings Ratio (P/E) = Market Price Per Share / Earnings Per Share

Step 5: Analyze Cash Flow

Cash flow is a vital indicator of a company’s financial health. Unlike profits, which are based on accrual accounting, cash flow shows how much money is actually coming in and out of the business. A negative cash flow may signal liquidity problems, even if the company is profitable.

Look at the operating cash flow, which shows the cash generated or used by the company’s core business activities. Positive operating cash flow is a sign of healthy business operations. If operating cash flow is consistently negative, it could be a red flag.

Also, examine investing and financing activities. Are they in line with the company’s strategy? High cash outflows for investments might indicate expansion, while excessive financing activities may indicate increasing reliance on debt.

Step 6: Evaluate the Company’s Financial Position

Now that you have all the financial data and ratios, it’s time to evaluate the overall financial position of the company. This involves synthesizing your horizontal and vertical analyses, ratios, and cash flow assessments into a cohesive narrative.

  • Strengths: Look for areas where the company is excelling. This could be strong revenue growth, healthy profit margins, or efficient use of assets.
  • Weaknesses: Identify areas of concern. Is the company heavily reliant on debt? Are margins shrinking? Is cash flow inconsistent?
  • Opportunities: Consider external factors that might benefit the company, such as emerging market trends or new products.
  • Threats: Recognize any potential risks that might hinder growth, such as rising costs, intense competition, or economic downturns.

Step 7: Conclusion and Recommendations

The final section of the financial statement analysis report should summarize your findings and provide recommendations for action. Based on your analysis, you may suggest areas for improvement or opportunities for growth. Recommendations could include:

  • Cost Reduction: If profitability margins are declining, you might suggest ways to streamline operations or reduce expenses.
  • Debt Management: If the company has high levels of debt, you might recommend focusing on reducing liabilities.
  • Investment Opportunities: If cash flow is strong, the company may be in a good position to invest in growth or expansion.

Final Thoughts

Creating a financial statement analysis report involves reviewing a company’s financial statements, calculating important ratios, and interpreting the results to assess financial performance. The process requires careful attention to detail, a clear understanding of financial metrics, and the ability to present findings in a structured and comprehensive way. By following the steps outlined in this guide, you can create a thorough report that provides valuable insights into the company’s financial health, helping stakeholders make informed decisions.
