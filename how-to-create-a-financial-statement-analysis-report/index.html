How to Create a Financial Statement Analysis Report


Creating a financial statement analysis report is a crucial skill for anyone involved in finance, accounting, or business management. This report provides insights into a company’s financial health, performance, and potential for future growth. In this comprehensive guide, we will walk you through the steps to create an effective financial statement analysis report, ensuring that you can communicate your findings clearly and concisely.

Understanding Financial Statements

Before diving into the analysis, it’s essential to understand the three primary financial statements that form the backbone of any financial analysis:

  1. Income Statement: This statement summarizes a company’s revenues and expenses over a specific period, ultimately showing the net profit or loss. It provides insights into operational efficiency and profitability.

  2. Balance Sheet: The balance sheet presents a snapshot of a company’s assets, liabilities, and equity at a specific point in time. It helps assess the company’s financial position and liquidity.

  3. Cash Flow Statement: This statement tracks the flow of cash in and out of the business, categorized into operating, investing, and financing activities. It is vital for understanding the company’s cash management and liquidity.

Step 1: Gather Financial Statements

The first step in creating a financial statement analysis report is to gather the necessary financial statements. You will typically need the most recent income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement. These documents can usually be found in the company’s annual report or financial filings.

Sources for Financial Statements

  • Company Website: Most public companies provide their financial statements in the investor relations section.
  • Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC): For U.S. companies, the SEC’s EDGAR database is a valuable resource for accessing financial filings.
  • Financial Databases: Platforms like Bloomberg, Yahoo Finance, or Morningstar can provide financial statements for various companies.

Step 2: Analyze the Income Statement

The income statement is the first place to start your analysis. Here are some key components to focus on:

Revenue Analysis

  • Trend Analysis: Compare revenue figures over multiple periods to identify growth trends. Look for consistent growth or any significant fluctuations.
  • Revenue Sources: Break down revenue by product line or service to understand which areas are driving growth.

Expense Analysis

  • Cost of Goods Sold (COGS): Analyze COGS to determine how efficiently the company is producing its goods. A rising COGS relative to revenue may indicate inefficiencies.
  • Operating Expenses: Examine selling, general, and administrative expenses (SG&A) to assess how well the company manages its overhead costs.

Profitability Ratios

  • Gross Profit Margin: Calculate this by dividing gross profit by total revenue. A higher margin indicates better efficiency in production.
  • Operating Profit Margin: This ratio shows the percentage of revenue that remains after covering operating expenses.
  • Net Profit Margin: This is the ultimate measure of profitability, calculated by dividing net income by total revenue.

Step 3: Analyze the Balance Sheet

The balance sheet provides insights into the company’s financial stability and liquidity. Here’s how to analyze it effectively:

Asset Analysis

  • Current Assets vs. Non-Current Assets: Assess the proportion of current assets (cash, inventory, receivables) to non-current assets (property, plant, equipment). A higher ratio of current assets indicates better liquidity.
  • Asset Turnover Ratio: This ratio measures how efficiently a company uses its assets to generate sales. Calculate it by dividing total revenue by average total assets.

Liability Analysis

  • Current Liabilities vs. Long-Term Liabilities: Evaluate the company’s short-term obligations against its long-term debt. A high level of current liabilities compared to current assets may indicate liquidity issues.
  • Debt-to-Equity Ratio: This ratio compares total liabilities to shareholders’ equity, providing insights into the company’s leverage and financial risk.

Equity Analysis

  • Retained Earnings: Analyze retained earnings to understand how much profit is being reinvested in the business versus distributed as dividends.
  • Return on Equity (ROE): Calculate ROE by dividing net income by average shareholders’ equity. A higher ROE indicates effective management of equity capital.

Step 4: Analyze the Cash Flow Statement

The cash flow statement is critical for understanding how a company manages its cash. Here’s how to analyze it:

Operating Cash Flow

  • Cash Flow from Operations: Assess whether the company generates sufficient cash from its core business activities. Positive cash flow from operations is a good sign of financial health.
  • Cash Flow Margin: Calculate this by dividing cash flow from operations by total revenue. A higher margin indicates better cash generation relative to sales.

Investing Cash Flow

  • Capital Expenditures: Analyze cash spent on capital expenditures to understand the company’s investment in growth. High capital expenditures may indicate expansion, but they should be balanced with cash flow from operations.
