How Corporate Valuation Methods Differ Between Public and Private Firms


Valuing a business is equal parts art and science, but when it comes to public and private companies, the canvas and palette look drastically different. Although both types of firms ultimately aim to establish how much they’re worth, their valuation journeys diverge because of differences in transparency, liquidity, market access, and regulatory requirements. Unpacking these nuances is essential for anyone engaging in mergers and acquisitions, private equity, venture capital, strategic planning, or even just pondering the fascinating world of corporate finance.

The Foundation of Corporate Valuation

At its core, corporate valuation boils down to estimating a company’s economic value—the answer to “What is this business worth?” Several well-established methods exist, including discounted cash flows (DCF), market comparables (comps), and precedent transactions. However, how these methods are applied depends greatly on whether the firm is public, with shares traded on a stock exchange, or private, with ownership restricted to a closely-held group.

Shared Methodologies, Divergent Realities

Both public and private company valuation can theoretically use similar approaches, but the devil is in the details. Public firms benefit from deep pools of data, dynamic price discovery via active trading, and regulatory disclosure. Private companies operate under a veil—thin or thick depending on their size—so appraisers must get creative. Here’s where their paths split.

Valuation Methods for Public Companies

Public firms are under a regulatory microscope, and the stock market provides a real-time valuation reference. This foundational difference streamlines valuations, but also introduces unique quirks.

1. Market Capitalization

For public companies, valuation starts with market capitalization. Multiply the current share price by total outstanding shares, and you have a baseline value grounded in a liquid market where buyers and sellers agree on prices in real time.

Advantages

  • Transparency: Continuous price discovery thanks to trading volumes.
  • Comparability: Easy to benchmark across similar firms or indices.
  • Immediate feedback: News or performance is reflected almost instantly in share price.

Shortcomings

  • Volatility: Market sentiment can swing prices wildly.
  • Market anomalies: Prices sometimes reflect short-term trends, not intrinsic value.
  • Not all-encompassing: Doesn’t include debt or account for minority interests, so practitioners often turn to enterprise value instead.

2. Enterprise Value Multiples

Market cap is just the starting point. Analysts often shift to enterprise value (EV), which accounts for debt, cash, and equivalents, painting a fuller picture of the company’s value to all stakeholders. The most common multiples—EV/EBITDA, EV/EBIT, and P/E ratios—are simple to calculate for public firms because the data is public and updated.

Why Multiples Work for Public Firms

  • Abundance of Data: You can build robust peer groups with just a few clicks.
  • Consistent Financials: Standardized reporting makes apples-to-apples comparisons easier.
  • Frequent Updates: Multiples update with market movements, reflecting latest consensus.

3. Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Models

DCF is a bedrock valuation technique in both public and private contexts. For public firms, reliable historical data and analyst forecasts make projecting cash flows less speculative. The discount rate—often derived from the company’s weighted average cost of capital (WACC)—can be calculated using observable market information.

Strengths

  • Customizable: Allows for nuanced assumptions tailored to firm specifics.
  • Theoretically sound: Focuses on the present value of actual cash generation capacity.

Limitations

  • Inputs can get subjective: Small changes in forecasts can have outsized valuation impact.
  • Market disconnect: Sometimes DCF outcomes diverge widely from market prices.

4. Precedent Transactions

Not as common for public companies unless considering takeover potential, but they do provide context when gauging acquisition premiums or market sentiment during sector M&A activity.

Valuation Methods for Private Companies

Without a ticker symbol or armies of analysts, private entities require more nuance and guesswork. Here’s where benchmarks and creativity matter.

1. Market Approach—Navigating Thin Air

Comparable company, or “comps,” analysis remains a staple, but finding appropriate peers is trickier.

Finding Comparables

  • Scarcity of Information: Few private firms disclose data, making peer selection subjective.
  • Adjustments Galore: Need to normalize for differences in size, growth, profitability, and capital structure.
  • Private Company Discount: Buyers often demand a discount due to illiquidity.

Reality Checks

  • Valuation Reports: Intermediaries compile databases of private transactions, but gaps and lags abound.
  • Market Multiples: Sometimes use public multiples, adjusted downward to reflect private company handicaps (illiquidity, control premium, smaller size).

2. Income Approach—Building a DCF Amid Fuzziness

DCF can be applied to private companies, but several hurdles arise:

Revenue and Expense Estimates

  • Historical financials may be unreliable or unaudited.
  • Projections are often less grounded, demanding more in-depth diligence.

Discount Rate Challenges

  • Determining the appropriate cost of capital is trickier—no observable equity beta, harder to estimate risk-free rates, and market risk premiums are a moving target.
  • An illiquidity discount often tacks on a few more percentage points, recognizing the harder path to cash-out for private owners.

3. Asset-Based Approach

For distressed, asset-heavy, or early-stage private companies, valuation might rely heavily on tangible asset values. This strategy is less common for public companies except in cases involving liquidation or real estate investment trusts (REITs).

4. Precedent Transactions—Blurry Mirrors

Examining prices paid for similar businesses is often cited in private company valuation, but most databases only capture a sliver of actual transactions, and context can be difficult to glean (Were there special circumstances? Was the transaction at arm’s length?).

Control, Liquidity, and Other Adjustments

Several key adjustments distinguish the valuation of private and public firms.

Control Premiums

A controlling stake (over 50% of voting shares) commands a premium, reflecting the buyer’s ability to direct company strategy, management, and liquidation. For public companies, buyers often must pay above the market price to gain control in a takeover. For private firms, most acquisitions already involve control, but buyers nonetheless factor in how much extra value is unlocked by gaining unfettered access and influence.

Minority Discounts

Conversely, minority stakes in private businesses lack control, so valuations are reduced accordingly. For public companies, minority stakes are the norm since shares are freely traded and confer little strategic control.

Illiquidity Discounts

Shares in public companies can be sold in seconds; private firm stakes may be stuck for years, awaiting the right buyer or market window. To offset this, private valuations generally reflect an illiquidity discount, sometimes significant (20–30% or more depending on market conditions and risk).

Data Availability and Transparency

The gulf between public and private valuation boils down to information.

  • Public Companies: The flow of data never stops—quarterly reports, earnings calls, regulatory filings, and analyst projections create a dynamic, transparent feedback loop.
  • Private Companies: Financials are often opaque or unaudited. Company management controls the flow of information, which can be selective or incomplete.

The limitations in private data trickle through every valuation approach, requiring more judgment calls, normalized adjustments, and ultimately, broader valuation ranges.

Regulatory and Legal Context

Public-company valuations are subject to intense regulatory oversight: the SEC, stock exchange listing rules, and voluminous GAAP or IFRS reporting standards. Private companies aren’t held to these standards, so diligence is more invasive—site visits, detailed management interviews, and even background checks on key executives become central.

Legal issues like shareholder agreements, transfer restrictions, or earnouts can also impact what a share of a private company is truly worth, whereas public shares rarely carry the same baggage.

Who’s Doing the Valuing—and Why?

The purpose behind a valuation can tilt the playing field:

  • Public firms: Investors, investment banks, and analysts mostly value for trading, investment, or deal-related purposes. Valuations are referenced widely by the market.
  • Private firms: Motivations vary more. Family business succession, tax issues (estate or gift tax), employee stock ownership plans (ESOPs), venture fundraising, or preparing for a possible IPO or sale all involve different standards, methods, and potential biases.

Specific use cases can drive the choice of method. For example, if selling only part of a business, minority discounts matter more. If an exit event is imminent, that potential dictates acceptable valuation boundaries.

The Role of Subjectivity

No matter how many spreadsheets are built, human judgment never disappears from the process. For public companies, subjectivity tilts less heavily; markets, with their multitude of actors, smooth out most extremes over time. For private companies, every assumption—about growth, margins, exit opportunities, or even who’s a relevant comparable—echoes louder.

This is why private company valuations are often reported as a range, not as a single point estimate. You might hear, “This business is worth $12–18 million,” rather than the pinpoint accuracy implied by a public firm’s share price multiplied by outstanding shares.

Specialized Valuation Situations

Some scenarios further widen the public/private divide, such as:

Venture-Backed Startups

Trying to apply standard DCF models to a pre-revenue startup is a lesson in futility. For these, future market size, competitive landscape, founder pedigree, and the likelihood of achieving the next funding milestone or exit drive valuation more than current cash flows. Valuation is as much negotiation as calculation.

Distressed or Underperforming Companies

Private companies in distress may be valued on a liquidation or asset basis, disregarding profitability altogether. Public companies in similar straits see their value yanked mercilessly down by markets, but asset covers and bankruptcy outcomes are still more transparent due to required disclosure.

Strategic Acquisitions and Synergies

In both public and private markets, a buyer may value a target based on unique synergies—the value that only that buyer can realize. This is easier to justify with public markets benchmarked by ongoing prices, but can be highly idiosyncratic in the private realm.

Evolving Practices and the Rise of Data

The gap between public and private valuation is narrowing in some ways. Secondary markets for private company shares are springing up. Platforms track private company financing rounds, capturing more of the data once reserved for public exchange participants.

Still, the day-to-day reality diverges for anyone actively engaged in valuation across both landscapes.

Conclusion: Navigating a Bifurcated Landscape

Valuing public and private companies requires shared financial concepts but sharply different methodologies in practice. For public companies, transparency, liquidity, and regulatory oversight mean valuers can rely more confidently on market-based methods and comparables. For private companies, where information is limited and the path to liquidity is murky, increased reliance on subjective adjustments—discounts for illiquidity, control premiums, estimate-heavy financial modeling—is the norm.

For analysts, investors, buyers, and owners, the key is to recognize these differences and tailor your approach accordingly. The next time you ask, “What is this company worth?” consider not just the numbers, but the unique context in which those numbers exist—public, or private, and everything those words imply about data, liquidity, comparability, and opportunity.
