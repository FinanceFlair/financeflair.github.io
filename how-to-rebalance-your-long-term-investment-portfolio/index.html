How to Rebalance Your Long-Term Investment Portfolio


Investing for the long term requires more than just choosing the right assets and leaving them untouched for years. The financial markets are dynamic, and as time passes, the allocation of your portfolio naturally shifts due to varying returns across asset classes. This gradual drift can lead your portfolio away from its intended risk and return profile. Rebalancing your portfolio is a critical step to maintain your investment goals and manage risk effectively.

In this in-depth guide, we’ll walk through the concept of portfolio rebalancing, explore the best practices, and provide actionable steps to keep your investments aligned with your long-term objectives.

Understanding Portfolio Rebalancing

Portfolio rebalancing is the process of realigning the weightings of assets in your investment portfolio to your original or desired allocation. For example, if your target portfolio allocation is 60% stocks and 40% bonds, but due to market movements, the stock portion has increased to 70%, rebalancing involves selling some stocks and buying bonds to bring the allocation back to 60/40.

Why is this necessary? Because over time, different assets will grow at different rates, potentially increasing your risk beyond what you’re comfortable with, or conversely, causing you to miss out on returns if you become too conservative.

The Effects of Drift

When markets are rising, equities often outperform bonds and cash. If left unrebalanced, your portfolio may become overweighted in stocks, leading to a higher risk exposure than you’ve planned for. Conversely, during downturns, equities may underperform, shifting your portfolio to be more conservative than intended, potentially reducing growth prospects.

Rebalancing ensures your investments stay in line with your risk tolerance and investment horizon.

When Should You Rebalance?

There’s no one-size-fits-all answer. There are several popular approaches to timing your rebalancing:

1. Calendar-Based Rebalancing

Rebalancing at regular fixed intervals such as monthly, quarterly, semi-annually, or annually is a straightforward method. This method provides discipline and simplicity, which many investors appreciate for its ease of execution.

  • Pros: Easy to remember, structured process.
  • Cons: May lead to rebalancing when not necessary, causing unnecessary transaction costs.

2. Threshold-Based Rebalancing

This method involves rebalancing only when a particular asset class deviates from its target allocation by a predefined percentage, such as 5% or 10%.

  • Pros: Responsive to market changes, avoids unnecessary transactions.
  • Cons: Requires more frequent portfolio monitoring.

3. Hybrid Approach

Some investors combine both methods by checking their portfolio at regular intervals and rebalancing only if asset weights exceed the defined thresholds.

Each approach has merits. The choice depends on your investment style, trading costs, and how actively you want to manage your portfolio.

Steps to Rebalance Your Long-Term Portfolio

Rebalancing doesn’t need to be complicated. Here’s a simple step-by-step guide to help you rebalance efficiently:

Step 1: Review Your Current Allocation

Gather the latest statements or use your brokerage’s portfolio tools to assess the current distribution of your holdings across various asset classes such as stocks, bonds, real estate, and cash equivalents.

Step 2: Compare Against Your Target Allocation

Determine the difference between your current allocation and your desired allocation. For example, if your target is 50% equities and 50% fixed income, but equities now constitute 57%, your equity allocation has drifted by 7%.

Step 3: Decide on the Rebalancing Threshold

If your portfolio drift exceeds your predetermined threshold (e.g., 5%), it’s time to rebalance. If you’re using a calendar-based approach, check if your date for rebalancing has arrived.

Step 4: Choose How to Rebalance

  • Selling Overweight Assets: Sell a portion of assets that have grown beyond your target allocation.
  • Buying Underweight Assets: Use the proceeds to increase funds in assets that have fallen below your target.
  • Using New Contributions: If you regularly invest new money, you can direct contributions to underweighted assets to reduce transaction costs from selling.

Step 5: Consider Tax Implications and Costs

Selling assets may trigger capital gains taxes. Prioritize rebalancing tax-advantaged accounts if possible, and be aware of trading fees that could eat into your returns.

Step 6: Execute Rebalancing

Implement your trades strategically, spreading orders to avoid market impact or timing mistakes.

Step 7: Document and Monitor

Keep a record of your rebalancing activities and revisit your strategy to ensure it remains appropriate as your financial situation or goals change.

Common Rebalancing Strategies

There are different ways to approach rebalancing depending on your investment vehicles and preferences:

Proportional Rebalancing

This strategy involves rebalancing exactly back to your target percentages by selling or buying assets in proportion to how much they have deviated.

Partial Rebalancing

Instead of restoring to the full target allocation immediately, you might rebalance only part of the excess or deficit — a gradual approach reduces transaction costs and potential tax impacts.

Cash Flow Rebalancing

Using incoming dividends or new investments to purchase underweighted asset classes instead of selling off overweight assets. This can be more tax-efficient and cost-effective.

Benefits of Maintaining a Rebalanced Portfolio

  • Risk Control: Ensures you don’t unintentionally expose yourself to more risk than planned.
  • Discipline: Encourages buying low and selling high by systematically trimming overgrown assets and adding to underperformers.
  • Alignment to Goals: Keeps your portfolio aligned with your investment objectives, time horizon, and risk tolerance.
  • Improved Returns: Studies show periodic rebalancing may improve risk-adjusted returns over the long term.

Potential Drawbacks and How to Mitigate Them

While rebalancing is beneficial overall, it also comes with challenges:

  • Transaction Costs: Frequent trading can increase fees and commissions. Mitigate this by choosing lower-cost brokers or rebalancing less frequently.
  • Tax Events: Realizing capital gains when selling assets can lead to extra tax bills. Use tax-advantaged accounts for rebalancing or harvest losses to offset gains.
  • Emotional Impact: Rebalancing requires selling assets that have done well, which can be emotionally tough. Keeping a clear plan and objective mindset helps greatly.

Rebalancing Different Types of Investments

Stocks and Bonds

Typically, these are the main components of long-term portfolios, making rebalancing straightforward. Consider sector or style diversification within stocks as part of your review.

Mutual Funds or ETFs

These funds can make rebalancing simpler, as you’re dealing with collections of assets rather than individual stocks. Many brokerage platforms allow for automatic rebalancing among funds.

Real Estate and Alternatives

Less liquid and more complex assets should be reviewed carefully. Use contributions or dividends to rebalance exposure in these asset classes.

Automating Rebalancing

Technology can simplify portfolio maintenance:

  • Robo-Advisors: These platforms automatically rebalance portfolios for you based on your risk tolerance and goals, handling all the calculations and trades.
  • Brokerage Tools: Many online brokers provide alerts when your allocation drifts beyond a certain threshold or allow auto-rebalancing features in managed accounts.

Should You Rebalance During Market Volatility?

Market volatility can shake up portfolio allocations quickly. Whether or not to rebalance amid turbulence depends on:

  • Your time horizon: Long-term investors may be better served by staying the course and rebalancing according to plan.
  • Degree of drift: If allocation has significantly shifted, a disciplined rebalance can mitigate unintended risks.
  • Cost and tax consideration: Avoid over-trading during volatile periods which might increase costs and taxes.

Tailoring Rebalancing to Your Personal Situation

Rebalancing isn’t a set-it-and-forget-it solution. Consider the following:

  • Age and Retirement Horizon: Younger investors might tolerate more drift toward equities, while those approaching retirement may prefer stricter control.
  • Financial Goals: A portfolio for wealth accumulation versus income generation may require different rebalancing frequencies.
  • Market Conditions: While timing the market is notoriously difficult, some investors adjust rebalancing plans in extreme markets with caution.

Final Thoughts

Maintaining a well-balanced long-term investment portfolio is essential for managing risk and pursuing your financial goals. Rebalancing keeps your portfolio aligned with your strategy, enabling you to benefit from market growth while protecting against unwanted exposures.

By understanding your asset allocation, choosing a rebalancing approach that suits your lifestyle, and executing trades thoughtfully, you can position your portfolio to weather market fluctuations and grow steadily over time.

Whether you DIY or use automated tools, revisiting your portfolio with the intent to rebalance is a vital habit that can enhance your investment success.

Maintaining portfolio discipline through thoughtful rebalancing is one of the cornerstones of sound investing — helping you to stay on track no matter which way the market moves.
