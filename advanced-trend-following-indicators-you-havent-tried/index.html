Advanced Trend Following Indicators You Haven't Tried


Trend following is one of the most enduring strategies in trading, praised for its simplicity and effectiveness. Yet, the basic moving averages, MACD, and RSI are often the first—and sometimes the only—tools traders rely on. If you’re looking to elevate your approach and explore less conventional but powerful indicators for trend following, this guide dives into advanced metrics that can sharpen your edge in the markets.

Let’s explore some of the most valuable and relatively underutilized trend following indicators, how they work, and how you might integrate them into your trading toolbox.

1. Detrended Price Oscillator (DPO)

While most trend followers aim to identify the direction of the long-term trend, the Detrended Price Oscillator focuses on eliminating long-term trends to highlight shorter cycles. This makes it particularly useful for recognizing the periodic highs and lows over intermediate time frames.

How DPO Works

The DPO subtracts a moving average (typically a simple or exponential moving average) from the price, adjusted for a lag that centers the oscillator around zero. Unlike other oscillators, DPO does not include the longer-term trend data, enabling traders to focus on price cycles without the distortion of overarching trends.

Application in Trend Following

  • Identifying Reversals in Trend Waves: While DPO is not a trend indicator per se, it can reveal cyclical turning points within an overall trend, helping traders time entries or partial exits.
  • Confirming Pullbacks: During strong trending markets, DPO can be used to identify whether price retracements are likely to hold, signaling continuation.
  • Suitable Time Frames: It works best in medium-term charts (e.g., daily or 4-hour) to spot swings within broader trends.

2. Kaufman’s Adaptive Moving Average (KAMA)

KAMA reacts dynamically to market volatility by adjusting its smoothing factor based on price “efficiency,” a metric that measures trending behavior versus sideways price action.

Why KAMA Stands Out

Most moving averages apply a static smoothing period. KAMA intelligently adapts to changing market conditions—tightening during trending phases for speed and loosening during ranges for noise reduction.

How to Use KAMA

  • Filtering Fake Signals: In choppy markets, KAMA filters out noise better than simple or exponential moving averages.
  • Dynamic Entry & Exit: Traders can trigger trades when the price crosses the KAMA line, exploiting its responsiveness during trends.
  • Crossovers: Comparing KAMA with a longer period moving average can generate robust crossover signals adaptable to volatility.

3. Chande Trend Meter (CTM)

Developed by Tushar Chande, the CTM offers a snapshot of overall market momentum across multiple time frames, enhancing the traditional trend-following framework.

What CTM Measures

Unlike indicators that analyze price in a single timeframe, CTM aggregates momentum signals from the short, medium, and long term, providing a composite score that reflects the strength of the trend.

Usage

  • Trend Confirmation: High CTM values suggest strong alignment across timeframes, validating trade setups.
  • Avoid False Breakouts: When CTM readings are mixed or low, it signals hesitation in the market, alerting traders to potential whipsaws.
  • Risk Management: Use CTM scores to adjust position size or trailing stops depending on trend confidence.

4. Volume Weighted Average Price (VWAP) with Standard Deviation Bands

VWAP is a well-known intraday tool that anchors the average price to both volume and time, giving a more meaningful benchmark than a simple price average. Extending VWAP with standard deviation bands can create a sophisticated trend-tracking envelope.

Why VWAP Bands Matter

Price oscillating above VWAP tends to suggest bullish control, while dips below indicate weakness. By applying standard deviation bands (similar to Bollinger Bands), traders can measure volatility and potential overextension.

Trading Insights

  • Trend Strength: Sustained trading above the upper band points to strong upward momentum.
  • Pullback Entries: Retracements to the VWAP line within an uptrend may provide lower-risk entry points.
  • Intraday & Swing Applicability: Though popular among day traders, the concepts behind VWAP bands can be adapted for swing trading by calculating volume-weighted averages over longer periods.

5. Fisher Transform Indicator

Unlike typical oscillators, the Fisher Transform converts price data into a Gaussian normal distribution, enhancing turning points and reducing lag.

Advantages for Trend Followers

Because trends can pull prices into extended zones, traditional oscillators sometimes struggle with late signals or false reversals. The Fisher Transform accentuates extremes, making momentum shifts or trend breaks clearer.

Implementation Tips

  • Signal Generation: Crossings of the Fisher Transform line over zero can signal potential trend reversals or confirmations.
  • Confluence: Combine it with moving averages or price action for higher probability setups.
  • Customization: Adjust the look-back period to fit trading style—shorter for faster signals, longer for more reliable trend detection.

6. Directional Movement Index (DMI) with ADX Enhancements

While many traders know DMI and the Average Directional Index (ADX), leveraging enhancements and advanced interpretations can breathe new life into these indicators.

Deep Dive into DMI

  • +DI and –DI Lines: Indicate bullish and bearish directional movement.
  • ADX: Measures trend strength without regard to direction.

Advanced Techniques

  • Smoothed ADX: Applying an additional smoothing factor reduces false trend strength signals.
  • Directional Cross Strength: Analyze the magnitude of separations between +DI and –DI to assess trend conviction.
  • Combined Signals: Use DMI/ADX with volume or volatility indicators for filtering trades.

7. Elder Impulse System (EIS)

Created by Dr. Alexander Elder, the Elder Impulse System is a unique blend of trend following and momentum, based on color-coded signals that provide clear guidance.

How the EIS Functions

It uses a combination of EMA direction and MACD histogram color changes to classify market momentum as accelerating, decelerating, or neutral.

Why It’s Useful

  • Intuitive Color Signals: Traders get visual cues like green for trend acceleration, red for deceleration, and blue for neutral.
  • Adaptability: Works across multiple timeframes, helping to keep traders focused on current momentum phases.
  • Trade Management: Beyond entry signals, it can be useful for trailing logic and partial profit-taking.

8. Chaikin Money Flow (CMF) with Trend Adaptations

Money flow indicators blend price and volume to gauge buying and selling pressure. The Chaikin Money Flow measures accumulation and distribution over a set period, usually 20 days.

Using CMF in Trend Following

  • Confirming Trends: Positive CMF values during price uptrends solidify trend sustainability.
  • Detecting Divergences: Declining CMF during rising prices hints that the trend may falter.
  • Trend Strength Assessment: Larger positive or negative CMF magnitudes reinforce momentum strength.

Integrating Advanced Indicators into Your Trading Strategy

Experimenting with multiple indicators can generate conflicting signals, so the key is thoughtful integration.

Steps to Harmonize Your Toolkit

  1. Select Complementary Indicators: Pair an adaptive trend indicator (like KAMA) with a momentum-based one (like Fisher Transform) to balance responsiveness and signal clarity.
  2. Test Timeframe Alignment: Make sure your chosen indicators align well on the trading timeframe you focus on.
  3. Backtest Extensively: Historical market testing will uncover how these indicators perform under different market cycles.
  4. Use Filters and Confirmations: Avoid relying on any one signal blindly. Confirm with volume, price patterns, or higher timeframe trend context.
  5. Keep It Manageable: Avoid overcrowding your chart with too many indicators. Focus on a few that complement rather than contradict each other.

Conclusion: Elevate Your Trend Following Game

If you’ve relied primarily on staples like moving averages or MACD, embracing these advanced trend following indicators can provide a newfound depth of market insight. Whether it’s the adaptive qualities of KAMA, the cycle detection of DPO, or the momentum clarity of the Fisher Transform, these tools offer nuanced views that go beyond traditional analysis.

The journey to proficiency involves trial, error, and adaptation. With disciplined testing and smart application, these lesser-known indicators can help you capture trends more efficiently and with greater confidence.

Celebrate the art and science of trend following—and push the boundaries of your trading prowess with these advanced tools today.
