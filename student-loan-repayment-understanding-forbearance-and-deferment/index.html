Student Loan Repayment: Understanding Forbearance and Deferment


When it comes to student loan repayment, navigating the various options can be overwhelming. Two common forms of relief during financial hardship are forbearance and deferment. Understanding the differences between the two can help borrowers make informed decisions about managing their student loans. In this blog post, we will delve into the intricacies of forbearance and deferment, discussing their eligibility criteria, benefits, and potential drawbacks. Forbearance is a temporary suspension or reduction of loan payments granted by the lender. It provides borrowers with short-term relief during periods of financial hardship. During forbearance, interest continues to accrue on the loan, which means the overall loan balance may increase.

Types of Forbearance

There are two types of forbearance available: discretionary and mandatory.

  1. Discretionary Forbearance: This type of forbearance is granted at the lender’s discretion. Borrowers can request discretionary forbearance when they experience financial difficulties but do not qualify for deferment. Lenders may consider factors like job loss, illness, or other unexpected circumstances when evaluating a forbearance request. Discretionary forbearance is typically granted for short durations, such as three months at a time.

  2. Mandatory Forbearance: Certain circumstances require lenders to grant mandatory forbearance if a borrower meets the eligibility criteria. These criteria include enrollment in a medical or dental internship, serving in AmeriCorps, or participating in a teaching program that qualifies for loan forgiveness. Unlike discretionary forbearance, which is subject to the lender’s approval, mandatory forbearance must be granted if the borrower meets the specified criteria.

Pros and Cons of Forbearance

Forbearance can offer temporary relief for borrowers facing financial challenges. Here are some advantages and disadvantages to consider:

Pros:

  • Provides immediate relief from monthly loan payments
  • Allows borrowers to focus on addressing immediate financial concerns
  • Can be a viable option when other alternatives, such as deferment, are not available

Cons:

  • Interest continues to accrue, potentially increasing the overall loan balance
  • Short-term solution that does not address the underlying debt
  • Limited duration, requiring borrowers to resume loan payments after the forbearance period ends

Deferment: A Pause Button for Repayment

Deferment is another option for borrowers facing financial difficulties. Like forbearance, deferment provides temporary relief from making loan payments. However, there are some key differences between the two.

Types of Deferment

Deferment can be granted for various reasons, including:

  1. In-School Deferment: Students enrolled at least half-time in an eligible educational institution can defer loan payments while pursuing their education. This deferment applies to both undergraduate and graduate programs.

  2. Economic Hardship Deferment: Borrowers facing significant financial hardship, such as unemployment or inability to find full-time employment, may qualify for an economic hardship deferment. This deferment is typically granted in increments of up to one year.

  3. Parental Leave Deferment: Borrowers who become parents and temporarily leave the workforce to care for a newborn or newly adopted child may be eligible for parental leave deferment. This deferment provides relief from loan payments during the specified period.

Pros and Cons of Deferment

Deferment offers borrowers more flexibility than forbearance. Consider these advantages and disadvantages:

Pros:

  • Loan payments are temporarily suspended, providing financial relief
  • Interest may not accrue on certain types of subsidized loans during deferment
  • Longer duration compared to forbearance, allowing borrowers more time to stabilize their financial situation

Cons:

  • Interest continues to accrue on unsubsidized loans during deferment, potentially increasing the overall loan balance
  • Deferment eligibility criteria may be specific and limited
  • Borrowers must reapply for deferment periodically, depending on the type of deferment granted

Making an Informed Decision

Understanding the differences between forbearance and deferment is crucial for borrowers facing financial hardships. While both options provide temporary relief from loan payments, they have distinct eligibility criteria and potential consequences. Borrowers should carefully assess their financial situation and consider long-term implications before choosing forbearance or deferment.

It’s important to note that both forbearance and deferment should be utilized sparingly, as they do not address the underlying debt and may result in increased loan balances. Exploring alternative repayment plans, such as income-driven repayment options, may be more beneficial in the long run.

Remember, managing student loan repayment requires careful consideration and planning. By understanding the nuances of forbearance and deferment, borrowers can make informed decisions that align with their financial goals and circumstances.
