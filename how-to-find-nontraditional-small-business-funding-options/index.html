How to Find Nontraditional Small Business Funding Options


Starting or expanding a small business often requires capital, and while traditional loans from banks are a common route, many entrepreneurs find themselves exploring less conventional paths to funding. Nontraditional small business funding options can offer greater flexibility, faster access to cash, and may suit businesses that don’t fit the standard lending model. This guide explores a variety of these alternative avenues, their advantages, drawbacks, and strategies to secure the best possible support for your business ambitions.

Why Consider Nontraditional Small Business Funding?

Before diving into the options, it’s worth understanding why traditional financing may not always be the best or available choice. Traditional bank loans often come with stringent requirements, including a strong credit history, collateral, and proven revenue streams. When businesses don’t meet these criteria, or when speed and flexibility are paramount, alternative funding sources become essential.

Nontraditional funding can provide:

  • Quicker access to capital: Many alternative lenders can process applications and disburse funds much faster than banks.
  • Flexibility in credit requirements: They may consider different factors beyond just credit scores.
  • Innovative funding solutions: Such as revenue-based financing or crowdfunding.
  • Access to niche investors: Like angel investors or community-focused grants.

With that in mind, let’s explore these pathways.

Crowdfunding: Empowering Communities to Fuel Your Growth

Crowdfunding has become a mainstream method for raising capital, especially for startups and product-driven businesses. Rather than borrowing money, you raise small amounts from many people, generally through dedicated platforms.

How It Works

You create a campaign describing your business idea, funding goal, and deliverables. People contribute funds often in exchange for early products, perks, or equity. There are three main types of crowdfunding:

  • Reward-based: Backers receive products, services, or other perks instead of monetary returns.
  • Equity crowdfunding: Investors receive shares in your company.
  • Debt crowdfunding (peer-to-peer lending): Borrowers receive loans from individuals, repaid over time.

Platforms to Explore

  • Kickstarter: Ideal for creative projects and product launches.
  • Indiegogo: Supports a wide range of businesses and flexible funding.
  • SeedInvest and WeFunder: Focus on equity crowdfunding.
  • LendingClub and Funding Circle: Leaders in peer-to-peer small business loans.

Pros and Cons

  • Pros:

    • Can generate marketing buzz and validate product-market fit.
    • Access to a broad pool of backers or investors.
    • No traditional credit checks.

  • Cons:

    • Time-intensive preparation and ongoing campaign management.
    • Upfront effort to create compelling pitches and marketing materials.
    • Uncertainty — no guarantee of reaching funding goals.

Angel Investors and Venture Capital: Personalized Funding with Strategic Support

For businesses poised for rapid growth or with a novel idea, angel investors and venture capitalists often fill the funding gap that traditional lenders won’t touch. These investors provide capital in exchange for equity and sometimes active involvement.

Who Are Angel Investors?

Angel investors are typically high-net-worth individuals interested in supporting startups. Unlike VCs, angels tend to invest smaller amounts and might invest based on personal interest or industry expertise.

Venture Capitalists (VCs)

VC firms manage pooled funds and invest larger sums. They usually seek startups with scalable growth potential and clear exit strategies, such as an acquisition or public offering.

Finding and Approaching Investors

  • Attend industry networking events, pitch competitions, and startup incubators.
  • Utilize platforms like AngelList to connect with accredited angels.
  • Prepare a compelling pitch deck emphasizing your business model, market size, traction, and team.

Advantages and Potential Drawbacks

  • Advantages:

    • Access to sizeable capital.
    • Strategic guidance, mentoring, and networking.
    • Credibility boost from reputable investors.

  • Drawbacks:

    • Ownership dilution.
    • Pressure for rapid growth and returns.
    • Possible loss of control over strategic decisions.

Revenue-Based Financing: Pay Back with Your Success

Revenue-based financing (RBF) is a flexible alternative wherein investors provide capital that is repaid as a percentage of your business revenue over time. This model aligns with businesses that have consistent sales but may not qualify for loans based on credit or collateral.

How It Works

You receive funds upfront and agree to pay back a multiple of that amount (for example, 1.3x to 2x) through a fixed share of your monthly or weekly revenue. Payments fluctuate with your cash flow, easing pressure during slower months.

Who Offers Revenue-Based Financing?

Some specialized funds and online lenders provide RBF, including companies like:

  • Clearbanc
  • Lighter Capital
  • Bigfoot Capital

Is RBF Right for Your Business?

  • Suitable for online businesses, SaaS companies, and those with recurring revenue streams.
  • Avoids monthly fixed payments typical of traditional loans.
  • Less risk of default as payments adjust to income.

Potential Challenges

  • The total cost of capital may be high due to the repayment multiple.
  • Requires steady revenue to maintain payments.
  • Usually involves some form of revenue verification.

Microloans and Community Development Financial Institutions (CDFIs)

Microloans are small loans typically under $50,000 designed for startups and small entrepreneurs. These loans often come from nonprofits or community-focused institutions emphasizing underserved markets.

What Are CDFIs?

Community Development Financial Institutions focus on economic development in low-income or underbanked areas. They often have more flexible credit requirements and provide additional business support.

Benefits of Microloans and CDFIs

  • More accessible for businesses lacking collateral.
  • Lower interest rates than some online lenders.
  • Additional educational resources and business counseling.

Notable Organizations

  • Accion Opportunity Fund
  • Opportunity Fund
  • Local CDFIs and economic development agencies

Applying for a microloan is often less complex, but it may involve thorough vetting of your business plan and community impact.

Merchant Cash Advances: Upfront Cash for Your Sales

Merchant cash advances (MCAs) offer quick access to capital by purchasing a percentage of future credit card sales. This option suits businesses with significant POS transactions, like retailers or restaurants.

How It Operates

You receive a lump sum upfront and agree to repay by a fixed percentage of daily credit card sales until the advance plus fees are repaid. The payment amount adjusts with your sales volume.

Advantages and Cautions

  • Advantages:

    • Fast funding often within days.
    • No collateral required.
    • Payments based on sales volume ease cash flow strain.

  • Cautions:

    • High effective interest rates can be costly.
    • Repayment terms may not be transparent.
    • Risk of declining cash flow affecting business operations.

MCAs should be carefully reviewed to understand total repayment amounts.

Grants and Competitions: Free Money Fueling Innovation

Business grants are nonrepayable funds provided by governments, corporations, or nonprofits to promote innovation, community development, and diversity. While competitive, grants bring the advantage of no repayment.

Where to Find Grants

  • Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR) grants
  • Small Business Technology Transfer (STTR) programs
  • Local or state economic development grants
  • Industry-specific or demographic-focused foundations

Tips for Success

  • Align your business goals closely with grant criteria.
  • Craft detailed proposals highlighting innovation and impact.
  • Engage with business assistance centers or grant consultants.

Though time-consuming, grants can be a lifeline for businesses in fields like tech, social enterprise, or environmental initiatives.

Equipment Financing and Leasing: Preserve Cash Flow

For businesses needing physical assets like machinery or vehicles, equipment financing or leasing can conserve capital compared to lump-sum purchases.

How It Differs

  • Financing: You borrow money specifically to buy equipment and repay over time.
  • Leasing: You rent equipment with the option to purchase later.

Benefits

  • Preserve working capital for other uses.
  • Potentially more accessible than traditional loans.
  • Predictable monthly payments.

Many banks and specialized lenders offer these options tailored to equipment types.

Alternative Lenders and Online Loan Marketplaces

The rise of fintech has created many online lending platforms focusing on small businesses, often providing fast funding with varying qualification standards.

Common Models

  • Term loans with quick approvals.
  • Lines of credit for ongoing cash flow management.
  • Invoice factoring or financing for receivables monetization.

Popular Platforms

  • OnDeck
  • Kabbage
  • BlueVine

These lenders often approve applicants based on cash flow and business performance rather than credit alone, but interest rates can be higher than traditional banks.

FAQs About Nontraditional Small Business Funding

Q: Can I combine multiple funding sources?
Absolutely. Many businesses use a mix of crowdfunding, microloans, and revenue-based financing to meet their capital needs in stages.

Q: What should I watch out for?
Understand total costs, repayment terms, and how funding aligns with your cash flow dynamics. Avoid hidden fees and inflexible repayment schedules.

Q: How long does it take to get funded?
It varies: crowdfunding campaigns might run for weeks, online lending can be as fast as 24-48 hours, and grants may take months.

Final Thoughts

Tapping into nontraditional small business funding options can be a game-changer for entrepreneurs seeking capital beyond the confines of banks. These alternatives present opportunities for fast access, tailored repayment, and community support, though they come with their nuances and costs. Taking the time to understand each option, matching funding sources to your business model, and preparing thorough applications or pitches will increase your chances of success.

Diversifying your financing strategy can ultimately empower your business to navigate growth confidently, innovatively, and resiliently. Exploring nontraditional funding isn’t just a fallback — it can be a smart strategic move fueling your entrepreneurial dreams.
