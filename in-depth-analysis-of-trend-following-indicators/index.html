In-Depth Analysis of Trend Following Indicators


Trend following indicators are a cornerstone of technical analysis, helping traders and investors navigate volatile markets, make informed decisions, and maximize profits. Despite their widespread use, these indicators often remain shrouded in mystery. In this in-depth analysis, we will demystify these tools and uncover their potential by examining some of the most popular and powerful trend indicators in detail.

Moving Average Convergence/Divergence (MACD)

One of the most widely used trend-following indicators, the MACD was created by Gerald Appel in the 1960s. Essentially, the MACD measures the difference between two moving averages - the 12-day and 26-day exponential moving averages. By analyzing the relationship between these averages, we can determine when the short-term trend crosses over the long-term trend, giving us buy and sell signals.

The MACD fluctuates above and below a “signal line,” which is typically a 9-day exponential moving average of the MACD. A signal line crossover - where the MACD moves above or below the signal line - generates a buy or sell signal. A solid approach is to monitor MACD crossings, combined with crossovers and moving back across the zero line.

Relative Strength Index (RSI)

Developed by J. Welles Wilder in the late 1970s, the Relative Strength Index (RSI) is not a trend-following indicator per se, but rather a momentum oscillator. By comparing the recent historical price activity, RSI calculates the up and down movements in an asset’s price, allowing traders to identify overbought and oversold conditions.

Typically, an RSI reading above 70 signals an overbought condition, while a reading below 30 indicates an oversold one. Trading signals can be triggered when RSI crosses the 70/30 boundary or diverges from price movements.

TrendLines and Their Significance

Trendlines are simple yet powerful tools in trend following analysis. By drawing lines connecting significant lows or highs in an asset’s price chart, we can determine the underlying trend and predict future price movements.

The angle and persistence of a trendline offer clues on the strength of the trend and its probable future direction. As price action breaches the trendline, it generates potential buy or sell signals, depending on whether the breach is upward or downward.

Bollinger Bands

Introduced by John Bollinger in the 1980s, Bollinger Bands envelop the price action within standard deviations from the mean, creating a visual display of volatility and providing insights on trend strength and potential exit points. As price action tests the outermost bands, we can anticipate increased volatility and potential trend reversals.

In conclusion, mastering trend-following indicators requires careful analysis, practice, and patience. Through meticulous study and application of these indicators, traders can hone their craft, improve their understanding of market movements, and achieve consistently profitable trades. By embracing these tools and tactics, you can unlock the depths of trend following strategy and navigate the complex ocean of finance with greater confidence and success.
