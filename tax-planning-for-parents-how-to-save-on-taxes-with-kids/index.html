Tax Planning for Parents: How to Save on Taxes with Kids


Being a parent often means juggling multiple priorities—raising your children, managing expenses, and planning for the future. However, many parents overlook a critical area where they can save money: taxes. The U.S. tax code includes numerous provisions designed to provide financial relief to families with children. By understanding these opportunities and planning your finances strategically, you can maximize your tax savings and free up resources for other priorities.

Let’s dive into the essential tax strategies for parents and explore how child-related credits, deductions, and savings options can help reduce your tax burden.

1. Leverage the Child Tax Credit (CTC)

The Child Tax Credit (CTC) is one of the most well-known tax benefits for parents, offering valuable relief depending on your income and the number of qualifying children you have. For tax year 2023 (and beyond, unless otherwise adjusted), the CTC provides up to $2,000 per qualifying child under age 17.

To fully maximize the CTC:

  • Ensure your children meet the eligibility requirements (they must be U.S. citizens, have valid Social Security numbers, and reside with you for over half of the year).
  • Review your Adjusted Gross Income (AGI). The credit starts to phase out at higher income levels ($200,000 for single filers and $400,000 for married couples filing jointly).
  • Remember, up to $1,500 of the credit is refundable, meaning you can receive this amount even if it exceeds your tax liability.

Keep this credit in mind as a foundational tax-saving tool. Annual changes in tax laws warrant keeping an eye on CTC updates to ensure you’re leveraging this benefit to its fullest extent.

2. Claim the Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)

The Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) is another benefit that can provide significant savings for low- and middle-income parents. The EITC amount varies depending on your income, filing status, and the number of qualifying children in your household.

For example:

  • In 2023, families with three or more children could claim a maximum credit of $7,430.
  • Even households with fewer children are eligible for substantial savings.

The EITC is refundable, meaning it can lead to a refund even if your tax liability is reduced to zero. To qualify, you must have earned income (wages, salary, or self-employment income) and meet specific income thresholds, which differ for single filers versus married joint filers. Be sure to check eligibility each year, as thresholds and amounts are adjusted annually.

3. Don’t Overlook the Dependent Care Credit

For working parents, childcare costs can quickly add up. Fortunately, the federal tax code allows deductions and credits to help alleviate these expenses. The Dependent Care Credit, in particular, enables you to claim a portion of your childcare costs for children under age 13—or for a dependent of any age who cannot care for themselves.

  • As of the 2023 tax year, you can claim up to $3,000 of eligible expenses for one child and $6,000 for two or more children.
  • The percentage of expenses you can claim is based on your income, with a maximum credit of up to 35% for lower-income households.

Eligible costs include daycare, summer camps, after-school programs, and even caregiving services by a nanny. Make sure to obtain your childcare provider’s Taxpayer Identification Number (TIN) and include it when filing your tax return.

4. Set Up a Dependent Care FSA

A Dependent Care Flexible Spending Account (FSA) is another excellent tax-saving strategy for families. Through a Dependent Care FSA offered by your employer, you can contribute pre-tax dollars to cover eligible childcare expenses, effectively lowering your taxable income.

Here’s how it works:

  • For 2023, the IRS allows parents to contribute up to $5,000 per year (or $2,500 if married filing separately) to a Dependent Care FSA.
  • Funds can be used for a wide range of qualifying expenses, from daycare and preschool to after-school programs.

Keep in mind that any amounts reimbursed through your FSA cannot also be claimed under the Dependent Care Credit to avoid double-dipping. To maximize benefits, evaluate whether the credit or FSA provides greater tax savings based on your situation.

5. Take Advantage of Education Tax Benefits

As your children grow, education-related tax breaks become a valuable resource. Here are two primary options to help save on education expenses:

a. The American Opportunity Tax Credit (AOTC)
The AOTC provides up to $2,500 annually for the first four years of a child’s post-secondary education. The credit is partially refundable (up to $1,000), and it covers tuition, fees, and required course materials.

b. The Lifetime Learning Credit (LLC)
Alternatively, parents can claim up to $2,000 per year through the LLC for qualifying education expenses, even beyond the four-year threshold. Note that the LLC phases out at higher income levels.

Additionally, there are other education savings vehicles, such as 529 plans, which allow you to save for college tax-free. Contributions to 529 plans are made with after-tax dollars, but qualified withdrawals for education expenses (including tuition and K-12 expenses) are tax-exempt at the federal level and potentially at the state level.

6. Rethink Filing Status

For single parents, your filing status can have a huge impact on your tax bill. If you qualify as a Head of Household (HOH), you may receive a larger standard deduction and lower tax rates compared to filing as Single. To qualify, you must:

  • Be unmarried or considered unmarried for the tax year,
  • Pay more than half the cost of maintaining your home, and
  • Have a qualifying dependent who lives with you for more than half the year.

Married parents should also evaluate whether filing jointly or separately yields the greatest tax benefits. While joint filing often offers advantages such as higher thresholds for credits, filing separately might make sense for families with unique financial circumstances.

7. Don’t Miss Out on Adoption Tax Benefits

If you’ve adopted children or are in the process of adoption, you can claim the Adoption Tax Credit to offset some of the related expenses. For 2023, the maximum adoption credit is $15,950 per child, which can be used for qualifying expenses such as:

  • Adoption agency fees,
  • Attorney fees, and
  • Expenses associated with traveling to adopt a child.

This credit is non-refundable, meaning it can reduce your tax liability to zero but won’t result in a refund. However, unused portions of the credit can be carried forward over the next five years.

8. Explore the Kiddie Tax Rules

Parents often want to invest for their children, whether through custodial accounts or other financial instruments. While this is a smart way to build long-term wealth, be mindful of the Kiddie Tax rules.

Under these rules:

  • A child’s unearned investment income over $2,500 (as of 2023) is taxed at the parent’s marginal tax rate.
  • Income below this threshold is taxed at the child’s lower tax rate.

To minimize the tax impact, consider tax-advantaged accounts such as 529 plans or custodial Roth IRAs.

9. Maximize Health-Related Deductions

If you face significant medical or dental expenses for your children, you may qualify to deduct these costs on your tax return. To claim the deduction, the total qualified medical expenses must exceed 7.5% of your AGI. Eligible expenses include:

  • Doctor visits and prescription medications,
  • Therapy or counseling for special needs children,
  • Medical equipment, and more.

Planning medical expenses strategically, such as scheduling elective procedures within the same tax year, can help you cross the deductible threshold and save on taxes.

10. Plan for Long-Term Savings with Tax-Advantaged Accounts

Finally, parents should explore ways to maximize tax savings while building financial security for their children. Accounts like Roth IRAs for teens with earned income, Health Savings Accounts (HSAs), and Coverdell Education Savings Accounts (ESAs) can offer valuable tax advantages in the long run.

Final Thoughts

Smart tax planning can make a significant difference for families with children. By claiming all credits, leveraging tax-advantaged accounts, and understanding the nuances of the tax code, parents can reduce their tax burden and redirect those savings toward future needs. Stay informed about updates to tax laws, consult with a tax professional when necessary, and plan ahead to reap every benefit available to you and your family.
