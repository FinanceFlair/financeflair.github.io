Understanding Hyperbolic Discounting in Investing: Evaluating Delayed Rewards


Imagine you are offered two investment opportunities: Option A offers a $100 return in one year, while Option B offers a $120 return in two years. Which one would you choose? If you’re like most people, you might be tempted to go for the immediate reward and choose Option A. After all, who wants to wait an extra year for just $20 more? This tendency to prefer immediate rewards over delayed rewards is known as hyperbolic discounting, and it has significant implications for investors.

What is Hyperbolic Discounting?

Hyperbolic discounting is a cognitive bias that causes people to prefer smaller immediate rewards over larger delayed rewards, even when the latter offers a higher overall value. It is a deviation from the normative economic theory of exponential discounting, where the value of future rewards is discounted at a constant rate over time.

To understand hyperbolic discounting, let’s go back to our investment example. Initially, the $100 return in one year seems more appealing than waiting for two years to receive $120. However, if we were to extend the timeline further, let’s say to five years, the difference becomes more apparent. One hundred dollars in one year may not be as attractive when compared to $120 in two years, but what about when it’s compared to $150 in five years? The longer we have to wait, the more we value the immediate reward.

Implications for Investing

Hyperbolic discounting can have significant implications for investors. It can lead to short-term thinking and impulsive decision-making, which may not align with long-term investment goals. Investors who give in to the allure of immediate rewards may miss out on potentially higher returns available in the future.

Furthermore, hyperbolic discounting can make it challenging to save and invest for retirement. Many individuals struggle to put aside money for their future selves because they prioritize immediate consumption over long-term financial security. By succumbing to hyperbolic discounting, they may find themselves ill-prepared for retirement.

Overcoming Hyperbolic Discounting

While hyperbolic discounting is a natural cognitive bias, there are strategies investors can employ to overcome its negative effects:

  1. Awareness: Recognize that hyperbolic discounting is influencing your decision-making process. By being mindful of this bias, you can make more rational and informed investment choices.

  2. Goal Setting: Set clear long-term financial goals and regularly remind yourself of their importance. By focusing on the bigger picture, you can resist the temptation of immediate rewards.

  3. Automated Investing: Consider setting up automated investment contributions. By automating your savings and investment process, you remove the need for constant decision-making and reduce the impact of hyperbolic discounting.

  4. Diversification: Diversify your investment portfolio to spread risk and increase the likelihood of achieving long-term returns. By having a well-diversified portfolio, you reduce the urge to chase short-term gains.

  5. Seek Professional Advice: Consult with a financial advisor who can provide objective guidance and help you navigate the complexities of investing. A professional can help you stay focused on your long-term goals and avoid the pitfalls of hyperbolic discounting.

Conclusion

Understanding hyperbolic discounting is essential for investors who want to make informed decisions and achieve their long-term financial goals. By recognizing this cognitive bias and implementing strategies to overcome its negative effects, investors can avoid impulsive decision-making and prioritize delayed rewards that offer higher overall value.

Remember, investing is a long-term game, and it’s important to resist the temptation of immediate gratification. By staying focused and disciplined, you can reap the rewards of delayed gratification and build a solid financial future.
