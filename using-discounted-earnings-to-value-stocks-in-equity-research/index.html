Using Discounted Earnings to Value Stocks in Equity Research


The practice of valuing stocks is a cornerstone of equity research, with countless methodologies championed by investors, analysts, and academics. Among these, discounted earnings models—often overshadowed by the more widely referenced discounted cash flow (DCF) analysis—offer a compelling lens through which to appraise a company’s intrinsic value. For those keen on solid, forward-looking analysis in pursuit of undervalued investments, understanding the discounted earnings approach presents real opportunity.

Demystifying Discounted Earnings

At its core, the discounted earnings model centers on estimating a company’s future net profits and then discounting those earnings back to their present value. It draws heavily on the same financial mathematics as the DCF, but instead of projecting streams of cash flows, it zeros in on after-tax earnings—typically net income, earnings per share (EPS), or sometimes free cash flow after debt obligations.

This method isn’t merely a mechanical number-crunching exercise. It’s about constructing a credible forecast of future profitability, grounded in the business’s true economic potential, industry trends, and management prowess. The model gives investors a direct tool to translate earnings projections into actionable equity values, especially useful when cash flow is obscured by heavy capital expenditures, high depreciation, or other accounting quirks.

Why Use Discounted Earnings?

The unique appeal of discounted earnings lies in its simplicity and focus. Cash flow-based models, while comprehensive, can be convoluted by non-operating items or capital structure complexity. Earnings, on the other hand, represent a direct snapshot of what the company retains from its revenue-generating activities. For established businesses with reliably growing profits and transparent accounting, the discounted earnings model can be both parsimonious and illuminating.

This approach can also be beneficial in cases where:

  • The company’s dividend or cash flow policy is unpredictable.
  • Depreciation and capital investment distort cash flow.
  • Analysts have more confidence in projecting earnings growth than capital reinvestment needs.

The Mechanics: How Discounted Earnings Works

Let’s break this down:

  1. Project the Earnings Stream: Typically, analysts forecast net income or EPS for a defined period, say 5-10 years into the future. These estimates reflect organic business growth, margin trends, competitive shifts, and macroeconomic influences.
  2. Select an Appropriate Discount Rate: The discount rate reflects not just the time value of money, but the riskiness of the earnings. Usually, the company’s cost of equity is chosen, arrived at through models like the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM).
  3. Calculate the Terminal Value: After the projection period, a “terminal value” estimates the present value of all earnings beyond the explicit forecast horizon. This is commonly done by assuming a perpetual growth rate, often conservative, to ward off over-optimism.
  4. Sum the Present Values: Each forecasted earnings figure, plus the terminal value, is discounted by the chosen rate and summed up. The result—the fair value equity per share or the total market capitalization.

The Calculation Step-by-Step

Assume you forecast a company will earn $5 per share next year, growing at 7% annually for five years. Choosing a discount rate of 10%, you project the next five years like this:

  • Year 1: $5.00
  • Year 2: $5.35
  • Year 3: $5.72
  • Year 4: $6.12
  • Year 5: $6.55

Discount each value to the present and sum. Next, estimate terminal value—for example, assuming earnings grow indefinitely at 3% after year five, apply the perpetuity formula:

Terminal Value = $6.55 × (1 + 3%) / (10% – 3%) = $6.55 × 1.03 / 0.07 = $96.38

Discount the terminal value back to today and add to the sum of discounted annual earnings. This gives you the intrinsic equity value per share.

Nuances, Advantages, and Drawbacks

When Discounted Earnings Shines

  • Clean Income Statement: For companies with clear, steady earnings unaffected by accounting noise.
  • Stable Payouts: Where management’s reinvestment strategy is understandable, and cash flow is reflective of earnings.
  • Projectable Growth: When future profitability can be forecast with reasonable confidence.

Where It Falters

  • Accounting Manipulation: Earnings can be smoothed or artificially bolstered through one-offs, aggressive revenue recognition, or under-reported expenses.
  • Capital Intensity: For businesses demanding constant, high capital investment, cash flows may diverge significantly from reported earnings.
  • Startups and Cyclicals: Young or volatile firms rarely present steady enough earnings to make the model robust.

Comparison With DCF and Other Approaches

Unlike DCF, which tracks the actual cash available to shareholders, discounted earnings may not fully reflect required reinvestment to sustain growth. However, when DCF inputs are unreliable or when working with earnings-centric industries (banks, insurers), the discounted earnings method is a useful alternative.

Relative valuation models—using P/E ratios or price/book multiples—focus on how the market compares companies to peers. Discounted earnings aims to cut through the noise and deliver an internally consistent, fundamental estimate of intrinsic value. This can highlight market mispricings, especially when sentiment diverges from a company’s genuine earnings power.

Building Reliable Earnings Forecasts: Best Practices

The heart of any discounted earnings model is the believability of its earnings projections. Here are techniques to enhance forecast quality:

  1. Historic Context: Analyze at least 5-10 years of earnings history, adjusting for nonrecurring items and restating for accounting changes.
  2. Industry Perspective: Place earnings in context. Is the business cyclical, or does it command secular growth due to technological advantages or regulation?
  3. Competitive Differentiators: Understand how the company earns its profit. Look at margins, pricing power, and cost structure.
  4. Management Credibility: Consider the historical accuracy of management’s guidance versus actual results.
  5. Macroeconomic Sensitivity: Factor in broader economic variables—rates, commodity prices, consumer demand.
  6. Scenario Analysis: Don’t rely on a single forecast. Assign probabilities to upside and downside cases, offering a range of potential equity values.

Case Study: Applying the Discounted Earnings Model

Suppose you’re researching a mature consumer staples company. The industry is slow-growth but highly resilient, boasting stable profit margins, modest reinvestment needs, and a loyal customer base.

Step 1: Projecting Earnings

  • Assume average revenue growth of 4% per year, with net margins steady at 15% due to robust brand power and minimal pricing pressure.
  • Last year’s net income: $2 billion. Shares outstanding: 500 million. So EPS is $4.
  • Five-year forward projection: By year five, EPS grows to $4.87.

Step 2: Choosing a Discount Rate

  • Company beta: 0.8, risk-free rate: 3%, equity risk premium: 6%
  • Cost of equity = 3% + (0.8 × 6%) = 7.8%

Step 3: Calculating Terminal Value

  • Terminal growth assumption: 2% per year (modest but sustainable)

Terminal Value (Perpetuity Formula):
Year 5 EPS = $4.87
TV = $4.87 × (1 + 2%) / (7.8% – 2%) = $4.87 × 1.02 / 0.058 = $85.63

Step 4: Discounting and Summing

Discount all projected EPS and terminal value at 7.8% to the present. Add together for total per-share equity value. If the market price is well below this figure, you’ve identified a potential bargain.

Avoiding Pitfalls: Issues to Watch Out For

Valuation is as much art as science. Watch for these traps:

  • Overly Optimistic Forecasts: Growth rates taper to the mean; avoid projecting outlier growth indefinitely.
  • Terminal Value Dominance: If the terminal value makes up the bulk of your result, challenge the model’s rigor—it could be driven more by terminal assumptions than near-term reality.
  • Ignoring Dilution: Stock options, convertible debt, or new issuance can erode per-share value if not accounted for.
  • Regulation and Taxation: Changes in corporate tax rates or regulatory policy can shift after-tax earnings substantially.

Integrating Discounted Earnings in Broader Research

While discounted earnings holds merit as a standalone tool, its power multiplies when paired with other valuation lenses in a holistic research process. Analysts often cross-check discounted earnings against:

  • DCF and dividend discount models (DDM) for cash-heavy or payout-oriented firms.
  • Relative valuation for benchmarking against industry norms.
  • Asset-based valuation methods for asset-heavy or liquidation-prone companies.

This combination helps prevent overreliance on any one assumption or model—critical in capital markets where uncertainty and bias lurk around every corner.

Discounted Earnings in the Current Investment Climate

In an environment marked by fluctuating interest rates, economic crosscurrents, and sometimes-unpredictable capital allocation, the discounted earnings method reasserts its simplicity. Its reliance on net earnings ensures analysts focus on the core business, not accounting anomalies or ephemeral market sentiment.

Moreover, as traditional growth companies mature and shift toward profitability (think of recent tech giants reaching positive net income after years of reinvestment), discounted earnings models become more salient.

Wrapping Up: Mastering Discounted Earnings in Equity Research

For equity researchers, investors, and financial enthusiasts alike, discounted earnings offers a robust, intuitive pathway to understanding company value. By centering on the true heart of corporate performance—net profitability—it strips out unnecessary complexity. By discounting this stream at an appropriate rate, it presents a clear, actionable guideline for decision-making.

Executing the discounted earnings model requires sharp analytical skills, a grounded understanding of business economics, and a steadfast commitment to realistic projection. Balance skepticism with optimism, rigor with creativity. When applied skillfully, discounted earnings can illuminate market opportunities that escape more formulaic models and help craft well-founded investment strategies.

Ultimately, the art of stock valuation demands flexibility, discernment, and constant learning. The discounted earnings model, with its focus on the present value of future profitability, is not only a tool, but a philosophy: seek out the real earning power behind each ticker symbol, and you’ll be well equipped to separate fleeting narratives from underlying value.
