How to Use Trend Following for Swing Trading


Trend following and swing trading are concepts that frequently come up in the world of trading and investing, but they’re often misunderstood or oversimplified. Digging into how these two approaches intersect offers practical strategies for traders aiming to capture substantial gains while limiting losses. This guide walks through why trend following principles fit so well with swing trading and how to execute them intelligently, with actionable insights for both beginners and more seasoned market participants.

What Is Trend Following?

Trend following is a trading methodology based on the idea that markets often move in extended trends—either up or down—and that traders can profit by identifying and aligning with these movements. Instead of predicting turning points, trend followers wait for a confirmed direction and stick with it until evidence suggests the trend has ended.

The core philosophy is simple: “The trend is your friend.” This means analysts watch for patterns of higher highs and higher lows in uptrends, or lower highs and lower lows in downtrends, to determine market direction.

Swing Trading In a Nutshell

Swing trading focuses on capturing gains over a few days to a few weeks. Swing traders seek to ride a “swing” within an overall trend, aiming to profit from expected moves within that time frame. It’s more active than buy-and-hold investing but less frenetic than day trading.

Swing traders tend to use technical analysis, scanning charts for price patterns, volume spikes, and other signals that indicate a move is underway or imminent. While fundamentals aren’t ignored, technical signals are usually the primary driver of trading decisions.

How Trend Following Enhances Swing Trading

At its core, swing trading looks to exploit short- to medium-term price momentum. Trend following amplifies the odds of success because it aligns your trades with the prevailing force in the market. Instead of fighting the tape or attempting to catch minor reversals, swing traders who follow trends can:

  • Stack the odds in their favor by trading in harmony with dominant market momentum
  • Filter out low-probability setups that go against the grain
  • Stay in winning trades longer when the trend is robust

Let’s break down some of the best ways to apply trend following concepts to swing trading.

Step 1: Identifying Tradable Trends

Recognizing Trends

Spotting a true trend is both an art and a science. Here are some classic methods:

Price Action
Scan for a consistent series of higher highs and higher lows in uptrends, or lower highs and lower lows in downtrends. Zoom out to spot the broader context—daily and weekly charts often reveal trends that short-term noise hides.

Moving Averages
A rising 20-day or 50-day simple moving average is a strong visual indicator of a developing trend. Price staying above a rising moving average suggests bullish conditions, while the opposite implies bearish momentum.

Trendlines
Draw lines connecting swing lows in an uptrend or swing highs in a downtrend. If price respects these trendlines, the trend is probably intact.

Volume Analysis
Rising volume during trend-confirming moves signals broad market participation, strengthening the trend’s validity.

Filter Out Choppy Markets

Avoid markets where price oscillates sideways and trends are unclear. Range-bound conditions often lead to whipsaws. Patience pays: only swing trade when a clear trend emerges.

Step 2: Trade Setup – Entry Points

After a trend is established, swing traders need an entry that balances risk and reward. Here are effective entry strategies:

Pullback Entries

One powerful way to join a trend is to wait for a pullback—an opposing move against the main trend—then enter when momentum resumes.

Suppose a stock’s in an uptrend. Watch for it to pull back to a rising moving average, support zone, or trendline. When price bounces, enter with a stop loss just below the swing low.

Breakout Entries

Some swing traders prefer breakout entries. These take place when price breaks above resistance (in an uptrend) or below support (in a downtrend), signaling the next “leg” higher or lower.

Breakout trades can be volatile, with some false starts (“fakeouts”). It pays to confirm breakouts with a spike in volume or multiple timeframes agreeing on the move.

Using Price Patterns

Classic chart patterns like flags, pennants, and triangles are helpful. In an uptrend, a bullish flag accompanied by declining volume often resolves with a sharp move upward. Entry is triggered when price clears the flag’s resistance.

Step 3: Setting Stop Losses and Managing Risk

No swing trading strategy survives without disciplined risk management. Trend following doesn’t mean blindly holding on—market conditions change, and reversals happen. Here’s how to protect yourself.

Position Sizing

Only risk a small percentage of your trading capital per swing trade—commonly 0.5-2%. This way, even a string of losses won’t deplete your account.

Placing Stops

Place a stop loss where the trend thesis no longer holds. In an uptrend, a swing trader might put the stop just below a recent swing low, under a moving average, or beneath a trendline. In downtrends, stops go above recent swing highs.

Trailing Stops

As the trade works out, consider using a trailing stop to lock in profits. Either move the stop up below each successive swing low or use a technical indicator like the ATR (Average True Range) to adjust the stop dynamically as volatility shifts.

Step 4: Timing Exits

One of the hardest parts about swing trading with trend following is knowing when to exit. Here are strategies for making that choice:

Target-Based Exits

Before entering a trade, define a target based on support/resistance levels, measured moves, or Fibonacci extensions. Once the price reaches your target, either exit the entire position or scale out partially.

Trailing Stop Exits

Letting profits run is a hallmark of trend following. Use a trailing stop as described above to give the trend room to continue while gradually protecting profit.

Reversal Patterns

Watch for reversal signals—bearish engulfing candles in an uptrend, bullish engulfing patterns in a downtrend, or breaks of key trendlines. These may indicate it’s time to exit.

Combining targets with trailing stops can be powerful: Take some profit at a pre-set target, then let the rest of the position ride until the trailing stop is hit.

Step 5: Evaluating and Refining the Strategy

Swing trading using trend following isn’t static. Markets evolve, and so should your tactics.

Keep a Trading Journal

Document every trade: your rationale, entry, exit, stop placement, and results. Review winning and losing trades alike to spot patterns and improve over time.

Track Performance Metrics

Analyze win rate, risk/reward ratio, and average hold time. If you find you’re regularly stopped out before a trend resumes, consider giving positions more space with wider stops or smaller position sizes.

Adapt to Volatility

Some strategies work better in calm markets, others in periods of high volatility. Update your approach based on prevailing market conditions—using tighter stops when markets are choppy, or riding trends longer when volatility is high.

Sample Trend Following Swing Trading Plan

Here’s an example framework many swing traders use with trend following elements:

  1. Scan for Stocks in Strong Trends:
    Use a screener to find stocks above their 50-day moving average with steady pattern of higher highs and higher lows.

  2. Look for Pullbacks:
    Wait for a pullback towards the 20-day moving average or recent support area.

  3. Set Entry Trigger:
    Buy when price bounces off support or a bullish candle forms, confirming the uptrend is resuming.

  4. Establish Stop Loss:
    Place stop just below the recent swing low or under the 20-day moving average.

  5. Determine Targets:
    Calculate expected move using chart patterns or previous resistance levels.

  6. Manage Position:
    Move stop up as price advances. Take partial profits at target, let remaining shares run with a trailing stop.

By sticking to a defined plan, emotional decision-making is minimized, and results become more consistent.

Psychological Edge: Riding Trends in Swing Trading

Swing trading with a trend following approach requires calm discipline. FOMO (“fear of missing out”) can tempt traders to chase entries; fear of giving back profits can prompt premature exits. Cultivating patience—waiting for a real trend, pulling the trigger on a pre-defined edge, and letting winners run—is key.

Often, big wins from strong trends offset the small losses from trades that didn’t work out. It’s the classic “cut losses short, let profits run,” brought to life.

Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

Overtrading Sideways Markets

Trading in non-trending conditions leads to churn. Use filters—such as ignoring stocks with a flat 20-day moving average—to stay out of chop.

Tight Stops Leading to “Death by a Thousand Cuts”

While controlling risk is essential, stops set too close are vulnerable to market noise. Anchor your stops outside recent volatility, and size positions accordingly.

Impatience With Trends

A trend following approach will inevitably have periods where there’s little to do. Forcing trades when the market isn’t trending can erode profits. Stay vigilant, but only act when criteria are met.

Practical Tools and Indicators

Several technical tools mesh well with trend following swing trading. Here’s a focused list:

  • Moving Averages (SMA and EMA): Identify trend direction and dynamic support/resistance.
  • Average True Range (ATR): Gauge recent volatility; useful for adjusting stops.
  • Relative Strength Index (RSI): Spot when a pullback is overextended.
  • MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence): Confirm trend momentum and spot turnarounds.
  • Volume Profile: Show areas of high trading interest—helpful support and resistance zones.

Using two or three tools together can reduce false signals and strengthen entries.

Backtesting and Forward Testing

Testing a trend following swing system on historical data helps reveal its strengths and weaknesses. Backtest your rules on different stocks and market conditions. Forward test with simulated or small real trades to ensure it performs as expected in real-time environments.

Look for:

  • Robustness across various stocks (not just recent winners)
  • Acceptable drawdown and return patterns
  • Logical, explainable results (not curve-fit oddities)

Final Thoughts

Integrating trend following techniques into a swing trading strategy creates a dynamic, rules-based system with the potential for significant profits. By identifying clear trends, timing entries on pullbacks or breakouts, setting logical exits, and maintaining strict risk control, swing traders can tilt the playing field in their favor.

The real edge comes from adhering to a disciplined process. Markets will always be unpredictable on a short-term basis, but trading in harmony with prevailing trends—and letting those trends do the heavy lifting—offers a reliable pathway to success.

As with all trading endeavors, continual learning, self-reflection, and adaptability remain vital. Study your trades, learn from patterns, and refine your approach. There are no guarantees, but trend following for swing trading puts you on the right side of the market’s momentum more often than not.
