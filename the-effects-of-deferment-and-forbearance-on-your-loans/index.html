The Effects of Deferment and Forbearance on Your Loans


Managing loan payments can sometimes become a challenge due to unexpected financial hardships, career changes, or educational pursuits. In such cases, options like deferment and forbearance offer temporary relief by allowing borrowers to pause or reduce their loan payments. While these tools provide much-needed breathing room, they come with specific implications that can impact the overall cost and timeline of your loan repayment. Understanding these effects thoroughly can help you make informed decisions and maintain a healthy financial trajectory.

What Are Deferment and Forbearance?

Before diving into their effects, it’s essential to define these two terms, as they are frequently confused but have different eligibility criteria, processes, and consequences.

  • Deferment is a period during which you can temporarily postpone loan payments. Interest might or might not accumulate during this time, depending on the loan type.

  • Forbearance allows you to pause or reduce payments for a specific duration, usually when you don’t qualify for deferment. Interest generally accrues on all loan types during forbearance.

Both options are designed to provide temporary relief without defaulting on your loan, but they don’t erase or reduce the total amount you owe.

When to Consider Deferment or Forbearance

Knowing when deferment or forbearance fits your situation can prevent needless financial strain. Typical scenarios include:

  • Deferment eligibility often applies to borrowers returning to school, undergoing economic hardship, active military duty, or unemployment.
  • Forbearance is generally considered when you face short-term financial difficulties but don’t meet deferment qualifications—such as medical emergencies, temporary unemployment, or natural disasters.

If you’re unsure which route is better for you, it’s usually wise to contact your loan servicer to discuss your options thoroughly.

Interest Accrual: A Critical Difference

One of the most impactful aspects to understand is how interest behaves during these periods.

Deferment Interest Policies

With federal student loans, interest may not accrue during deferment if you have subsidized loans (like Direct Subsidized Loans), meaning the government pays the interest. However, for unsubsidized loans, private loans, and certain federal loans like PLUS loans, interest generally continues to build up during deferment.

Forbearance Interest Policies

Interest accrues on all types of loans during forbearance, meaning your loan balance increases. This can cause the total repayment amount to grow significantly, especially if the forbearance period is extended.

Effects on Loan Balance and Repayment Timeline

Loan Balance Growth

Because unpaid interest adds up during deferment and forbearance (except subsidized loan deferment), your loan balance can grow, a process referred to as capitalization once interest is added to the principal. Essentially, you start paying interest on a higher amount, which increases monthly payments over time.

Extended Repayment Period

Using deferment or forbearance pauses the repayment clock, which extends the total time you’ll spend paying off your loan. This may seem beneficial when money is tight, but it means you’ll be in debt longer, leading to more interest paid over the life of the loan.

Impact on Credit Score

Entering deferment or forbearance doesn’t directly harm your credit score; it’s preferable to missing payments entirely. Loan servicers report both deferment and forbearance statuses to credit bureaus as “in good standing.” However, if you stop communicating with your lender or fail to apply for these options properly, your account could become delinquent, negatively affecting your credit.

Deferment and Forbearance: Federal vs. Private Loans

Federal Loans

Federal loans often come with better consumer protections. They allow for deferment and forbearance under specific conditions, with clear terms regarding interest accrual, and provide options like income-driven repayment afterward.

Private Loans

Private lenders may offer forbearance or deferment, but terms are highly variable and less standardized. Interest almost always continues to accrue, and approval is discretionary, requiring negotiation directly with your lender.

How to Apply for Deferment or Forbearance

Applying involves:

  1. Contacting your loan servicer: They provide the necessary forms and explain eligibility.
  2. Filling out the application: You may need to supply proof of your situation, like income statements or school enrollment.
  3. Awaiting approval: Continue making payments until you receive formal approval to avoid delinquency.
  4. Confirming the terms: Understand interest accrual, duration, and repayment resumption post-deferment/forbearance.

Strategies to Minimize Negative Effects

If you need to use deferment or forbearance, consider these tactics:

  • Pay the interest if possible: Even small interest payments during deferment or forbearance prevent capitalization.
  • Use forbearance sparingly: Reserve it for brief, unavoidable financial hardship to avoid prolonged loan timelines.
  • Explore alternative repayment plans: Income-driven plans or loan consolidation might provide better long-term outcomes.
  • Communicate proactively: Regular dialogue with your loan servicer helps avoid surprises and keeps your account in good standing.

Alternatives to Deferment and Forbearance

While these are common tools, some alternatives might better suit your situation:

  • Income-Driven Repayment Plans: Allow monthly payments based on your income, often reducing payment amounts without accruing extra interest.
  • Loan Refinancing: Can reduce interest rates and lower monthly payments though it may extend terms and remove federal protections.
  • Partial Payments: Continuing to pay what you can helps reduce interest accumulation and keep your account current.

Real-Life Implications: Examples

Consider Anna, who faced unemployment and applied for a six-month deferment on her subsidized federal loan. During this time, no interest accrued, so her principal remained steady. Conversely, Mark used forbearance on a private loan during a medical crisis; interest accrued every month, nearly doubling his balance over 12 months.

These examples highlight how specific loan types and the chosen relief method impact your financial outcome.

Final Takeaway

Deferment and forbearance offer crucial support during times of financial strain, but their use affects your loan balance, interest payments, and repayment timeline differently. By understanding the nuances between these options, monitoring interest accrual, and maintaining communication with your loan servicer, you can navigate loan challenges more successfully and minimize long-term financial burdens.

Managing your loans wisely by evaluating all options ensures that temporary relief today doesn’t translate into overwhelming debt tomorrow.

By grasping these complex dynamics, you equip yourself for smarter loan management decisions—ultimately leading to greater financial stability and peace of mind.
