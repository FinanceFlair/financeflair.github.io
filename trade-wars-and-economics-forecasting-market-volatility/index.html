Trade Wars and Economics: Forecasting Market Volatility


Trade wars, the shifting tectonics of international commerce, have recently become headline-grabbing topics. This particular Cocktail Has Bitters 1 of global economic interactions can cause market volatility and ripple effects far beyond the countries directly involved.

Trade wars can be traced back to ancient structures of trade like the Silk Road, yet the instant, interconnected world we live in today makes their impact exponentially more pronounced.

The thing about trade wars is that they seldom occur in isolation; they’re intertwined with other economic phenomena. Tariffs and import taxes are the instruments used within trade wars. These instruments can create uncertainty about what qualifies as a trade barrier, which eventually leads to violence in the marketplace.

The Expectations Theory of Interest 2 explains the volatility that emerges from these uncertain times. In essence, it claims that we base our investment decisions on the future value of money - both in terms of buying and selling currencies that might be subject to fluctuations in value due to regulatory changes. It’s like a game of poker: the more uncertain the cards on the table (or in this case, the economic status), the greater the risk of the market shifting suddenly.

But how do we navigate the increasingly uncertain economic seas that a trade war injects into the market? The question is, can we forecast market volatility before it hits the fan?

One conceivable solution is applied econometrics which analyses economic timing 3. Econometrics seeks to quantify and explain the effects of definable variables and expressions of economic phenomena. Econometricians examine data, test theories, and use statistical equations to explore phenomena. This particular blend of economics and mathematics allows us to identify patterns in historical data which can potentially predict future market trends.

Another strategy involves understanding disruptions to market dynamics. Typically, a change in trade dynamics can be pegged to shifts in production costs, prices, employment, and GDP. As these elements interplay over time, they create a market environment rich in fluctuations, yet possessing a discernible pattern to those able to read the data.

In the end, understanding and navigating the choppy waters of an increasingly interconnected world economy starts with economics. By focusing on economic theories such as supply and demand, Theory of Comparative Advantage 4, and even the Domino Theory 5, investors can build a framework for understanding how trade wars can catch economies in their crossfire.

While these theories don’t magically guard against market volatility, they do offer a roadmap for how global trade works, the mechanisms behind different economic structures, allowing for predictive modelling. By examining the data critically and constantly, we can plot trends and perhaps mitigate risk before it materializes in market volatility.

In the end, the challenge of forecasting market volatility is one that will likely remain a cat-and-mouse-game between global economies. The trade war is not just a fight between countries, but also a battle of mindsets, an ideological clash between protectionism and free trade, forever shaping the ever-shifting sands of the global economic landscape. But by continually seeking to understand and adjust our investments to these changes, we can navigate an increasingly unpredictable economic world.

  1. Cocktail Has Bitters: A somewhat whimsical representation of the interwoven concepts of trade, economy, and market affectations.
  2. The Expectations Theory of Interest: Advocates that the real yield on a debt security reflects the market’s current expectations of future inflation.
  3. Economic Timing: Timing refers to making the optimal moves to maximize gains or minimize risk in the stock market.
  4. Theory of Comparative Advantage: A principle in economics that asserts that an entity can profit by focusing on what it can produce most efficiently, even if other entities can produce the same product more efficiently.
  5. Domino Theory: The idea that if one economy adopts protectionist measures, other economies will follow the same course, leading to a chain reaction of economic restrictive measures.
