Kickstarter vs Indiegogo: Which Crowdfunding Platform Is Right for You?


Crowdfunding has rewritten the rules of entrepreneurship, transforming big ideas into real businesses with the help of backers across the globe. Two platforms dominate the landscape: Kickstarter and Indiegogo. Both offer creators the chance to fund projects, test concepts, and cultivate communities—but the devil, as they say, is in the details.

If you have a revolutionary gadget, a boundary-pushing creative project, or a quirky product looking for market validation, your choice of platform can influence everything from how much you can raise to the kind of supporters you attract. In this comprehensive guide, we’ll delve deep to highlight the differences, strengths, and challenges of Kickstarter and Indiegogo, helping you decide where your next big idea belongs.

The Basics: Setting the Stage

Kickstarter launched in 2009, focusing on creative endeavors including tech innovations, music albums, films, and design projects. It prides itself on fostering creativity and community. Indiegogo, founded in 2008, takes a broader approach: alongside tech inventions and creative works, you’ll find health devices, social causes, and even personal finance campaigns.

At first glance, they seem similar: both allow you to set up a campaign page, share your story via videos and description, offer rewards for backers, and promote your project. But beyond these basics, each platform has evolved its own ecosystem, rules, and advantages.

Eligibility and Project Types: Who Gets In?

Kickstarter’s Curation

Kickstarter is curated. Only certain project categories are allowed, and your campaign must comply with strict guidelines. It doesn’t permit charity, financial investment offers, or general business expenses. The focus is on tangible outcomes—a finished product, book, album, etc. Submissions require review; you need to demonstrate a clear timeline, budget, and deliverable rewards.

  • Best For: Inventors, artists, and creators with a clear, creative vision and concrete outcomes.
  • Not For: Fundraisers for personal needs, charities, or offering equity stakes.

Indiegogo’s Flexibility

Indiegogo is considerably more open. There’s no lengthy review process; campaigns go live with fewer restrictions. Nearly any idea can find a home, whether it’s an innovative gadget, a new film, or even a medical expense fundraiser (though for charitable needs, Indiegogo partners with GoFundMe).

  • Best For: Innovators who need a flexible and fast launch, those whose projects don’t fit Kickstarter’s requirements, or creators seeking ongoing preorders.
  • Not For: Projects violating basic legal guidelines.

Verdict: If your idea is unconventional or time-sensitive, or you simply want a lower barrier to entry, Indiegogo stands out. Creators seeking community validation and brand prestige often prefer Kickstarter.

Funding Models: All-or-Nothing vs. Flexible

Kickstarter: The All-or-Nothing Approach

Kickstarter pioneered the all-or-nothing funding model: set a goal, and if you reach it, you get the money; if not, nobody is charged. This creates urgency, encourages backers to share, and protects everyone if funding falls short of what’s needed to deliver rewards.

  • Pros: Motivates backers; minimizes risk for creators who need a minimum budget.
  • Cons: If you miss your target, you get nothing—even if you’re within sight of your goal.

Indiegogo: Flexible or Fixed

Indiegogo lets campaigners choose between flexible funding—where you keep whatever you raise, even if you fall short—and fixed funding, similar to Kickstarter’s model.

  • Pros: Flexible funding helps if you can proceed with partial funds or want to gauge market interest without risk of losing everything.
  • Cons: Risk that underfunded projects disappoint backers, affecting creator reputation.

Which Should You Pick?

  • All-or-nothing: Opt for this if you must hit a minimum budget to deliver, or want to harness the motivating power of urgency.
  • Flexible: Useful for creators confident they can fulfill promises regardless of funding level, or who want to run more experimental campaigns.

Audience & Backer Communities

Kickstarter’s Legacy and Trust

Kickstarter boasts more high-profile successes, a reputation for serious creative projects, and a backer community accustomed to supporting innovation. Many backers actively seek out new projects, helping campaigns gain organic momentum. The platform’s curated nature creates a premium, trustworthy atmosphere that attracts major media coverage.

Indiegogo’s Global Reach and Diversity

Indiegogo’s backer community is broader and more international, buying into everything from tech gadgets to social impact campaigns. The audience here is used to seeing a wide range of ideas; this can be great for products outside the creative arts.

Backer Behavior Differences

Because of Kickstarter’s stricter guidelines and all-or-nothing model, its backers tend to scrutinize projects more closely. On Indiegogo, the diversity of campaigns draws a more eclectic crowd—but sometimes less engagement for creative-specific projects.

Platform Features and Tools

Kickstarter’s Project Dashboard

Kickstarter supplies detailed analytics, tools for sending updates, backer management, and integrations with fulfillment partners. The platform’s activity feed and media integrations make it easy to showcase progress.

Indiegogo’s Flexible Tools

Indiegogo offers a powerful dashboard, optional InDemand (for continued preorders post-campaign), and robust promotional tools. They support add-ons, secret perks for targeted backer groups, and seamless integrations with marketing partners.

Unique Features

  • Kickstarter: Social discovery via categories, “Projects We Love” badges, deep integration with creative arts.
  • Indiegogo: Pre- and post-campaign marketing tools, Google Analytics integration, retargeting ads.

Fees and Payment Processing

Kickstarter’s Fee Structure

Kickstarter takes 5% of funds raised, with payment processing fees around 3-5%. All pledges go through Stripe and are processed only if the campaign is successful.

Indiegogo’s Fee Structure

Indiegogo has the same 5% platform fee, with payment processing fees similar to Kickstarter. However, if you don’t hit your goal in a fixed campaign, all money is returned to backers; in flexible campaigns, you keep everything (less fees, of course).

Currency Support

Kickstarter mainly supports creators in select countries, with set currencies. Indiegogo accepts more currencies and allows more creators worldwide.

Marketing and Exposure

Kickstarter’s Social Power

Kickstarter campaigns can be spotlighted by the platform, earning “Projects We Love” badges and newsletter promotions. Media often cover high-performing or unusual Kickstarter projects, giving campaigns viral potential.

Indiegogo’s Marketing Ecosystem

Indiegogo has built an ecosystem for ongoing sales: InDemand lets you continue accepting orders after your campaign ends, capturing latecomers and maximizing momentum. The platform also offers marketing support and paid promotional boosts.

External Promotion Still Matters

Regardless of platform, most campaign traffic comes from creator outreach: social media, email, PR, partnerships. Both Kickstarter and Indiegogo give you basic SEO via campaign pages, but successful creators often invest in paid ads, influencer outreach, and launch updates.

Transparency, Risks, and Backer Protection

Kickstarter’s Transparency Focus

Kickstarter demands creators explain how they’ll use the money, share risks, and provide regular updates. Its all-or-nothing model helps filter out less-prepared projects.

Indiegogo’s Openness

Indiegogo recommends, but doesn’t require, the same level of creator commitment. With flexible funding, backers need to trust creators’ realistic promises.

Both Platforms: Buyer Beware

Neither Kickstarter nor Indiegogo guarantees fulfillment. Backers are not “buying” products—they’re supporting ideas with the hope of reward. Delays and failures happen. However, the two platforms differ in project support and response to campaign issues.

Success Stories (and Notable Failures)

Kickstarter Successes

Kickstarter has launched mega-hits: Pebble Watch, Exploding Kittens, Coolest Cooler, and thousands of smaller creators now household names. These successes reflect the platform’s ability to attract buzz and media attention.

Indiegogo Hits

Indiegogo has produced standouts like Flow Hive, Microdrone, and Ubuntu Edge. The platform is especially strong in high-tech consumer products, innovation, and projects from global teams.

Learning from Failures

Both platforms have seen headline-grabbing failures. Some creators overpromise and underdeliver; others run into manufacturing nightmares or blow their budgets. Crowdfunding is inherently risky for both creators and backers.

Customer Support and Community

Kickstarter: Community First

Kickstarter’s staff actively support creatives, celebrate innovation, and nurture communities. Forum discussions, regular newsletters, and robust help docs give creators a sense of belonging.

Indiegogo: Business-Focused Support

Indiegogo offers extensive support but with a business and product focus—there are more tools related to lead generation, sales funnels, and ongoing engagement. This appeals especially to tech startups and “serial crowdfunders.”

How to Choose: A Practical Decision Framework

Faced with the Kickstarter vs Indiegogo decision, step back and consider these key questions:

1. What’s Your Project Type?

  • If you’re making an album, a board game, or a creative work, Kickstarter’s audience and rules likely fit. If your project is a gadget, social cause, or unorthodox idea, Indiegogo opens more doors.

2. Do You Need All-Or-Nothing Funding?

  • For projects requiring a critical funding threshold, Kickstarter protects you from overcommitting. If partial funding works, or your project is already underway, Indiegogo’s flexibility wins out.

3. Where Are Your Backers?

  • Kickstarter has a huge US and European audience drawn by culture and design. Indiegogo’s reach is more international and tech-focused.

4. How Fast Do You Want to Launch?

  • Kickstarter’s approval process takes time. Indiegogo lands you live faster, and is less strict about who gets in.

5. Are Ongoing Sales Important?

  • Kickstarter campaigns end on closing day. On Indiegogo, you can keep selling via InDemand.

6. How Much Do You Value Community?

  • If building a tribe around creative work or joining a network of artists is your goal, Kickstarter has the better-established network.

7. What’s Your Plan for After the Campaign?

  • For long-term product businesses, Indiegogo’s ongoing sales tools are a bonus. If you’re one-and-done, Kickstarter’s prestige might help elevate your project’s profile.

Final Thoughts

Kickstarter and Indiegogo each offer a unique set of strengths. Kickstarter’s curation, all-or-nothing model, and creative community deliver massive launch day momentum—and media buzz—for projects that fit its guidelines. Indiegogo’s flexible rules, global audience, and post-campaign tools appeal to creators who crave agility and ongoing sales beyond the initial campaign.

Choosing between these platforms isn’t just about rules and fees. It’s about how your project fits into each ecosystem, the kind of supporters you want to reach, and your long-term goals. Dive into both, look at recent campaigns similar to yours, analyze their comments and backer engagement, and weigh the risks and rewards.

When your campaign launches, the real work begins: telling your story, delivering on promises, and turning bold ideas into tangible results. Let your choice of platform fuel—not dictate—your creative hustle.

Ready to launch? Be bold, stay transparent, and build something that inspires your supporters. Whether you claim your spot on Kickstarter or carve a niche on Indiegogo, your journey from spark to reality starts with a single, audacious step.
