Short Term Trading: Using Moving Averages Convergence Divergence (MACD)


Moving Averages Convergence Divergence, commonly known as MACD, is a widely-used indicator for stock market trading. Developed by Gerald Appel in the 1960s, the MACD is designed to capture trends and momentum shifts, helping traders make better-informed decisions.

Understanding and implementing the MACD can significantly enhance your short term trading strategy. This technical analysis tool not only helps in picking the right entry points but also aids in managing your investments by offering precise signals.

What MACD Tells About Price Movements

MACD, a trend-following momentum indicator, calculates the difference between exponentially moving averages. The default settings involve a 12-day, 26-day, and 9-day exponential moving averages.

When a security’s price moves above or below the two moving averages, the MACD crosses its signal line, generating trading signals. Here’s how a typical setup looks:

  1. Bullish trend: The MACD is above the signal line, indicating a potential uptrend. Longer the MACD bars, the stronger the bullish momentum.
  2. Bearish trend: The MACD is below the signal line, hinting at a bearish trend. Longer the bearish MACD bars, the stronger the downward momentum.
  3. Divergence: When the price curve diverges from the MACD line, it could signify an impending price reversal, be it an uptrend or downtrend.

Using MACD for Short Term Trading: A Step-by-Step Guide

  1. Sizing Your Trade: MACD triggers buy or sell signals when the fast and slow moving averages cross or when the MACD line crosses the signal line. But, you must also consider the volume and volatility of the security to size your trade.

  2. Timing: Timing the trade, especially in short term trading, is crucial. Use ‘crossovers’ to time your trades. The crossovers indicate a trend change – the 12-day and 26-day moving averages crossing over each other signifies a shift in the medium-term trend.

  3. Managing Risk: Risk management is a crucial aspect of trading. Set your stop-loss orders to limit your losses on any given trade. You could place it below your entry price for a bearish trade and above your entry for bullish trades.

  4. Using Divergences: Divergences on the MACD chart can be instrumental in anticipating price reversal. A bullish divergence occurs when prices make lower lows but the MACD line makes higher lows, indicating an oversold condition. Conversely, a bearish divergence signals an overbought condition.

Enhancing Your MACD-Based Strategy

While MACD is helpful, adding other indicators will give you a comprehensive insight into the market dynamics. Moreover, blending it with other technical indicators like RSI, Bollinger Bands, and Fibonacci Retracements can significantly enhance your trading skills.

Optimizing your trading strategy using the MACD, however, takes practice and consistency. Regularly analyzing market data and adjusting your strategy accordingly can lead to better decision-making and consistent profitability.

In conclusion, the MACD serves as a potent tool in your short term trading toolkit. By utilizing it effectively, you can improve your entry and exit timing and strengthen your trading decisions. Yet always remember, no strategy yields guaranteed success, making it crucial to stay attentive to market changes and adapt your strategies accordingly. Happy trading!
