The Role of Women in Emerging Market Economies


As the world witnesses the rapidly evolving landscape of emerging market economies, a significant trend has been unfolding. Women, long overlooked and marginalized in the economic sphere, are stepping into the limelight as a force to be reckoned with. The catalyst for this phenomenon is multifaceted, propelled by changing social norms, access to education, and technological advancements. Democratization of Economic Opportunities

In the past, women's entry into the workforce was often limited due to lack of education, social and cultural constraints, and societal expectations. However, the tide is shifting in emerging markets such as India, China, and Southeast Asia. Education has become more accessible, and women are armed with the knowledge and skills to pursue careers in diverse fields, including the sciences, technology, engineering, and mathematics (STEM). This shift is altering the dynamics of the workforce, with women embracing entrepreneurial ventures and assuming leadership roles. The proliferation of technology has also empowered women in emerging markets. Mobile commerce and digital transactions have increased access to financial services, allowing women to manage their finances independently and engage in e-commerce. According to a report by IBM, women entrepreneurs in developing countries are leveraging digital platforms to launch their businesses, mainly in the areas of retail and food services. Similarly, mobile banking has enabled women to access financial services previously inaccessible to them due to remote locations or limited mobility. Digital wallets and mobile payment systems such as M-Pesa in Kenya and bKash in Bangladesh have not only increased financial inclusion but also allowed women to transact money with better control over their finances. A summary of a report in The Economist stated "Statistics from countries such as Tunisia, Morocco and Egypt show that between 2017 and 2020, mobile-phone-based microloans to women grew by over 100%. Societal norms, once limiting women's economic participation, are gradually changing as well. There's a growing understanding within traditional families and communities that women's participation in the workforce enhances household income, education, and overall quality of life. Women in many emerging markets are now taking up roles in various industries, shifting family roles and expectations.
