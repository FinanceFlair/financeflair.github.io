How to Adjust Trend Following Systems for Bear Markets


Trend following is one of the most time-honored trading strategies, deeply rooted in the concept of capitalizing on sustained price directions. It thrives during bull markets, as rising trends provide ample opportunities for profits. However, bear markets, characterized by persistent downward price action and heightened volatility, pose unique challenges to trend following systems. To survive—and even excel—when the tides turn bearish, traders need to adjust their approach with precision. This guide explores how to fine-tune trend following systems to navigate bear markets effectively.

Understanding the Challenge of Bear Markets for Trend Followers

Before diving into adjustments, it’s essential to grasp why bear markets test trend followers so severely:

  • Frequent False Signals: Downward trends often include aggressive rallies (“bear market rallies”) that can trigger premature entries.
  • Whipsaw Movements: Prices may swing widely, generating numerous stop-outs that erode gains.
  • Reduced Trend Strength: Apart from brief rallies, overall trends may lack the clear, sustained momentum seen in bull markets.

Because traditional trend following systems typically depend on identifying strong, uninterrupted trends, bear markets can result in more losses and lower overall performance if no adaptations are made.

Key Principles for Adjusting Trend Following to Bear Markets

1. Shift the Focus to Shorter Time Frames

In bear markets, long-term trends can be volatile and unreliable. Moving to shorter time frames can help capture smaller, more frequent trends that develop in a choppy market.

  • Why: Shorter trends are more common during bearish phases due to swift changes in sentiment.
  • How: Instead of relying solely on 50-day or 200-day moving averages, consider incorporating 10-day or 20-day averages to detect emerging trends quicker.
  • Benefit: This reduces the risk of late entries and large stop losses.

2. Implement Stricter Entry and Exit Criteria

Enhanced precision can minimize false signals and reduce losses:

  • Confirm with Multiple Indicators: Combine moving averages with momentum oscillators such as RSI or MACD to confirm trend strength.
  • Use Volume Confirmation: A strong trend is often accompanied by significant volume. Avoid entering trades on weak volume.
  • Trailing Stops: Adopt tighter trailing stops to protect against sharp reversals.

3. Incorporate Downtrend-Specific Filters

Incorporate system rules that recognize the bear market context:

  • Trend Direction Filters: Only take short positions or exit longs when long-term trend indicators confirm the bearish environment.
  • Avoid Long Positions When in a Downtrend: This prevents getting caught in prolonged declines.
  • Adjust Position Sizing: Reduce trade size to minimize exposure during uncertain periods.

4. Embrace Counter-Trend Trades Selectively

While trend following is inherently pro-trend, bear markets reward flexibility:

  • Bear Market Rallies: Use counter-trend strategies to capitalize on powerful, short-lived upward moves within the downtrend.
  • Set Tight Targets and Stops: These trades should aim for quick profits with minimal risk.

However, counter-trend trades should complement, not replace, core trend following rules.

5. Monitor Volatility and Adjust Stops Accordingly

Bear markets often bring increased price volatility:

  • Adaptive Stop Losses: Use volatility-based stops (like ATR multipliers) instead of fixed stops.
  • Avoid Stops That Are Too Tight: Being stopped out frequently diminishes returns.
  • Avoid Stops That Are Too Loose: Overly wide stops risk large drawdowns.

Balancing this is key to maximizing net profits.

Practical Steps to Implement Adjustments

Step 1: Backtest Your System on Bear Market Data

No adjustment should be done blindly. Testing your existing and modified trend following system over historical bear markets helps:

  • Identify weaknesses.
  • Measure improvements.
  • Understand expected drawdowns and profit potential.

Step 2: Optimize Moving Average Lengths for Bear Markets

Experiment with various moving average lengths on bear market samples:

  • Test shorter moving averages (10-20 days) for entry and exit signals.
  • Compare performance against traditional average periods.

Step 3: Combine Trend and Momentum Indicators

Try combining trend measures (MA crossovers) with momentum indicators:

  • Only enter when both align with a downward trend.
  • Use RSI oversold/overbought levels to avoid counterproductive entries.

Step 4: Adjust Position Sizing Dynamically

Create rules to reduce exposure during choppy phases:

  • Lower the size of each trade when volatility exceeds a threshold.
  • Increase size when clearer trends re-emerge.

Step 5: Use Trailing Stops Based on Volatility

Instead of fixed points, use ATR-based trailing stops:

  • Example: Exit when price declines 1.5 times the current ATR below peak.
  • Adjust multiplier based on historical bear market volatility.

Advanced Techniques to Enhance Bear Market Trend Following

Incorporating Market Regime Filters

Use other quantitative signals to detect market regimes:

  • Volatility Regimes: Identify whether the market is in a calm or turbulent state.
  • Sentiment Indicators: Incorporate investor sentiment data to avoid crowding into false moves.
  • Macro Indicators: Use economic data to validate the likelihood of trend continuation.

Adjust your trend following rules accordingly—for instance, becoming more conservative in high-volatility, low-confidence regimes.

Multi-Asset Diversification

Bear markets often affect assets unevenly:

  • Apply adjusted trend following systems across a diversified basket of assets (stocks, bonds, commodities, currencies).
  • This can smooth overall portfolio performance and reduce drawdowns.

Hybrid Systems: Trend Following with Mean Reversion Elements

Marry trend following with mean reversion strategies to balance capturing prolonged trends and exploiting short-term counter-trend moves prevalent in bear markets.

  • For example, add rule-based exits or partial profit-taking on extreme RSI levels.
  • Use oscillators like Stochastic to time minor retracements.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid When Adjusting for Bear Markets

  • Overfitting: Tweaking parameters excessively on past bear data may produce poor forward results.
  • Ignoring Psychological Stress: Bear markets test discipline; ensure your system and position sizing accommodate your risk tolerance.
  • Chasing Every Bounce: Not all rallies represent opportunities; patience is valuable.
  • Neglecting Risk Management: Adjusting systems should never come at the expense of solid risk controls.

A Sample Bear Market Adjusted Trend Following Strategy

Here’s a simplified example combining many of these concepts:

  • Entry: Enter short when the 10-day moving average crosses below the 20-day moving average AND RSI is below 50.
  • Exit: Exit when the 10-day MA crosses back above the 20-day or if price increases above a trailing stop set at 1.5 x ATR.
  • Position Size: Allocate 50% of typical size to reduce exposure.
  • Trading Universe: Focus on highly liquid equity ETFs with high volume.
  • Regime Filter: Only activate this system when the 200-day MA slope is negative.

This setup aims to identify shorter, reliable downtrends while managing risk and reducing false signals prevalent in bear markets.

Final Thoughts

Adjusting trend following systems for bear markets demands a nuanced blend of flexibility, precision, and discipline. The goal is not just survival, but to extract meaningful alpha where traditional trend following may falter. Less reliance on rigid, long-term trend markers, tighter stops calibrated to volatility, selective counter-trend trading, and dynamic position sizing create a robust framework for enduring tough market cycles.

By incorporating these adjustments and continuously monitoring performance relative to evolving market conditions, traders can maintain an edge—even when the market heads south. Bear markets aren’t just periods of loss; with a properly adapted trend following system, they can also be fertile ground for opportunity.
