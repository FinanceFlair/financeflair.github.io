What Are the Pros and Cons of Variable Life Insurance?


Variable life insurance is a unique and flexible life insurance product that offers both death benefit protection and the potential for cash value growth. It combines the features of traditional life insurance with the investment opportunities available in various market-driven subaccounts, which can offer the policyholder growth potential. However, like any financial product, variable life insurance has its benefits and limitations.

In this post, we’ll explore the pros and cons of variable life insurance in detail, helping you decide if it’s the right option for your long-term financial goals. Whether you’re considering a policy for yourself or a loved one, it’s crucial to understand how it works, its risks, and its rewards.

What Is Variable Life Insurance?

Variable life insurance is a permanent life insurance policy that allows policyholders to allocate a portion of their premiums into different types of investment subaccounts. These subaccounts can include stocks, bonds, mutual funds, and other types of investment vehicles. Unlike traditional whole life policies that have fixed premiums and cash values, variable life insurance offers more flexibility, with the opportunity for the policyholder to adjust the death benefit, premium payments, and investment choices.

The key feature of a variable life insurance policy is the investment component. The value of the cash value account can fluctuate based on the performance of the underlying investments. This makes it possible for policyholders to benefit from market growth, but it also exposes them to the risk of market downturns.

Pros of Variable Life Insurance

1. Investment Growth Potential

One of the main advantages of variable life insurance is its investment growth potential. With a traditional whole life policy, the cash value grows at a guaranteed rate set by the insurance company. However, with variable life insurance, the policyholder can invest the cash value in various subaccounts, potentially earning a higher return depending on market conditions.

This allows for the possibility of a larger cash value accumulation, which can be used to increase the death benefit, pay premiums, or provide a source of income in the future.

2. Flexibility in Premium Payments

Variable life insurance offers more flexibility compared to traditional life insurance policies. Policyholders have the ability to adjust their premium payments based on their financial situation. If you’re in a tight financial spot, you can reduce the premium payments or even skip them (as long as there’s enough cash value to cover the cost of insurance). Conversely, if you have a surplus of funds, you can increase your premium payments to build cash value faster.

This flexibility makes variable life insurance appealing for individuals who want a policy that can adapt to changes in their financial circumstances over time.

3. Adjustable Death Benefit

Variable life insurance policies typically offer an adjustable death benefit. This means that policyholders can increase or decrease the death benefit depending on their needs. For example, if you experience a significant life change—such as getting married or having children—you may want to increase your death benefit to provide more financial protection to your loved ones.

Additionally, as the cash value grows, policyholders may use it to supplement their death benefit, ensuring that their beneficiaries receive a larger payout.

4. Tax-Deferred Growth

The cash value component of a variable life insurance policy grows on a tax-deferred basis. This means you do not have to pay taxes on the growth of the investments within the policy until you withdraw the funds. If you use the funds to pay premiums or increase the death benefit, they remain tax-deferred, providing an additional tax advantage.

In the case of a loan taken against the cash value of the policy, the loan is not subject to tax unless it exceeds the cost of insurance or the policy lapses. This can be a significant benefit for individuals looking to build wealth over time while minimizing their tax liabilities.

5. Potential for Accessing Cash Value

Another benefit of variable life insurance is the ability to access the accumulated cash value. Policyholders can borrow against the cash value or even surrender the policy for its cash value (minus any surrender charges or outstanding loans). This can provide an additional source of funds in case of an emergency or if you need capital for another investment.

However, it’s important to note that any loans taken against the policy’s cash value will accrue interest, and the loan balance may reduce the death benefit if not repaid.

6. Customizable Investment Options

One of the most attractive features of variable life insurance is the range of investment options available. Policyholders can choose from a variety of subaccounts, including stocks, bonds, and mutual funds, to create a diversified portfolio tailored to their risk tolerance and investment goals. This level of customization allows individuals to take a hands-on approach to managing their life insurance policy’s cash value, unlike the more passive growth seen with whole life policies.

Cons of Variable Life Insurance

1. Investment Risk and Market Volatility

While the potential for high returns is an advantage of variable life insurance, it also comes with significant risks. Since the cash value is linked to the performance of underlying investments, it is susceptible to market volatility. Poor market performance or a downturn can result in negative returns, which may affect the growth of the cash value and the death benefit.

If the policyholder’s investments underperform, they may need to pay higher premiums to maintain the death benefit or to keep the policy in force. In extreme cases, poor performance could lead to the policy lapsing, especially if the cash value is depleted and premiums are not paid.

2. High Fees and Expenses

Variable life insurance policies tend to have higher fees compared to traditional life insurance policies. These fees can include:

  • Mortality and expense charges: These fees cover the cost of insurance and other administrative expenses.
  • Investment management fees: The investment subaccounts come with their own fees, which can erode investment returns.
  • Surrender charges: If you decide to cancel the policy within a certain period (usually the first 10 to 20 years), you may incur a surrender charge.

The combination of these fees can significantly reduce the overall performance of the policy, especially in the early years of the policy.

3. Complexity and Lack of Transparency

Variable life insurance can be difficult to understand, especially for those who are not familiar with investment products. The policy’s structure can be complex, with multiple components including the death benefit, cash value, premium payments, and investment options. Additionally, the performance of the investments can be hard to track, and the policyholder may not always be fully aware of how fees and charges are affecting their returns.

Because of this complexity, some policyholders may not be able to take full advantage of the policy’s potential. It’s essential to fully understand the policy and its terms before committing to it.

4. Risk of Insufficient Cash Value

As mentioned earlier, the cash value of a variable life insurance policy is directly tied to the performance of the investments within the subaccounts. If the investments underperform or market conditions are unfavorable, the cash value may not grow as expected. In some cases, the policyholder may even be required to pay higher premiums to keep the policy active.

If the cash value falls too low, it may not be enough to cover the cost of insurance, leading to a policy lapse or a reduced death benefit. This creates the risk that the policyholder may not have enough insurance coverage when it’s needed most.

5. Premium Payments Can Increase

Although variable life insurance offers flexibility, the premiums can increase if the policy’s investments do not perform well. Unlike term life or whole life policies, where premiums are fixed, variable life premiums can fluctuate depending on the policy’s cash value performance. This means that policyholders must be prepared for the possibility of higher premiums in the future if their investments do not produce the expected returns.

6. Potential for Lapse if Not Managed Properly

Since variable life insurance relies on the cash value to cover the cost of insurance, policyholders must manage the policy carefully. If the cash value is not sufficient to cover the cost of insurance and premiums, the policy could lapse. This is a real risk for policyholders who do not monitor their investments and cash value closely. Without adequate planning and ongoing management, the policy may not provide the expected death benefit when the policyholder passes away.

Is Variable Life Insurance Right for You?

Variable life insurance can be a powerful tool for individuals seeking flexible life insurance with the potential for investment growth. However, it’s not suitable for everyone. The complexity, market risk, and high fees may be a deterrent for some policyholders, particularly those who prefer simplicity and stability in their financial planning.

Before purchasing variable life insurance, it’s essential to thoroughly understand the policy, including the investment options, fees, and potential risks. Consulting with a financial advisor can help you determine whether this type of life insurance aligns with your long-term goals and risk tolerance.

Ultimately, the decision to choose variable life insurance should be based on your personal financial situation, investment preferences, and the level of risk you’re willing to accept. If you’re looking for a policy that combines insurance protection with the opportunity for growth, and you’re comfortable with managing your investments, variable life insurance could be an excellent choice. However, if you prefer a more straightforward, lower-risk option, other types of life insurance may be more suitable.
