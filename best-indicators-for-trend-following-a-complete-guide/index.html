Best Indicators for Trend Following: A Complete Guide


Trend following remains one of the most popular and time-tested strategies in trading and investing. At its core, trend following involves identifying the direction of an asset’s price movement and making trades that align with that momentum. Successful traders rely on reliable indicators to determine the strength, direction, and potential duration of trends. This guide delves into the best indicators for trend following, explaining how they work, how to interpret their signals, and how to blend them into a powerful trading strategy.

What Is Trend Following?

Before jumping into the indicators, it’s useful to understand the essence of trend following. Unlike speculative trading strategies that try to predict market tops and bottoms, trend followers aim to ride the wave. The fundamental belief is that assets which have been trending will continue to do so for some time, and catching these moves yields profitable opportunities.

Effective trend following requires timing: entering once a trend is confirmed and exiting when momentum fades. Indicators are essential tools for these decisions, reducing guesswork through analysis of price data, volume, and momentum.

Characteristics of Effective Trend Following Indicators

Not all technical indicators are made equal when it comes to trend following. Ideal indicators for this strategy typically share these characteristics:

  • Clear trend identification: They distinguish trending markets from ranging or choppy ones.
  • Lag but confirm: While trend indicators often lag (because they average past data), they should confirm the trend’s presence reliably.
  • Quantifiable signals: They generate signals that can be objectively interpreted for buying and selling.
  • Complementarity: Used in combination, they should cover trend direction, strength, and potential reversals.

With these criteria in mind, here are the best indicators across different categories for trend following.

Moving Averages: The Backbone of Trend Following

Moving averages smooth out price data by averaging past prices over a set period, filtering noise and highlighting trends.

Simple Moving Average (SMA)

The SMA calculates the average closing price over a fixed number of periods. For example, a 50-day SMA averages the last 50 closing prices.

  • Use: Identify trend direction by price relative to the SMA.
  • Interpretation: Price above the 50-day SMA indicates an uptrend; below suggests downtrend.
  • Crossovers: A popular signal is when a shorter SMA crosses above a longer SMA (golden cross) signaling bullish momentum, or below (death cross) signaling bearish momentum.

Exponential Moving Average (EMA)

The EMA assigns more weight to recent prices, making it more responsive than the SMA.

  • Use: Faster trend detection and timelier signals.
  • Popular EMAs: 20-day and 50-day EMAs are widely followed for trend following.
  • Combining EMAs: The crossover between a 20-day and 50-day EMA smooths out false signals while confirming trends.

Weighted Moving Average (WMA)

The WMA weights recent prices linearly, providing even more sensitivity than EMA.

  • Use: For traders who want highly responsive trend indication.
  • Consideration: With increased sensitivity comes increased noise, so it’s most effective when combined with other indicators.

Strategy Insights

  • Moving averages work best in trending markets and can generate false signals in sideways markets.
  • Employ filters to avoid trades during low-trend environments by incorporating momentum indicators or volatility filters.

Momentum Indicators: Measuring Trend Strength

Identifying a trend’s direction is one thing; confirming its strength is another. Momentum indicators evaluate the velocity of price movement, indicating whether the trend is accelerating or losing steam.

Average Directional Index (ADX)

The ADX is a popular indicator that measures trend strength without indicating direction.

  • Range: 0 to 100, where values above 25 typically indicate a strong trend.
  • Components: Directed Movement Index (DMI) includes +DI and –DI, which show trend direction.
  • Usage: A rising ADX with +DI above –DI indicates a strong bullish trend; the opposite indicates a bearish trend.

Relative Strength Index (RSI)

While RSI is primarily used to determine overbought or oversold conditions, it also reflects momentum strength.

  • Range: 0 to 100; traditionally, above 70 is overbought, below 30 is oversold.
  • Use in trend following: Strong trends often keep RSI in the overbought or oversold region for extended periods.
  • Interpretation: During an uptrend, RSI valorizes staying above 40 and bouncing off it as support; in a downtrend, it may remain below 60.

Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD)

MACD shows the relationship between two EMAs (typically 12 and 26 periods).

  • Signal Line Crossovers: MACD crossing above its 9-day EMA signal line can be a bullish momentum signal, and vice versa.
  • Histogram: Shows distance between MACD and the signal line, highlighting momentum changes.
  • Usage: Confirms that momentum supports the identified trend.

Volume-Based Indicators: Volume Confirms Trends

Volume plays an essential role in trend following by affirming the conviction behind price moves.

On-Balance Volume (OBV)

OBV adds volume on up days and subtracts on down days, creating a cumulative volume measure that often leads price.

  • Use: Rising OBV confirms an uptrend; falling OBV supports a downtrend.
  • Divergences: If price is rising and OBV is flat or declining, it signals a weakening trend or potential reversal.

Volume Weighted Average Price (VWAP)

VWAP is the average price adjusted for volumes.

  • Use: Traders use VWAP to determine the average price at which an asset has traded during the day.
  • Trend following use: Consistent price trading above VWAP indicates bullish dominance, below VWAP signals bearish pressure.

Trend Oscillators: Combining Trend and Momentum

Some indicators fuse elements of trend identification and momentum to provide smoother, clearer signals.

Parabolic SAR (Stop and Reverse)

Parabolic SAR plots dots above or below price to indicate trend direction and possible reversal points.

  • Usage: When the dots are below price, it implies an uptrend; above price implies downtrend.
  • Advantages: Provides trailing stop levels to protect profits.
  • Limitations: Can produce misleading signals during choppy markets.

Ichimoku Kinko Hyo

A comprehensive indicator that maps trend, momentum, and support/resistance levels.

  • Components: Includes Tenkan-sen, Kijun-sen, Senkou Span A and B (cloud boundaries), and Chikou Span (lagging line).
  • Usage: Trading signals are generated based on price and cloud positions, crossovers of conversion and base lines, and trailing support/resistance from clouds.
  • Benefits: Clearly distinguishes trending vs ranging markets and potential entry/exit points.

Combining Indicators for a Robust Trend Following Strategy

While each indicator offers valuable insights independently, combining them enhances reliability.

Sample Setup

  • Trend direction: Use the 50-day EMA to determine primary trend direction.
  • Trend strength: Confirm strength with ADX above 25.
  • Momentum check: Use MACD to find momentum alignment.
  • Volume confirmation: Check OBV for supporting volume trends.
  • Entry timing: Use Parabolic SAR or Ichimoku crossovers to time entries.

This multi-layer approach balances lagging and leading indicators, improving signal quality and reducing false entries.

Avoiding Common Pitfalls

False Signals in Sideways Markets

Trend indicators can produce whipsaws when the market lacks clear direction. Incorporating filters like ADX or RSI can help avoid this.

Overfitting with Too Many Indicators

More isn’t always better. Using too many correlated indicators can muddy decision-making. Stick to a few complementary tools that answer different questions.

Ignoring Market Context

Indicators work best within certain market regimes. Always consider larger macro trends, news, and events in your decision-making process.

Practical Tips for Trend Followers

  • Use multiple timeframes: Confirm a trend on a longer timeframe (daily, weekly) before trading on a shorter one.
  • Set clear entry and exit rules: Define when to enter on trend confirmations and when to exit on reversals or weakening momentum.
  • Risk management: Use stop losses, position sizing, and trailing stops to protect capital.
  • Backtest strategies: Validate your indicator combinations on historical data to identify strengths and weaknesses.

Conclusion: Crafting Your Ideal Trend Following Toolbox

Trend following relies on identifying, confirming, and capitalizing on sustained price movements. The best indicators for this approach blend trend identification (moving averages), momentum measurement (MACD, RSI, ADX), and volume validation (OBV, VWAP).

Understanding how to interpret these indicators individually and in combination is critical. This approach gives traders the confidence to follow trends with disciplined entries, strong confirmation, and risk controls that protect against unpredictable market behavior.

Mastering trend following indicators demands practice, patience, and adaptation, but it remains a highly effective trading philosophy in all markets and timeframes. With the guidance above, traders can develop frameworks customized for their risk profile and trading goals, tapping into the powerful forces of market momentum.
