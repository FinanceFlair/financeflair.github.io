How to Use the Excess Earnings Method for Corporate Valuation


Establishing the value of a company is a crucial aspect of various financial activities, including mergers, acquisitions, investments, and restructuring. One such approach to determine the worth of a business is the Excess Earnings Method (EEM). In this article, we will explore the process of using the Excess Earnings Method for corporate valuation, presenting the rationale behind the technique and walking you through the steps involved.

The Rationale Behind the Excess Earnings Method

The Excess Earnings Method is built on the notion that the value of a company is equivalent to the present value of its future excess earnings over a specific asset-based baseline. This approach assumes that a company has two primary sources of income: earnings from its assets (referred to as the asset multiple) and unrelated earnings (earning a return on the investment of excess capital).

By subtracting the net income one would expect from the asset base alone from the actual net income, we determine the excess earnings. Consequently, we can calculate the value of the business by discounting these excess earnings to their present value.

Steps to Use the Excess Earnings Method

To apply the Excess Earnings Method, follow these steps:

  1. Identify the asset multiple

To calculate the asset multiple, start by determining the value of the company’s assets. You can do this using various methods, such as the market approach, income approach, or cost approach.

  1. Determine the company’s unlevered beta

Unlevered beta represents the inherent risk of a company’s investments, excluding the impact of debt. This figure can be obtained from various data providers or by calculating it using historical returns and fluctuations.

  1. Assume a capital structure

Assume a hypothetical capital structure, as this calculation does not take debt into account. This step simplifies the valuation process.

  1. Discount the excess earnings

Subtract the income one would expect from the asset base alone from the overall net income to determine the excess earnings. Calculate the discounted rate by using the capital asset pricing model (CAPM), incorporating the unlevered beta, risk-free rate, and the market-risk premium.

Discount the excess earnings using the calculated rate to arrive at their present value, which represents the value of the company.

  1. Refine assumptions

To enhance the accuracy of your valuation, consider refining the assumptions in steps two, three, and four based on the company’s unique aspects, such as its history, industry, and management quality.

Restrictions and Limitations

While the Excess Earnings Method offers a clear and logical process for determining a company’s value, it comes with important restrictions and limitations. Firstly, the chosen asset multiple can impact the final valuation significantly. Therefore, it is essential to use a suitable and accurate method to derive this figure.

The assumed capital structure may not hold true in all scenarios, as debt levels could vary over time. Moreover, the EEM assumes a steady growth rate for the excess earnings, which might not be achievable in practice.

In conclusion, the Excess Earnings Method serves as a versatile and intuitive approach to determining the value of a company.
