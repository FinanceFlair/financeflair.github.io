Real Assets and Economic Resilience: A Case Study


Economic resilience has emerged as a crucial concern for policymakers and business leaders in recent years. Amidst economic shifts and uncertainties, those who can adapt and thrive are the ones with the necessary foundation to ride out turbulent times and emerge stronger. Real assets play a pivotal role in this capacity, not merely as financial instruments but as tangible contributors to economic resilience. This blog delves into a case study that exemplifies how real assets can foster economic resilience and also explores the concept of productive non-use of assets. Understanding Economic Resilience Economic resilience refers to a region's or a business's ability to withstand economic shocks or stressors. It involves adapting to changing economic conditions, leveraging opportunities, and minimising the depth or duration of economic downturns. In essence, resilience is about being able to bounce back and forth between different states of economic activity. This concept is vital in today's globally interconnected and ever-changing market landscape, where economies can face stressors from various angles, including changes in government policies, technological advancements, environmental factors, and global economic shifts. The Role of Real Assets in Economic Resilience Real assets, as the name suggests, are tangible assets that can be directly seen, touched, or used. Examples include properties, farmlands, and precious metals. While financial assets provide liquidity, real assets offer a different kind of security and potential opportunity. Unlike financial assets, real assets typically increase in value over time and can provide a steady income stream. The value of real assets can appreciate due to factors such as rental income from leased properties or mining royalties. This steady income stream can help businesses and economies weather financial storms, thereby enhancing their resilience. Case Study: Real Assets in a City's Economic Revitalization Consider the city of gentrified Brooklyn, New York, as an example of how real assets contributed to its economic revitalization. Brooklyn, especially its boroughs of Williamsburg and Bushwick, underwent significant revitalization in the early 2000s. Several factors contributed to this revamp, but one key aspect was the strategic use of real assets. Developers and entrepreneurs saw an opportunity to transform derelict industrial or commercial properties into trendy apartments, restaurants, art galleries, and boutiques. The end result was the transformation of what once seemed like unprofitable assets into thriving hubs of business and culture. Understanding Profitable Non-Use of Assets In the above scenario of Brooklyn's revitalization, the concept of productive non-use of assets comes into play. Productive non-use refers to the act of utilizing an asset in non-traditional or creative ways that generate income or increase its value. In Brooklyn, the old warehouses were repurposed beyond their original use of storage or industrial production. By repurposing these buildings as residential and commercial spaces, the new uses generated significant revenue and contributed to the area's economic growth. This example highlights how creative thinking can turn previously unproductive or underutilized assets into significant contributors to a locality's economy. Harnessing Resilience through Real Assets Economies can build resilience by leveraging real assets. Here are a few key strategies:

  1. Diversification Spread investments across different sectors, geographic locations, or types of assets. This way, if one sector or location experiences a downturn, the diversified portfolio can continue to generate revenue, mitigating the economic impact.
  2. Environmental Sustainability Products and systems that are sustainable in terms of environmental impact are not only beneficial for the planet but can also be more resilient. Green infrastructure and eco-friendly technologies offer less dependence on non-renewable resources, thereby reducing vulnerability to price hikes and supply chain disruptions.
  3. Long-Term Planning Future-proofing through strategic long-term planning is key. Investing in projects that contribute to economic and social development can provide continuous returns and serve as a buffer against economic shocks. Conclusion Economic resilience is not just about weathering storms, but about leveraging opportunities for growth and development. Real assets, when properly utilized, can be the foundation upon which resilience is built. The case of Brooklyn's revitalization serves as a testament to the potential of real assets in fostering economic resilience through strategic, productive use even in the face of adversity. By incorporating these strategies of diversification, environmental sustainability, and long-term planning, nations and businesses can build enduring economic resilience, ensuring they remain resilient and thrive through economic uncertainty.
