How to Make Your Side Hustle Tax-Deductible


When you start a side hustle, it’s thrilling to watch your ideas come to life, especially when it starts generating extra income. However, as exciting as it is, one of the less glamorous aspects is figuring out how taxes work. The good news is that there are several strategies you can use to make your side hustle tax-deductible, which could ultimately save you money at the end of the year.

Running a side hustle means you’re essentially operating a small business on the side. As such, the IRS allows you to deduct certain business expenses. This can significantly reduce the amount of tax you owe. In this post, we’ll break down the essentials of making your side hustle tax-deductible, explore the various expenses you can deduct, and offer tips on keeping everything organized for tax season.

Understand the Basics of Business Expenses

Before diving into specific deductions, it’s important to understand what qualifies as a business expense. The IRS defines a business expense as any expense that is both “ordinary and necessary” for the operation of your business. “Ordinary” means the expense is common and accepted in your field of business, and “necessary” means the expense is helpful and appropriate for your business.

For example, if you run a freelance writing business, expenses such as a laptop, internet connection, and subscriptions to writing software may qualify as tax-deductible because they are necessary for the job. If you’re a photographer, you could deduct the cost of camera equipment, editing software, and studio rental fees.

The key is that these expenses should be directly related to your side hustle. Personal expenses or those unrelated to your side business won’t qualify.

Keep Detailed Records and Documentation

A crucial step in ensuring that your side hustle is tax-deductible is keeping detailed records of all your business-related expenses. The IRS requires that you maintain proper documentation to back up your deductions. This includes receipts, bank statements, invoices, and any other paperwork that can show proof of your spending.

Without clear records, you could be in trouble if the IRS decides to audit you. So, invest in tools or apps to track your business expenses, categorize them, and keep everything organized throughout the year. Some people prefer using simple spreadsheets, while others might opt for accounting software that automatically categorizes expenses and generates reports.

Common Deductions for Side Hustles

Now that we understand the importance of tracking expenses, let’s dive into some common tax-deductible items for side hustlers.

1. Home Office Deduction

If you use part of your home exclusively for your side hustle, you may be eligible for a home office deduction. This deduction allows you to write off a portion of your home expenses, such as rent or mortgage interest, utilities, insurance, and property taxes.

To qualify for this deduction, your home office must be used regularly and exclusively for your business. This means it cannot double as a guest room or a family space. The IRS offers two ways to calculate this deduction:

  • Simplified Method: You can deduct $5 per square foot of your home office, up to 300 square feet. This makes calculating the deduction easy.
  • Regular Method: This method allows you to deduct a percentage of your home’s total expenses (e.g., utilities, mortgage, or rent). The percentage is based on the proportion of your home that is used for business.

2. Vehicle Expenses

If you use your car for business purposes, you may be able to deduct car-related expenses. The IRS offers two ways to claim these deductions:

  • Standard Mileage Rate: You can deduct a fixed amount per mile driven for business purposes. For example, in recent years, the IRS has set the rate at 56 cents per mile, but it may vary yearly.
  • Actual Expense Method: This method involves deducting a portion of your car-related expenses, such as gas, maintenance, insurance, and depreciation. You calculate the percentage of time the car is used for business and apply that percentage to the total expenses.

Remember to track your mileage, and keep records of when, where, and why you use your car for business.

3. Business Equipment and Supplies

Any equipment or supplies that you purchase for your side hustle are potentially tax-deductible. This includes computers, software, tools, office supplies, and furniture that are used for business purposes. These deductions can be especially helpful if you’ve had to purchase expensive equipment to get your business up and running.

If you buy something that will last more than a year (such as a laptop or camera), you may need to depreciate the cost over time, which means deducting a portion of the cost each year. On the other hand, if the equipment is used up within a year (like office supplies), you can deduct the full amount in the year you purchase it.

4. Professional Services

If you hire anyone to help with your business, such as accountants, attorneys, or virtual assistants, those services are tax-deductible. This includes both one-time consultations and ongoing services. The same applies if you use services like freelance platforms or hire contractors to assist with your business.

Keep in mind that any payments to independent contractors should be reported properly, especially if you pay them over $600 within a year.

5. Marketing and Advertising Costs

Marketing is an essential part of growing any side hustle, and luckily, many marketing expenses are tax-deductible. You can deduct costs related to advertising, online promotions, website hosting, and email marketing services. This also includes the cost of printing business cards, flyers, and other marketing materials.

Any fees you pay to platforms like Google Ads, Facebook Ads, or any other online advertising services can also be deducted, as long as they’re directly related to your business.

6. Education and Training

If you take courses, attend seminars, or purchase books and other resources to improve your skills for your side hustle, you may be able to deduct these expenses. The key is that the education must be directly related to the skills needed for your business.

For instance, if you’re a graphic designer and you take a course on new design software, the cost of that course can be deducted. However, a cooking class might not be deductible unless it is directly related to your side hustle (for example, if you’re a food blogger or caterer).

7. Insurance Premiums

If you have insurance for your side hustle, such as liability insurance or business property insurance, these premiums can be deductible. Additionally, if you’re a sole proprietor and pay for your health insurance, you may be able to deduct those premiums as well.

This deduction can apply to health insurance, long-term care insurance, and any business-related insurance that you’re paying for out-of-pocket.

8. Meals and Entertainment

While the IRS limits the types of meals and entertainment you can deduct, you may be able to deduct 50% of the cost of meals related to business activities. This can include meals with clients or business partners, as long as the primary purpose of the meal is business-related.

However, personal meals and entertainment are not deductible. So, it’s essential to be mindful of what qualifies for this deduction.

9. Travel Expenses

Travel costs associated with your business are another potential deduction. This includes flights, hotels, meals, and transportation while you’re traveling for business purposes. The key is that the trip must have a legitimate business purpose.

Keep in mind that if your trip combines both personal and business activities, you can only deduct the portion related to business. Be sure to keep detailed records of your travel expenses, including receipts and a clear breakdown of the business-related activities you engaged in during the trip.

Set Up a Separate Business Account

One of the most effective ways to ensure that your side hustle is tax-deductible is by setting up a separate business account. Having a dedicated checking account or credit card for your business makes it easier to track your expenses and avoids mixing business and personal finances. This is particularly important if your side hustle is generating significant revenue.

Additionally, using a separate account makes it simpler when it comes time to file taxes. You won’t have to sift through your personal bank statements to separate business expenses, which can be time-consuming and error-prone.

Stay Organized and Plan Ahead

The best way to ensure your side hustle remains tax-deductible is to stay organized and plan ahead. Throughout the year, maintain a system for tracking your expenses and keep up with your bookkeeping. At tax time, you’ll be prepared, and you’ll be able to take full advantage of the deductions available to you.

Consider working with an accountant or tax professional, especially if your side hustle has grown into a significant source of income. They can help ensure that you’re maximizing your deductions and staying compliant with tax laws.

Final Thoughts

Making your side hustle tax-deductible isn’t just about saving money—it’s about understanding the rules and taking advantage of the benefits available to you. By keeping detailed records, tracking your expenses, and claiming deductions appropriately, you can reduce your taxable income and increase the profitability of your side hustle.

So, get organized, stay diligent, and don’t be afraid to seek professional help if needed. By doing so, you’ll be able to grow your side hustle while keeping your taxes in check. Happy hustling!
