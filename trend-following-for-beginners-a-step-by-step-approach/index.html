Trend Following for Beginners: A Step-by-Step Approach


If you’ve ever watched the market race ahead and wondered how so many traders seem to catch big moves, trend following might just be your gateway into the crowd riding those waves. Designed for both rookies and old hands, this methodology stands out for its elegant simplicity – buy assets that are going up, sell assets that are going down – but, as always, the devil is in the details. Get a coffee and settle in, because by the end of this guide you’ll know the basics, the must-know concepts, how to spot real trends, when to act, and how to manage the inevitable bumps along the way.

What Is Trend Following?

Trend following is an investment strategy that aims to capitalize on the momentum of price movements in financial markets. The guiding principle is straightforward: markets have tendencies to move in sustained directions for periods of time, whether it’s up (an uptrend), down (a downtrend), or sideways (no clear trend). Trend followers enter positions in the direction of the trend and attempt to ride it until evidence shows it’s exhausted.

At its heart, the philosophy embraces three ideas:

  • Price contains all information needed.
  • Trends develop due to mass psychology, not random chance.
  • Reacting, not predicting, puts the odds in your favor.

While this sounds almost too simple, successful trend following requires discipline and a framework that survives real-world market noise.

Trend Following vs. Other Trading Styles

Unlike day trading, which seeks quick profits from intraday volatility, or value investing, which hunts for fundamentally undervalued assets, trend following is agnostic to asset fundamentals and short-term price swings. The main goal isn’t to buy at the bottom or sell at the top, but to capture the “meat” in the middle of a sustained move.

Let’s break down what sets trend following apart:

  • It’s systematic. Decisions are based on predefined rules, helping to eliminate emotion.
  • It focuses on what is, not what should be. You don’t care why a trend exists, just that it does.
  • Trend followers aren’t married to their positions. If the trend reverses, they quickly exit.
  • Trend following works on many markets: stocks, forex, commodities, and even crypto.

Key Principles of Trend Following

To effectively use trend following strategies, there are several foundational principles:

1. Cutting Losses Quickly

Large losses are the enemy. Every trade starts with an exit strategy. Strict stop-losses are the bedrock of survival.

2. Letting Winners Run

Don’t be in a hurry to close trades when you’re in profit; trends can last longer than many think. The big money comes from staying in the game as the trend unfolds.

3. Ignoring Predictions

Nobody knows where the market will go next. Trend followers make no attempt to predict tops or bottoms. The market decides.

4. Risk Management Comes First

Allocating a fixed percent of your capital per trade prevents devastation from a single losing streak. Diversify across assets, manage position sizes, and never double down out of frustration.

5. Process over Outcome

Focus on following your rules every day. A series of losses doesn’t mean there’s something wrong with your edge.

Essential Tools for Trend Followers

You don’t need a crystal ball, but a few basic tools make trend following execution far smoother:

Price Charts

Start with daily or weekly price charts. Visualizing trends is far easier here than with a wall of numbers.

Moving Averages

These smooth out price data and make trends more obvious. Popular choices include the 50-day and 200-day simple moving averages (SMA), or the 20-day and 50-day exponential moving averages (EMA).

When a short-term moving average crosses above a longer-term one, it’s often seen as a bullish signal (called a “golden cross”); the opposite is a bearish signal (“death cross”).

Breakouts

A trend often accelerates when price breaks above resistance or below support. Many trend followers enter as price closes beyond a recent high or low – the classic “breakout” method.

Technical Indicators

Beyond moving averages, you might use Average True Range (ATR) to gauge volatility, or Relative Strength Index (RSI) to identify overly stretched conditions (though classic trend followers often ignore overbought/oversold warnings).

Position Sizing Tools

A simple rule like the 1% rule (risk no more than 1% of capital per trade) keeps you in the game during unlucky streaks.

Step-by-Step: Building Your First Trend Following System

Ready to put together a beginner trend following strategy? Here’s how to craft a basic, robust system.

Step 1: Define Your Trading Universe

Decide if you’re focusing on stocks, forex, commodities, crypto, or a mix. Broader universes help find more trendy candidates, but keep it manageable if you’re starting out.

Step 2: Choose Timeframes

Daily or weekly timeframes work best for beginners. Shorter timeframes increase trading frequency and noise, not necessarily profit.

Step 3: Identify Trends

Use moving averages to identify trend direction. For instance:

  • Uptrend: Price > 50-day SMA, and 50-day SMA > 200-day SMA.
  • Downtrend: Price < 50-day SMA, and 50-day SMA < 200-day SMA.

Alternatively, look for new 20-day or 50-day highs (for longs) or lows (for shorts).

Step 4: Set Entry Rules

Some classic entry rules:

  • Buy if price closes at a 20-day high and moving averages support an uptrend.
  • Sell short if price closes at a 20-day low and moving averages confirm a downtrend.

Step 5: Place Stops and Exits

This is vital. Use ATR-based stops (e.g., 2x average true range below/above your entry), or below the recent swing low/high. Strictly obey these stops.

For exits, you could:

  • Exit when price closes below the 50-day moving average (for long trades).
  • Use a trailing stop to exit only when the trend bends against you.

Step 6: Size Your Position

Never risk more than 1–2% of your capital per trade. Calculate position sizes to match your stop distance.

Step 7: Diversify

Hold multiple uncorrelated positions when possible, so no single trade dominates your account.

Step 8: Review Regularly

Keep a trading journal. Every week, review what’s working, what isn’t, and if you’re following your plan.

The Psychology of Trend Following

It’s not just technical. Trend following success relies heavily on psychology and discipline.

Handling False Starts

Most breakouts will fail. Yes, you read that right. Expect whipsaws – lots of small losses before you catch a big trend. Don’t get discouraged by “being wrong” frequently.

Sticking With Winners

Watching a big open profit shrink hurts, but prematurely closing winners can sabotage your edge. Trust your system’s exit criteria.

Embracing Uncertainty

Markets are noisy. Even a great trend following strategy will have periods of drawdown and lackluster performance. Emotional detachment and consistency matter more than perfect predictions.

Coping With Drawdowns

Accept that losses add up, and sometimes winning trades can be rare. This is why risk management and position sizing are not optional.

Real-World Trend Following Examples

A few examples put theory into perspective:

Example 1: The S&P 500 Long Trend

Suppose you use a 200-day simple moving average rule: buy when price is above, sell when below. Over the decades, this basic system would have avoided some of the market’s worst drawdowns, and captured big bull market rallies.

Example 2: Currency Trends

Forex markets are renowned for their strong, persistent trends. Buying EUR/USD when price makes a 52-week high and exiting below a 20-day low is a classic approach for currency trend followers.

Example 3: Commodity Booms

Commodity markets tend to experience dramatic surges and collapses. Trend followers often catch these moves riding them up (oil in 2008, gold in 2011, etc.) and getting out when the trend falters.

Advantages and Weaknesses of Trend Following

Advantages

  • Works across asset classes and markets.
  • No need for in-depth fundamental research.
  • Has historically produced profits during major bull and bear markets.
  • Rules-based, so it eliminates a lot of emotional decision-making.

Weaknesses

  • Many small losses before big winners.
  • Markets can chop sideways for extended periods, bleeding your account with whipsaws.
  • Performance may lag “buy and hold” during certain regimes.
  • Requires strict discipline – emotional traders struggle.

Common Mistakes for Beginners

Learning trend following comes with a few traps:

  • Trading too short-term. Intraday “trends” are often just noise.
  • Overleveraging. Using margin can blow out your account quickly during losing streaks.
  • Ignoring stops. Not sticking to your planned exits when trades turn against you.
  • Curve-fitting. Over-optimizing a system for past data cripples future performance.

Must-Read Books on Trend Following

If the subject grabs you, these books have stood the test of time:

  • Trend Following by Michael Covel
  • Following the Trend by Andreas Clenow
  • Stocks on the Move by Andreas Clenow
  • The Complete TurtleTrader by Michael Covel

Software and Resources for Trend Followers

Getting started is smoother with the right tools:

  • TradingView and StockCharts: Excellent for charting and backtesting trend strategies.
  • MetaTrader (for forex): Highly customizable for systematic rules.
  • Excel or Google Sheets: For journaling and risk management calculations.

Many brokers now offer paper trading accounts – practice your trend following system without risking real money at first.

Getting Started: Practical Tips

  • Start slow: Test your system on historical data, then use a simulated or very small live account.
  • Track every trade: Keep a journal with entry/exit rules and your emotions at the time.
  • Monitor yourself: The system is only as good as your willingness to follow it.
  • Stay curious: Markets evolve. Adapt and learn as you go.

Final Thoughts

Trend following isn’t magic, but it does stack the odds in your favor by harnessing human behavior and price momentum. If you’re willing to put ego aside, be patient, and stick to your rules, you stand a fair shot at catching big moves instead of watching them fly by.

Remember, nearly every professional trading legend has credited at least part of their success to a systematic approach. Trend following offers a powerful, time-tested path for any beginner willing to learn the ropes, accept losses as part of the process, and soldier on until the odds finally tilt their way.

Don’t overthink it. Markets trend, then they don’t. But when they do, the prepared trader catches the wave. Now that you’ve learned the basics, it’s time to put them into practice. The next big move is always just around the corner.
