How to Reduce Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) for Better Cash Flow


Managing days sales outstanding (DSO) is a crucial aspect of maintaining a healthy cash flow for businesses. The highest possible DSO can create financial distress, impose stringent financial discipline, and disrupt the smooth operation of your company. Days sales outstanding is the average time it takes for a business to collect its accounts receivable, which can be a significant factor affecting the cash flow of a business. Controlling DSO is necessary to reduce the usage of working capital, decrease payment delays, and maintain consistent cash flow. Good DSO management also helps in securing more favorable payment terms with credit suppliers, avoiding disputes with clients, and reducing the bad debt potential. Good payment practices also promote faith and trust between your business and its clients, leading to improved customer relationships and increasing loyalty. Understanding Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) DSO is calculated by dividing the total accounts receivable by the average daily sales. It is usually expressed in days. Accounts receivable includes any receivables that are over 30 days old, and these can impact the revenues and cash flows of the business. Keeping a check on DSO can be a way to save your business from unexpected shortages in money or valuation shortages that companies could constantly face. It is estimated that one third of sales might occasionally become stuck in debt and fail to be settled on time due to many factors including lack of good posture, technology issues, miscommunications, among many more of which may be entirely preventable. Focusing on streamlining accounts receivable management processes is crucial in criticising suitable cost-saving actions that benefit businesses instantly. It is worth noting that industries such as energy, construction and financial sectors tend to have long DSO rates, accounting for up to 60 or more days.
