The Best Tax Strategies for Newlyweds


Getting married is one of the most exciting and transformative moments in life. Along with the joy of joining your lives together, there’s the practical side of combining finances, adjusting to new responsibilities, and—unfortunately—navigating the complexities of taxes. While taxes may not be the most thrilling part of marriage, understanding tax strategies can save you money and provide long-term financial benefits.

In this guide, we’ll dive deep into the best tax strategies for newlyweds, helping you make informed decisions that will maximize your savings and set you up for financial success. From choosing the right filing status to making the most of deductions and credits, here’s everything you need to know to optimize your taxes as a married couple.

1. Understand Your Filing Status Options

One of the first and most important tax decisions newlyweds need to make is choosing their filing status. The IRS offers two primary filing statuses for married couples: Married Filing Jointly and Married Filing Separately.

Married Filing Jointly

Filing jointly generally offers more tax advantages, and for most couples, it’s the best option. By filing jointly, you and your spouse combine your incomes and deductions, which often results in a lower overall tax liability. Here are some of the benefits of filing jointly:

  • Higher Income Limits for Tax Brackets: Married couples filing jointly benefit from higher income limits, which can place you in a lower tax bracket than if you were filing separately.
  • Eligibility for Tax Credits and Deductions: You become eligible for a wide range of tax credits and deductions, including the Child Tax Credit, Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC), and the American Opportunity Tax Credit.
  • Standard Deduction: The standard deduction for married couples filing jointly is typically double that of single filers, providing a significant tax break.

Married Filing Separately

While most married couples benefit from filing jointly, there are situations where filing separately might make sense. Here are a few reasons you might choose this option:

  • Separation of Finances: If you and your spouse want to keep your finances entirely separate, filing separately may make sense.
  • Tax Implications of One Spouse’s High Medical Expenses: If one spouse has significant medical expenses, filing separately may allow them to claim a larger deduction for medical expenses.
  • Student Loan Repayment: If one spouse is on an income-driven repayment plan for student loans, filing separately might reduce the monthly payment.

However, it’s important to note that filing separately often leads to higher taxes overall because you lose out on many tax credits and deductions.

Making the Choice

For most newlyweds, the best option is Married Filing Jointly. However, it’s important to evaluate your unique financial situation with a tax professional, especially if one spouse has significant student loan debt or high medical expenses.

2. Take Advantage of Tax Bracket Savings

Tax brackets play a significant role in determining how much tax you pay. When you’re married and filing jointly, your combined income is taxed at a higher threshold than if you were filing separately.

For example, the income threshold for the 12% tax bracket for a married couple filing jointly is higher than for a single filer, which means you can keep more of your income in lower tax brackets. This can save you a substantial amount of money.

Strategy: Minimize High-Income Years

One of the most effective ways to save on taxes is to minimize your taxable income during high-income years. Newlyweds often experience a jump in income, especially if both partners are working or if there’s a change in one partner’s job status. If this is the case, consider the following strategies:

  • Contribute to Retirement Accounts: Contributing to tax-deferred retirement accounts such as a 401(k) or Traditional IRA can lower your taxable income and reduce your overall tax liability.
  • Utilize Flexible Spending Accounts (FSAs): Contributing to an FSA for healthcare or dependent care can reduce your taxable income.

3. Maximize Deductions and Credits

Tax deductions and credits are two powerful ways to reduce your tax burden. For newlyweds, there are several opportunities to maximize these benefits, especially when filing jointly.

Standard vs. Itemized Deductions

When filing taxes, you can either take the standard deduction or itemize your deductions. For 2025, the standard deduction for married couples filing jointly is $27,700, which is significantly higher than the deduction for single filers.

Itemizing deductions makes sense if your eligible expenses exceed the standard deduction. Some common deductible expenses include:

  • Mortgage interest
  • Charitable donations
  • Medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of your adjusted gross income (AGI)
  • State and local taxes (up to $10,000)

However, most newlyweds will benefit from the standard deduction, as it’s often higher than the total of itemized expenses.

Tax Credits

Tax credits are even more valuable than deductions because they directly reduce the amount of tax you owe. Some credits available to married couples include:

  • Child Tax Credit: If you have children, you can receive up to $2,000 per qualifying child under the age of 17.
  • Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC): This credit is designed for lower- to moderate-income taxpayers and can significantly reduce the amount of tax owed, or even result in a refund.
  • American Opportunity Tax Credit: If you or your spouse are pursuing higher education, you may be eligible for this credit, which provides up to $2,500 per year for qualified educational expenses.

Strategy: Plan for Tax Credits and Deductions

Be proactive in planning your finances to ensure you maximize credits and deductions. For instance, if you’re expecting a child or pursuing education, start planning early to take advantage of available credits. This can involve contributing to educational savings plans or increasing charitable contributions throughout the year.

4. Review and Adjust Withholding

When you get married, you may need to adjust your tax withholding. This refers to the amount of federal income tax your employer takes out of your paycheck. If you and your spouse are both employed, the combined income might push you into a higher tax bracket, resulting in higher tax withholding.

Adjusting Withholding to Avoid Surprises

If you want to avoid underpaying or overpaying your taxes, consider adjusting your withholding. You can do this by filling out a new Form W-4 with your employer, which allows you to specify the amount of tax to be withheld from your paycheck. If you expect to have additional income or qualify for more deductions and credits, you can adjust your withholding to reflect that.

5. Consider Estate Planning and Beneficiary Designations

Marriage can have significant implications for estate planning. Newlyweds should consider creating or updating their will and other estate planning documents to reflect their new marital status. This is particularly important for tax purposes, as married couples enjoy certain estate tax benefits that unmarried individuals do not.

Spousal Exemption

Married couples can transfer assets between each other without incurring estate or gift taxes, thanks to the unlimited marital deduction. This means that if one spouse passes away, the surviving spouse can inherit assets without paying estate taxes, regardless of the value of the estate.

Review Beneficiary Designations

It’s also essential to review the beneficiary designations on life insurance policies, retirement accounts, and other assets. After getting married, you may want to name your spouse as the primary beneficiary on these accounts to ensure that your estate is properly distributed according to your wishes.

6. Plan for Future Tax Implications

Finally, as newlyweds, it’s important to think about future tax implications, especially when it comes to retirement, homeownership, and children. Here are a few long-term strategies to keep in mind:

  • Retirement Savings: Consider opening a Roth IRA for additional retirement savings, which provides tax-free growth and tax-free withdrawals in retirement. Contributing to a 401(k) or a Roth IRA will not only help you save for the future but also lower your current tax liability.
  • Homeownership: If you plan to buy a home, be aware of the mortgage interest deduction and property tax deductions that may be available to you.
  • Children: If you plan to have children, start preparing for the tax credits and deductions that come with parenthood, such as the Child Tax Credit and the Dependent Care Credit.

Conclusion

Navigating taxes as newlyweds can seem overwhelming, but with the right strategies in place, you can minimize your tax liability and take advantage of the many benefits available to married couples. From choosing the right filing status to maximizing deductions, credits, and retirement savings, these tax strategies can help you and your spouse build a strong financial foundation for the years to come.

Take the time to assess your unique financial situation, adjust your withholding, and make proactive decisions to ensure you’re making the most of your tax benefits. By planning ahead and staying informed, you can make tax season much less stressful and more financially rewarding.
