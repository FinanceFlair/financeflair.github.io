How to Choose Between the Income-Based Repayment Plan and PAYE


Navigating the world of student loan repayment can be complex, especially when deciding which repayment plan suits your financial situation best. Two popular options, the Income-Based Repayment (IBR) plan and the Pay As You Earn (PAYE) plan, often come into consideration for borrowers aiming to manage their federal student loans based on income rather than fixed monthly payments. Understanding the critical differences, eligibility criteria, benefits, and potential drawbacks of each plan can empower you to make a well-informed choice. This comprehensive guide explores everything you need to know about IBR and PAYE, helping you select the repayment strategy that aligns perfectly with your financial goals.

Understanding Income-Based Repayment (IBR)

Income-Based Repayment is a federal student loan repayment plan designed to ease the monthly repayment burden for borrowers by capping payments at a percentage of their discretionary income. The main appeal of IBR is that payments adjust based on your current income and family size, providing flexibility and potentially lowering your monthly obligations when your earnings are modest.

Eligibility for IBR

  • You must have federal student loans that fall under the “eligible loan” category, primarily including Direct Loans and some Federal Family Education Loan (FFEL) Program loans.
  • You demonstrate a partial financial hardship, meaning your standard 10-year repayment amount exceeds 15% of your discretionary income.
  • Both new and older borrowers can qualify under different conditions. New borrowers after July 1, 2014, have slightly different payment structures and forgiveness timelines compared to those who borrowed earlier.

How Payments Are Calculated

Under IBR, your monthly payments are generally capped at 15% of your discretionary income if you borrowed prior to July 1, 2014. For newer borrowers who meet the “new borrower” definition after July 1, 2014, the payment cap is 10%. Discretionary income is calculated as the difference between your adjusted gross income (AGI) and 150% of the federal poverty guideline for your family size and state of residence.

Forgiveness and Payback Period

IBR offers forgiveness after 25 years of qualifying payments for borrowers who began loans before July 1, 2014. For those who borrowed afterward, the forgiveness period shortens to 20 years. Upon completion of these terms, any remaining loan balance can be forgiven. However, keep in mind that student loan forgiveness under IBR may be considered taxable income under current IRS rules, potentially resulting in a significant tax bill.

Understanding Pay As You Earn (PAYE)

Pay As You Earn is another income-driven repayment option introduced as an improved and more generous alternative to IBR. PAYE aims to provide lower monthly payments while maintaining similar forgiveness terms but with enhancements beneficial to many borrowers.

Eligibility for PAYE

  • You must be a “new borrower” as of October 1, 2007, with no outstanding federal student loan balance prior to October 1, 2011.
  • Borrowers must demonstrate partial financial hardship, where their standard 10-year repayment amount is higher than 10% of discretionary income.
  • Eligible loan types include Direct Loans but exclude FFEL loans unless consolidated into Direct Loans.

How Payments Are Calculated

Payments under PAYE are capped at 10% of discretionary income, generally lower than IBR for older borrowers. Like IBR, discretionary income is income above 150% of the federal poverty level, adjusted for your family size and location.

Forgiveness and Payback Period

PAYE offers forgiveness after 20 years of qualifying payments, which is five years sooner than IBR for many borrowers. As with IBR, any forgiven balance may be treated as taxable income. However, under the current tax laws in effect until at least 2025, certain forgiveness balances related to public service jobs are excluded from taxable income.

Factors to Consider When Choosing Between IBR and PAYE

Choosing between IBR and PAYE boils down to your specific loan details, income, career plans, and repayment goals. Several key factors should guide your decision:

1. Are You a New Borrower?

If you first borrowed federal student loans after October 1, 2007, and did not have any outstanding balances before October 1, 2011, PAYE is an option designed especially for you. PAYE generally offers lower monthly payments at 10% of discretionary income and a shorter forgiveness period of 20 years. If you are an older borrower with loans predating these milestones, IBR may be your choice since PAYE won’t be available.

2. Your Monthly Payment Affordability

PAYE often results in lower monthly payments than IBR for borrowers who meet the eligibility since it caps payments at 10% versus IBR’s 15% for older loans. However, if you qualify for IBR as a newer borrower, your payment cap also becomes 10%, making the two plans quite similar. Use loan simulators or the Federal Student Aid Repayment Estimator tools to estimate payments under both plans.

3. Loan Types and Consolidation

If you hold mainly Direct Loans, both plans are accessible. However, if you have FFEL loans, you must consolidate them into Direct Loans to qualify for PAYE. IBR accepts certain FFEL loans without consolidation, so that’s an important consideration when your loan portfolio is mixed.

4. Forgiveness Timeline

If you aim to have your loans forgiven sooner, PAYE offers forgiveness after 20 years compared to IBR’s 25 years for older borrowers. For newer borrowers, both plans have forgiveness at 20 years. The difference in timing can change your overall repayment strategy, especially if you’re pursuing a public service loan forgiveness (PSLF) strategy.

5. Potential Tax Consequences

While loan forgiveness is attractive, it’s essential to plan for possible tax implications. After the repayment period, the remaining loan balance forgiven under either plan is currently counted as taxable income unless legislative changes apply or you qualify for complete forgiveness under PSLF, which is tax-free.

How to Decide Step By Step

Step 1: Assess Your Loan Profile

Gather detailed information about your federal loans: types, amounts, interest rates, and when each loan was disbursed. Knowing your loan portfolio helps determine which plans you qualify for.

Step 2: Calculate Your Discretionary Income

Using your current Adjusted Gross Income (AGI), subtract 150% of the federal poverty guideline for your household size. This amount is your discretionary income, which is the foundation for calculating your payments under both IBR and PAYE.

Step 3: Estimate Monthly Payments Under Both Plans

Use online calculators or the Federal Student Aid website’s repayment estimator to calculate potential monthly payments under both IBR and PAYE. Compare figures carefully, considering how monthly payments fit with your current budget.

Step 4: Consider Your Career and Long-Term Plans

If you plan to work in public service or nonprofit organizations, think about how both plans work with PSLF. Both IBR and PAYE qualify for PSLF, but the time frames and payment amounts can affect how much you repay before forgiveness.

Step 5: Review Forgiveness Strategy and Tax Implications

Evaluate how soon forgiveness will occur and what tax liabilities you might face on the forgiven amount. If you expect significant taxable income from forgiveness, proactive tax planning might be necessary.

Step 6: Make Your Choice and Enroll

Once you’ve chosen a plan, contact your loan servicer or apply online through the federal student loan portal. Remember to recertify income and family size annually, as this can significantly adjust your required payments.

Common Questions and Myths About IBR and PAYE

Q: Can I switch between IBR and PAYE?
Yes, borrowers can switch repayment plans if they become eligible for a plan that better fits their financial situation. Switching requires reapplication and income documentation.

Q: Does either plan reduce the total interest on my loan?
Neither plan directly reduces your interest rate; however, lower payments could mean that more interest capitalizes over time. It’s important to evaluate how interest accrual might impact total repayment costs.

Q: Will using IBR or PAYE hurt my credit score?
Choosing an income-driven repayment plan itself doesn’t negatively affect your credit. Timely payments under these plans continue to build positive credit history.

Q: Is there any way to avoid tax on forgiven loan balances?
Certain borrowers qualifying for Public Service Loan Forgiveness do not pay taxes on forgiven amounts. Otherwise, until any changes in tax law, forgiven amounts under IBR or PAYE generally count as taxable income.

Summary: Choosing Your Best Path Forward

Choosing between the Income-Based Repayment Plan and Pay As You Earn boils down to your eligibility, loan makeup, and long-term repayment goals. PAYE often provides lower monthly payments and a shorter forgiveness timeline for newer borrowers while requiring stricter eligibility rules. IBR offers broader access but may come with higher payments or longer forgiveness periods depending on your borrowing history.

By carefully evaluating your current finances, career plans, and loan types—with attention to repayment tools and forgiveness implications—you can select the plan that eases your payment burden while helping you progress toward debt freedom. Regularly revisiting your repayment strategy ensures you take advantage of any new opportunities or changes in loan policies.

Making an informed choice between IBR and PAYE is a powerful step towards taking control of your student loans and building a stable financial future.

If you need assistance estimating your payments or understanding your eligibility further, your loan servicer’s representatives and federal student aid resources are valuable starting points. Taking the initiative to understand your options keeps you in charge of your repayment journey.
