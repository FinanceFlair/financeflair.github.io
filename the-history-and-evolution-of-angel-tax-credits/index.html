The History and Evolution of Angel Tax Credits


Angel tax credits have become a critical tool for entrepreneurs and investors seeking to bridge the funding gap in the early stages of startup development. This financial innovation has undergone significant transformations since its inception, influencing the startup ecosystem and paving the way for innovation and growth. This article delves into the historical background of angel tax credits, their evolution over time, and the growth they have experienced in recent years. Early Origins: A Stepping Stone for Venture Capital The concept of tax credits to incentivize investment dates back to the 1960s in the United States. During this period, policymakers recognized the challenge of providing capital to small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) without driving them into debt. The Treaties on the Prevention of Double Taxation, a global agreement aimed at eliminating tax barriers to trade, set the foundation for future practices like tax credits. In the early 1980s, the US passed the Economic Recovery Tax Act, which introduced the concept of offsetting tax losses against gains from capital gains. The legislation leveraged tax credits as a method of making capital more readily available to businesses. Initially, these incentives were extended to encourage private investment in venture capital funds and small businesses. With the Credit for Qualified Small Business Stock (SQSB) introduced in 2004, the approach broadened to become inclusive of individual investors, firm-specific angel investing tax credits emerged to complement the emerging US tax credit programs. 2007 and Beyond: Global Expansion Post the 2007 global economic crisis, governments sought ways to address pressing economic challenges and stimulate growth. Innovative financing mechanisms such as tax credits became prominent in supporting vulnerable segments of the economy. Recognizing the impact of angel investing on the growth of startups, some countries like the UK established schemes to encourage investment. The Irish government spearheaded a tax break initiative in 2007, directing up to 25% of the investment towards preferred publicly issued shares. Governments such as Canada, introduced tax credits under the Investment of Small Firm Salary Equity (ISHares) Act in 2010, exempting growth-stage investments from ordinary income steps in attention in tax products. This laid the groundwork for leveraging private investment channels to build profitable businesses.
