Building a Diversified Trend Following Strategy


Trend following has long been a cornerstone technique in quantitative trading and systematic investing. It thrives on the premise that markets exhibit persistent trends, and by identifying these movements early, traders can capture significant returns over time. However, the challenge lies not only in spotting trends but also in constructing a robust and diversified strategy that can withstand market fluctuations and varying economic regimes.

In this deep dive, we’ll explore how to build a diversified trend following strategy that amplifies strengths while minimizing risks, boosting resilience across asset classes and market environments.

Understanding the Core of Trend Following

At its simplest, trend following involves buying assets that are trending upwards and selling or shorting those on a downtrend. Unlike mean reversion strategies, which bet on prices returning to an average, trend followers expect trends to continue rather than reverse abruptly.

Key elements of trend following include:

  • Price-based signals: Trend identification typically relies on moving averages, breakout systems, or momentum indicators.
  • Systematic execution: To remove emotion, trend following often uses predefined rules and automated trading.
  • Risk management: Position sizing and stop-loss mechanisms are crucial to preserving capital.

This foundation explains why trend following thrives during sustained bull or bear markets but can struggle during sideways, choppy conditions.

Why Diversification Matters in Trend Following

While trend following has yielded admirable returns over decades, relying on a single asset or style can expose you to significant drawdowns. Diversification helps spread risk across multiple dimensions:

  1. Asset classes: Stocks, commodities, currencies, and bonds each display unique trend behaviors.
  2. Timeframes: Shorter horizons capture quicker trends, while longer horizons smooth noise and identify enduring movements.
  3. Markets and geographies: Different regions may be out of phase or driven by unique macroeconomic factors.

Combining these elements creates a smoother equity curve and reduces vulnerability to regime shifts.

Step 1: Selecting Appropriate Markets and Instruments

An effective trend following strategy typically spans a broad universe of liquid markets. Consider the following asset classes and instruments:

  • Equity indices: S&P 500, Russell 2000, Euro Stoxx 50.
  • Commodities: Crude oil, gold, copper, agricultural products like wheat and corn.
  • Currencies: Major pairs such as EUR/USD, USD/JPY, GBP/USD.
  • Fixed income: Government bonds like U.S. Treasuries, German Bunds.

Each market moves due to distinct economic drivers. For example, commodity trends might hinge on supply shocks or geopolitical events, while currency trends reflect monetary policies and trade flows.

Liquidity and transaction costs are vital considerations. Highly liquid futures contracts or ETFs often provide the best balance for trend following given their tight spreads and low slippage.

Step 2: Defining Trend Metrics

At the heart of trend following lie quantitative signals to detect emerging trends. Some common and effective trend metrics include:

Moving Average Crossovers

This simple technique uses two moving averages - one short-term, one long-term. When the shorter average crosses above the longer, it’s a buy signal; when crossing below, a sell or short signal.

  • Example: 50-day MA crosses 200-day MA.
  • Benefits: Easy to compute and interpret.
  • Drawbacks: Can lag during rapid moves, prone to whipsaws.

Donchian Channel Breakouts

The Donchian channel marks the highest high and lowest low over a lookback period.

  • A price crossing above the upper band signals an upward breakout.
  • A price breaking below the lower band signals a downward breakout.

This method emphasizes breakout confirmation, making it applicable to momentum-driven trends.

Rate of Change (ROC) or Momentum

Measures the percentage change in price over a given period.

  • Positive momentum suggests strength; negative implies weakening trend.
  • Helps filter signals to reduce false entries.

Average Directional Index (ADX)

Reflects trend strength rather than direction.

  • High ADX values confirm strong trending conditions.
  • Can be paired with directional signals to improve timing.

Step 3: Incorporating Multiple Timeframes

Short-term trends (e.g., 5 to 20 days) often capture quick market moves but increase exposure to noise and false signals. Conversely, long-term trends (e.g., 50 to 200 days) smooth data but may delay entry and exit, sacrificing potential gains during volatile phases.

A balanced trend following strategy monitors multiple timeframes simultaneously:

  • Primary trend: Typically longer-term, defines overall market direction.
  • Intermediate trend: Medium-term timeframes to confirm momentum.
  • Secondary or tactical trend: Shorter-term signals for fine-tuning entries.

By aligning positions only when multiple trends concur, you filter out less probable signals and enhance the probability of success.

Step 4: Risk Management and Position Sizing

Without robust risk controls, even the best trend following system can suffer catastrophic losses. Key aspects include:

  • Volatility-adjusted position sizing: Allocate capital based on the current volatility of each instrument. More volatile markets receive proportionally smaller allocations.
  • Stop losses: Mechanical stops tightened around recent volatility levels limit drawdowns.
  • Portfolio-level risk cap: Define a maximum risk threshold for the combined portfolio to protect capital during correlated downturns.
  • Diversified exposure: Avoid concentration risks by capping position sizes in particular markets or sectors.

For example, if crude oil experiences heavy swings, your position size should shrink accordingly to reduce total risk exposure.

Step 5: Combining Strategies for Diversification

A single trend following rule may perform well in specific markets but fail elsewhere or in certain regimes. Combining multiple trend signals enhances robustness.

  • Mix moving average crossovers with breakout systems to capture different trend styles.
  • Blend technical momentum with fundamental overlays such as macroeconomic trends to improve signal quality.
  • Utilize adaptive algorithms that tweak parameters based on recent volatility or regime identification.

Backtesting combined strategies against historical data highlights synergies and helps discard poor combinations early.

Step 6: Implementation and Execution

Building the algorithm is only half the battle. Proper execution ensures realized profits match theoretical expectations.

  • Data integrity: Use clean, reliable price data with sufficient history.
  • Slippage and transaction costs modeling: Factor realistic costs to avoid overestimating strategy performance.
  • Automated order execution: Automated entries and exits reduce latency and emotional bias.
  • Regular strategy review: Markets evolve, so periodically reassess parameters and assets.

Emphasize consistent, disciplined application. Deviations often cause unexpected drawdowns and volatility.

Step 7: Monitoring and Adjusting for Market Regimes

Markets cycle between trending and ranging regimes. Some methods to identify regimes include:

  • Volatility filters: High volatility often indicates trending conditions.
  • Trend strength indicators: ADX or similar metrics can gauge trend validity.
  • Machine learning clustering: Segment market regimes based on multiple features.

When the system detects a non-trending or choppy regime, it may reduce exposure or switch to alternative models optimized for such environments.

Practical Example: A Hypothetical Diversified Trend Following Portfolio

Imagine a diversified portfolio constructed as follows:

  • Equities: 30% allocation across global equity indices using a 50/200-day moving average crossover.
  • Commodities: 30% split among oil, gold, and agricultural futures with Donchian breakout signals on 20-day channels.
  • Currencies: 20% exposure to major FX pairs employing a momentum oscillator combined with volatility-adjusted position sizing.
  • Fixed Income: 20% in government bonds guided by a 100-day moving average trend filter.

Position sizes adjust monthly based on trailing 20-day volatility. Stop losses are set at 1.5x ATR (Average True Range).

Backtested over 20 years, such a portfolio typically demonstrates reduced drawdowns relative to single-market strategies and smoother monthly returns.

Challenges and Common Pitfalls

Building a diversified trend following system is complex and requires vigilance against:

  • Overfitting: Tailoring parameters too closely to past data reduces future robustness.
  • Ignoring transaction costs: Frequent trading can erode profits in less liquid markets.
  • Underestimating correlation during crises: Assets often become more correlated when trends reverse rapidly.
  • Neglecting psychological discipline: Even systematic strategies require consistent execution and review.

Conclusion

Building a diversified trend following strategy is both an art and a science. By leveraging multiple asset classes, timeframes, and trend metrics, traders can capture persistent market movements while smoothing risk and drawdowns. Incorporating rigorous risk management, adaptive elements, and disciplined execution creates an investment framework well-suited to navigate diverse market environments.

Success requires continuous learning, adaptation, and a commitment to process over prediction. When done thoughtfully, diversified trend following can become a powerful addition to any investment toolkit, delivering robust returns across cycles.

Ready to start crafting your own diversified trend following approach? Begin by expanding your market universe, testing various trend identification methods, and rigorously managing risk. The markets reward patience, discipline, and diversification – all pillars of a successful trend following system.
